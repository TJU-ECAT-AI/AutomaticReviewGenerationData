How do factors such as lattice oxygen species and H2 pretreatment influence catalyst performance and deactivation in PDH reactions?

Advantages and limitations of MCM-41 and SBA-15 supported VOx and CrOx catalysts in PDH reactions compared to traditional metal oxide supports lie in their ordered mesoporous structure. This structure provides high surface area and uniform pore size, enhancing the dispersion of metal species and improving their access to reactants. The introduction of alkaline earth metals like magnesium (Mg) through a one-pot synthesis method improves the dispersion of metal particles, strengthens the interaction between the metal and support, and reduces the risk of carbon deposition. 

Magnesium incorporation modifies the metal phase structure, stabilizes the oxidation state of tin (Sn), and facilitates the migration of coke deposits from active metal sites to the support surface. This enhances the catalytic activity and stability of the catalyst. However, excessive magnesium can weaken the interaction between Sn and support, reducing the catalytic performance. 

Lattice oxygen species play a crucial role in the catalytic activity and deactivation. In PDH reactions, the presence of oxygen leads to the formation of metal oxides, which can inhibit the reaction by blocking active sites. H2 pretreatment can reduce the oxygen content on the catalyst surface, thereby improving the deactivation resistance and increasing the selectivity of propene production. 

In summary, MCM-41 and SBA-15 supported VOx and CrOx catalysts offer superior performance in PDH reactions due to their ordered mesoporous structure, enhanced metal dispersion, and improved interaction between metal species and support. Factors like lattice oxygen species and H2 pretreatment significantly influence the catalyst's performance and deactivation, with careful optimization leading to increased selectivity and activity.
