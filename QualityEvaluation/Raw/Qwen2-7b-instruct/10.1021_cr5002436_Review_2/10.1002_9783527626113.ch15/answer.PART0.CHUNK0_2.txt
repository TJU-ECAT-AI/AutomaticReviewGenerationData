1. Chromium oxide-based catalysts have been utilized since their discovery in 1933, primarily for dehydrogenation processes. Key industrial applications include converting light alkanes to alkenes, particularly propane to propene and butane to butenes. Research focuses on understanding catalyst structure, active sites, support materials, promoters, and reaction mechanisms to enhance efficiency and selectivity.

2. The dehydrogenation process involves the endothermic conversion of light alkanes into alkenes. This process is equilibrium limited at temperatures below 1000 K, necessitating periodic regeneration of the catalyst through oxidation to maintain its effectiveness. 

3. A variety of characterization techniques, such as X-ray powder diffraction (XRD), UV-Visible diffuse reflectance spectroscopy (UV-Vis DRS), Raman spectroscopy, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR), infrared spectroscopy, and temperature programmed reduction (TPR), have been employed to analyze both fresh and used catalysts.

4. Catalyst preparation typically involves impregnation of alumina or zirconia with chromium-containing solutions like ammonium dichromate, chromic acid, or chromium nitrate. The surface species transition from monochromate to polychromate to α-Cr2O3 as the loading increases.

5. The predominant species in an as-prepared catalyst are chromium (III) and (VI) oxidation states. However, recent studies suggest that chromium (III) plays a crucial role as the active species in the catalytic process.

6. The classic dehydrogenation process uses a regenerating gas in the same direction as the process gas, leading to an initial temperature gradient at the feed and exit. The operating conditions for current commercial reactors typically involve 0.3 to 0.5 atmospheres and temperatures ranging from 823 to 923 Kelvin.

7. The retention of residual hydrogen in the catalyst after reduction can be confirmed through pulsed experiments with deutero-propane or deuterium, demonstrating hydrogen's role in the activation process.

8. The initial suggestion of chromium (II) as the active species has been challenged by recent evidence supporting chromium (III) as the more prominent active site.

9. The active species remains a subject of debate, with no definitive statement regarding the specific chromium oxidation state.

10. Butane dehydrogenation primarily results in 1-butene as the product, with some conversion of other components like butanes to butenes.

11. Both chromium oxide and vanadium oxide-based catalysts experience carbon deposition during dehydrogenation, leading to the formation of polynuclear aromatic compounds. 

12. The Raman spectrum of a coked catalyst reveals characteristic bands associated with polynuclear aromatic carbon, providing insights into the nature of the carbon deposits.

13. Extended use of catalysts leads to the production of various polynuclear aromatic compounds, including pyrene, fluorethene, benzanthracene, and perylene, which contribute to catalyst deactivation.

14. Carbonaceous material on the catalyst surface is periodically desorbed as methane during the dehydrogenation process, indicating a dynamic surface chemistry.

15. The deactivation process in butane dehydrogenation involves the formation of coke species, which can be desorbed at room temperature with the presence of oxygen, indicating an oxidative displacement mechanism.

16. Vanadia catalysts, despite not being used commercially, have been extensively studied for their potential in dehydrogenation reactions. Their structure under redox conditions is crucial for catalytic performance, with reduced vanadia species proposed as active sites.

17. Techniques like Temperature Programmed Reduction (TPR), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), Electron Spin Resonance (ESR), UV-Visible DRS, X-ray absorption fine structure spectroscopy (XAFS), and Raman spectroscopy have been used to investigate the valence state and molecular structure of supported vanadia catalysts.

18. Challenges in detecting reduced vanadia species using conventional Raman measurements have led to the exploration of using multiple excitation wavelengths for more complete structural information.

19. Studies have identified a distribution of vanadia species on the catalyst surface, including isolated VOx species, polyvanadates, and V2O5, with the latter forming at higher surface densities.

20. UV Raman spectroscopy offers a means to detect reduced vanadium oxide species, especially at low loadings, by avoiding fluorescence interference and enhancing sensitivity.

21. The onset of reduction in vanadia catalysts follows the weight loading, with complete reduction occurring by 873 K. The weight loss during reduction correlates with the amount of oxygen removed, maintaining a 1:1 ratio between vanadium and oxygen atoms.

22. The regeneration of vanadia catalysts via oxidation restores the catalyst's structure, with the surface area reducing post-regeneration, suggesting transformations in the nature of the support material.

23. A structure-function relationship has been established between the nature and amount of coke formed and the structure of vanadia catalysts, highlighting the importance of catalyst design in dehydrogenation processes.

24. Both chromium and vanadium oxide catalysts have evolved significantly since their discovery, driven by advancements in understanding their chemistry, structure, and application in dehydrogenation processes. Ongoing research aims to optimize their performance, stability, and selectivity.

25. The use of oxide systems other than chromium is being explored to enhance alkane alkene conversion processes, focusing on developing more selective and stable catalysts.

26. Supported vanadia catalysts demonstrate good performance in both oxidation and reduction reactions, making them promising candidates for dehydrogenation and oxidative dehydrogenation of alkanes. However, the mechanism of deactivation/coke deposition and the structure of supported vanadia species under dehydrogenation conditions remain unclear.

27. The deactivation of vanadia catalysts by coke species occurs over time, posing challenges in maintaining catalyst activity. Addressing these issues requires further investigation into the mechanism of coke formation and the catalyst's structure-function relationship.

28. The combination of reactivity, UV-Visible DRS, NMR, TEOM, and UV Raman studies has helped elucidate the structure-coke relationship in butane dehydrogenation on vanadia/θ-Al2O3 catalysts, indicating a dependency of coke nature and amount on catalyst structure.

29. Typical operating conditions for current commercial reactors involved 0.3 to 0.5 atmospheres and temperatures ranging from 823 to 923 Kelvin, optimizing the dehydrogenation process for practical applications.

30. The literature indicates ongoing efforts to understand and improve the performance of chromium and vanadium oxide catalysts in alkane dehydrogenation, aiming to develop more efficient, selective, and stable catalysts for industrial use.

11. 铬氧化物和钒氧化物催化剂在脱氢过程中经历了碳沉积，导致形成多核芳香化合物，这些化合物导致催化剂失活。
