          The evolution of chromium oxide-based catalysts since their discovery in 1933 has been marked by advancements in understanding their structure, active sites, support materials, promoters, and reaction mechanisms. Key industrial applications include dehydrogenation processes in refining, air purification, CO gas sensor technology, and utilization in closed-cycle CO2 lasers. While precious metals like platinum, palladium, rhodium, and gold are traditionally used for their efficiency near ambient temperatures, efforts have been made to develop less costly alternatives using base metals and multi-component systems. Chromium oxide surfaces have been studied for their impact on the adsorption behavior of CO, CO2, and O2, with recent investigations focusing on the effects of gamma irradiation on the surface and catalytic properties of Cr2O3/Al2O3. The role of chromium oxide content and calcination temperature in influencing the physical, surface, and catalytic properties of the Cr2O3/Al2O3 system has been a focus of research. The catalyst activity has been found to increase with the content of chromium oxide, suggesting that while increasing the amount of chromium oxide and the calcination temperature does not alter the reaction mechanism, it does affect the concentration of active catalyst components without changing their energetic nature.

Promoters, which are substances added to improve the catalyst's performance, are also a critical area of study. Understanding the precise locations and functionalities of active sites on the catalyst surface, as well as the interactions between the catalyst and the substrate, is crucial for advancing catalyst design and application.
