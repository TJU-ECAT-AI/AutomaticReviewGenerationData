Answer: The propane direct dehydrogenation (PDH) technology offers several key advantages over conventional production methods. Firstly, it addresses the challenge of feedstock availability by utilizing propane, which is widely accessible and abundant. Unlike traditional processes that often rely on expensive and limited resources such as naphtha or heavy oil fractions, PDH can efficiently convert propane into propylene. Secondly, PDH demonstrates high process efficiency, allowing for the production of propylene at lower temperatures compared to conventional methods, which can significantly reduce energy consumption and operational costs. Additionally, PDH exhibits superior product selectivity, minimizing the formation of unwanted byproducts such as carbon oxides, which can lead to higher yields of propylene. This is particularly beneficial considering the high demand for propylene in various industries. The use of vanadium-based catalysts, especially when supported on mesoporous SiO2 like MCM-41, enhances the stability and selectivity of the PDH process, enabling longer on-stream operation without significant degradation in performance. The integration of in situ UV/Vis analysis and temperature-programmed oxidation techniques provides valuable insights into the deactivation mechanisms of the catalysts, allowing for optimization of the process and the development of more durable catalyst formulations.
