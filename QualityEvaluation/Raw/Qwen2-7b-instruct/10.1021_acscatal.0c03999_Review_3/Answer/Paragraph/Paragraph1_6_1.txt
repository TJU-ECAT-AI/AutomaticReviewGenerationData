        Deactivation mechanisms, regeneration strategies, and performance optimization of metal oxide catalysts (Cr-based and VOx) in oxidative dehydrogenation processes, with emphasis on coke mitigation and CO2 utilization, are comprehensively reviewed here. The primary causes of deactivation, including overoxidation, sintering, and poisoning, are highlighted, along with the effectiveness of various regeneration methods such as reoxidation with air, CO2, or O2. The temperature requirements for these methods differ significantly, with air reoxidation generally less effective than O2 reoxidation due to its lower oxygen concentration. CO2 addition is noted as a method to mitigate overoxidation in VOx-catalyzed reactions, though its effectiveness varies across different studies. For instance, regeneration in CO2 at high temperatures allows for a complete recovery in the second cycle but cannot prevent further deactivation beyond that point. CO2 addition in the feed gas of propane-oxygen mixtures is proposed as a promising approach to reduce surface molecular oxygen species, thereby mitigating overoxidation.
