The Mars-van Krevelen mechanism operates in oxide-catalyzed oxidation reactions through a series of steps where the formation of an oxygen vacancy (ΔEv) plays a crucial role in determining both the catalytic activity and the oxidizing ability of the oxide surface. When methane undergoes oxidation, the energy required for oxygen vacancy formation influences the efficiency of the reaction. A lower ΔEv indicates a higher catalytic activity because it suggests easier access to the reactive surface sites, leading to faster and more effective oxidation processes. The significance of ΔEv lies in its impact on the reaction kinetics and the overall efficiency of the oxide surface in catalyzing the oxidation of methane. By lowering the energy barrier for oxygen vacancy formation, the oxide surface enhances its catalytic activity and oxidizing ability, facilitating the complete oxidation of methane to CO2 and H2O.
