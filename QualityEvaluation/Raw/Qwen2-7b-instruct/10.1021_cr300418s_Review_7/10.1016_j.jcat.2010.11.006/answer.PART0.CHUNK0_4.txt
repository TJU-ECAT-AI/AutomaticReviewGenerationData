The Mars-van Krevelen mechanism operates in oxide-catalyzed oxidation reactions through a series of steps where oxygen is abstracted from a substrate molecule, typically accompanied by the formation of a surface-bound oxide species. Oxygen vacancy formation (ΔEv) plays a crucial role in this mechanism as it influences the surface properties and reactivity. A lower ΔEv indicates that oxygen vacancies are more easily formed, enhancing the surface's ability to participate in oxidation reactions. This process affects both the catalytic activity and the oxidizing ability of the oxide surface by altering the surface's structure and the availability of active sites for reaction. The significance of ΔEv lies in its impact on the reaction kinetics and the overall efficiency of the oxidation process, thereby determining the effectiveness of the oxide catalyst in facilitating the conversion of substrates into products.
