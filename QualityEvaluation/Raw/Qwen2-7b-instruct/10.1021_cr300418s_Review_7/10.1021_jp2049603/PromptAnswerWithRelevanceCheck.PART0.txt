<EnglishVersionAnswer>
The Mars-van Krevelen mechanism operates in oxide-catalyzed oxidation reactions by utilizing the oxygen atoms on the surface of the catalyst. In this mechanism, the catalyst provides an environment for the oxidation of a substance, typically involving the loss of electrons by the substance to be oxidized. The energy required for oxygen vacancy formation (ΔEv) plays a significant role in determining both the catalytic activity and the oxidizing ability of the oxide surface. When the energy of oxygen-vacancy formation is low, the catalyst is more likely to lose oxygen atoms easily, which enhances its oxidizing ability. However, if the energy is too low, the catalyst might lose oxygen too easily, potentially reducing its activity and selectivity in the oxidation process. Thus, a balance must be achieved to ensure that the catalyst can effectively remove oxygen atoms for oxidation purposes without losing its functionality too quickly.
</EnglishVersionAnswer>
