<EnglishVersionAnswer>
1. Propane dehydrogenation (PDH) is considered a viable alternative to traditional oil-based cracking processes for producing propene, an essential industrial chemical. The innovative approach involves the development of environmentally compatible supported catalysts based on commercial ZnO.

2. The catalyst design strategy involves combining ZnO with a support material, which can be zeolite or a common metal oxide, either as a physical mixture or in a layered structure with ZnO as the top layer. 

3. Upon reductive treatment at temperatures above 550°C, Zn atoms from ZnO react with OH groups on the support surface to form active ZnOx species. Defective OH groups play a crucial role in the formation of these active species.

4. The newly developed ZnOsilicalite-1 catalyst was tested alongside a commercial analogue of KCrOx/Al2O3 under industrially relevant conditions for approximately 400 hours on a propane stream, demonstrating high propene productivity at similar propene selectivity.
</EnglishVersionAnswer>

<EnglishVersionAnswer>
The propane dehydrogenation (PDH) technique addresses the challenges of increasing industrial demand for propylene and limitations of traditional production methods by offering an alternative to oil-based cracking processes. It produces propene, a crucial industrial chemical. Commercial PDH technologies employing catalysts with chromium or platinum suffer from environmental concerns due to the toxicity of chromium compounds and the ecological harm caused by using chlorine for catalyst regeneration. An innovative catalyst design strategy involves the preparation of environmentally compatible supported catalysts based on commercial zinc oxide (ZnO). These catalysts are designed to overcome deactivation issues, enhance stability, and improve recyclability in both oxidative and non-oxidative PDH processes. The development utilizes a mixture or layered structure of ZnO with a support material such as zeolite or a common metal oxide. The active ZnOx species are formed through a reaction of support hydroxyl (OH) groups with zinc atoms generated from ZnO upon reductive treatment above 550°C. The role of defective OH groups is identified as crucial for the formation of active ZnOx species. The developed ZnOsilicalite-1 and its commercial analogue, KCrOx/Al2O3, are tested under industrially relevant conditions, demonstrating a significant increase in propene productivity while maintaining comparable propene selectivity.
</EnglishVersionAnswer>
