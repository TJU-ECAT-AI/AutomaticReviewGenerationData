            Propane dehydrogenation (PDH) techniques address the increasing industrial demand for propylene and the limitations of traditional production methods by efficiently converting propane into propylene. Innovations in catalyst design strategies tackle deactivation issues, enhance stability, and improve recyclability in both oxidative and nonoxidative PDH processes. Key catalyst designs include the use of late transition metals, main-group metals, and rare-earth metal oxides, as well as single-atom-doped Ga2O3 catalysts and MOF-derived iron catalysts. These strategies leverage the properties of catalyst surfaces and electronic structures to optimize catalytic activity, selectivity, and longevity.

            The evolution of PDH as a promising alternative for propylene production is underscored by its potential to reduce energy consumption and environmental impact compared to conventional processes. However, challenges remain, particularly in maintaining catalyst stability and preventing deactivation over time. Innovative approaches, such as rational design of catalysts with optimized electronic structures and surface properties, aim to overcome these limitations.
