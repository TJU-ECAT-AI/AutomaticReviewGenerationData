The unique reactivity of propane in halogen-assisted dehydrogenation processes is attributed to its larger size and complex interactions with halogen species, differing significantly from other alkanes like methane and ethane. These methods, including oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH), oxyhalogenation, and molten metal halide salt approaches, offer advantages over conventional catalytic methods due to the high reactivity of halogen species involved. Halogen-assisted dehydrogenation leads to the formation of alkyl radicals, which are subsequently oxidized to alkenes. Oxyhalogenation involves the reaction of an alkane with an oxyhalide, while molten metal halide salt approaches utilize a molten salt bath containing a halide ion as the oxidant. Propane's reactivity in these halogen-assisted methods is notably higher than that of methane and ethane, providing an efficient route for the production of propene from propane.
