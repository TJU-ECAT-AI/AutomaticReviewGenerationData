The reactivity of propane in these processes differs significantly from other alkanes like methane and ethane due to the unique chemical properties of propane and the specific mechanisms involved in each method.

1. **Dehydrogenation with Halogens**: This process involves the removal of hydrogen atoms from propane molecules using halogen compounds, typically HCl. The halogen acts as a catalyst, facilitating the dehydrogenation reaction. The selectivity and efficiency of this method depend on the halogen's ability to selectively remove hydrogen atoms without causing unwanted side reactions.

2. **Oxidative Dehydrogenation**: This method combines dehydrogenation with oxidation, often using oxygen as the oxidizing agent. It is a more complex process that aims to selectively convert propane into propene while minimizing the formation of other byproducts. The reaction conditions typically involve high temperatures and pressures, along with a suitable catalyst that can promote the desired dehydrogenation and oxidation reactions simultaneously.

3. **Oxyhalogenation**: This refers to the conversion of propane to propene through the addition of halogen atoms (primarily chlorine) in the presence of oxygen. The oxyhalogenation process is similar to the oxychlorination described in the literature, where propane reacts with hydrogen chloride and oxygen to form propene. The selectivity and efficiency of oxyhalogenation depend on the catalyst's ability to control the reaction pathways, particularly in preventing the formation of polyhalogenated compounds and coke.

4. **Molten Metal Halide Salt Approaches**: These methods utilize molten salts containing metal halides as the reaction medium. The high ionic conductivity and chemical stability of these salts enable efficient heat and mass transfer, making them suitable for dehydrogenation reactions. The specific conditions and catalysts used can vary widely depending on the metal halide salt system, but the goal remains to selectively dehydrogenate propane while controlling the reaction to prevent unwanted side reactions.

The key differences in reactivity between propane and other alkanes stem from propane's structure and the nature of the halogen-assisted reactions. Propane's higher reactivity and sensitivity to halogen compounds make it more challenging to control the dehydrogenation process selectively. Factors such as the catalyst's ability to modulate reaction pathways, the selectivity of the halogen compounds, and the optimization of reaction conditions play crucial roles in achieving high selectivity for propene production.
