Describe and compare the various halogen-assisted methods for selectively dehydrogenating propane to propene, including dehydrogenation with halogens, oxidative dehydrogenation, oxyhalogenation, and molten metal halide salt approaches. Explain the reaction conditions for each method and analyze why propane's reactivity in these processes differs significantly from that of other alkanes such as methane and ethane.

<EnglishVersionAnswer>
Halogen-assisted methods for selectively dehydrogenating propane to propene include dehydrogenation with halogens, oxidative dehydrogenation, oxyhalogenation, and molten metal halide salt approaches. Dehydrogenation with halogens uses halogenated compounds as catalysts, typically bromine or iodine. Oxidative dehydrogenation involves air or oxygen as the oxidizing agent, often carried out with a metal catalyst such as copper or silver. Oxyhalogenation employs the reaction of propane with halogen compounds like bromine or iodine in the presence of oxygen, producing alkyl halides and alkenes. Molten metal halide salt approaches utilize molten salts containing halides like lithium chloride or potassium chloride as catalysts, with propane dehydrogenated at high temperatures. Propane's reactivity in these processes differs from that of methane and ethane due to its larger size and tertiary carbon atom, making it more susceptible to dehydrogenation. Ethane and methane, with smaller size and primary or secondary carbon atoms, are less reactive and thus less prone to dehydrogenation reactions.

</EnglishVersionAnswer>
