To address the question regarding how the diverse properties of niobium oxides influence their behavior as dopants in NiO catalysts for ethane oxidative dehydrogenation, and the challenges in reconciling theoretical predictions with experimental observations, we must consider the context of mixed metal oxides and their surface chemistry. 

Niobium oxides, due to their multiple stable forms and oxidation states, offer unique opportunities for doping NiO catalysts. Their ability to exist in various forms, such as Nb2O5, NbO2, and Nb2O3, can lead to different electronic structures and surface functionalities when introduced into NiO. This can potentially alter the redox properties, acidity, and Lewis basicity of the catalyst, thereby influencing its activity and selectivity for ethane oxidative dehydrogenation.

The challenges in reconciling theoretical predictions with experimental observations often stem from the complexity of mixed metal oxides. Theoretical models may not accurately capture the intricate interplay between the bulk structure, surface chemistry, and the catalytic activity. Factors such as the precise location and coordination of dopants, the stability of the dopant species under reaction conditions, and the dynamic changes in surface properties can significantly deviate from static theoretical calculations.

Experimental difficulties, including the difficulty in preparing homogeneous catalysts, the variability in synthesis methods, and the complexity of interpreting spectroscopic data, also contribute to discrepancies between theory and practice. Moreover, the subtle differences in catalyst performance under various operational conditions (e.g., temperature, pressure, and gas flow) can be hard to predict theoretically, especially when considering the influence of the supporting material and the presence of impurities.

Therefore, the key challenge lies in developing a comprehensive understanding of how the complex electronic and structural features of mixed metal oxides affect their catalytic performance. This requires a combination of advanced characterization techniques, such as XRD, XPS, and operando spectroscopy, alongside sophisticated theoretical modeling to bridge the gap between structure and function in mixed metal oxide catalysts.
