The document discusses the complexities of catalytic processes, particularly in the conversion of hydrocarbons, which are fundamental in refining crude oil, modifying fats in food industries, and synthesizing pharmaceutical products and fine chemicals. Due to the complexity, little is known about the molecular-level steps involved. High pressures used in these processes are difficult to replicate under vacuum conditions used in surface science studies, leading to challenges in detecting intermediate alkyl groups. Open surfaces with low coordination metal atoms offer higher reactivity for C-H bond breaking under vacuum. The document also mentions various methods to overcome these difficulties, including using surfaces with low coordination metal atoms, supersonic beams for activation, and studying alkyl halide activation on metal surfaces. The process of converting saturated hydrocarbons remains poorly understood. Techniques such as X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), reflection-absorption infrared spectroscopy (RAIRS), and hydrogen thermal programmed desorption (TPD) are used to understand the surface chemistry of hydrocarbons on surfaces. The document also highlights the importance of hydrogen/deuterium coadsorption in simulating catalytic hydrocarbon conversion reactions under vacuum conditions. Kinetic parameters for hydrocarbon conversion reactions on transition metal surfaces are estimated using TPD experiments.
