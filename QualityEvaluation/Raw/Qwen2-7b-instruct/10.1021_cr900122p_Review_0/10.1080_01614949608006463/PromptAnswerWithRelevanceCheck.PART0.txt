The role of isolated metal centers on oxide materials facilitates C-H bond activation in heterogeneous catalysis by providing a stable environment for the metal ions, allowing them to act as Lewis acids and abstract hydrogen atoms from the substrate. This process is crucial for the selective oxidation of alkanes, such as the conversion of butane to maleic anhydride. In this context, the Mars-van-Krevelen mechanism plays a significant role, where the metal center activates the C-H bond by coordinating with it and then abstracting a hydrogen atom through a hydride transfer reaction. The H-abstraction leads to the formation of an alkyl radical, which can then react further in the desired oxidation pathway.

2. The isolation of vanadium ions in a quasi-inert matrix enhances their activity and stability during the selective oxidation of alkanes. This isolation prevents the vanadium ions from interacting with other components in the catalyst, minimizing side reactions and promoting a higher yield of the desired product. By maintaining the vanadium ions in a well-defined, stable structure, the catalyst can efficiently perform the oxidation process, particularly in the conversion of butane to maleic anhydride, ensuring a high selectivity towards the target product.

3. Acidity and basicity play critical roles in the mechanism of selective oxidation of paraffins. The acidity of the catalyst surface facilitates the activation of the C-H bonds, while the basicity can stabilize the intermediate species formed during the oxidation process. The balance between these properties ensures that the catalyst efficiently converts the alkanes into the desired products, such as maleic anhydride, while minimizing the formation of unwanted byproducts. The combination of acidic and basic sites on the catalyst surface optimizes the reaction pathway, leading to enhanced selectivity and efficiency in the conversion of butane to maleic anhydride.
