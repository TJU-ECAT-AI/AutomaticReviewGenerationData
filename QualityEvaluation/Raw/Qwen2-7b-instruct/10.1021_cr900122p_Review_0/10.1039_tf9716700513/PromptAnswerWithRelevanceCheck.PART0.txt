<EnglishVersionAnswer>
The isolated metal centers on oxide materials, particularly alumina, facilitate C-H bond activation in heterogeneous catalysis through mechanisms like Mars van Krevelen and H-abstraction. These processes are crucial in industrial selective oxidation of alkanes, such as the conversion of butane to maleic anhydride. The Mars van Krevelen mechanism involves the transfer of a hydrogen atom from an alkane to an oxygen atom in a catalyst, forming a carbocation. Subsequently, this carbocation reacts with the oxygen atom to form a product. In the case of H-abstraction, a hydrogen atom is abstracted from the alkane, leading to the formation of a carbocation. This carbocation can then react with oxygen to produce a variety of products, depending on the specific conditions and catalyst used.

The conversion of butane to maleic anhydride is a prime example of the application of these principles. Butane is oxidized in the presence of a suitable catalyst, such as a supported vanadium catalyst, to produce maleic anhydride. The catalyst provides a surface for the reaction to occur, and the metal centers facilitate the breaking of the C-H bonds in butane, allowing for the selective oxidation to maleic anhydride. The high selectivity and yield of maleic anhydride make this process industrially valuable.
</EnglishVersionAnswer>
