        The evolution of bimetallic catalysts in heterogeneous catalysis since their commercial introduction has significantly advanced, primarily due to their unique electronic and chemical properties. These properties have enabled researchers to bridge the "materials gap" and "pressure gap" between model surfaces and industrially relevant supported catalysts, contributing to advancements in both fundamental research and practical applications. The unique electronic and chemical properties of bimetallic catalysts, especially those concerning near-surface alloys (NSAs), have played a pivotal role in enhancing catalytic efficiency. NSAs, characterized by a solute metal present near the surface of a host metal in concentrations different from the bulk, have exhibited superior catalytic behavior for hydrogen-related reactions. Some NSAs bind atomic hydrogen (H) as weakly as noble metals (Cu, Au) while dissociating H2 more easily, making them potential low-temperature, highly selective catalysts for pharmaceutical production and robust fuel-cell anodes.

        The use of bimetallic catalysts has significantly evolved in heterogeneous catalysis, particularly through the development of near-monodisperse NiCu bimetallic nanocrystals. These materials exhibit variable compositions and controlled synthesis, enhancing their catalytic activity for hydrogen generation. Their unique electronic and chemical properties enable them to bridge the "materials gap" and "pressure gap" between theoretical model surfaces and industrially applicable catalysts, facilitating progress in both fundamental research and practical applications.

        Representative studies have reviewed and discussed the correlation between chemical alterations that modify the chemisorption and catalytic properties of the overlayers and changes in the structural and electronic properties of bimetallic systems. These studies have highlighted the importance of understanding these properties in the context of bimetallic catalysts' performance and efficiency.

        The evolution of bimetallic catalysts in heterogeneous catalysis has been driven by their unique electronic and chemical properties, which bridge the "materials gap" and "pressure gap" between model surfaces and industrially relevant supported catalysts. These features effectively address the challenges posed by the gap between theoretical models and practical applications, offering substantial value in both basic research and industrial settings.

        In summary, the evolution of bimetallic catalysts in heterogeneous catalysis has been marked by significant advancements in understanding their unique electronic and chemical properties.
