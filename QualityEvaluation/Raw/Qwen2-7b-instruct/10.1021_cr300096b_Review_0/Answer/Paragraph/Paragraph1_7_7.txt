        The evolution of bimetallic catalysts since their commercial introduction has been marked by significant advancements in understanding their unique electronic and chemical properties. These properties enable them to effectively address the 'materials gap' and 'pressure gap' between model surfaces and industrially relevant supported catalysts, facilitating progress in both fundamental research and practical applications. Representative studies have shown that these catalysts can be designed to possess distinct structural and electronic characteristics that contribute to their enhanced performance. For instance, the use of near-surface alloys (NSAs) can confer novel catalytic properties to surfaces, as seen in the case of AuAg alloys that exhibit superior catalytic behavior for hydrogen-related reactions. These alloys bind atomic hydrogen (H) as weakly as noble metals (Cu, Au) while dissociating H2 more easily, making them suitable for low-temperature, highly selective catalysis in pharmaceutical production and robust fuel-cell anodes. Periodic, self-consistent density functional theory calculations have proven to be powerful tools in assessing the stability of NSAs in hydrogen-rich environments and evaluating hydrogen reactivity on stable surfaces. This highlights the potential of NSAs to serve as catalysts that bridge the theoretical gap between model surfaces and real-world industrial catalysts.

        The unique electronic and chemical properties of bimetallic catalysts have also enabled the development of near-monodisperse NiCu bimetallic nanocrystals, which exhibit variable compositions and controlled synthesis to enhance catalytic activity for hydrogen generation. These materials demonstrate the capability to bridge the gap between theoretical model surfaces and industrially applicable catalysts, thereby facilitating advancements in both fundamental research and practical applications.
