        The characteristics of catalyst support pore structures, including pore size, connectivity, uniformity, and hierarchical arrangements, significantly impact coke formation, accommodation, and overall catalyst performance in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions. These factors are crucial for the design of optimal and effective catalysts. The pore size, particularly when larger, increases the likelihood of coke formation due to reduced connectivity and hindered diffusion of oxygen species, which can limit the spillover effect and thus elevate the oxidation temperature of coke. Hierarchical pore structures, however, can enhance catalyst performance by offering better accommodation for reactants and products, potentially improving the diffusion pathways and reducing coke accumulation. Uniformity in pore size contributes to more consistent catalytic activity across the support surface, ensuring a more stable and predictable reaction environment.

        The implications for designing optimal and effective catalysts in PDH reactions are significant. Researchers must carefully consider the pore structure characteristics of the support material, aiming to balance pore size, connectivity, and uniformity. This involves developing materials with specific pore structures that facilitate efficient oxygen diffusion while minimizing coke formation and accumulation. For instance, mesoporous supports can provide a suitable framework for the spillover effect, enhancing the catalytic activity and stability of the catalyst. Incorporating features like periodicity or anisotropy in the pore structure can further optimize the diffusion paths for oxygen species, thereby enhancing the catalytic activity and stability of the catalyst.
