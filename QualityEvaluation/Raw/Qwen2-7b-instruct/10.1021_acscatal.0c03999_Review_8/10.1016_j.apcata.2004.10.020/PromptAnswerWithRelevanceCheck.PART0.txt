Analyzing the use of nitrous oxide (N2O) as an oxidant in the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP), it becomes evident that N2O offers significant advantages in terms of propylene selectivity compared to other oxidants. Studies by Nowinski et al. (ref [2]) and Pe rez-Ram rez and colleagues (refs [3], [4]) have demonstrated that iron zeolites, particularly Fe-ZSM-5, exhibit high propylene yields (up to 20% and 25%, respectively) when using N2O as the oxidant. This is notably advantageous because other oxidants, such as O2, typically result in lower propene yields (10-30%).

The use of iron zeolites as catalysts for ODHP is further highlighted by the work of Bula nek et al. (ref [5]), who achieved propylene yields of 25% over steamed Fe-ZSM-5 zeolites with lower iron loadings (0.03-0.12 wt.% Fe). However, a critical challenge associated with this process is the strong deactivation of iron zeolites during ODHP with N2O, primarily due to coke formation. The literature acknowledges that the deactivation behavior and activity of iron zeolites under similar conditions have not been adequately reported.

To address these limitations, steam treatment of the iron zeolites has proven effective in enhancing their catalytic performance. As demonstrated by Chen and Sachtler (ref [6]), steaming leads to the creation of highly dispersed mononuclear iron sites within the zeolite, which significantly boosts the turnover frequency (TOF) for propylene production. This improvement in performance is particularly pronounced in steamed commercial H-ZSM-5 zeolites, which induce relatively low conversions of propene and N2O but achieve a high propylene selectivity (90%) and minimal COx formation.

The correlation between the TOF for propane oxydehydrogenation to propylene and the Fe/Al ratio in the zeolites has been established. The highest TOF is observed in steamed H-ZSM-5 (Fe/Al = 0.007), followed by a decrease in TOF by three orders of magnitude in the catalyst prepared through chemical vapor deposition (Fe/Al = 1). This indicates the extraordinary quality of iron traces in the commercial zeolite sample for the ODHP reaction, especially after steam treatment. The isolated nature of the iron species in this sample supports the model where mononuclear Fe sites are essential for propylene production via ODHP, aligning with the findings of N2O-mediated oxidation of benzene to phenol over iron zeolites.

To make N2O-ODHP a more viable option for practical implementation, future research should focus on developing strategies to mitigate catalyst deactivation and enhance stability.
