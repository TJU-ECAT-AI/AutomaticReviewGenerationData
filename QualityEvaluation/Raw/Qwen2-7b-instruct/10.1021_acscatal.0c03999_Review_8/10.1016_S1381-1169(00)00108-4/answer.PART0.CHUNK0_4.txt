<EnglishVersionAnswer>
The use of nitrous oxide (N2O) as an oxidant in the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) offers several advantages. Its reactivity correlates with the strength of the C-H bond, indicating that it selectively interacts with stronger bonds. In the process, methane is capable of alkylating the aromatic ring of benzene, yielding toluene and xylenes. This selective interaction enhances propylene production, as indicated by the high propylene selectivity observed. 

However, the process faces challenges related to catalyst deactivation and stability. The catalysts employed in this process, such as HZSM-5, must maintain their activity over multiple cycles while resisting degradation. Strategies to address these limitations might involve developing more robust catalysts with enhanced stability or exploring ways to regenerate deactivated catalysts. Additionally, optimizing reaction conditions, such as temperature and pressure, could improve catalyst efficiency and prolong its lifespan.

Research directions that could make N2O-ODHP more feasible for practical implementation include investigating the underlying mechanisms of N2O's selective reactivity towards C-H bonds. Understanding how N2O interacts with different hydrocarbons and identifying the key intermediates involved could lead to the development of more efficient catalysts. Furthermore, studying the impact of different process parameters on reaction kinetics and product distribution might reveal optimal conditions for maximizing propylene yield while minimizing byproduct formation.
