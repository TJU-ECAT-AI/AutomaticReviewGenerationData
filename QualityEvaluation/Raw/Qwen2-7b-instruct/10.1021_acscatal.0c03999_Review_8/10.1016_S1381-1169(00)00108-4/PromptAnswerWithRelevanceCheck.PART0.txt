Compared to other oxidants, N2O enables higher selectivity towards propylene due to its unique interaction with the catalyst and the hydrocarbon feedstock. The process typically involves the conversion of hydrocarbons on a zeolite catalyst, such as HZSM-5, where the reactivity correlates with the strength of the C-H bond. Challenges associated with catalyst deactivation and stability in N2O-ODHP processes include the potential for rapid deactivation over time, necessitating frequent regeneration or replacement. This is partly due to the formation of coke and other byproducts that accumulate on the catalyst surface, reducing its activity. Strategies to address these limitations might include the development of more robust catalyst materials that can withstand the harsh conditions of N2O oxidation or the implementation of advanced regeneration techniques that maintain high catalyst efficiency while minimizing downtime. Research directions could focus on optimizing the operating conditions, such as temperature, pressure, and feed composition, to enhance the stability of the catalyst and improve overall process efficiency. Additionally, exploring the use of alternative catalyst supports or modifying the catalyst structure to facilitate easier desorption of byproducts could also mitigate deactivation issues.
