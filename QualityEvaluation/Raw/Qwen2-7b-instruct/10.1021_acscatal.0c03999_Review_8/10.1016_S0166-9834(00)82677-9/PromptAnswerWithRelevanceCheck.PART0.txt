Compared to other oxidants, N2O allows for a higher yield of propylene, which is a crucial product in petrochemical industries. This is due to the unique reactivity profile of N2O, which enables more efficient conversion of propane to propylene under certain conditions. 

In the context of this study, multiple catalysts were employed to facilitate the ODHP process. These include platinum loaded on H-ZSM-5 zeolite, which is particularly effective due to its bifunctional nature. The Pt particles within the zeolite pores enhance the conversion efficiency by being located near the acid sites of the zeolite, leading to better performance than a physical mixture of Pt/SiO2 and H-ZSM-5.

However, the process faces significant challenges related to catalyst deactivation and stability. Over time, the catalysts experience a decline in activity, primarily due to the formation of coke on the surface, which can block the pores and hinder the reaction pathways. To address these limitations, researchers can focus on developing more robust catalyst materials that are less susceptible to deactivation. Additionally, optimizing the operating conditions, such as temperature, pressure, and feedstock composition, can help maintain the catalyst's performance.

Furthermore, investigating alternative oxidants or combining N2O with other promoters could potentially increase the propylene selectivity and reduce the formation of byproducts. Research in this area might involve the development of novel catalyst systems, enhanced reaction mechanisms, and advanced process engineering techniques aimed at improving the overall efficiency and sustainability of the ODHP process.
