          The use of nitrous oxide (N2O) in the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) provides several advantages, notably higher propylene selectivity leading to increased yields and reduced production costs. Different catalysts like CeO2 and NiO-modified CeO2 nanocrystals are utilized. Challenges with catalyst deactivation and stability persist, prompting strategies such as the creation of more durable catalyst materials and optimization of reaction conditions. Potential future research might focus on alternative oxidants or catalyst modifications to improve selectivity and stability.

          The investigation of using nitrous oxide (N2O) as an oxidant in the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) process highlights its ability to achieve high propylene selectivity. Multiple catalysts, such as CeO2 and NiO-modified CeO2 nanocrystals, have been applied in this context. Significant challenges, particularly concerning catalyst deactivation and stability, specifically temperature control and catalyst regeneration, are encountered. Addressing these limitations may involve refining reaction conditions, enhancing catalyst design, and creating effective regeneration techniques.
