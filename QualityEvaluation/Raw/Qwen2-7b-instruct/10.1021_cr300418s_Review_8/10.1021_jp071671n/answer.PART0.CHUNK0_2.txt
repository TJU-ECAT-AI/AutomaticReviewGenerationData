3.2. The Location of the Dopant: at the Surface or in the Bulk? To be useful in catalysis, the dopant must replace a Zn atom at the surface of the oxide or in the first layer below the surface. In experiments, the position of the dopant will depend on the method of preparation; the kinetics of preparation will control the structure. If the preparation temperature is low or no annealing is performed so that the mobility of the dopant is very small, the dopant will be stuck in the position it happened to be when the solid oxide was formed. Given the complexity of the preparation methods, a reliable (or even an unreliable) simulation of the distribution of the dopant in the solid is not possible at this time.

The total energy of the slab with the dopant at the surface minus the total energy of the same slab with the dopant in the bulk are given in Figure 2 at the bottom of each box (in italic font). For these calculations, the energies have been sampled only at the gamma point. A negative value indicates that the dopant prefers to be located at the surface. As with the OVFE, we find no general pattern in the results, but there is a propensity that dopants that decrease OVFE prefer to be located at the surface. However, Ca, Sr, Ba, La, Ce, Re, Fe, Ru, Sn, and Pb, which increase OVFE, also segregate to the surface. However, some rationalization of these results is possible. A dopant at the surface forms fewer bonds with oxygen than a dopant in the bulk. If the dopant-oxygen bond is weaker than the ZnO bond, then less binding energy is lost if the dopant is at the surface. This may explain why the dopants that decrease OVFE prefer to substitute a surface Zn atom. In addition, when the size of a dopant is bigger than that of Zn, it will prefer to be located at the surface where the stress can be released by a displacement of the dopant toward the vacuum.
