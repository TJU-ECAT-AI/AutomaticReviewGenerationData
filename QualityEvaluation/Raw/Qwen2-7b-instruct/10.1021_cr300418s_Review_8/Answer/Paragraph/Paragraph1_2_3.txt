        <div>
            <p>Studies on the influence of dopants on the catalytic properties of metal oxides, particularly focusing on same-valence dopants (SVDs) and high-valence dopants (HVDs), reveal nuanced effects on the energetics and reactivity of these materials. These findings are rooted in both computational and experimental analyses, shedding light on the mechanisms underlying the impact of dopants on the catalytic performance of various metal oxides. The insights gained from these investigations highlight the critical role of factors such as Lewis acid-base interactions and the nature of the host oxide (reducible vs. irreducible) in determining dopant behavior. However, formulating general predictive rules for dopant behavior remains a challenge due to the complexity and variability of these factors.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
            <p>The incorporation of SVDs and HVDs into metal oxides, such as zinc oxide (ZnO), leads to a reduction in the energy required for oxygen vacancy formation (OVFE). This is often observed when dopants preferentially locate at the surface of the oxide rather than within its bulk. Notably, dopants like gold (Au) and silver (Ag) significantly decrease OVFE, comparable to the effect of alkali metal dopants. On the other hand, HVDs that increase OVFE tend to substitute zinc (Zn) atoms within the bulk of the host oxide, except for certain elements (Ca, Sr, Ba, La, Ce, Re, Fe, Ru, Co, Sn, Pb) that prefer to substitute a Zn atom at the surface. Despite efforts to correlate the magnitude of OVFE with properties such as ionization energy, electronegativity, chemical hardness, and atomic radii, such correlations have proven elusive.</p>
        </div>
        <div>
            <p>The placement of dopants at the surface of an oxide, rather than within its bulk, is attributed to the fact that these dopants form fewer bonds with oxygen, thereby weakening the bond between the oxygen atoms in the surface layer and the oxide. This results in less binding energy loss when the dopant is situated at the surface, especially if the dopant-oxygen bond is weaker than the ZnO bond. The model employed in these studies has identified two key limitations: the relevance of the calculations is limited to the flat facets of dispersed catalysts, which have numerous surface features, and the calculations are pertinent only for the flat facets of such oxides.</p>
        </div>

        </div>
        <div>
            <p>掺杂剂位于氧化物表面而不是其内部的原因在于，这些掺杂剂与氧形成的键较少，从而削弱了表面层中氧原子与氧化物之间的键。这导致了当掺杂剂位于表面时，由于掺杂剂-氧键弱于ZnO键，因此损失的结合能较少。模型在这些研究中识别出了两个关键限制：对于高度分散催化剂而言，计算仅适用于表面特征众多的平滑面，而这些计算仅适用于此类氧化物的平滑面。</p>
