        Detailed analysis has been conducted on the influence of same-valence dopants (SVDs) and high-valence dopants (HVDs) on the catalytic properties of different metal oxides, based on studies such as \[10.1021/jp071671n\], \[10.1021/jp203112\], and \[10.1021/jp103604b\]. These studies highlight how dopants can alter the energetics of oxygen vacancy formation and affect the reactivity of the oxides towards various gases, particularly CO. For instance, gold (Au) and silver (Ag) were found to significantly reduce the energy of oxygen vacancy formation (OVFE) and are more likely to be located at the surface of zinc oxide (ZnO) rather than in the bulk, comparable to the effect of alkali metals. On the other hand, HVDs that increase OVFE tend to substitute zinc (Zn) atoms in the bulk of the host oxide, except for certain divalent and trivalent dopants like Ca, Sr, Ba, La, Ce, Re, Fe, Ru, Co, Sn, and Pb, which prefer to substitute a Zn atom at the surface.

        Computational and experimental studies, as reported in \[10.1021/jp203112\], have shown that factors such as Lewis acid-base interactions and the nature of the host oxide (reducible vs. irreducible) play crucial roles in determining dopant behavior. However, formulating general predictive rules for dopant behavior remains challenging due to the complexity and variability of these factors.

        An integrative understanding and prospective outlook based on these findings suggest that there are opportunities to explore new methodologies and predict the behavior of dopants in metal oxides more accurately. Future research might focus on developing models that incorporate the morphological features of highly dispersed catalysts and improving predictions for the energetics of dopant incorporation and vacancy formation. This could pave the way for optimizing the catalytic performance of metal oxides in various applications.

        In summary, the complex interplay between dopant valence, location within the oxide, and the physical and chemical properties of the host oxide dictates the catalytic behavior. Understanding these relationships will be crucial for the design of more efficient catalysts.
