        The evolving landscape of propylene production: From traditional methods to the emergence of propane dehydrogenation (PDH), with a focus on the development and ongoing scientific debate surrounding Ga/H-ZSM-5 catalysts for PDH, including the understanding of active Ga species and recent research findings that have contributed to our current knowledge of these catalysts. The growing demand for propylene has driven the development of new production methods, particularly PDH, aiming to replace costly platinum-based catalysts and mitigate environmental concerns associated with chromium usage. The Catofin and Oleflex processes, employing CrOx/Al2O3 and Pt-based catalysts respectively, have been the standard, but challenges such as high costs and deactivation have led to a shift towards more environmentally friendly alternatives. Gallium (Ga)-based catalysts, notably Ga/H-ZSM-5, have shown promise for their high stability and propylene selectivity, particularly in the dehydrogenation of propane to propylene. The ongoing debate around the nature of active Ga species in these catalysts highlights the complexity and nuances in the field of catalysis, underscoring the catalyst's pivotal role in propylene production efficiency and selectivity. Recent studies have advanced our understanding of Ga/H-ZSM-5 catalysts, emphasizing the importance of rational design for nanocatalysts and the exploration of cost-effective transition metals in PDH. This evolution reflects the dynamic response of the chemical industry to meet the escalating demand for propylene while addressing environmental and economic challenges.
