The document discusses various catalysts used in the non-oxidative dehydrogenation of light olefins. Noble metal-based catalysts, particularly Platinum, have been used historically in processes like the PACOL process by UOP for producing linear alkenes for detergents, and in the Oxo-D process by Petrotex for producing 1,3-butadiene. Platinum catalysts offer high activity and selectivity, making them effective for these reactions. On the other hand, metal oxide-based catalysts such as CrOx, gallium, indium, vanadium, zinc, and molybdenum oxides have been developed for specific applications, including the dehydrogenation of isobutane to produce MTBE as a high-octane fuel additive. These metal oxides can be more cost-effective alternatives compared to noble metals, although they may exhibit different performance characteristics, such as stability and selectivity. Recent developments include the use of these catalysts in various industrial processes, aiming to improve efficiency and reduce costs. The potential advantages of these catalysts lie in their ability to operate under certain conditions where noble metal catalysts might not be suitable, offering flexibility in industrial applications.
