Noble metal-based catalysts, particularly Platinum, are known for their high efficiency in the nonoxidative dehydrogenation of light olefins. They are highly selective and show excellent stability under various reaction conditions. Platinum catalysts are commonly used in the petrochemical industry due to their ability to promote the desired reactions efficiently. Recent developments in this area have focused on improving the stability and reducing the cost of these catalysts through alloying and surface modification techniques.

Metal oxide-based catalysts, including CrOx, gallium, indium, vanadium, zinc, and molybdenum oxides, offer an alternative approach to the nonoxidative dehydrogenation process. These catalysts are often cheaper compared to noble metals, making them attractive for industrial applications. They can be tailored to specific reaction conditions and exhibit good activity over a wide range of temperatures. Metal oxides are typically less selective than noble metal catalysts, leading to the formation of a broader range of products. However, they are more robust and can handle harsher conditions, which makes them suitable for certain industrial processes. Recent research has aimed at improving the selectivity and activity of metal oxide catalysts through doping, structuring, and the development of composite materials.

Both types of catalysts play crucial roles in the conversion of natural gas into higher-value chemical intermediates. Noble metal catalysts, especially Platinum, are preferred for their superior performance and selectivity, while metal oxide catalysts offer cost-effectiveness and adaptability to different conditions. The choice between these catalysts depends on factors such as cost, reaction conditions, and the desired product distribution.
