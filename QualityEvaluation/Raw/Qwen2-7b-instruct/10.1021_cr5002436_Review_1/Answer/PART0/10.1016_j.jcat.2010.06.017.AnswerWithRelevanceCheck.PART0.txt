          Comparing noble metal-based catalysts, especially Platinum, and metal oxide-based catalysts used in nonoxidative dehydrogenation of light olefins, the key differences lie in their compositions, effectiveness, industrial applications, recent developments, and potential advantages. Noble metals, notably Platinum, are renowned for their high activity and selectivity in this process. On the other hand, metal oxides such as CrOx, gallium, indium, vanadium, zinc, and molybdenum oxides offer stability and lower costs. These catalysts find extensive use in petrochemical industries for the production of olefins from hydrocarbons. Recent advancements focus on improving catalyst performance, often involving modifications to noble metals or the incorporation of co-catalysts. Noble metals provide superior activity and selectivity, yet they may come with higher costs and risk of deactivation. Metal oxides, while ensuring better stability and potentially lower costs, might fall short in achieving the high activity levels of noble metals.

      <English>
          A new method is outlined for creating PtGa bimetallic particles on a calcined Mg(Ga)(Al)O support for the dehydrogenation of light alkanes to alkenes. Gallium (Ga), introduced via transfer from a calcined Mg(Ga)(Al)O hydrotalcite-like material, modifies the Pt particles. Starting from an organometallic precursor, Pt nanoparticles are dispersed on the support, followed by reduction. Upon high-temperature reduction at 773873 K, PtGa alloys are observed. It is theorized that hydrogen atoms produced on the metal particle surface migrate to the support, reducing Ga3+ ions to atomic Ga. This atomic Ga then interacts with the supported Pt, forming PtGa alloys.
      </English>
