            Recent studies highlight the use of soft oxidants for the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP), with iron-based catalysts, notably FeZSM-5, demonstrating exceptional efficiency in converting propane to propene using nitrogen dioxide (N2O). Achieving initial propene yields of 20-25% at temperatures around 723-773 K, these catalysts enable a cyclic process for continuous propene production through alternating reaction and regeneration cycles. This innovative approach has been successfully demonstrated, showcasing the reversibility of catalyst deactivation and its potential for optimizing the ODHP process.

            However, persistent challenges include catalyst corrosion and reaction kinetics. The reverse water gas shift reaction is a promising method for CO2 mitigation, and nanocrystalline MgO has emerged as a favorable support for nickel-based catalysts. Despite recent advancements, the large difference in nickel particle size between different loadings significantly affects kinetic parameters, product selectivity, and pathways for CO and CH4 formation. High activity and lower rates of deactivation are observed with ex-FeZSM-5, likely due to its mesoporous structure and different iron constitution compared to conventional catalysts.

            The use of sulfur vapor for ODHP offers a promising alternative to oxygen, suppressing overoxidation. Studies on metal sulfide catalysts reveal the critical role of metal-sulfur bond strength in determining activation energy and propylene selectivity, with selectivities ranging from 38% to 86%. Supported alkali metal chloride melts emerge as new groups of catalysts for applications requiring minimized metal cation surface concentrations, offering improved selectivity towards olefins.

            Transition metal/metal oxides, particularly chromium (Cr) catalysts supported on SBA-15, show high activity due to Cr3+ sites, but deactivation occurs during reactions. Nickel (Ni) promotion enhances catalyst activity by increasing CO2's effectiveness as an oxidant and regenerating reduced Cr2+ states back to active Cr3+, stabilizing the catalytic process. Exsitu XPS measurements confirm the regeneration of active Cr3+ states under Ni promotion.

Research focuses on improving C-H bond activation, catalyst regenerability, and refining reaction systems to mitigate these challenges and optimize ODHP processes.
