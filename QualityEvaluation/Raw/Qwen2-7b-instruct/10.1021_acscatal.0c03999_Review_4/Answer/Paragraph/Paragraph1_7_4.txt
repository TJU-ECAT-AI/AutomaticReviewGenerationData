Comprehensive understanding derived from the examination of how the structural characteristics, surface properties (including acidity, basicity, and Lewis acid sites), and the interplay between them significantly influence the catalytic performance of Ga2O3 polymorphs (α-, β-, γ-, and δ-) in CO2-ODHP reactions. The initial and steady-state activity trends, selectivity towards propene, and the mechanisms underlying these phenomena are elucidated based on the detailed investigation of each polymorph's unique features. The acidity and basicity of the surface play pivotal roles in determining the initial activity and selectivity, with higher acidity often correlating with higher initial activity but leading to coke formation and subsequent deactivation. The β- and γ-Ga2O3 polymorphs, characterized by tetrahedral Ga cations and coordinatively unsaturated Ga3+ sites, demonstrate superior activity and selectivity due to their specific Lewis acid sites. On the other hand, the octahedral Ga3+ in α- and δ-Ga2O3 polymorphs, which are more coordinated with weaker acidity, allow for the potential reconstruction to tetrahedral structures, generating additional surface Lewis acid sites. The surface basic sites, however, are insufficient for effective CO2 adsorption and activation, leading to lower CO2 conversion compared to propane. The surface base site density correlates with initial activity, indicating its promotional effect in the initial stages of the reaction. The weak reducibility of Ga2O3 and the presence of Ga+ and Ga3+ suggest that the reduction of Ga cations occurs initially, benefiting dehydrogenation but only during the reaction's onset. At steady state, the reaction proceeds via a heterolytic dissociation mechanism on Ga oxide without undergoing a redox cycle. Various methods, including nanocasting and urea-based hydrothermal processing, have been employed to tailor the textural properties of Ga2O3, enhancing its catalytic performance for CO2-ODHP reactions.
