The number of zeolite structures containing 3-rings has grown rapidly during the past few years. This rapid growth partly stems from the recent advancements in structure determination methods, especially those based on X-ray and electron crystallography. The computer modeling techniques are increasingly recognized as not only a complementary tool to traditional structure determination methods but also a powerful method for predicting hypothetical zeolite structures. With the rapid development of both computer hardware and software, the number of hypothetical zeolite structures has seen an explosive growth. With the aid of various recently developed structure evaluation methods, it is now possible to filter out infeasible structures that are unlikely to be realized, allowing researchers to concentrate on the most promising ones.

Regarding the synthesis methods for metal-zeolite catalysts used in PDH reactions, MFI and BEA topological zeolites dominate due to their unique structural characteristics. MFI zeolites, with their 10-membered rings, offer a high surface area and pore volume, which is advantageous for the adsorption and diffusion of reactants and products. BEA zeolites, characterized by their 12-membered rings, provide a larger internal surface area and enhanced stability against high temperatures, which are crucial for the PDH process. The structural characteristics of these zeolites, combined with the appropriate metal loading and interaction with the zeolite framework, contribute to their effectiveness as PDH catalysts.

The effectiveness of MFI and BEA zeolites in PDH processes can be attributed to several factors. Firstly, the pore structure allows for the efficient adsorption and diffusion of propane molecules, promoting the dehydrogenation reaction. Secondly, the stability of these zeolites under high temperatures ensures prolonged activity, reducing the need for frequent regeneration. Lastly, the metal-zeolite interaction enhances the catalytic activity by promoting the formation of intermediate species that are more reactive in the dehydrogenation process.

For future developments in PDH catalyst design, incorporating more complex zeolite topologies with larger pore sizes and higher surface areas could potentially improve the catalyst's performance. Additionally, exploring the synergistic effects of different metal promoters and optimizing the metal loading could further enhance the catalytic activity. The integration of computer modeling techniques with experimental methods could also aid in the rational design of zeolite catalysts, allowing for the prediction and synthesis of catalysts with tailored properties for the PDH process. This would pave the way for the development of more efficient and sustainable PDH catalysts, contributing to advancements in the petrochemical industry.
