Analyzing the primary synthesis methods for metal-zeolite catalysts used in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions, it is evident that MFI and BEA topological zeolites dominate due to their well-defined and ordered mesoporous structures. These zeolites offer high surface areas and pore volumes, which are advantageous for the efficient confinement and stabilization of metal nanoparticles. The ordered structure ensures a high dispersion of metal species, prevents agglomeration, and facilitates the movement of carbon deposits from the active metal sites to the support surface. This not only enhances the interaction between the metal and the support but also stabilizes the oxidation states of certain metal promoters like tin, improving catalytic activity and stability. 

The implication for future developments in PDH catalyst design is the optimization of metal-support interactions through the strategic use of metal promoters like magnesium, which can improve dispersion and interaction of metal nanoparticles, as well as stabilize the oxidation states of tin. The one-pot synthesis method is highlighted as particularly effective in maintaining the mesoporous structure of the support and achieving a better distribution of magnesium species. This leads to improved catalytic performance, as demonstrated by the PtSn/2Mg-SBA-15 catalyst with 2 wt.% magnesium, which showed a high propane conversion and propene selectivity.

In summary, the effective synthesis methods for metal-zeolite catalysts in PDH reactions involve the use of MFI and BEA topological zeolites, strategic metal promoter incorporation, and the utilization of efficient synthesis methods like one-pot synthesis to achieve optimal metal dispersion and support interaction, ultimately enhancing catalytic activity and stability.
