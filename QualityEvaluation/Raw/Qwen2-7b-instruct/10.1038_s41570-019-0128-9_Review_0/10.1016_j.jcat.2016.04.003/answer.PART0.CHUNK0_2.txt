<quotes>
<p>CO2 coordination to metal centers represent by far the most common bonding mode of CO2. In [Ni(CO2)(PCy3)2], the NiAC and NiAO1 bond distances are, respectively, 1.84 and 1.99 Å, showing that both carbon and one of the oxygen atoms are bound to the nickel center, which exhibits a planar coordination.</p>
<p>The complex [(IPr)Ni]2(l-CO)(l2-g2,g2-CO2) documents an interesting case of a l2-g2,g2-coordination, in which each C@O bond of the bridging CO2 ligand is, respectively, side-on coordinated to a Ni atom.</p>
<p>In principle, side-on adducts may be considered as three-membered oxametallacycles resulting from a formal oxidative addition of one of the p-bonds of the CO2 ligand to the metal center or as p-complexes, wherein the metalCO2 interaction involves both electron donation from the O-centered 1puy(5a1) and 1pgy(4b2) orbitals of CO2 to empty d-orbitals of M and, at a greater extent, electron back-donation from a filled d-orbital of metal to the empty C-centered 2puy(6a1) orbital of the ligand.</p>
<p>The role of O-sink may be played by the metal atom itself, or with less oxophilic metals (late transition metals, for instance) by CO2 itself with conversion of two CO2 molecules into CO and O2.</p>
</quotes>

<EnglishVersionAnswer>
1. Coordination of CO2 to metal centers is the predominant bonding mode of CO2. In [Ni(CO2)(PCy3)2], both carbon and one of the oxygen atoms are bound to the nickel center, forming a planar coordination.

2. The [(IPr)Ni]2(l-CO)(l2-g2,g2-CO2) complex demonstrates a unique case of l2-g2,g2 coordination, with each C@O bond of the bridging CO2 ligand side-on coordinated to a Ni atom.

3. Side-on adducts can be considered as three-membered oxametallacycles, formed through oxidative addition of one of the p-bonds of the CO2 ligand to the metal center, or as p-complexes involving electron donation and back-donation between the metal and CO2 orbitals.

4. The deoxygenation of CO2 can be performed using suitable metal centers and oxophilic substances at room temperature, converting CO2 into CO and O2.

</EnglishVersionAnswer>
