<EnglishVersionAnswer>
Many catalysts for oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) of light alkanes can be grouped into three categories: alkali and alkaline earth-based catalysts, reducible transition metal oxides, and other catalysts such as B/P oxides, Ga/zeolite, LaF3/SmO, and Sn/P. Alkali and alkaline earth-based catalysts, especially those containing alkali metals, exhibit selective behavior in the ODH of ethane. Reducible transition metal oxides, like vanadium, molybdenum, and niobium, are active in oxidation at lower temperatures, typically around 473-838 K. For propane and butane, vanadium-based systems, such as vanadates and vanadia dispersed on various oxides, and molybdenum-containing catalysts, like molybdates and supported MoO3, are commonly used. The mechanisms of ODH reactions involve interactions of alkane with the catalyst surface, rupture of the C-H bond, reaction of the alkyl species with an adjacent surface oxygen, and cyclic reduction/reoxidation of the catalyst. Kinetic models used for ODH of light alkanes include the Eley-Rideal model, Langmuir-Hinshelwood model, rake model, Mars-Van Krevelen model, and power law model. In the case of ethane, the selectivity to ethene increases with temperature, and at around 873 K, the rate constant of ethene oxidation becomes comparable to that of ethane oxidation. The kinetic models and reaction networks for the oxidation of ethane have been proposed based on experimental data.
