The oxidative dehydrogenation of ethane and propane is discussed in the literature, focusing on the performance of various catalysts and their ability to produce light olefins. Catalysts based on ions and oxides of Group IA and IIA metals, such as Li/Mg/O, are active in ethane oxydehydrogenation, forming ethyl radicals which then react to form ethylene. High selectivities and yields to ethylene can be achieved with this catalytic system, especially when chlorine-containing compounds are fed to the reactor or when the catalyst is doped with halides. The selectivity to ethylene increases with temperature, as the ethyl radical at high temperatures desorbs and forms ethylene in the gas phase via reaction with molecular oxygen. At lower temperatures, a surface ethoxy species is preferred, which is a precursor of COx formation. H2 can be formed via thermal or catalyzed dehydrogenation, or through the water gas shift equilibrium over rare earth oxides or the decomposition of intermediate ethoxy species to CO, C, and H2. Various support materials such as SiO2, MCM-41, H-ZSM-5, Al2O3, ZrO2, and CeO2 affect the activity, selectivity, stability, and mechanistic pathways of chromium oxide catalysts in CO2 oxidative dehydrogenation reactions. The CO2 partial pressure also plays a significant role. For instance, alumina-Cr2O3 catalysts have been reported to be active in the oxidative dehydrogenation of ethane and propane. The Li/Mg/O catalyst system is particularly successful, involving C-H heterolytic scission with radical formation without requiring a redox-type cycle.
