Intramolecular C-H bond activation plays a pivotal role in the deactivation of supported alkene polymerization and metathesis catalysts, differing significantly between lanthanide/actinide-based and silica-supported Re-based catalysts. For lanthanide/actinide-based catalysts, the deactivation process often involves the formation of π-allyl surface complexes, which can undergo β-H transfer reactions leading to catalyst deactivation. The β-H transfer typically proceeds from a more hindered position to a less hindered one, destabilizing the catalyst and reducing its activity. On the other hand, silica-supported Re-based catalysts might deactivate through a similar mechanism involving surface complexes and β-H transfer, though the specifics and exact nature of these complexes may vary due to differences in the properties of the Re-based catalysts compared to their lanthanide/actinide counterparts. The potential consequences of these deactivation pathways on catalyst performance and longevity can be substantial, including decreased yield of desired products, increased production costs, and environmental impacts necessitating more frequent catalyst regeneration or replacement.
