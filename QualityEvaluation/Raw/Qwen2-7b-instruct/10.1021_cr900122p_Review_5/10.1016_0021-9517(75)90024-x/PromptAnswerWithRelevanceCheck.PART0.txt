Analyzing the comprehensive mechanism of alkane metathesis catalyzed by silica-supported tantalum hydrides involves understanding the role of both two-site and one-site schemes, the processes involved in transforming linear and cyclic alkane structures, the formation and function of key intermediates such as alkenes and metallacyclobutanes, and how these elements contribute to reaction selectivity and catalyst performance in various alkane conversions.

The silica-supported tantalum hydrides serve as efficient catalysts for alkane metathesis due to their ability to facilitate the rearrangement of alkene bonds. In the two-site scheme, the catalyst enables the transfer of a double bond from one alkene to another, forming two new alkene bonds. The one-site scheme, on the other hand, allows for a single alkene bond to be cleaved and reform, resulting in a rearrangement of alkyl groups.

During the transformation of linear alkane into cyclic alkane or vice versa, the catalyst plays a crucial role in creating and breaking alkene bonds. The formation of alkenes as intermediates is a critical step in this process, as they subsequently undergo rearrangements facilitated by the catalyst to form the desired products.

Key intermediates, such as alkenes and metallacyclobutanes, are essential components in alkane metathesis. Alkenes are produced during the cleavage of alkene bonds and serve as precursors for the formation of the desired alkane products. Metallacyclobutanes, on the other hand, are cyclic complexes formed by the coordination of metal centers with alkene ligands, which can undergo further reactions to yield the final alkane products.

The selectivity and performance of the catalyst in alkane conversions are influenced by factors such as the structure and properties of the silica support, the concentration and distribution of tantalum hydrides, and the reaction conditions, including temperature and pressure. These factors collectively determine the efficiency and specificity of the catalytic process, ensuring the production of desired alkane compounds with minimal side reactions and waste products.
