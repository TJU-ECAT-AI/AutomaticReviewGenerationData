Abstract 
Aluminosilicate zeolites are widely used as acid catalysts, and different types of acid sites 
on these materials have been identified and characterized thoroughly. However, distinct acid sites 
on zeolites generated during reaction, such as the Br√∏nsted acid sites on NaY upon exposure to 
organic acids and esters shown in our recent works (referred to as extrinsic sites), remain largely 
unexplored. In this work, we determine the density of extrinsic Br√∏nsted acid sites generated on 
NaY by acetic acid and methyl acetate by quantitative FTIR spectroscopy. Kinetic investigations 
using isopropanol dehydration as a model reaction show that the catalytic characteristics of the 
extrinsic Br√∏nsted acid sites on NaY are not only different from intrinsic Br√∏nsted acid sites on 
proton-exchanged faujasites, but also dependent on the precursor molecule, e.g., acetic acid and 
methyl acetate. The rate of isopropanol dehydration on NaY is found to scale linearly with the 
square root of the partial pressure of co-fed acetic acid, rather than the quantity of spectroscopically 
measured Br√∏nsted acid sites. We propose that the self-dissociation of acetic acid in the highly 
ionic zeolite pore network generates protons that are active for the dehydration reaction, and only 
fraction of these protons, i.e., those that are associated with the zeolite framework, can be identified 
spectroscopically. When co-feeding methyl acetate, the rate of isopropanol dehydration on NaY 
increases linearly with the quantity of spectroscopically measured Br√∏nsted acid sites, which is 
attributed to the fact that the self-dissociation of methyl acetate does not generate active protons 
within the zeolite pore.

1. Introduction 
 
Zeolites are crystalline, microporous materials with well-defined and tunable acid sites, 
and are used extensively as catalysts for a broad range of chemical transformations, especially in 
the petrochemical industry.1-3 The isomorphic substitution of Al for Si within the zeolite topology 
generates a negative framework charge, which must be balanced by a cation in the zeolite pore. 
The acid-base properties of zeolites can be tuned by changing the identity of this balancing cation. 
Proton-exchanged zeolites are Br√∏nsted acidic, while metal cation-exchanged zeolites exhibit 
Lewis acidic or even basic characteristics.4-8 Due to their industrial significance, acid sites on 
several common zeolite frameworks have been thoroughly characterized by numerous research 
techniques, including various spectroscopies, computational models, temperature-programmed 
desorption of probe molecules, and model reactions.3-13 Experimental techniques generally aim to 
characterize intrinsic zeolitic acid sites, i.e., those present on the material regardless of the reaction 
system, because these sites are generally assumed to be exclusively responsible for the catalytic 
properties of the zeolite. However, our recent works demonstrate that Br√∏nsted acid sites (BAS) 
can be generated on a solely Lewis acidic zeolite, NaY, upon the dissociative adsorption of organic 
acids (Scheme 1). The acidic hydrogen of the organic species exchanges with surface Na+ cations, 
generating an adsorbed sodium salt and a BAS on the zeolite framework. The BAS generated via

In this work, we employ a combination of spectroscopic and kinetic techniques to 
demonstrate that the catalytic properties of the extrinsic BAS on NaY are not only fundamentally 
different from those of intrinsic BAS on faujasites, but also dependent on the identity of the organic 
acid or ester precursor. Acetic acid (AA) and methyl acetate (MA) are used as precursors to 
generate the extrinsic BAS on NaY, and the density of generated sites is determined using 
quantitative FTIR spectroscopy. The ion exchange is governed by a Langmuir-type adsorption 
equilibrium, where framework negative charges act as immobile adsorption sites. Employing 
isopropanol (IPA) dehydration as a model reaction, the activation energies and TOF over both 
extrinsic and intrinsic BAS are measured, demonstrating the two types of sites have different 
catalytic properties. The IPA dehydration rate exhibits a linear dependence on the square root of 
the partial pressure of co-fed AA, leading us to propose a novel model to describe how the 
dissociative adsorption of AA on NaY generates the extrinsic BAS. The self-dissociation of AA 
within the highly ionic and confined zeolite pore generates two types of extrinsic BAS with

2.3 Transmission FTIR Spectroscopy 
 
In-situ Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra were obtained on a ThermoFisher 
Scientific Nicolet 6700 FTIR Spectrometer equipped with an MCT detector and a custom in situ 
transmission cell, collecting 128 scans per spectrum at a spectral resolution of 2 cm-1. Temperature 
control in the transmission cell is achieved through the use of heating tapes and insulation 
connected to a tuned PID controller. Vacuum levels of <0.1 mTorr are achieved through 
connection to a vacuum manifold containing both a mechanical pump and a diffusion pump. For 
experimental tests, a self-standing zeolite wafer is loaded into a custom-made sample holder for 
vertical alignment in the infrared beam. The samples were annealed at 450 ¬∞C under vacuum to 
completely remove surface adsorbates before cooling to the desired temperature. The appropriate 
species or mixture was then introduced to the transmission cell via the vacuum manifold. The 
amount of species in each dose, measured via the total system pressure, is controlled via a vacuum 
manifold.

measured using the following procedure. The density of Br√∏nsted acid sites on a proton-exchanged 
faujasite sample was measured via an isopropylamine decomposition quantification procedure as 
described in a previous publication.20 This sample was then loaded into the transmission FTIR cell, 
annealed, and cooled to 225 ¬∞C; a background spectrum was taken at this temperature. Pyridine 
was then introduced into the transmission cell for several minutes prior to evacuation, and this 
dosing procedure was repeated until the adsorbed pyridinium band increased by less than 1%

kinetic experiments. The identical catalytic activity of both H+asso and H+free is also supported by 
the constant activation energy across the PAA range investigated, because the ratio of the two 
species changes but the kinetic parameters do not.

4. Conclusions 
 
Through a combination of spectroscopic and kinetic experiments, we have elucidated the 
catalytic properties of the extrinsic BAS generated on NaY by the dissociative adsorption of AA 
and MA. We demonstrate that the generation of sodium acetate species on NaY via ion exchange 
is governed by a Langmuir-type chemisorption equilibrium, where framework negative charges 
act as immobile adsorption sites. The generated sodium acetate salt species are shown to be 
strongly adsorbed within the zeolite pore (‚àÜGads = -101 kJ/mol and -88 kJ/mol for AA and MA, 
respectively). The activation energy and turnover frequency for IPA dehydration are determined 
in a broad range of partial pressures of co-fed AA or MA, and the results show that the catalytic 
properties of the extrinsic BAS depend on the identity of the precursor species. While AA-
promoted sites exhibit a lower activation barrier and faster rate for IPA dehydration than MA-
promoted sites, both extrinsic BAS are one order of magnitude less active than the intrinsic BAS 
on partially proton-exchanged NaHY. The linear correlation of the dehydration rate with the square 
root of AA partial pressure leads to a model for the generation of the extrinsic sites. Within the
