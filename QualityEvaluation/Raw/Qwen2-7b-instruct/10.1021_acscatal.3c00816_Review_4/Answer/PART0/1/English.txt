The ALD process significantly enhances the activity of the Y zeolite, increasing its conversion rate to 1116% at 550°C when prepared through 13 cycles of ZnO ALD. The nanolith catalytic system outperforms conventional powders in oxidative dehydrogenation of cyclohexane, demonstrating both efficiency and reduction in over-oxidation. The ALD process generates high-density ZnO sites on the support, which boosts catalytic activity but also risks coke formation and crystallization of catalytic phases, leading to accelerated catalyst deactivation. The micropore size of Y zeolite is notably larger than that of DEZ, allowing precursor molecules to easily enter the micropores during the first three ALD cycles. Post-stability testing, the catalyst color transitions from white to black, indicating potential deactivation mechanisms.

The preparation methods, composition, and zeolite topology significantly influence the performance of zeolite-based catalysts in propane dehydrogenation reactions. Specifically, the addition of phosphorus (P) to a nickel-phosphorus (NiP) catalyst improves its performance in isobutane dehydrogenation. The BEA topological structure of the zeolite enhances catalytic activity, while silicoaluminophosphate (SAPO) zeolites exhibit better stability and selectivity towards propylene compared to other types of zeolites. These factors contribute to improved propylene selectivity, conversion rates, and catalyst stability in PDH reactions.

The preparation methods, composition, and zeolite topology play crucial roles in enhancing the performance of zeolite-based catalysts used in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions. Specifically, the Ga+-chabazite zeolite was found to be highly selective for non-oxidative PDH, demonstrating improved propylene selectivity, conversion rates, and catalyst stability compared to other zeolites. BEA topologic and silicoaluminophosphate zeolites also exhibited enhanced performance attributes due to their unique structural characteristics.

Questions focus on the impact of zeolite topology, preparation methods, and composition on the performance of zeolite-based catalysts in propane dehydrogenation (PDH), particularly regarding the role of BEA topology and silicoaluminophosphate zeolites. The discussion centers on how indium species, especially in the form of [InH2]+, affect the catalytic activity, selectivity, and stability of the catalysts. The relevance is evident in the exploration of how factors like indium loading, support materials, and the structure of the zeolite influence the catalyst's efficiency in converting propane to propylene. The mention of specific phenomena, such as the formation of extra-framework indium species (In-CHA), the role of Si/Al ratios, and the stability of catalysts over time, all contribute to the relevance of the answers to the questions.

The high-temperature stability of the subnanometric Pt species in Pt@MCM-22 has been demonstrated through oxidation-reduction treatments at 650°C, resulting in the formation of small Pt nanoparticles with sizes around 1nm. These nanoparticles are found in Pt@MCM-22 after one cycle of treatment at this temperature. Individual Pt atoms and small Pt clusters within the MCM-22 crystallites aggregate to form larger Pt clusters or small Pt nanoparticles in the range of 1 to 2nm. Considering the exceptional stability of Pt@MCM-22, its catalytic activity for dehydrogenating propane has been tested. It is noted that the dehydrogenation of short alkane molecules is crucial for alkane activation and requires frequent regeneration cycles at high temperatures. At 550°C, Pt@MCM-22 demonstrates higher activity than the Pt/MCM-22-imp catalyst.

The preparation methods, composition, and zeolite topology significantly influence the performance of zeolite-based catalysts in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions. Dealuminated Beta (SiBeta) zeolite, particularly when modified with cobalt (Co), enables the creation of ultrasmall Co particles confined within the zeolite's silanols. This configuration facilitates high propane conversion and exceptional propylene selectivity. The CoOx species, which can easily be reduced to metallic Co particles, are crucial for the catalyst's activity. The presence of CoOx species in the T-sites of the SiBeta support contributes to the catalyst's superior performance. The CoSiBeta catalysts with 0.5 wt% Co exhibit state-of-the-art propylene productivity and selectivity, matching that of commercial Pt and CrOx catalysts. The small confined Co particles serve as the active sites, showing great catalytic activity with high product selectivity and excellent reusability. This research provides a green, cost-effective, and highly active catalyst for PDH reactions, offering new insights into the active phase of cobalt catalysts in PDH.
