<EnglishVersionAnswer>
1. Hydrogenation of methylacetylene to form propylene is the most favored reaction pathway on all three surfaces, accounting for approximately 20% of the adsorbed monolayer.
2. The amount of reversibly adsorbed methylacetylene increases from none on Pt(111) to 60% of the adsorbed layer on the (E3×E3)R30Sn/Pt(111) surface alloy.
3. The (2×2)-Sn/Pt(111) surface alloy has the highest selectivity for other reaction products, depending on the Sn concentration.
4. The (2×2) surface alloy shows three high temperature desorption peaks at 370, 460, and 510 K.
5. The E3 surface alloy shows only a single intense high temperature desorption peak at 370 K.
6. The (2×2) surface alloy displays three methylacetylene peaks at 370, 460, and 505 K.
7. The E3 surface alloy's TPD spectra for methylacetylene is dominated by a single peak at 370 K.
8. Increasing the Sn concentration in the Pt(111) surface shifts the H2 desorption temperature for dehydrogenation during TPD.
9. The H2 TPD spectra demonstrate the desorption of methylacetylene after exposure to yield a monolayer coverage on the Pt(111) and Sn/Pt(111) surface alloys.
10. Cracking fractions associated with the desorption of the second, physisorbed layer of methylacetylene are observed at 130 K.
11. Propylene (C3H6, 42 amu) is the primary product, occurring in a broad feature from 270 to 350 K.
12. Butane (C4H10) is observed during the TPD process.
13. The peak at 165 K in the butene TPD spectrum corresponds to the cracking fraction of butane.
14. Benzene (78 amu) desorbs in a broad, reaction rate-limited peak at 550 K.
15. The amount of propylene produced on the three surfaces is determined by comparing the TPD spectrum from a saturation dose of propylene adsorbed on Pt(111).
</EnglishVersionAnswer>
