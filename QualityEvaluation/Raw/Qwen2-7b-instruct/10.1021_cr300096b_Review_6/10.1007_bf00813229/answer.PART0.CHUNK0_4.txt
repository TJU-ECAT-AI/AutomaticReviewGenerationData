<quotes>
<p>The interaction of sulfur with Pt(111), Zn/Pt(111) and Cu/Pt(111) has been examined using X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), X-ray excited Auger electron spectroscopy (XAES), and thermal desorption mass spectroscopy (TDS).</p>
<p>At temperatures between 300 and 600 K, the exposure of Pt(111) to S2 gas produces a chemisorbed layer of sulfur without the formation of bulk sulfides.</p>
<p>Exposure of S2 to a Zn/Pt(111) alloy, at room temperature, results in a breakdown of the alloy and formation of a zinc-sulfide film on Pt(111).</p>
<p>Further S2 exposure at 550 K sulfidizes the remaining metallic zinc without affecting platinum.</p>
<p>For the Cu/Pt(111) surface alloy, exposure to S22 at 550 K leads to sulfidation of the platinum.</p>
<p>Platinum can effectively compete for sulfur atoms bonded to copper but not for those bonded to zinc.</p>
<p>The reaction of S2 gas with Cu/Pt(111) surfaces produces copper sulfides that promote the sulfidation of Pt by providing surface sites for the dissociation of S2, and by favoring the diffusion of S into the bulk of the system.</p>
</quotes>

<EnglishVersionAnswer>
The study investigated the interaction of sulfur with Pt(111), Zn/Pt(111), and Cu/Pt(111) surfaces using various spectroscopic techniques. At 300-600 K, sulfur exposure to Pt(111) resulted in a chemisorbed layer without bulk sulfides. Room temperature exposure of S2 to a Zn/Pt(111) alloy led to alloy breakdown and zinc-sulfide film formation on Pt(111). At 550 K, S2 exposure sulfidized the remaining zinc while leaving platinum unaffected. On the Cu/Pt(111) surface, sulfur promoted platinum sulfidation by forming copper sulfides that facilitated sulfur dissociation and diffusion into the bulk system. Platinum could compete for sulfur atoms bonded to copper but not to zinc.
</EnglishVersionAnswer>
