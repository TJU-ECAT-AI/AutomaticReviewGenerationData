            Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The exploration of hierarchical zeolites has illuminated the path for enhanced accessibility and performance in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions. The integration of micropores and mesopores/macropores within these materials optimizes the surface area and pore size distribution, enabling more efficient reactant and product diffusion. The synthesis of these materials, whether through hydrothermal treatment, sol-gel processes, or template-directed assembly, has demonstrated their superiority over traditional zeolites in terms of catalytic activity and stability. For instance, the use of mesoporous-zeolite hybrids and templated macroporous zeolites offers additional pathways for reactants and products, enhancing the efficiency of the catalytic process. Moreover, the strategic incorporation of metals like Ga, In, and Zn within hierarchical zeolite frameworks has led to the development of highly selective and active catalysts for PDH reactions. The future of PDH catalysis might focus on refining these materials to achieve even higher activity and selectivity, exploring novel synthesis methods to tailor the pore structure for specific applications, and investigating the underlying mechanisms that govern the enhanced catalytic performance of hierarchical zeolites. Additionally, there is potential in leveraging the synergistic effects between hierarchical zeolites and other materials, such as metal nanoparticles or organic ligands, to create multifunctional catalysts with enhanced properties for PDH and other chemical transformations. The horizon of hierarchical zeolites in PDH catalysis is promising, offering a platform for advancing the efficiency and sustainability of propane conversion processes.
