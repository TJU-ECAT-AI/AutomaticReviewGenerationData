<EnglishVersionAnswer>
The hierarchical zeolites combine micropores and mesopores/macropores to enhance accessibility for PDH reactions by providing a larger surface area for reactants to interact with the catalyst. This structure allows for better diffusion of reactants and products, improving the performance of PDH reactions compared to traditional zeolites. The main types of these materials include metal-organic frameworks (MOFs), mesoporous silicas, and ordered mesoporous materials (OMMs). Synthesis methods vary depending on the type of material but generally involve techniques such as sol-gel, template-directed synthesis, and microwave-assisted synthesis. These materials improve the performance of PDH reactions by facilitating faster reaction kinetics, enhancing selectivity towards desired products, and reducing the risk of catalyst deactivation through mechanisms such as coke formation.

The hierarchical zeolites' enhanced performance compared to traditional zeolites stems from their ability to optimize pore size distribution, which is crucial for the efficient adsorption and desorption of reactants and products. The micropores provide sites for adsorption, while the mesopores/macropores ensure efficient mass transport and allow for easier product release. This combination of features minimizes the diffusion limitations often encountered in traditional zeolites, leading to improved catalytic activity and stability under PDH reaction conditions.
