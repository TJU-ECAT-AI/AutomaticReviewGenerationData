The key strategies and approaches that have been recently developed to enhance the catalytic stability of supported metal catalysts in direct dehydrogenation processes involve the use of intermetallic compounds such as the surface hexagonal Pt1Sn1 intermetallic on Pt nanoparticles. These advancements specifically address the challenges in catalyst design, fabrication, and performance optimization for industrial applications by improving selectivity and stability, thereby increasing efficiency and reducing costs.

The high chemical stability of the CH bonds in ethane and propane necessitates high temperatures for direct dehydrogenation reactions to achieve satisfactory olefin yields. However, for these direct hydrodealkylation (DH) catalysts, multiple issues may still impact their catalytic performance, including selective activation of CH bonds over CC bonds, olefin desorption, catalyst structure stability, and anti-coking ability. To sustain a viable operational lifetime, the routine removal of coke from used catalysts through frequent regeneration processes is necessary, which is often impractical for industrial-scale operations. Hence, significant efforts have been dedicated to the development of Pt-catalyzed PDH processes, focusing on enhancing catalyst efficiency and stability by leveraging insights into the interplay between structure and performance, as well as the catalytic mechanisms.

Key strategies and approaches that have been recently developed to enhance the catalytic stability of supported metal catalysts in direct dehydrogenation processes include the development of new support materials, modification of metal surface properties, incorporation of dual-metal systems, and the use of porous supports like carbon or metal-organic frameworks (MOFs). These advancements aim to increase the catalyst's resistance to sintering and deactivation, thereby improving its stability and efficiency, especially under high-temperature operations.
