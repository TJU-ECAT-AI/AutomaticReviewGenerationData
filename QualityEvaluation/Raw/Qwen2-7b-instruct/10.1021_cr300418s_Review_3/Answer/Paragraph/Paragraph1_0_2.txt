        The coprecipitation method is a key technique in the synthesis of doped ceria catalysts, as detailed in references \[10.1021/cr300418s_3\] and \[10.1134/s0023158408050121\]. It involves dissolving cerium(III) salts and dopant salts, with the use of citric acid in the case of iron doping, to precipitate both cations simultaneously. Different salts of the dopant are used based on its nature (nitrate for lanthanides, chloride for niobium and tantalum, and oxynitrate for zirconium). This method yields a range of doped ceria catalysts, including those with niobium doping, as highlighted in references \[10.1021/jp803775j\] and \[10.1021/cm800144q\].

        Preparation techniques significantly impact the structural properties and catalytic performance of these catalysts, as demonstrated in references \[10.1021/ja064899h\] and \[10.1021/cr300418s_3\]. For instance, the type of dopant, like niobium, can notably influence both the structure and catalytic efficiency, as seen in the enhanced activity of niobium-doped ceria catalysts in n-butane combustion reactions, per \[10.1021/jp803775j\].

        The incorporation of dopants like niobium into the lattice of ceria forms solid solutions such as cubic Ce1-xFxO2, as discussed in \[10.1134/s0023158408050121\]. This results in catalysts with improved performance over undoped ceria, as evidenced by higher combustion rates and lower activation energies for n-butane combustion, as observed in \[10.1021/jp803775j\].

        The pore size distribution and specific surface area of these catalysts, critical factors for the adsorption, diffusion, and reaction of organic molecules, are also influenced by the type and amount of dopant, as shown in \[10.1021/cm800144q\] and \[10.1021/ja064899h\]. For example, a small amount of iron doping leads to a higher specific surface area and narrower pore size distribution, enhancing the catalytic efficiency.
