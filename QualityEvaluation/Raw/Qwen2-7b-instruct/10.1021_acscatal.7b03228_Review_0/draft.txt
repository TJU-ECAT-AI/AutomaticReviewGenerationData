The theoretical analysis of transition-metal catalysts for formic acid decomposition highlights the crucial roles played by geometric and electronic effects in determining their catalytic performance. It is evident that the synergistic interplay between these effects leads to enhanced catalytic activity and selectivity. Innovative strategies for exploiting this interplay involve designing catalysts with optimized alloy compositions and structures, aiming to surpass the performance limitations of traditional monometallic catalysts.

        Increasing the amount of Zn in Pd nanoparticles, for instance, leads to changes in the EXAFS spectrum and an increase in the linearly bonded CO by IR spectroscopy, indicating shifts in reactivity. Further increases in Zn content enhance PdZn interactions, elevate the linear-to-bridging CO ratios by IR, and completely eliminate neopentane conversion. Bimetallic catalysts like PdZn demonstrate a preference for dehydrogenation over hydrogenolysis, particularly for propane, indicating distinct reactive ensembles required for different reactions. The decrease in hydrogenolysis selectivity is attributed to the isolation of active Pd atoms by inactive metallic Zn, underscoring the necessity of specific active sites for hydrogenolysis reactions. Understanding which factor—geometric or electronic—is more critical for a given reaction can guide the design of more effective catalysts.
