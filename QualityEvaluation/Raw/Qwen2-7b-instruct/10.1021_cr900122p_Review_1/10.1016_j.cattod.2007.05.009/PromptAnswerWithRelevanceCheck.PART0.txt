The oxidative dehydrogenation of alkanes (ODH) is a complex process that involves various catalysts, both bulk metal oxides and supported systems, to convert alkanes into olefins. Challenges in achieving high selectivity and conversion arise due to the intricate interplay between these factors. Catalysts based on vanadium oxide and molybdenum oxide are commonly studied, with each exhibiting unique characteristics affecting their performance. For instance, in propane ODH, the catalytic activity of vanadium oxide catalysts peaks at a certain loading, balancing between surface vanadia species' activity and accessibility to reactants. Selectivity to the corresponding olefins is optimized at intermediate values of active phase loading, corresponding to the development of the monolayer structure. 

Mechanisms of C-H bond activation and the formation of M-alkyl intermediates are central to understanding the reaction pathways. A Mars-van Krevelen redox mechanism, involving lattice oxygen in CH bond activation, is consistent across both propane and ethane ODH. The extent of vanadium reduction during steady-state ODH is notably lower than stoichiometric reduction, and only a fraction of reduced centers are active in catalytic turnovers. 

The support plays a crucial role in the reactivity of vanadium oxide in alkane ODH, influencing both the extent of propane combustion and the selectivity to propylene formation. Various studies suggest that the identity of the support can significantly affect the reaction parameters. These insights collectively impact the process efficiency by influencing the selectivity and conversion rates. Site isolation strategies, such as isomorphous substitution of active metal species into microporous and mesoporous materials, aim to enhance selectivity by isolating active sites, thereby addressing the selectivity issues encountered in traditional catalyst systems.
