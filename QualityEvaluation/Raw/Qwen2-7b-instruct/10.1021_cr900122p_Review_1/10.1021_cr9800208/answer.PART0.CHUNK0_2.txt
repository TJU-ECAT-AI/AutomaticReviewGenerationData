The oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) of alkanes involves the activation of C-H bonds and subsequent dehydrogenation to form alkenes or aromatics. This process is challenging due to the requirement for high selectivity and conversion, often requiring a balance between redox and acidic functionalities on the catalyst surface. The literature discusses the use of various catalysts, including bulk metal oxides and supported systems, to achieve these goals.

Niobium oxide, when supported on or mixed with TiO2, demonstrates significant catalytic activity in the ODH of alkanes. Surface niobium oxide species on titania play a crucial role in increasing the activity and selectivity of the catalysts. The presence of these species can enhance the selectivity towards nitrogen formation in the selective catalytic reduction (SCR) of NO, reaching essentially 100% at complete conversion of NO.

The SCR process typically involves a dual-site mechanism, where a surface vanadia redox site and an adjacent surface nonreducible metal oxide site collaborate. The addition of tungsten oxide to the surface of titania-supported vanadia catalysts can increase activity but may lead to a slight decrease in the selectivity towards N2 formation at 100% conversion of NO. Site isolation, through the generation of dual sites on the catalyst surface, is proposed to enhance the SCR activity and selectivity.
