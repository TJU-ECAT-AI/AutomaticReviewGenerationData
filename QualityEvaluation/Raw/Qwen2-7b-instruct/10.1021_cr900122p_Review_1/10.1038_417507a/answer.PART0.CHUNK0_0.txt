<quotes>
<p>Alkanes do react at high temperatures, as encountered in combustion, but such reactions are not readily controllable and usually proceed to the thermodynamically stable and economically unattractive products, carbon dioxide and water.</p>
<p>Although cracking and thermal dehydrogenation convert alkanes to valuable olefins,these processes require high temperatures and are energy intensive.</p>
<p>Similarly, although alkanes can be induced to react by exposure to highly reactive species such as superacids or free radicals, these reactive species are usually demanding and expensive to make, and offer little control over product selectivity.</p>
<p>Developing efficient strategies for the direct conversion of methane to methanol or other liquid fuels or chemicals could thus significantly improve methane utilization.</p>
<p>In principle, it should be possible to similarly activate the inert CH bonds of alkanes, but the examples just mentioned all involve small molecules with lone electron pairs and/or p orbitals that can interact with empty orbitals of the metal centres; alkanes possess neither of these.</p>
<p>During the 1970s, examples of significant interaction between a metal centre and an alkane CH bond suggested that this difficulty might have been overestimated.</p>
<p>Using systems designed to produce stable products, two groups demonstrated intermolecular alkane activation in 1982 (refs 15, 16).</p>
<p>The controlled activation of small, relatively inert molecules has been a ubiquitous theme in transition metal chemistry since its renaissance began in the late 1950s.</p>
</quotes>

<EnglishVersionAnswer>
1. Alkanes react at high temperatures during combustion, but these reactions are not easily controllable and typically lead to stable, economically unappealing products like carbon dioxide and water. Cracking and thermal dehydrogenation processes convert alkanes into valuable olefins but require high temperatures and are energy-intensive. Exposure to superacids or free radicals can induce reactions in alkanes, but these reactive species are costly and offer limited control over product selectivity.
   
2. The direct conversion of methane into methanol or other liquid fuels or chemicals could greatly enhance the utilization of methane resources. Transition metal chemistry, particularly the activation of small, inert molecules, offers potential for alkane conversion.

3. While small molecules like olefins and carbon monoxide become more reactive upon coordination to a metal center, alkanes lack the necessary electron pairs or p orbitals to interact effectively with metal centers. However, during the 1970s, significant interactions between metal centers and alkane CH bonds suggested that alkane activation might be possible.

4. In 1982, two research groups demonstrated intermolecular alkane activation using systems that produced stable products. This marked a significant advancement in the field of alkane conversion.

5. The ability to activate the inert CH bonds of alkanes through transition metal complexes holds promise for catalytic alkane conversion. The controlled activation of small, inert molecules is a well-established theme in transition metal chemistry, suggesting potential for effective alkane conversion techniques.
</EnglishVersionAnswer>
