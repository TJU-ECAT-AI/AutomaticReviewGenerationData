Analyzing the oxidative dehydrogenation of alkanes (ODH), we find that various catalysts, both in bulk metal oxides and supported systems, play crucial roles in the process. Challenges in achieving high selectivity and conversion arise due to the complexity of the reactions involved, which often include difficulties in controlling the consecutive oxidation of alkanes to carbon oxides. Site isolation, which refers to the separation of active sites on the catalyst surface, plays a significant role in addressing selectivity issues. By isolating active sites, it becomes possible to tailor the reaction pathways and enhance the yield of desired products. Factors influencing process efficiency include the nature of the catalyst, reaction conditions, and the interplay between the catalyst and reactants. The proposed reaction mechanisms, such as C-H bond activation, the formation of M-alkyl intermediates, and 2H-abstraction, collectively contribute to the overall efficiency of the process. These mechanisms involve the activation of alkane molecules through the breaking of C-H bonds, the formation of alkyl species, and the subsequent reaction of these species with surface oxygen to form alkenes. The role of site isolation in addressing selectivity issues lies in its ability to control the reaction pathways and optimize the conversion of alkanes to alkenes, thereby enhancing the overall efficiency of the ODH process.
