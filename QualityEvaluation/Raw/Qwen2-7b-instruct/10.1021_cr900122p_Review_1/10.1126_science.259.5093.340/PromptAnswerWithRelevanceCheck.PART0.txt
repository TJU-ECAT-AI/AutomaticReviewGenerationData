The oxidative dehydrogenation of alkanes (ODH) involves the conversion of hydrocarbon molecules into shorter-chain hydrocarbons or functionalized compounds through the breaking of C-H bonds. Challenges in ODH include achieving high selectivity and conversion, as well as the development of efficient catalysts. The use of bulk metal oxides and supported systems can offer different levels of activity and selectivity depending on their composition and structure. Site isolation plays a crucial role in addressing selectivity issues by confining the reaction to specific sites on the catalyst surface, thereby preventing unwanted side reactions. Catalysts such as mercury (Hg(II)) and thallium (Tl(III)) can be used for selective oxidation of methane due to their ability to mediate electrophilic reactions through intermediate alkyl metal species. The high yield and mild conditions of the Hg(II)-sulfuric acid system suggest that it is well-suited for studying the mechanism of alkane oxidation, including the activation of C-H bonds and the formation of M-alkyl intermediates. The reoxidation step, where mercurous bisulfate is converted back to mercuric bisulfate, indicates the efficiency of the sulfuric acid as an oxidant in this system.
