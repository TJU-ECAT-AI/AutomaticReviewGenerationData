<EnglishVersionAnswer>
The substitutional cation doping in metal oxide catalysts modifies surface chemistry and disrupts chemical bonding by altering the composition and structure of the oxide surface. This disruption influences the catalytic activity by creating new reactive sites or modifying existing ones, potentially enhancing the efficiency of industrially relevant reactions. The nature and location of active centers can be either the oxygen atoms near the dopant or the dopants themselves. For instance, VOx clusters supported on ceria exhibit higher activity for methanol oxidation compared to their bulk counterparts. However, there is insufficient evidence proving that improvements in catalytic activity result from the substitution of oxygen atoms with halogens. Computational studies are valuable in predicting qualitative trends and rules for catalyst design, aiding in the identification of better oxide catalysts. These calculations classify dopant-oxide pairs based on common properties, offering insights into whether experimental results align with computational predictions.
</EnglishVersionAnswer>

Substitutional cation doping in metal oxide catalysts modifies surface chemistry and disrupts chemical bonding by altering the electronic structure and coordination environment around the dopant and nearby oxygen atoms. This modification can lead to enhanced catalytic activity by creating new active sites, either involving the oxygen atoms near the dopant or the dopant itself, depending on the specific doping element and the reaction mechanism.

2. Replacing anions with other anions in metal oxide catalysts can also be beneficial. This anion doping might change the optical properties of the catalyst, which could impact its efficiency in light-driven processes like photocatalysis.

3. Computational methods are valuable in identifying qualitative trends and rules for catalyst design. They can help predict the behavior of dopant-oxide pairs, aiding in the search for improved catalysts by providing insights into how different doping strategies affect catalytic activity and stability.

4. Calculations have classified dopant-oxide pairs based on their common properties, offering guidance on which combinations might yield the most effective catalysts. However, these predictions need to be corroborated with experimental results, as computational models do not account for the complexities of real-world working conditions.
