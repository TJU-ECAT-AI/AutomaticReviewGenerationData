The dopants easily integrate into the NiO lattice and create solid solutions, irrespective of the valence of the foreign element and with a comparable ionic radius to Ni2+. The substitution process, triggered upon introducing the foreign elements, is accountable for the variation in nickel oxides' non-stoichiometry. Results from temperature-programmed oxygen desorption and temperature-programmed isotopic oxygen exchange methods align with the principle of controlled valence, affirming that the dissolution of lower/equal to nickel valence cations (Li1+, Mg2+) boosts the non-stoichiometric oxygen content in NiO, whereas higher valence cations (Al3+, Ga3+, Ti4+, Nb5+, Ta5+) function as electron donors, reducing the positive p+ hole concentration and the electrophilic O radicals of the NiO acceptor. A linear connection exists between the rate of 16O exchange in the TPIE experiments and the rate of ethane consumption in the ODH activity tests, confirming the active involvement of monitored oxygen species in the ethane activation and conversion mechanism.
