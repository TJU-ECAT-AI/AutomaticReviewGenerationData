<quotes>
<p>The combustion method involves rapid heating of an aqueous solution containing stoichiometric amounts of corresponding metal salts and hydrazine based fuels. During the combustion, the temperature reached for a short period (3060 s) is about 1000 C, and it cooled to 300 C in <1 min. Thus, the oxide formed at high temperature is quenched in the process. Evolution of a large amount of gases during the process is responsible for fine crystallite formation in the range 2535 nm.</p>
<p>In combustion synthesized Cu/CeO2, either the Cu2+ ions should get separated into copper oxide species from the support or the Cu2+ ions should get incorporated into the ceria lattice. If there is an ionic substitution of Cu2+ ions for Ce4+ sites in the CeO2 lattice, an oxide ion vacancy should be created in order to maintain the charge neutrality due to lower valent ionic substitution in addition to a decrease of the lattice parameter. Rietveld analysis of Cu/CeO2 catalysts has clearly indicated the formation of Ce1-xCuxO2-δ with oxide ion vacancy.</p>
<p>Examination of the X-ray diffraction data for the catalysts shows that the diffraction lines due to CuO are not detected, even when the 2θ region (3050) is expanded where CuO peaks are expected. Peaks due to Cu2O are also not detected.</p>
<p>TEM images of the catalysts are similar to pure CeO2 images, and there is no agglomerated CuO observed in the case of the Cu/CeO2 crystallite surface.</p>
<p>EPR studies show that the spectra of the catalysts contain Cu2+ species in a ceria matrix.</p>
<p>XPS studies reveal that the surface concentration of Cu is 32% in 5 atom % Cu/CeO2.</p>
<p>The EXAFS studies clearly indicate Cu2+ ion stabilization in Ce4+ sites in the form of Ce1-xCuxO2-δ solid solution.</p>
<p>The structural studies of Cu/CeO2 in comparison with pure CeO2 demonstrate that, in the combustion synthesized Cu/CeO2 catalysts, Cu2+ ions are incorporated into the CeO2 nanoparticles in the form of Ce1-xCuxO2-δ.</p>
</quotes>

These techniques address the challenges in establishing a clear relationship between dopant presence, structural changes, and enhanced catalytic performance by:

1. XRD provides insights into the crystal structure and phase purity of the catalysts, confirming the formation of a solid solution phase with the expected chemical composition.
2. TEM offers detailed information on the morphology and size of the catalysts, revealing the absence of separated CuO phase and the decrease in crystallite sizes in Cu/CeO2 catalysts compared to pure CeO2.
3. EPR and XPS confirm the presence of Cu2+ species in the CeO2 matrix and their oxidation state, highlighting the chemical interaction between Cu and CeO2 that affects the redox potentials of Cu species.
4. CV studies demonstrate the direct redox behavior of Cu species in the CeO2 matrix, indicating the synergistic effect of CeO2 on reducing the energy required for electron transfer during catalytic reactions.
5. EXAFS spectroscopy provides information on the local structure and coordination environment of Cu in the catalysts, showing the presence of oxide ion vacancies around Cu2+ ions and the formation of Cu2+OCe4+ linkages, which contributes to the stabilization of Cu2+ ions and the formation of a solid solution phase.

These techniques collectively address the challenges by providing a detailed understanding of the dopant incorporation, structural changes, and their impact on catalytic performance, thus ruling out alternative explanations and establishing a clear relationship between dopant presence, structural modifications, and enhanced catalytic activity.

The structures of these catalysts were refined as Ce1-xCuxO2-δ (x = 0.03 and 0.05; δ ≥ 0.13 and 0.16) with a fluorite structure and 58% oxide ion vacancy. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) did not detect CuO particles in the 5 atom % Cu/CeO2 catalyst. Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) studies confirmed the presence of Cu2+ species in the CeO2 matrix. The redox potentials of Cu species in the CeO2 matrix are lower than those in CuO. EXAFS studies revealed an average coordination number of 3 around the Cu2+ ion in the first shell at 1.96 Å, indicating an O2- ion vacancy around the Cu2+ ion. The CuO bond length is shorter compared to that in CuO. The second and third shells around the Cu2+ ion in the catalysts consist of Cu2+O2-Cu2+ at 2.92 Å and Cu2+O2-Ce4+ at 3.15 Å, respectively.
