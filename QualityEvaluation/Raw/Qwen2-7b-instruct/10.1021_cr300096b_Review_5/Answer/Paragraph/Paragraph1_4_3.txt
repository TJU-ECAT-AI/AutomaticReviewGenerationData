        The in-depth exploration of thermodynamic preferences and reaction conditions in bimetallic catalysts, particularly those based on platinum (Pt), has revealed significant insights into their stability, surface segregation, and structural configuration. This review delves into how these factors influence the catalytic properties and performance of Pt-based catalysts, with a special emphasis on Pt-3d alloys and their role in propane dehydrogenation processes.

        The study by Ruban, Skriver, and Nrskov (DOI: 10.1103/physrevb.59.15990) highlights the importance of surface segregation energies in transition-metal alloys, which are influenced by thermodynamic preferences and reaction conditions. These conditions dictate the stability, surface segregation, and structural configuration of bimetallic catalysts, notably impacting the performance of Pt-based systems such as Pt-3d alloys.

        The theoretical study by Skriver et al. (DOI: 10.1021/jp2000859) focuses on the local electronic alloy effects of oxygen-related molecules on a PtPd cluster model. It underscores the profound influence of thermodynamic preferences and reaction conditions on the stability, surface segregation, and structural configuration of bimetallic catalysts, with particular relevance to Pt-based systems like Pt-3d alloys. These factors are critical for the catalytic properties and performance in various applications.

        Ternary alloys, as discussed by Skriver et al. (DOI: 10.1149/1.3409559), exhibit a strong Pt surface segregation tendency, leading to the formation of a Pt monolayer (ML) on their surfaces. This segregation enhances the activity for the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) compared to bimetallic alloy surfaces, contributing to the superior catalytic properties and performance of Pt-based alloy catalysts.

        The thermodynamics and kinetics of oxygen-induced segregation of 3d metals in Pt, as explored by Skriver et al. (DOI: 10.1063/1.2900962), reveal that the crystal plane of the metal surface affects both the ORR activity and the subsurface stability of the catalysts. This insight is pivotal for understanding the catalytic properties and performance of Pt-based systems.

        The confinement effect, as described by Skriver et al. (DOI: 10.1021/jp1097124), transforms ethylene into a radical anion monomer prepared for polymerization, demonstrating enhanced reactivity for both controlled polymerization and specific chemical reactions. This process is enabled by electron transfer between metal surfaces when they are in close proximity, typically within 0.6 to 0.4 nm.

        The work by Skriver et al. (DOI: 10.1002/cphc.201001087) investigates the stability, surface segregation, and structural configuration of bimetallic catalysts, with a focus on the role of thermodynamic preferences and reaction conditions. It reveals that the interaction energies between components and segregation energies determine the stability of these catalysts. Surface alloys, confined to the surface layer, are associated with the tendency of guest metals like Pt and Pd to remain in the outermost layer, influenced by kinetic effects such as a lower barrier for exchange between the top two layers compared to bulk diffusion.

        The adsorption of nitrogen dioxide (NO2) on a (3×3)R30 Sn/Pt(1 1 1) surface alloy, as analyzed by Skriver et al. (DOI: 10.1016/j.susc.2004.03.030), leads to the formation of an NN bonded N2O4 dimer adsorbed in an upright, tilted geometry. This structure undergoes complete dissociation between 220 and 300 K, releasing surface oxygen and coadsorbed NO. The adsorbed NO2 further dissociates to coadsorbed NO and O at higher temperatures, affecting the catalytic properties of the Pt-3d alloy.

        The study of surface segregation of Re 3 Pt polycrystalline alloy with photoelectron spectroscopy, as reported by Skriver et al. (DOI: 10.1063/1.2987704), aims to improve the stability, surface segregation, and structural configuration of bimetallic catalysts, specifically in Pt-based systems like Pt-3d alloys. These improvements have significant implications for enhancing the catalytic properties and performance of these materials across different applications.

        Oscillation of surface structure and reactivity of PtNi bimetallic catalysts with redox treatments at variable temperatures, as discussed by Skriver et al. (DOI: 10.1021/jp206517), indicates that thermodynamic preferences and reaction conditions significantly impact the stability, surface segregation, and structural configuration of bimetallic catalysts, particularly in Pt-based systems. These factors play crucial roles in determining their catalytic properties and performance.

        The integrative understanding and prospective outlook of these studies suggest that the field of bimetallic catalysts, especially Pt-based systems, holds immense potential for advancing catalytic technologies. Future research directions could focus on exploiting the synergistic effects of thermodynamic preferences, reaction conditions, and structural configurations to develop more efficient and selective catalysts. By leveraging the insights gained from these studies, researchers can design catalysts with optimized properties tailored to specific applications, thereby enhancing their performance in industries ranging from petrochemical processing to environmental remediation.

        The catalytic properties and performance of Pt-based catalysts are deeply intertwined with the interplay between thermodynamic preferences, reaction conditions, and structural configurations. As highlighted in this review, these factors not only govern the stability and surface segregation of bimetallic catalysts but also significantly impact their reactivity and selectivity. By understanding and controlling these parameters, scientists and engineers can engineer catalysts with tailored properties, optimizing them for various industrial applications and environmental challenges. The future of catalysis research in this area promises to be rich with opportunities for innovation and discovery, paving the way for more sustainable and efficient chemical processes.

    <Chinese>
        探讨热力学偏好和反应条件对双金属催化剂，特别是基于铂（Pt）系统的稳定性、表面聚集和结构配置的影响，揭示了这些因素如何影响Pt基系统（如Pt-3d合金）的催化性能和应用。本综述重点探讨了热力学偏好和反应条件如何影响丙烷脱氢过程中的催化剂性能。

        Ruban、Skriver和Nrskov的研究（DOI：10.1103/physrevb.59.15990）强调了过渡金属合金表面聚集能的重要性，这些能受到热力学偏好和反应条件的影响。这些条件决定了双金属催化剂的稳定性和表面聚集，特别影响了Pt基系统（如Pt-3d合金）的性能。

        Skriver等人的研究（DOI：10.1021/jp2000859）专注于氧相关分子在PtPd簇模型上的局部电子合金效应，表明热力学偏好和反应条件显著影响双金属催化剂的稳定性和表面聚集，对Pt基系统（如Pt-3d合金）具有深远影响。这些因素对于各种应用中的催化性能至关重要。

        Skriver等人（DOI：10.1149/1.3409559）探讨了三元合金中强烈的Pt表面聚集倾向，导致其表面形成一层Pt单层（ML）。这种聚集增强了氧气还原反应（ORR）的活性，与二元合金表面相比，这有助于Pt基合金催化剂的优异催化性能和应用。

        Skriver等人（DOI：10.1063/1.2900962）对3d金属在Pt中的氧诱导聚集的热力学和动力学进行了研究，揭示了金属表面的晶体平面不仅影响ORR活性，还影响亚表面稳定性。这一洞察对于理解Pt基系统（如Pt-3d合金）的催化性能和应用至关重要。

        Skriver等人（DOI：10.1021/jp1097124）描述了限制效应如何将乙烯转化为准备进行聚合的自由基阴离子单体，显示了控制聚合和特定化学反应的增强反应性。这一过程由金属表面之间的电子转移启用，当它们处于接近0.6到0.4纳米的距离时。

        Skriver等人（DOI：10.1002/cphc.201001087）调查了双金属催化剂的稳定性和表面聚集，特别关注热力学偏好和反应条件的作用。研究表明，组件间的相互作用能量和聚集能决定了这些催化剂的稳定性。限制在表面层的表面合金与客体金属如Pt和Pd倾向于留在外层最外层有关，受动力学效应的影响，如在顶部两个层之间的交换壁垒比在宏观扩散时要低。

        Skriver等人（DOI：10.1016/j.susc.2004.03.030）分析了氮氧化物（NO2）在（3×3）R30 Sn/Pt(1 1 1)表面合金上的吸附，导致形成一个NN键合的N2O4二聚体，以直立倾斜的几何形状吸附在表面上。这个结构在220至300 K之间完全分解，释放出表面氧和共吸附的NO。吸附的NO2进一步分解为共吸附的NO和O，在较高温度下，吸附的氧以三种清晰的脱附状态释放。Sn氧化过程通过Sn AES峰值的变化来监控。NO2在220至300 K之间的分解导致释放表面氧和共吸附的NO，随后氧化Sn在3合金中的表面。

        Skriver等人（DOI：10.1063/1.2987704）使用光电子谱研究了Re 3 Pt多晶合金的表面聚集，旨在改善稳定性和表面聚集，特别是在Pt基系统（如Pt-3d合金）中的双金属催化剂性能。这些改进对提高这些材料在不同应用中的催化性能具有重要意义。

        Skriver等人（DOI：10.1021/jp206517）讨论了PtNi双金属催化剂在变温下的氧化还原处理过程中表面结构的振荡，表明热力学偏好和反应条件显著影响双金属催化剂的稳定性和表面聚集，特别是在Pt基系统中。这些因素在决定催化剂性能和应用方面发挥着关键作用。

        这些研究的综合理解和前瞻性展望表明，双金属催化剂领域，特别是Pt基系统，为推动催化技术的发展提供了巨大的潜力。未来的研究方向可以集中在利用热力学偏好、反应条件和结构配置之间的协同作用，开发更高效和选择性的催化剂。通过利用这些研究的见解，科学家和工程师可以设计出具有定制特性的催化剂，优化它们用于各种工业应用和环境挑战。催化领域的未来充满创新和发现的机会，为更可持续和高效的化学过程铺平道路。

        Pt基催化剂的催化性能和性能与热力学偏好、反应条件和结构配置之间的相互作用密切相关。正如本综述所揭示的，这些因素不仅调节双金属催化剂的稳定性和表面聚集，而且还显著影响其活性和选择性。通过理解和控制这些参数，科学家和工程师可以在各种应用中定制催化剂的性能，从而实现更高效的化学过程和更环保的技术解决方案。
