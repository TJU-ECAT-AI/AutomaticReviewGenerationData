Challenges in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) for industrial propylene production are multifaceted, encompassing the necessity for catalysts that demonstrate high-temperature stability, high propylene selectivity, and robustness against side reactions. These hurdles are compounded by the inherent thermodynamic constraints of the dehydrogenation process. Researchers are tackling these issues through the development and refinement of novel catalyst compositions, with a particular emphasis on materials such as gallium oxide (Ga2O3), molybdenum oxide (MoOx), and vanadium oxide (Vox) as alternatives to traditionally employed noble metals like platinum (Pt) and chromium oxide (CrOx). These alternatives address concerns over cost, environmental impact, and the rapid deactivation caused by coke formation.

            Opportunities for PDH technology are burgeoning, particularly in the realm of reducing reliance on crude oil as a propylene feedstock by leveraging abundant natural gas resources. The recent increase in shale gas production has rendered natural gas a more economically viable feedstock compared to crude oil, stimulating interest in alternative methods such as oxidative dehydrogenation and methanol-to-olefins (MTO) processes. These alternatives offer competitive advantages over conventional techniques like fluid catalytic cracking and steam cracking, paving the way for more efficient and sustainable PDH technologies.

            To circumvent thermodynamic limitations, researchers are exploring synergies between catalyst materials and oxidizing agents that enable complete conversion of paraffins in oxidative dehydrogenation while preserving high olefin selectivity. Solid oxygen carriers, including ceria (CeO2), antimony oxide (SbO4), indium oxide (In2O3), tungsten oxide (WO3), lead oxide (PbOx), thallium oxide (Tl2O3), and bismuth oxide (BiO3), are under investigation for their capability to selectively oxidize hydrogen produced during the dehydrogenation reaction. Noble metals like palladium (Pd) and gold (Au) also play a role in catalyzing selective hydrogen combustion, albeit requiring oxygen input to the reactor to facilitate the reaction.

            Alternative strategies involve the utilization of membranes to remove hydrogen from the reaction mixture, facilitating a shift in equilibrium towards the desired olefin product. This approach allows for nearly complete conversion of paraffins with minimal side reactions. However, careful management of the interplay between catalyst deactivation and regeneration is crucial in industrial settings to optimize process efficiency and reduce downtime. Recent advancements in operando spectroscopy techniques, such as Raman/UV-vis spectroscopy, are enhancing the precision of catalyst deactivation monitoring and coke formation insights, enabling dynamic adjustments to dehydrogenation and regeneration phases.
