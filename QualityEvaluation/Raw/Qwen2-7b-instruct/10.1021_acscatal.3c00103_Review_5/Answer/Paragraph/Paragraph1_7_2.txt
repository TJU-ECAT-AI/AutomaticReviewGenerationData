Researchers are tackling these issues through the development and enhancement of catalyst formulations. For instance, metal oxides like gallium oxide (Ga2O3), molybdenum oxide (MoOx), and vanadium oxide (Vox) are being explored as alternatives to traditionally used noble metals (e.g., Pt, CrOx), which suffer from high costs, environmental concerns, and rapid deactivation due to coke formation. Strategies for controlling coke formation, improving catalyst stability, and enhancing selectivity are crucial areas of focus.

        Opportunities exist in reducing the reliance on crude oil for propylene production by leveraging abundant natural gas resources. The increase in shale gas production has made natural gas a more economical feedstock compared to crude oil, sparking interest in alternative methods such as oxidative dehydrogenation and methanol-to-olefins (MTO) processes. These offer competitive alternatives to conventional methods like fluid catalytic cracking and steam cracking. Advancements in catalyst design, reaction engineering, and process control are anticipated to lead to more efficient and sustainable PDH technologies.

Solid oxygen carriers, including ceria (CeO2), antimony oxide (SbO4), indium oxide (In2O3), tungsten oxide (WO3), lead oxide (PbOx), thallium oxide (Tl2O3), and bismuth oxide (BiO3), are under investigation for their ability to selectively oxidize hydrogen produced during the dehydrogenation reaction.
