Zeolites, with their well-defined pore structures and ability to modulate acidity, are crucial in supporting catalysts for CO2-ODHP reactions. Acidity modification techniques such as dealumination and weakening aim to optimize the acidity of zeolites, thereby enhancing the catalytic performance.

Dealumination refers to the removal of aluminum atoms from the zeolite framework, which can alter the acidity and porosity of the material. This process can create more acidic sites that are beneficial for certain reactions, such as dehydrogenation or oxidative processes, by providing active sites for hydrogen abstraction or oxygen transfer.

Weakening methods involve adjusting the strength of the zeolite framework, often by introducing defects or altering the structure. This can also affect the acidity and reactivity of the material, making it more suitable for specific catalytic reactions.

Key considerations in selecting and implementing these strategies depend on the specific requirements of the CO2-ODHP reaction. Different types of zeolites, such as HZSM-5, Y, or MFI-type zeolites, have distinct properties that may necessitate different approaches. For instance, the choice of dealumination or weakening might depend on the balance between acidity and stability needed for the reaction conditions.

Overall, the goal is to tailor the zeolite support to enhance the catalytic activity, stability, and selectivity for the desired products in CO2-ODHP reactions. The effectiveness of these strategies is often evaluated through a combination of experimental data and theoretical modeling to ensure optimal performance under various operational conditions.
