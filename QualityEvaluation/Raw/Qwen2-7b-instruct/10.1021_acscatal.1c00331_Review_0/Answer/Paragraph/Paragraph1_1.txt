        The catalytic direct dehydrogenation of propane (PDH) serves as a pivotal method for propene production, driven by the escalating demand for alkenes. High temperatures, while advantageous for propene generation, often lead to coke formation and catalyst deactivation, thereby reducing product yield. Carbon dioxide (CO2), a significant greenhouse gas, has been recognized for its capability to enhance the PDH process by increasing olefin yield and promoting reactions on catalysts like silica-supported Cr2O3, rare earth vanadates, and β-Ga2O3. Its utilization is economically and ecologically beneficial, particularly at temperatures exceeding 550°C, where it effectively activates β-Ga2O3, indicating its potential for improving catalyst performance and mitigating coke formation.

        The process of propane dehydrogenation to produce propene is crucial, with key considerations encompassing optimized reaction conditions, catalyst types, and industrial applications. The efficiency of PDH processes can be notably impacted by coke formation on Pt-based catalysts, which reduces the surface area available for reactions, necessitating strategies to mitigate this issue and improve catalyst regeneration. Promoters such as Sn can be employed to enhance catalyst activity and stability, addressing the critical challenge in PDH processes.

        Earth-abundant and environmentally benign metals, such as iron, have shown promise in forming in situ carbide phases that exhibit high activity and selectivity for PDH reactions. Density Functional Theory (DFT) elucidates the high selectivity of Fe3C surfaces in PDH reactions, with carbon-rich surfaces showing much higher selectivity for propylene production over competing cracking reactions compared to iron-rich surfaces. Strategies to mitigate coke formation and improve catalyst regeneration include modifying catalyst composition, enhancing cooling rates, and implementing more efficient regeneration protocols.

        In the face of the growing demand for propylene and the decline in propylene production from steam cracking, PDH catalysis emerges as an attractive method for propene production. Commercialized PDH catalysts are based on Pt or Cr, which are either expensive or toxic. Earth-abundant metals, particularly iron, form in situ carbide phases that offer a cost-effective, eco-friendly alternative. The high selectivity of Fe3C surfaces in PDH reactions, along with the strategies to mitigate coke formation and improve catalyst regeneration, highlights the ongoing advancements in propene production technology.
