        The catalytic direct dehydrogenation of propane (PDH) serves as a pivotal method for propene production, offering an efficient pathway amidst growing demand for alkenes. The process hinges on the thermal decomposition of propane molecules, yielding propene and hydrogen, with the choice of catalyst and operational conditions being critical factors for achieving optimal results. Key considerations include the selection of suitable catalysts, such as noble metals like platinum (Pt) and their potential for deactivation due to coke formation, which can significantly impair process efficiency.

        Recent advancements in the field highlight the utilization of carbon dioxide (CO2) as an oxidant, which not only enhances olefin yield but also promotes the reaction on catalysts like silica-supported Cr2O3, rare earth vanadates, and β-Ga2O3, thereby offering a more sustainable approach to PDH. The economic and ecological advantages of employing CO2 further underscore its potential in enhancing catalyst performance and reducing coke formation.

        However, the formation of coke on Pt-based catalysts remains a significant challenge, impacting process efficiency by reducing the available surface area for reactions. Strategies to mitigate this issue include optimizing operating conditions, such as temperature and pressure, and using catalyst supports with low Brnsted acidity, along with the incorporation of promoters like tin (Sn) to enhance catalyst activity and stability.

        In the realm of catalyst design, the exploration of single-atom catalysts and the use of atomically dispersed Pt on supports like Al2O3 are providing novel approaches to achieve high activity and selectivity, addressing the need for cost reduction while maintaining high propylene selectivity and improving anticoking abilities.

        The integration of these strategies, coupled with the ongoing research into the mechanism of coke formation and its effects on catalyst deactivation, holds promise for the development of more efficient and sustainable PDH processes. This includes not only the optimization of existing catalyst systems but also the discovery of new catalysts with improved properties and enhanced resistance to coke formation.

        Looking ahead, the future of PDH catalysis might focus on the development of catalysts that can operate under milder conditions, reducing energy consumption and environmental impact, alongside the implementation of advanced regeneration protocols to extend catalyst life and minimize the need for costly and energy-intensive regeneration processes.
