          The catalytic direct dehydrogenation of propane (PDH) functions as a method for propene production by converting propane into propene through a chemical reaction facilitated by a catalyst. Key considerations for PDH include reaction conditions, such as temperature, pressure, and the presence of a suitable solvent; catalyst types, including metal-based catalysts like palladium (Pt) or iron (Fe); and industrial applications, which involve optimizing the process for cost-effectiveness and efficiency on a large scale. Coke formation on Pt-based catalysts can significantly impact process efficiency because coke buildup reduces the surface area available for reactions, leading to decreased catalytic activity and higher energy consumption. Strategies to mitigate coke formation and improve catalyst regeneration might include using catalysts with enhanced stability against coke formation, employing advanced regeneration techniques, or designing processes that incorporate continuous catalyst regeneration to maintain high efficiency.
