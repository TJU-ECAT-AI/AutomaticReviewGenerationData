However, limitations remain, including high energy consumption in endothermic reactions, catalyst deactivation risks, and environmental impacts from by-product generation. Optimizing reaction conditions, developing sustainable catalysts, and integrating waste heat recovery systems are essential for ongoing technology development and implementation.

            Palladium membranes, with their exclusive selectivity to hydrogen, have emerged as a promising solution, allowing for efficient hydrogen product separation. Optimization of sweep gas flow rates in membrane reactors can significantly boost conversion, with reported increases of up to 55%. Nonetheless, refining permeability data and addressing potential contamination issues in membrane reactors are critical areas for future research. The ability of membrane reactors to operate under less stringent conditions also mitigates catalyst coking and reduces regeneration frequency.

            To further enhance dehydrogenation processes, ongoing research should concentrate on developing more robust catalysts with reduced coking tendencies, expanding the operational range of palladium membranes, and refining reactor designs to optimize heat and mass transfer, thus improving energy efficiency and environmental sustainability. The development of palladium alloy membranes, which demonstrate lower coking tendencies compared to alternatives, represents a significant advancement in this direction.
