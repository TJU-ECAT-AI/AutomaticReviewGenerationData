<EnglishVersionAnswer>
The mechanisms of C-H bond activation and catalyst regeneration in oxidative dehydrogenation of alkanes vary significantly between redox-active and redox-inactive metal oxide catalysts. Redox-active catalysts such as V2O5 and VOx/SiO2 facilitate both the reduction of CO2 and the oxidation of alkane molecules through redox reactions, which can lead to more efficient activation of CO2 for oxidative dehydrogenation of propane. In contrast, redox-inactive catalysts like CeO2 might require additional reductants for CO2 reduction, resulting in a separate step before the oxidative dehydrogenation process begins. Transition metal clusters could offer a compromise, potentially providing a single-step mechanism for CO2 activation and alkane oxidation due to their ability to act as both oxidants and reductants. Traditional oxidants play a crucial role in the oxidative dehydrogenation of alkanes, often functioning alongside the catalysts to drive the reaction forward. The energy barriers for CO2 dissociation are an important consideration in these processes, with lower barriers typically indicating higher activation efficiency and thus better performance of the catalysts in transforming CO2 into valuable products.
