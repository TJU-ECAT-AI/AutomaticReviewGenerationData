How does the multidimensional nature of the ODHP reaction mechanism over B-based catalysts, encompassing both surface and gas-phase radical reactions, contribute to our understanding of the overall reaction network, and what are the implications of their synergistic effects for catalyst performance and design optimization?

The multidimensional nature of the ODHP reaction mechanism over B-based catalysts, which includes both surface and gas-phase radical reactions, contributes significantly to our understanding of the overall reaction network. By involving surface-mediated formation of radicals over metastable sites, followed by their sequential propagation in the gas phase, this mechanism elucidates the high selectivity and product distribution observed with boron-based catalysts. The synergistic effects of these reactions imply that optimal catalyst performance can be achieved through a balanced interplay between surface-initiated reactions and gas-phase radical chemistry. This understanding can guide the design of more selective catalysts by focusing on the generation of reactive H-abstracting species that favor the abstraction of secondary CH bonds in propane and minimizing interactions with intermediates and products to prevent fast consecutive surface oxidation steps.

The implications of these synergistic effects for catalyst performance and design optimization are profound. They suggest that catalysts should be engineered to efficiently generate reactive species in the gas phase, which can then propagate and react with propane substrates, leading to the formation of desired products. Additionally, catalyst surfaces should be optimized to facilitate the initiation of radical reactions while minimizing the formation of intermediates and products that could lead to fast surface oxidation. This knowledge can inform the development of catalyst architectures with enhanced void spaces to promote gas-phase reactions and the creation of catalyst materials that can effectively generate HOO radicals, which are crucial for the dehydrogenation of propane. Furthermore, the dynamic nature of the surface changes during ODH may benefit from computational investigations of metastable surface sites, beyond thermodynamically stable species, to identify novel active sites that could further enhance catalyst performance.
