1. Active carbon has been widely used as a support for various catalysts, including those used in hydrogenation processes. It is a promising material for alkane dehydrogenation catalysts due to its properties.

2. Active carbon supports have been tested in the dehydrogenation of ethylbenzene and isobutane under conditions with carbon dioxide.

3. Propene is crucial as a raw material for various chemical intermediates and polymers.

4. The demand for propene, particularly in the production of polypropylene, acrylonitrile, propylene oxide, and cumene, has spurred research into alternative methods of propene production.

5. Recent studies have focused on the dehydrogenation of propane using carbon dioxide as an alternative to traditional oxidative dehydrogenation processes. 

6. In this alternative process, propene is produced with a higher yield than in conventional methods.

7. Numerous catalysts have been examined for use in the dehydrogenation of propane in the presence of carbon dioxide.

8. Various metal oxides (Ga, Cr, V, Fe, Zr, and Zn) have been utilized as active components, while potassium oxide has served as a promoter. 

9. Inorganic oxides (SiO2, Al2O3, TiO2, ZrO2, MgO), mesoporous sieves (MCM-41, SBA-15, MSU-x), and carbon-based materials (diamond, active carbon) have been considered as support materials.

10. Some studies have indicated that the enhancing effect of carbon dioxide on propane dehydrogenation largely depends on the properties of the support material.

11. The authors of the document demonstrated the positive role of active carbon in the catalytic process of propane dehydrogenation with carbon dioxide.

12. The study explored the true function of the active carbon support through thermogravimetric analysis of fresh and used chromium oxide supported on active carbon in inert and oxidizing atmospheres.

13. The optimal catalyst composition was identified for a reaction conducted at a thermodynamically suitable temperature of 600°C.

14. The influence of chromium loading on initial propane conversion and product selectivity was illustrated in Figures 3A and B.

15. Compared to other catalysts tested for propane dehydrogenation with carbon dioxide, the activity of the Cr(5.0)/AC catalyst was notably high.

16. The rate of homogeneous phase reaction and the catalytic performance of the Cr(5.0)/AC sample were compared at different temperatures.

17. The active carbon-based catalysts experienced rapid deactivation over time.

18. The thermal analysis method was employed to investigate the reasons behind the loss of activity.

19. The non-modified active carbon began reacting with carbon dioxide at temperatures above 850°C.

20. The CrOx phase was found to have a catalytic effect on the gasification of the active carbon support with carbon dioxide.

21. Depending on the experimental conditions, the Cr(5.0)/AC sample started to be oxidized at 670°C in a CO2 atmosphere or 690°C in a He atmosphere.

22. It was proven that carbon dioxide had minimal effect on the carbonaceous support under the conditions utilized in the catalytic process.

23. The study highlights the importance of active carbon as a support in the dehydrogenation of propane with carbon dioxide and provides insights into the reasons for catalyst deactivation.
