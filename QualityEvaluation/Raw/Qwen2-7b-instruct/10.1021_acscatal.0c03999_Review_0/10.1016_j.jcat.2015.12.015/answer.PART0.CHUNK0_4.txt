Answer: The main challenges in current propylene production methods, such as steam cracking, Fluid Catalytic Cracking (FCC), and dehydrogenation (DH), include being endothermic processes that produce coke as a byproduct. Oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) addresses these issues by offering lower reaction temperatures and more effective coke inhibition. However, ODHP faces the limitation of low propylene selectivity due to the risk of complete oxidation of propane and propylene at high conversion levels. The addition of tungsten oxide (WOx) enhances the performance of vanadium oxide-based catalysts in ODHP, improving selectivity towards propylene and reducing carbon formation. Carbon dioxide (CO2) can enhance the dehydrogenation of alkanes and ethylbenzene, but its role in oxidative dehydrogenation and the reaction mechanism are still unclear and depend on the nature of the catalyst and supporting material. CO2 acts as a mild oxidant, capable of re-oxidizing catalysts to V2O4 instead of V2O5, which helps to suppress the formation of surface carbon. The oxidative dehydrogenation of propane in the presence of CO2 is limited by the activation of the secondary CAH bond, while re-oxidation of the catalyst with CO2 occurs on a faster timescale.
