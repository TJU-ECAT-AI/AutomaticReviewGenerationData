<p>Thrombin, one of the key pathway proteins in the coagulation cascade, is a serine protease that initiates the physiological formation of fibrin clots and platelet activation but has also been implicated in tumour growth, apoptosis, metastasis, pathological tissue remodelling and severe inflammation.</p>
<p>It is generated by the enzymatic cleavage of two sites on prothrombin by activated factor X (FXa).</p>
<p>Free thrombin can covert inactive forms of coagulation factors XI, VIII and V to their active forms and fibrinogen to fibrin.</p>
<p>In addition, thrombin can promote platelet activation and aggregation by activation of PARs on their surface membrane.</p>
<p>Importantly, after binding to the cell membrane protein thrombomodulin, thrombin can activate protein C.</p>
<p>Activating protein C (APC) is an enzyme that inactivates factors Va and VIIIa and signals through PAR-1 after association with endothelial protein C receptor (EPCR).</p>
<p>Finally, thrombin can cleave members of the complement system, C3 and C5, though the precise biological relevance of these functions is not known.</p>
</quotes>

The document provides extensive details on the roles of thrombin in various physiological and pathological processes. Thrombin, as a serine protease, plays crucial roles in coagulation, platelet activation, and inflammation. It is generated by the enzymatic cleavage of prothrombin by activated factor X (FXa). Thrombin can convert inactive coagulation factors to their active forms, including factors XI, VIII, and V, and fibrinogen to fibrin. Additionally, it activates PARs on platelet surfaces, promoting platelet activation and aggregation. After binding to thrombomodulin, thrombin can activate protein C, which in turn inactivates factors Va and VIIIa through signaling through PAR-1 after binding to the endothelial protein C receptor (EPCR). Thrombin also cleaves components of the complement system, C3 and C5, though their specific biological functions are not fully understood.
