        The significance of selective dehydrogenation reactions in chemical production, fuel synthesis, and hydrogen storage is underscored by the pivotal role they play in transforming hydrocarbons and other hydrophilic compounds into valuable fuels and chemicals. These reactions facilitate the conversion of substances like hydrazine into hydrogen at room temperature, contributing substantially to the energy sector. Reactor studies and ultra-high vacuum (UHV) techniques are indispensable in advancing our understanding of these processes, particularly concerning the dehydrogenation of C-H and N-H bonds over supported catalysts. Key research methods encompass the examination of reaction kinetics, product yields, and selectivities under diverse operational conditions, including temperature, pressure, and feedstock composition. These experiments offer insights into the efficacy of different catalysts and their ability to optimize desired reactions while mitigating unwanted side reactions. UHV studies provide a microscopic perspective by characterizing surface properties such as the coordination environment around metal centers, bond strengths, and adsorbate coverage. Techniques like X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectroscopy are crucial in revealing the electronic states and local structures of catalysts, providing critical information on active sites and their dynamics during dehydrogenation processes.

        Optimization of catalytic processes hinges on a profound understanding of dehydrogenation reaction mechanisms. By evaluating the effectiveness of various catalysts and conditions, researchers can tailor processes for specific applications. For instance, the study of Rh/Pt catalysts on a zirconia carrier for the steam reforming of ethanol/gasoline blends at an energy-efficient condition (with a steam-to-carbon ratio slightly higher than stoichiometric and temperatures near 650°C) elucidates catalyst deactivation mechanisms and the development of regeneration and preemptive air treatment methods to maintain catalyst stability. Aging tests with sulfur-free E85 under varying space velocities and catalyst loadings reveal that under high space velocity conditions, the catalyst is unstable regardless of sulfur presence. The addition of 5 ppm sulfur leads to significant deactivation after 22 hours, making hydrogen production industrially unfeasible. Despite deactivation, air treatment can regenerate the catalyst to initial activity, albeit with deactivation occurring more quickly post-regeneration. A preemptive regeneration process extends the stable activity period for sulfur-containing fuels.

        XPS studies indicate a shift in precious metal oxidation state during continuous operation versus periodic air treatments. This highlights the importance of understanding the precise role of metals like rhenium in catalysts, especially in bimetallic systems such as PtSn and PtRe. The addition of tin notably modifies selectivity by inhibiting cracking reactions, while the presence of tin changes the electronic environment of platinum atoms. The EXAFS profiles of PtSnA0.4 and PtSnA1.6 samples exhibit some differences, and the coexistence of an unalloyed metallic Pt phase can be confirmed considering the quantity of Pt and Sn atoms in the PtSnA1.6 and PtSnS0.7 catalysts. For all studied catalysts, there is a diminution in the reaction rate as the Sn(0)/Pt ratio increases. Bimetallic interaction in PtRe/Al2O3 catalysts is highly dependent on pretreatment conditions, with the highest degree of interaction found when the catalyst is not dehydrated prior to reduction.

        In summary, the comprehensive evaluation of selective dehydrogenation reactions, catalytic processes, and reactor studies underscores the necessity for a nuanced understanding of catalyst behavior under various conditions. This knowledge not only enhances the efficiency and selectivity of catalytic processes but also informs the design and improvement of catalysts for applications in chemical production, fuel synthesis, and hydrogen storage.

    <Chinese>
        选择性脱氢反应在化学生产、燃料合成和氢存储中扮演着至关重要的角色，它们能够将烃类和其他亲水化合物转化为有价值的燃料和化学品，对能源领域贡献巨大。反应器研究和超真空(UHV)技术在理解和掌握这些过程方面至关重要，特别是在支持催化剂上进行的C-H和N-H键的脱氢。关键的研究方法包括考察在不同操作条件下的反应动力学、产物产率和选择性，如温度、压力和原料组成。这些实验提供了不同催化剂及其优化所需反应的效率以及最小化副反应的洞察力。UHV研究提供了微观视角，通过表征金属中心的配位环境、键强度和吸附剂覆盖等表面属性来了解催化剂。X射线光电子能谱(XPS)和扩展X射线吸收精细结构(EXAFS)光谱技术对于揭示催化剂上的电子状态和局部结构至关重要，为活性位点及其在脱氢过程中动态行为提供了关键信息。

        优化催化过程依赖于对脱氢反应机制的深刻理解。通过评估各种催化剂和条件的有效性，研究人员可以为特定应用定制过程。例如，对于在能量高效条件下（蒸汽与碳原子比略高于化学计量比，温度接近650°C）进行乙醇/汽油混合物蒸汽重整的Rh/Pt催化剂在载体上的研究，揭示了催化剂失活机制以及再生和预先空气处理方法以维持催化剂稳定性的方法。老化测试使用无硫E85在不同的空间速度和催化剂负载下进行显示，在高空间速度条件下，催化剂无论是否有硫均不稳定。添加5 ppm硫导致22小时后测量到失活，使得工业级氢生产变得不可行。尽管存在失活，空气处理可以将催化剂恢复至初始活性，但失活后恢复得更快。预先再生过程能够延长含硫燃料稳定活动期。

        XPS研究表明，在连续运行与周期性空气处理之间，贵金属氧化态发生变化。这突出了理解如铼在催化剂中的确切作用的重要性，尤其是在PtSn和PtRe双金属系统中。添加锡显著修改了选择性，抑制了裂解反应，而锡的存在改变了铂原子的电子环境。PtSnA0.4和PtSnA1.6样品的EXAFS轮廓存在差异，并且可以通过考虑Pt和Sn原子的数量来证实PtSnA1.6和PtSnS0.7催化剂中存在未合金化的纯铂相。对于所有研究的催化剂，随着Sn(0)/Pt比例的增加，反应速率下降。PtRe/Al2O3催化剂中的双金属相互作用高度依赖于预处理条件，最高程度的相互作用出现在催化剂在减少前未脱水时。

        总之，对选择性脱氢反应、催化过程和反应器研究的全面评估强调了理解催化剂在各种条件下的行为的必要性。这种知识不仅提高了催化过程的效率和选择性，而且还指导了设计和改进用于化学生产、燃料合成和氢存储应用的催化剂。
