The significance of selective dehydrogenation reactions in chemical production, fuel synthesis, and hydrogen storage is highlighted, underscoring the pivotal role they play in converting hydrocarbons and hydrophilic compounds into valuable fuels and chemicals, thus contributing significantly to the energy sector. Reactor studies and ultra-high vacuum (UHV) techniques significantly enhance our understanding of these processes by elucidating the mechanisms of dehydrogenation over supported catalysts, focusing on C-H and N-H bond dehydrogenation. These studies often involve examining reaction kinetics, product yields, and selectivities under various operational conditions such as temperature, pressure, and feedstock composition. Characterization techniques like temperature-programmed reduction (TPR) and adsorption microcalorimetry are employed to analyze catalyst behavior, while careful control of reaction conditions optimizes catalytic processes, providing insights into enhancing catalyst performance and selectivity. UHV studies offer a microscopic perspective by characterizing surface properties, such as coordination environments, bond strengths, and adsorbate coverage, which are instrumental in revealing the electronic states and local structures of catalysts, thereby providing critical information on active sites and their dynamics during the dehydrogenation process. Optimizing catalytic processes relies on understanding the mechanisms of dehydrogenation reactions, and by evaluating the effectiveness of different catalysts and conditions, researchers can tailor processes for specific applications. The studies presented here exemplify how selective dehydrogenation reactions facilitate efficient chemical production, fuel synthesis, and hydrogen storage, underscoring the importance of these processes in modern energy solutions.
