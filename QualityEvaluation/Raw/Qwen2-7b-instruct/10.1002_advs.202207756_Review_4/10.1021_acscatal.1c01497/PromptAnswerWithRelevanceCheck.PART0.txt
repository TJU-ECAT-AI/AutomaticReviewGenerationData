The primary focus is on how these Ga species influence the catalytic performance for propane dehydrogenation (PDH).

The alkyl mechanism in PDH reactions on gallium-based catalysts typically starts with the activation of propane molecules through a series of steps. Initially, propane molecules adsorb onto the catalyst surface. Subsequently, the adsorbed propane undergoes a carbocation intermediate formation, followed by a ring-opening reaction to produce olefinic products. The specific pathway and efficiency of this process are influenced by the presence and arrangement of Ga species within the catalyst.

The carbenium mechanism, often associated with other types of catalysts, differs in its approach to activation. In the context of Ga/H-ZSM-5 catalysts, the alkyl mechanism is favored due to the unique electronic properties and surface chemistry of gallium, which enable efficient activation of propane molecules. The CH activation step is proposed as the rate-determining step (RDS) because it involves the most energetically demanding transformation, controlling the overall reaction kinetics.

The alkyl mechanism applies to different Ga species structures by leveraging the specific coordination environment around Ga atoms. For instance, Ga species that form more stable and active coordination complexes can enhance the catalytic activity for PDH. The precise nature of these Ga species, including their oxidation state, coordination number, and local environment within the ZSM-5 framework, significantly impacts the efficiency of the alkyl mechanism.

In summary, the correlation between Ga speciation and PDH activity on Ga/H-ZSM-5 catalysts is rooted in the ability of gallium species to facilitate the efficient activation of propane molecules through the alkyl mechanism. This mechanism, characterized by a CH activation step as the RDS, exploits the unique electronic properties and surface chemistry of gallium to drive the catalytic conversion of propane into olefinic products.
