The specific catalyst composition affects the type of active sites available for alkane reactions, which in turn influences the reaction pathways and products formed. For example, in the context of the document, gallyl ions (GaO+) in Ga/ZSM-5 zeolites serve as active sites for ethane dehydrogenation. The structure of these catalysts determines the accessibility of alkane molecules to the active sites and the stability of the intermediates formed during the reactions. Reaction conditions, such as temperature and pressure, can alter the kinetic and thermodynamic favorability of different reaction pathways.

The efficiency of alkane transformations is also influenced by the stability of the intermediates and the ease of regeneration of the active sites. In the case of gallyl ions, the formation of stable intermediates via the primary dehydrogenation routes prevents the catalytic cycles from closing. However, secondary reactions, such as concerted dehydrogenation, allow for multiple catalytic cycles to occur before the active site is reduced. The overall activation energy for ethane dehydrogenation over GaO+ ions is estimated to be below 150 kJ mol-1, facilitated by these secondary reactions. Despite this, the gallyl ions reduce rapidly to produce univalent gallium ions, limiting the formation of ethylene molecules per active site to no more than three.

The industrial significance of these catalysts lies in their ability to efficiently transform alkane molecules into more valuable products, such as alkenes, through processes like oxidative dehydrogenation.
