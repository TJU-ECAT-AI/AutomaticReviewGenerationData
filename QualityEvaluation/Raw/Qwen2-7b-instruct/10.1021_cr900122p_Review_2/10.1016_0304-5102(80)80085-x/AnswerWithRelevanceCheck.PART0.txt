          Metal-based catalysts, including supported vanadium oxides and metal-exchanged zeolites, significantly influence the mechanisms and efficiency of alkane transformations, especially in processes like oxidative dehydrogenation and zeolite-catalyzed reactions. The composition, structure, and reaction conditions of these catalysts play a crucial role in determining the efficiency of alkane conversions, with factors such as selectivity towards desired products, stability under reaction conditions, and resistance to harsh operating environments contributing to their industrial significance. Supported vanadium oxides are highly active in oxidative dehydrogenation, while metal-exchanged zeolites enable controlled kinetics in zeolite-catalyzed reactions. The efficiency of these catalysts is further influenced by the reaction conditions, which can affect the rates of isomerization and hydrogenation. Steric hindrance controls the rates of isomerization, with different rates observed for various olefins, and higher temperatures can enhance isomerization rates. The catalysts' ability to efficiently convert alkanes into valuable products with high yields and selectivities is crucial for their industrial application, making them attractive for producing chemicals, fuels, and polymers.
