The Horiuti-Polanyi mechanism describes the catalytic dehydrogenation process, which typically involves four main steps:

1. **Hydrogen adsorption**: Hydrogen molecules (H2) adsorb onto the catalyst surface.

2. **Hydrogen activation**: Adsorbed hydrogen atoms become activated, often through the assistance of the catalyst, allowing them to participate in the following reactions.

3. **Bond cleavage**: The activated hydrogen atoms break the C-H bonds in a hydrocarbon molecule, releasing hydrogen atoms and forming alkene or alkyne species.

4. **Product release**: The newly formed alkene or alkyne products are released from the catalyst surface.

The ongoing debate regarding which step is considered rate-limiting is largely due to the complexity of the mechanism and the interplay between the steps. The rate of dehydrogenation depends on factors such as the strength of hydrogen adsorption, the efficiency of hydrogen activation, the ease of bond cleavage, and the kinetics of product release.

- **Environmental considerations**: Efficient dehydrogenation processes can reduce environmental impacts by minimizing energy consumption and waste generation.
