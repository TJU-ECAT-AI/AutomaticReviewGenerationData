It comprises four key steps: dissociative adsorption of the alkane, followed by dehydrogenation, hydrogenation of the resulting alkene, and finally, desorption of the product. While there is general agreement on the overall mechanism, the specifics of the rate-limiting step are a subject of debate. The precise degree of dehydrogenation of C2Hx surface species, crucial for cleaving the CC bond, varies across different surfaces and lacks universal consensus. Moreover, the number of hydrogen atoms in the adsorbed C2Hx molecule that participate in CC bond cleavage can differ from one surface to another. Cortright et al.'s study (31) using a combination of reaction kinetics, calorimetry, spectroscopy, and quantum chemical methods revealed that the primary reaction pathways for CC bond cleavage occur through less stable ethyl (C2H5) and ethylidene (CHCH3) species, not typically observed by spectroscopic techniques. More prevalent surface species, such as ethylidyne and di-σ-bonded ethene, do not directly participate in the primary reaction pathways but impact the observed kinetic rates by blocking active sites.
