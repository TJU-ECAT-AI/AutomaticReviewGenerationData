The dehydrogenation reaction on chromia/alumina and chromia/zirconia are catalyzed by the Cr3+ species available to the alkane molecules [1,3,6,7,9].

On alumina the high oxidation state species reduce in the presence of alkanes, hydrogen, and carbon monoxide to produce Cr3+ and possibly some Cr2+ [1,6,7]. 

According to De Rossi et al. [9] on zirconia the Cr6+ and Cr5+ species reduce under carbon monoxide to Cr2+ and Cr3+ species, respectively.

The catalytically active species in the dehydrogenation reaction on chromia/alumina and chromia/zirconia are the Cr3+ species available to the alkane molecules [1,3,6,7,9].

The Horiuti-Polanyi mechanism for catalytic dehydrogenation reactions involves four main steps: 
1. Adsorption of the reactant molecule onto the catalyst surface.
2. Formation of a reactive intermediate species on the surface.
3. Activation of the molecule by the catalyst, often involving breaking of a bond.
4. Desorption of the product molecule from the catalyst surface.

The ongoing debate regarding which step is considered rate-limiting is due to the complexity of the reaction mechanisms and the interplay between the steps. Some researchers argue that the rate-limiting step is the adsorption/desorption process, while others suggest that the activation step is rate-limiting. The specific conditions, such as temperature, pressure, and reactant concentration, can influence which step dominates. Despite nearly a century of study, the exact sequence and relative rates of these steps remain subjects of active research and debate in the field of catalysis.
