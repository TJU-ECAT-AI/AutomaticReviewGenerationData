        A comprehensive overview of the physical vapor deposition (PVD) technique, particularly thermal evaporation, has been provided in this document, which focuses on creating bimetallic surfaces in ultra-high vacuum (UHV) environments. This method is compatible with the stringent conditions required for surface science studies. Thermal evaporation involves depositing a metal overlayer on the target substrate through line-of-sight deposition. Common PVD methods include magnetron sputtering, pulsed laser deposition, e-beam sputtering, and physical evaporation of the overlayer metal via thermal filament.

        The metal source typically consists of a thin tungsten or tantalum wire, wrapped with a smaller wire of the metal of interest. The evaporation filament is housed in a stainless steel cylinder with a small opening to direct the metal deposition onto the substrate and prevent evaporation onto other components in the UHV system. To ensure high purity, the UHV system pressure during evaporation should be kept at 5 × 10^-10 Torr or lower.

        AES and XPS are often used to quantify the elemental compositions of bimetallic surfaces. In LEIS experiments, the surface is bombarded with positive ions of an inert element at relatively low energy to detect the topmost layer's composition. LEED is a useful technique to determine the overlayer structure in bimetallic surfaces, and STM assesses the structure and morphology of the metal overlayer.

        For instance, when 3d metals like Fe, Cu, Ni, and Co are deposited onto a Pt(111) single-crystal disk, a monolayer of the metal is formed on the surface at a rate of 0.2 to 0.3 ML/min. AES measurements are crucial in monitoring the deposition process and determining the composition of the deposited layers. The AES quantifies the physical vapor deposition and thermal behavior of both Fe and Cu on Pt(111), revealing distinct differences in their deposition characteristics.

        After deposition, the surfaces are heated to high temperatures, and AES scans are performed once the surfaces are cooled to below 500 K, to determine changes in the surface composition. The AES ratios for Fe-Pt-Pt(111) and Cu-Pt-Pt(111) surfaces are calculated to be 1.7 and 1.8, respectively, consistent with experimental findings.

        Maintaining surface cleanliness is paramount in all surface experiments, particularly in UHV chambers, to avoid misleading results. Precise data can only be obtained from initially well-defined surfaces. The use of techniques such as heating the sample and ion bombardment is essential for cleaning the surface within the UHV chamber.

        The physical vapor deposition (PVD) technique, particularly thermal evaporation, is a powerful tool for creating bimetallic surfaces in UHV environments, providing valuable insights into surface science studies. By ensuring high purity and maintaining surface cleanliness, researchers can achieve optimal results and advance our understanding of atomic and molecular interactions with solid surfaces.

        对物理气相沉积（PVD）技术，特别是热蒸发，在超高压真空（UHV）环境中创建双金属表面的全面概述已经在本文件中提供。这种方法与表面科学研究所需的严格条件兼容。热蒸发涉及通过视线沉积在目标基板上沉积金属覆盖层。常见的PVD方法包括磁控溅射、脉冲激光沉积、电子束溅射以及通过热丝蒸发过层金属。

        金属源通常由厚度为0.2至0.5毫米的钨或钽线组成，围绕着直径为0.05至0.1毫米的所需金属线。蒸发丝通常位于不锈钢圆筒内，前面上有一个小开口，用于将金属沉积到基板上，并防止蒸发到UHV系统中的其他部件。为了确保高纯度，蒸发过程中的UHV系统压力应保持在5×10^-10托或更低。

        AES和XPS常被用来量化双金属表面的元素组成。在LEIS实验中，表面受到惰性元素正离子的低能轰击，以检测最顶层的组成。LEED是一种有用的工具，用于确定双金属表面的覆盖层结构，而STM评估金属覆盖层的结构和形态。

        例如，当3d金属如Fe、Cu、Ni和Co沉积到Pt(111)单晶盘上时，会在表面上形成一层单分子层，沉积速率约为每分钟0.2至0.3 ML。AES测量对于监控沉积过程并确定沉积层的组成至关重要。AES量化了Fe和Cu在Pt(111)上的物理气相沉积和热行为，揭示了它们在沉积特性方面的显著差异。

        在沉积后，表面被加热到高温，并在冷却到低于500 K后进行AES扫描，以确定表面成分的变化。Fe-Pt-Pt(111)和Cu-Pt-Pt(111)表面的AES比率分别计算为1.7和1.8，与实验结果一致。

        维持表面清洁是所有表面实验的共同难点，特别是在UHV室中，以避免误导性的结果。只有从最初定义良好的表面上才能获得精确的数据。采用诸如加热样品和离子轰击等技术对表面进行清洁，对于UHV室内的表面清洁至关重要。

        物理气相沉积（PVD）技术，特别是热蒸发，是创建UHV环境中双金属表面的强大工具，提供了对表面科学研究有价值的见解。通过确保高纯度和维持表面清洁，研究人员可以实现最佳结果，并深化我们对原子和分子与固体表面相互作用的理解。
