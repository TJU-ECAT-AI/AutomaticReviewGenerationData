The physical vapor deposition (PVD) technique, particularly thermal evaporation, is the preferred method for creating bimetallic surfaces in ultra-high vacuum (UHV) environments, compatible with the stringent conditions required for surface science studies. This method involves depositing a metal overlayer on the target substrate using line-of-sight deposition. Common PVD techniques include magnetron sputtering, pulsed laser deposition, e-beam sputtering, and physical evaporation of the overlayer metal via thermal filament. The metal source typically consists of a thin tungsten or tantalum wire wrapped with a smaller wire of the metal of interest. The evaporation filament is housed in a stainless steel cylinder with a small opening to direct the metal deposition onto the substrate and prevent evaporation onto other components in the UHV system. To ensure high purity, the UHV system pressure during evaporation should be kept at 5 × 10^-10 Torr or lower.

        The process parameters for thermal evaporation include the rate of deposition, which is typically 0.2 to 0.3 monolayers (ML) per minute to form a monolayer of the 3d metal on the surface. AES and XPS are often used to quantify the elemental compositions of bimetallic surfaces. In LEIS experiments, the surface is bombarded with positive ions of an inert element at relatively low energy to detect the topmost layer's composition. LEED helps determine the overlayer structure, and STM assesses the structure and morphology of the metal overlayer in bimetallic surfaces. The AES quantifies the physical vapor deposition and thermal behavior of both Fe and Cu on Pt(111), revealing distinct differences in their deposition characteristics.

        Critical to the success of this method is the maintenance of surface cleanliness, which can significantly affect the outcome of surface science studies. Experiments conducted on clean, perfect surfaces can produce a large amount of structural information of high precision. Precise data can only be obtained from surfaces that are initially well-defined.

        In summary, the PVD technique, especially thermal evaporation, provides a versatile and reliable method for preparing high-purity bimetallic surfaces in UHV environments, facilitating a wide range of surface science studies and ensuring optimal results through careful control of experimental parameters and surface cleanliness.

        从参考文献[10.1021/cr300096b_1]中建立详细的分析。整合理解源自参考文献[10.1016/j.jcat.2008.05.013]和[10.1007/978-1-4684-8777-0_7]。物理蒸镀（PVD）技术，特别是热蒸发法，是创建超真空（UHV）环境下的双金属表面的首选方法，适用于表面科学研究所需的严格条件。该方法通过视线沉积在目标基板上沉积金属层。常见的PVD技术包括磁控溅射、脉冲激光沉积、电子束溅射以及通过热丝蒸发过层金属。金属源通常由较薄的钨或钽线缠绕而成，其中包含较小的金属线。蒸发丝位于不锈钢圆筒中，并在前端开孔以引导金属沉积至基板并防止蒸发至UHV系统的其他组件。为了确保高纯度，蒸发过程中UHV系统的压力应保持在10^-10托或更低。

        热蒸发过程参数包括沉积速率，通常为每分钟0.2到0.3个单层（ML），以形成基板上的单层3d金属。原子发射光谱（AES）和X射线光电子能谱（XPS）常用于定量双金属表面的元素组成。在低能离子散射（LEIS）实验中，表面被惰性元素的正离子轰击，能量较低以检测最顶层的组成。低能电子衍射（LEED）有助于确定覆盖层结构，而扫描隧道显微镜（STM）评估金属覆盖层的结构和形态。AES量化了Fe和Cu在Pt(111)表面上的物理蒸镀和热行为，揭示了它们在沉积特性上的显著差异。

        维持表面清洁是成功的关键，这可以显著影响表面科学研究的结果。在干净、完美的表面上进行的实验可以产生大量高精度的结构信息。只有从一开始就定义良好的表面才能获得精确的数据。

        总结，PVD技术，尤其是热蒸发法，提供了一种灵活可靠的方法来准备UHV环境下的高纯度双金属表面，通过仔细控制实验参数和表面清洁度，支持广泛的表面科学研究并确保最佳结果。
