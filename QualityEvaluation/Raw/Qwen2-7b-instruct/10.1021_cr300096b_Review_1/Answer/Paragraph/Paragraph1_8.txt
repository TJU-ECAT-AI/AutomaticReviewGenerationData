The physical vapor deposition (PVD) technique, particularly thermal evaporation, is the preferred method for creating bimetallic surfaces in ultra-high vacuum (UHV) environments. This approach is compatible with the stringent conditions required for surface science studies. It involves depositing a metal overlayer on the target substrate through line-of-sight deposition, utilizing common techniques such as magnetron sputtering, pulsed laser deposition, e-beam sputtering, and physical evaporation via a tungsten or tantalum filament. The metal source typically comprises a thin tungsten or tantalum wire wrapped with a smaller wire of the desired metal. The evaporation filament is housed in a stainless steel cylinder with a small opening to direct the metal deposition onto the substrate and prevent evaporation onto other components in the UHV system. To ensure high purity, the UHV system pressure during evaporation should be kept at 5 × 10^-10 Torr or lower. Procedures for preparing bimetallic surface structures under UHV conditions have been detailed previously. Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES) and X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) are often used to quantify the elemental compositions of bimetallic surfaces. In Low-Energy Ion Scattering (LEIS) experiments, the surface is bombarded with positive ions of an inert element at relatively low energy to detect the topmost layer's composition. Low-Energy Electron Diffraction (LEED) helps determine the overlayer structure, and Scanning Tunneling Microscopy (STM) assesses the structure and morphology of the metal overlayer in bimetallic surfaces.

        The major difficulty in all surface experiments is the need to maintain the surface clean, as experiments on clean, perfect surfaces can produce a large amount of structural information of high precision. Obviously, precise data can only be obtained from surfaces that are well-defined in the first place. Techniques such as heating the sample and ion bombardment are used to clean the surface within an ultrahigh vacuum (UHV) chamber. The presence of impurities can lead to misleading results, particularly in low-energy electron diffraction (LEED) experiments, which are highly sensitive to surface cleanliness.
