        A comprehensive overview of the physical vapor deposition (PVD) technique, particularly thermal evaporation, for fabricating bimetallic surfaces in ultra-high vacuum (UHV) environments is presented, with a focus on its application in surface science studies. This method is compatible with the stringent conditions required for surface science research, enabling the deposition of a metal overlayer on a target substrate through line-of-sight deposition. Common PVD techniques encompass magnetron sputtering, pulsed laser deposition, e-beam sputtering, and thermal evaporation, utilizing a tungsten or tantalum filament as the metal source. The filament is typically housed in a stainless steel cylinder with a small opening to direct metal deposition onto the substrate while preventing evaporation onto other chamber components. To ensure high purity, the UHV system pressure during evaporation should be maintained at 5 × 10^-10 Torr or lower. AES and XPS are utilized to quantify the elemental compositions of bimetallic surfaces, while LEIS and LEED provide insights into surface composition and structure, respectively. STM assesses the structure and morphology of the metal overlayer.

        In the context of propane dehydrogenation catalysts, PVD techniques, especially thermal evaporation, play a pivotal role in preparing high-purity bimetallic surfaces under UHV conditions. This process involves depositing a 3d metal monolayer (ML) at a rate of 0.2 to 0.3 ML/min on a Pt(111) single-crystal disk, forming a 1 ML layer of the 3d metal. The deposition is carried out in a UHV chamber equipped with AES and MS for surface characterization and TPD experiments. The quantity of metal deposited is determined by the AES peak-to-peak ratio, with an accelerating voltage of 3 kV used in all AES measurements. AES quantifies the physical vapor deposition and thermal behavior of metals like Fe and Cu on Pt(111), revealing distinct differences in their deposition characteristics. Post-deposition, surfaces are heated to high temperatures, and AES scans are performed to assess changes in surface composition. The AES ratios for Fe-Pt-Pt(111) and Cu-Pt-Pt(111) surfaces are calculated to be 1.7 and 1.8, respectively, aligning with experimental outcomes.

        The major challenge in all surface experiments is maintaining surface cleanliness. Clean surfaces yield precise structural information, and precise data can only be obtained from initially well-defined surfaces. Surface cleaning techniques, including heating the sample and ion bombardment within an ultrahigh vacuum chamber, are crucial to eliminate impurities that could lead to misleading results, particularly in sensitive experiments like LEED.

        Future research endeavors might focus on exploring emerging fields and innovative methodologies to enhance the efficiency and selectivity of propane dehydrogenation catalysts. Potential directions could involve the development of new PVD techniques, the design of novel catalyst architectures, and the integration of advanced surface science tools to probe the reaction mechanisms in greater detail.

        物理气相沉积（PVD）技术，特别是热蒸发法，在超真空（UHV）环境中制备双金属表面方面具有广泛的应用，并在表面科学研究中发挥着核心作用。该方法适用于表面科学研究所需的严格条件，通过视线沉积方式在目标基底上沉积一层金属膜层。常见的PVD技术包括磁控溅射、脉冲激光沉积、电子束溅射以及通过热丝蒸发法使用钨或钽丝作为金属源。蒸发丝通常被安置在一个不锈钢筒中，前端有一个小开口以引导金属沉积至基板并防止向其他系统组件蒸发。为了确保高纯度，蒸发过程中的UHV系统压力应维持在5×10^-10托或更低。AES和XPS用于量化双金属表面的元素组成，而LEIS和LEED则有助于确定表面组成和结构。STM则评估金属覆层的结构和形态。

        在丙烷脱氢催化剂领域，PVD技术，尤其是热蒸发法，在准备UHV条件下具有高纯度的双金属表面方面发挥着关键作用。这一过程涉及在Pt(111)单晶盘上以每分钟0.2到0.3 ML的速度沉积一层3d金属单层，形成一层1 ML的3d金属层。沉积是在配备有AES和MS进行表面表征和TPD实验的UHV腔中进行的。金属沉积量由AES峰值对峰值比确定，所有AES测量均采用3 kV加速电压。AES量化了Fe和Cu在Pt(111)上的物理气相沉积和热行为，揭示了它们在沉积特性上的显著差异。沉积后，表面被加热至高温，然后进行AES扫描以评估表面组成的变化。Fe-Pt-Pt(111)和Cu-Pt-Pt(111)表面的AES比率分别计算为1.7和1.8，与实验结果一致。

        所有表面实验的主要挑战是保持表面清洁。清洁的表面可以产生精确的结构信息，只有从初始定义良好的表面上才能获得精确的数据。通过在超真空腔内加热样品和离子轰击等表面清洁技术是至关重要的，以消除可能导致误导性结果的杂质，尤其是在对表面清洁高度敏感的实验如LEED中。
