Physical vapor deposition (PVD) techniques, particularly thermal evaporation, are pivotal in creating bimetallic surfaces in ultra-high vacuum (UHV) environments, aligning with the stringent conditions requisite for surface science studies. This methodology entails the deposition of a metal overlayer on the target substrate through line-of-sight deposition. Common PVD techniques encompass magnetron sputtering, pulsed laser deposition, e-beam sputtering, and physical evaporation of the overlayer metal via a tungsten or tantalum filament. The metal source typically comprises a thin tungsten or tantalum wire wrapped with a smaller wire of the desired metal. The evaporation filament is typically contained within a stainless steel cylinder, featuring a small opening at the front to direct metal deposition onto the substrate and to prevent the evaporation of metals onto other components in the UHV system. To ensure high purity, the UHV system pressure during evaporation should be maintained at 5 × 10^10 Torr or lower. Procedures for preparing bimetallic surface structures under UHV conditions have been delineated previously. Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES) and X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) are frequently utilized to quantify the elemental compositions of bimetallic surfaces. In Low-Energy Ion Scattering (LEIS) experiments, the surface is bombarded with positive ions of an inert element at relatively low energy to detect the topmost layer's composition. Low-Energy Electron Diffraction (LEED) aids in determining the overlayer structure, and Scanning Tunneling Microscopy (STM) evaluates the structure and morphology of the metal overlayer in bimetallic surfaces. Maintaining surface cleanliness is paramount in all surface experiments, as evidenced by the necessity of cleaning techniques like heating the sample and ion bombardment within an ultrahigh vacuum chamber to ensure precise data acquisition, especially in Low-Energy Electron Diffraction (LEED) experiments, which are highly sensitive to surface cleanliness. Precise data can only be obtained from surfaces that are initially well-defined.
