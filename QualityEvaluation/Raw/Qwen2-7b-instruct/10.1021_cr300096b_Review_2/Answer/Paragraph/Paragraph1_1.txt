        The characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces involves depositing a Ni monolayer on Pt(111) single crystal or polycrystalline Pt substrates. This process is crucial for structural modeling, DFT calculations, and understanding the electronic and chemical properties of these bimetallic surfaces. It utilizes synchrotron radiation, specifically the SA73b beamline with a TGM7 monochromator, for experiments conducted in a stainless-steel ultra-high vacuum (UHV) chamber equipped with a hemispherical analyzer for photoelectron spectroscopy (PES), a cylindrical mirror analyzer (CMA) for Auger electron spectroscopy (AES), and LEED apparatus. Clean Cu(100) single-crystal surfaces are obtained through cycles of argon-ion sputtering (600 eV, 8 μA cm−) followed by annealing at 850 K. The Pt 4f core-level photoemission line, with a bulk binding energy of 7 1.15 eV, is studied using 150 eV photons polarized in the p direction. The growth process up to 2 ML reveals the formation of an extended Pt-Cu surface alloy, characterized by a bulk-like peak (B*) at 71.0 ± 0.03 eV and a surface-like peak (S*) at 70.6 ± 0.03 eV. The relative variation of the intensity is +10%. Annealing affects the alloy structure, causing the intensities of B and S to decrease and the peaks to shift by 150 meV towards lower binding energy to the B* and S* positions, respectively. After annealing, the LEED patterns become sharp c(2 × 2) with low background intensities.

        The effects of annealing are twofold: first, the intensities of B and S decrease, indicating that Pt diffuses into the Cu substrate, and second, the B and S peaks shift by 150 meV towards lower binding energy to the B* and S* positions, respectively. This process also facilitates stress relief via island formation of an isotropically strained bimetallic surface layer, leading to the mesoscopic morphology of the Ag/Pt (111) surface alloy.

        The difference in microscopic work function between Ag and Pt clusters in the Ag/Pt surface alloy is much smaller than that between Ag large islands and Pt terraces on a Ag-deposited Pt(111) surface, despite their similar Ag coverages and macroscopic work functions. This feature, occurring when the dimensions of the clusters are a few nanometers, is due to the long-range effect of work function reduction observed for Cs-adsorption surfaces.

        Atomic scale investigations of the Co/Pt(111) interface structure and magnetic properties, as reported in reference \[10.1063/1.1388156\], provide further insights into the intricate interactions at the metal-metal interface. For instance, the morphology and stability of Pd films on Pt(1 1 1) were examined using in-situ surface X-ray scattering (SXS) and FTIR measurements, revealing that electrochemically deposited films are pseudomorphic, occupying commensurate Pt sites to maintain the ABC stacking of the Pt(1 1 1) substrate.

This includes the use of various spectroscopic techniques like AES, LEED, LEIS, XPS, and STM to determine the surface composition and structure, as well as the influence of subsurface Ni atoms on the electronic structure of the surface Pt atoms, leading to weaker metal-adsorbate bonds.

        退火的影响是两方面的：首先，B和S的强度降低，表明Pt扩散进入Cu基体；其次，B和S的峰向较低结合能移动150 meV到B*和S*位置。此过程也促进了异向性拉伸双金属表面层的应力释放，导致Ag/Pt (111)表面合金的微观形态。
