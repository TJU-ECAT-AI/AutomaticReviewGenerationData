    <English>
        The in-depth characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces, synthesized by depositing a Ni monolayer on Pt(111) single crystal or polycrystalline Pt substrates, plays a pivotal role in advanced surface science and catalysis research. This process is indispensable for structural modeling and density functional theory (DFT) calculations, enabling a comprehensive understanding of the electronic and chemical properties of these bimetallic surfaces. The use of synchrotron radiation, specifically the SA73b beamline with a TGM7 monochromator, for experiments in a stainless-steel ultra-high vacuum (UHV) chamber equipped with various spectroscopic techniques such as photoelectron spectroscopy (PES), Auger electron spectroscopy (AES), and LEED, facilitates detailed analysis. Clean Cu(100) single-crystal surfaces are prepared through cycles of argon-ion sputtering followed by annealing at 850 K. The Pt 4f core-level photoemission line, with a bulk binding energy of 7 1.15 eV, is studied under 150 eV photons polarized in the p direction. Up to 2 ML of Pt deposition reveals the formation of an extended Pt-Cu surface alloy, characterized by a bulk-like peak at 71.0 ± 0.03 eV and a surface-like peak at 70.6 ± 0.03 eV. Annealing affects the alloy structure, causing the intensities of the peaks to decrease and shift by 150 meV towards lower binding energy. After annealing, the LEED patterns become sharp c(2 × 2) with low background intensities.

        The synthesis and characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces are essential for understanding the intricate interplay between surface structure, electronic properties, and catalytic activity. These surfaces offer unique opportunities for enhancing catalytic performance due to the synergistic effects of Ni and Pt at the interface. The detailed analysis provided by these techniques allows researchers to tailor the surface properties for optimized catalytic applications, contributing significantly to advancements in surface science and catalysis.

        Looking ahead, the exploration of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces opens avenues for the development of more efficient catalysts and materials. Future research could focus on the investigation of novel synthesis methods, the optimization of surface structures, and the integration of these surfaces into practical applications. By leveraging the insights gained from structural modeling, DFT calculations, and detailed surface characterization, scientists can push the boundaries of catalytic efficiency and surface science, paving the way for innovations in areas such as energy conversion, environmental remediation, and materials synthesis.

        In summary, the characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces is a cornerstone of advanced surface science and catalysis research, providing a foundation for the development of novel materials and catalytic processes. As the field continues to evolve, the detailed understanding of these surfaces will remain crucial for unlocking their full potential in technological applications.
