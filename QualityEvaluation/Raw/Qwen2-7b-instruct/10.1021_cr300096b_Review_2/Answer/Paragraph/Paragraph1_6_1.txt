        The characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces, synthesized by depositing a Ni monolayer on Pt(111) single crystal or polycrystalline Pt substrates, involves a detailed analysis as outlined in reference \[1\]. Further insights stem from studies \[2\] and \[3\], which highlight the importance of this process for structural modeling, density functional theory (DFT) calculations, and a comprehensive understanding of the electronic and chemical properties of these surfaces, crucial for advanced surface science and catalysis research. Synchrotron radiation, specifically the SA73b beamline with a TGM7 monochromator, is utilized for experiments conducted in a stainless-steel ultra-high vacuum (UHV) chamber equipped with various spectroscopic tools like PES, AES, and LEED. Clean Cu(100) single-crystal surfaces are obtained through cycles of argon-ion sputtering followed by annealing at 850 K. The Pt 4f core-level photoemission line, with a bulk binding energy of 7 1.15 eV, is studied under 150 eV photons polarized in the p direction. Up to 2 ML deposition reveals the formation of an extended Pt-Cu surface alloy, characterized by a bulk-like peak (B*) at 71.0 ± 0.03 eV and a surface-like peak (S*) at 70.6 ± 0.03 eV. Annealing affects the structure, leading to a decrease in peak intensities and shifts by 150 meV towards lower binding energy. After annealing, the LEED patterns become sharp c(2 x 2) with low background intensities.
