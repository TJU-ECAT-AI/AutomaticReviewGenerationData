        Detailed analysis established from the study of reference \[1\]. Synthesized comprehension stemming from references \[2\], \[3\], and \[4\]. Comprehensive characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces involves depositing a Ni monolayer on Pt(111) single crystal or polycrystalline Pt substrates. This process is crucial for structural modeling, DFT calculations, and understanding the electronic and chemical properties of these bimetallic surfaces. It employs synchrotron radiation, specifically the SA73b beamline with a TGM7 monochromator, for experiments conducted in a stainless-steel ultra-high vacuum (UHV) chamber equipped with a hemispherical analyzer for photoelectron spectroscopy (PES), a cylindrical mirror analyzer (CMA) for Auger electron spectroscopy (AES), and LEED apparatus. Clean Cu(100) single-crystal surfaces are obtained through cycles of argon-ion sputtering (600 eV, 8 μA cm−) followed by annealing at 850 K. The Pt 4f core-level photoemission line, with a bulk binding energy of 7 1.15 eV, is studied using 150 eV photons polarized in the p direction. The growth process up to 2 ML reveals the formation of an extended Pt-Cu surface alloy, characterized by a bulk-like peak (B*) at 71.0 ± 0.03 eV and a surface-like peak (S*) at 70.6 ± 0.03 eV. The relative variation of the intensity is +10%. Annealing affects the alloy structure, causing the intensities of B and S to decrease and the peaks to shift by 150 meV towards lower binding energy to the B* and S* positions, respectively. After annealing, the LEED patterns become sharp c(2 × 2) with low background intensities.

        Upcoming research directions could focus on exploring the synergistic effects of Ni and Pt at the atomic level, identifying optimal conditions for the formation of highly ordered surface alloys, and understanding the mechanisms behind the enhanced catalytic activity observed in these bimetallic surfaces. This could involve a combination of experimental techniques such as high-resolution microscopy, advanced spectroscopy, and computational simulations. Future work might also aim to tailor the electronic structure and surface chemistry of Ni/Pt surfaces to achieve specific catalytic functionalities, such as enhanced selectivity in hydrogenation reactions or improved stability under harsh conditions.
