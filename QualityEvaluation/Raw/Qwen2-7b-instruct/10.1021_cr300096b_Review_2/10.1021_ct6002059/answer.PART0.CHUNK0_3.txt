Answer: 
These studies on the oxygen reduction reaction (OER) mechanisms on different surfaces, including pure platinum (Pt), palladium (Pd), and bimetallic systems like Pd0.75Co0.25 and Pt0.75Co0.25, have utilized density functional theory (DFT) to analyze the adsorption of oxygen electroreduction intermediates and to calculate free energy profiles. This analysis helps to understand the possible pathways for oxygen reduction, including a direct pathway that leads to water production and a series pathway that involves hydrogen peroxide as an intermediate. The research also acknowledges that the inclusion of water in the overall reaction and its impact on adsorption energies and the overall reaction will be explored in future studies, where the findings will be compared with the current results. Additionally, the study indicates that the highest thermodynamic barriers for both mechanisms are found in the first hydrogenation step, suggesting that both the direct and series OER mechanisms might operate simultaneously. The hypothesis supports the idea that the O2 reduction mechanism to water could be occurring in parallel, highlighting the complex interplay between different reaction pathways.
