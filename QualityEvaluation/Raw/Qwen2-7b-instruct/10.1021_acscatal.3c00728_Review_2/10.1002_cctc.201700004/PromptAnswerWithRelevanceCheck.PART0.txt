The 2-D dispersed active sites in boron-based catalysts for the Oxidative Dehydrogenation of Propane (ODHP) reaction are developed through steam hydroxylation of boron nitride at its edges. These edge-hydroxylated sites offer several advantages over linearly dispersed edge sites. Firstly, they provide a higher density of active sites due to the increased number of exposed edges, enhancing the catalytic performance. Secondly, these unique structural features enable the selective activation of the CH bond in propane, facilitating the desired dehydrogenation process while suppressing overoxidation to CO2, thus improving the selectivity towards propylene. Answer: How are 2-D dispersed active sites in boron-based catalysts developed for the Oxidative Dehydrogenation of Propane (ODHP) reaction, what specific advantages do they offer over linearly dispersed edge sites in terms of active site density and catalytic performance, and why have these unique structural features attracted significant research interest in the field?

The boron nitride catalysts for the Oxidative Dehydrogenation of Propane (ODHP) reaction feature 2-D dispersed active sites, specifically at the edges after steam hydroxylation. These sites offer a high selectivity toward propylene, reaching up to 80.2% at a propane conversion of 20.6%. At a lower conversion level of 6.5%, the selectivity towards propylene is even higher, reaching 97.4%. The dynamic generation of active sites through hydrogen abstraction of BOH groups by molecular oxygen allows for selective breaking of the CH bond in propane, while simultaneously preventing the overoxidation of propylene to CO2. The 2-D dispersion of these active sites increases the surface area available for reactions compared to linearly dispersed edge sites, enhancing both the density of active sites and catalytic performance. This unique structural feature, combined with improved selectivity and stability, has attracted significant research interest in developing more efficient and selective catalysts for the ODHP reaction.
