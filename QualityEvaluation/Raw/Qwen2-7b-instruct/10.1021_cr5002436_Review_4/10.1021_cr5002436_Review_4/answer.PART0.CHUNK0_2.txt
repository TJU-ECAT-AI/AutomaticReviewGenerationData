Various carbon-based materials, including coke deposits, carbon nanofibers, and hybrid nanocarbons, exhibit unexpected catalytic activity in alkane dehydrogenation reactions through several mechanisms. Activated carbon and its derivatives like coke deposits facilitate the dehydrogenation process by forming radicals on their surfaces, which act as catalysts for the reaction. Carbon nanofibers (CNFs) and hybrid nanocarbons display catalytic activity due to the presence of functional groups or structural defects that enhance their reactivity. Elevated temperatures can lead to a decrease in selectivity, as cracking becomes the dominant reaction, but certain catalysts maintain high selectivity, as evidenced by McGregor et al.'s work showing 80% selectivity towards butenes. Nitrogen-doped carbon nanotubes (CNTs) and other doped carbon materials show some activity in dehydrogenation, albeit with lower conversion and selectivity compared to other catalysts. Surface oxygen groups on CNFs contribute to their activity in oxidative dehydrogenation, while surface defects on hybrid nanocarbons, such as structural imperfections in the graphitic material, also play a role in enhancing catalytic performance. These findings suggest that the development of novel and efficient carbon-based catalysts can be advanced by understanding and optimizing the surface chemistry and functionality of carbon materials.

The implications of this phenomenon for the development of novel and efficient carbon-based catalysts are significant. It highlights the potential of carbon-based materials, especially those with specific surface modifications, in catalyzing alkane dehydrogenation reactions. By controlling the surface chemistry and structure of these materials, researchers can tailor them to exhibit high activity, selectivity, and stability, leading to improved catalyst performance. This knowledge can guide the design of new carbon-based catalysts that are more efficient and sustainable, contributing to advancements in chemical synthesis and energy conversion processes.
