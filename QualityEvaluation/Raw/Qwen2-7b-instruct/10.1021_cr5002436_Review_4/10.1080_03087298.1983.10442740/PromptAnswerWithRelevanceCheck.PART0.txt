Coke deposits, for instance, have a high surface area and a porous structure which can enhance the adsorption and desorption of reactants, facilitating the dehydrogenation process. Carbon nanofibers possess a large surface area and well-defined pore structures, which can provide an extensive active site for the reaction, contributing to the high catalytic efficiency. Hybrid nanocarbons combine the advantages of both carbon and metal components, offering enhanced stability and selectivity in catalysis.

Influencing factors such as temperature and surface chemistry play crucial roles in determining the performance of these carbon-based catalysts. Surface chemistry, including the presence of functional groups and defect sites, can significantly impact the reactivity and selectivity of the catalysts. For example, the presence of oxygen-containing functional groups can promote the activation of alkane molecules, while defects in the carbon lattice can serve as additional active sites for the dehydrogenation reaction.

The implications of this phenomenon for the development of novel and efficient carbon-based catalysts are significant. The discovery of unexpected catalytic activity in carbon-based materials opens up new avenues for the design and synthesis of catalysts with improved performance, particularly in the context of sustainable and eco-friendly chemical processes.
