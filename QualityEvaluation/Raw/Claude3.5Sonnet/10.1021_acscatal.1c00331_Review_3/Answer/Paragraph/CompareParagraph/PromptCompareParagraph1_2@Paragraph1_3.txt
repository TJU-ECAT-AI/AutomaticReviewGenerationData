The characteristics of catalyst support pore structures significantly influence coke formation, accommodation, and overall catalyst performance in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions. This comprehensive review analyzes the impact of various pore structure features on PDH catalyst performance and provides insights for optimal catalyst design.

1. Pore Size:
Pore size plays a crucial role in catalyst performance and coke formation. Smaller pores generally lead to higher propane conversion and catalytic activity [10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]. However, the relationship between pore size and coke formation is complex. While smaller pores can limit coke formation, they are also more susceptible to blockage [10.1002/aic.16410]. Studies have shown that coke yield diminishes with decreasing pore size [10.1007/s10562-009-0196-4]. The optimal pore size should balance reactant diffusion, active site accessibility, and coke accommodation.

2. Pore Connectivity:
Three-dimensional, interconnected pore networks have shown superior performance compared to one-dimensional structures. Zeolites with three-dimensionally interconnected mesopores enhance catalytic performance by facilitating reactant and product diffusion [10.1038/s41586-020-2671-4]. Higher pore connectivity can maintain catalyst activity for longer periods by providing alternative pathways when some pores become blocked [10.1002/aic.16410].

3. Uniformity:
Uniform pore structures, such as those found in zeolites, contribute to better catalyst performance. Uniform microporous structures with shape-selective properties can influence product distribution and coke formation [10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]. However, non-uniform pore structures can lead to uneven coke distribution and localized deactivation [10.1002/aic.16410].

4. Hierarchical Arrangements:
Hierarchical pore structures, combining micropores and mesopores, have shown promising results in PDH catalysts. These structures balance high surface area for active site dispersion with improved mass transfer properties [10.1038/s41586-020-2671-4]. Hierarchical-porous zeolites, such as modified ZSM-5, exhibit enhanced capacity to accommodate coke and improved catalyst stability [10.1002/jctb.4686].

5. Coke Formation and Accommodation:
The pore structure significantly affects coke formation and accommodation. Supports with more uniform, highly connected, and larger-sized pores can accommodate more coke, potentially extending catalyst lifetime [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_3]. However, the relationship is not straightforward, as evidenced by studies showing higher coke formation in some microporous materials despite better overall performance [10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031].

6. Surface Area and Acidity:
While not directly related to pore structure, surface area and acidity of the support material interact with pore characteristics to influence catalyst performance. Higher surface areas generally lead to better initial catalyst performance but may also promote coke formation [10.1007/s10562-010-0527-5]. The acidity of the support can be modulated by introducing hierarchical pores, which can inhibit coke formation on metal sites [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_3].

Implications for Optimal Catalyst Design:
1. Develop supports with hierarchical pore structures to enhance coke accommodation, improve mass transfer, and increase catalyst stability.
2. Optimize pore size distribution to balance metal dispersion, reactant/product diffusion, and coke resistance.
3. Utilize three-dimensional, interconnected pore networks to enhance reactant-catalyst contact and accessibility to active sites.
4. Consider the interplay between pore structure and surface acidity to achieve desired catalytic performance and coke resistance.
5. Design pore structures that restrict metal particle agglomeration during reactions while facilitating the migration of coke precursors away from active sites.
6. Focus on creating a balance between micropores, mesopores, and macropores to maximize catalytic performance and longevity.


Integrative understanding and prospective outlook:

While significant progress has been made in understanding these relationships, several areas warrant further investigation:

1. Advanced characterization techniques: Developing and applying in-situ and operando characterization methods could provide deeper insights into the dynamic changes in pore structures during PDH reactions and coke formation processes.

2. Multiscale modeling: Integrating molecular simulations with macroscopic reactor models could help predict optimal pore structures for specific reaction conditions and catalyst compositions.

3. Tailored hierarchical structures: Exploring novel synthesis methods to create precisely controlled hierarchical pore structures with optimized connectivity and size distributions for PDH catalysts.

4. Smart catalyst design: Investigating the potential of stimuli-responsive pore structures that can adapt to changing reaction conditions or coke accumulation to maintain optimal performance over extended periods.

5. Alternative support materials: Exploring novel support materials beyond traditional zeolites and alumina, such as metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) or covalent organic frameworks (COFs), which offer unique pore structures and tunable properties.

6. Synergistic effects: Further investigating the synergistic effects between pore structure characteristics and other catalyst properties, such as metal-support interactions and surface functionalization.

7. Machine learning approaches: Utilizing machine learning algorithms to analyze large datasets of catalyst properties and performance metrics to identify optimal pore structure designs and predict catalyst behavior.

These research directions could lead to the development of next-generation PDH catalysts with enhanced performance, longer lifetimes, and improved resistance to coking, ultimately contributing to more efficient and sustainable propylene production processes.
