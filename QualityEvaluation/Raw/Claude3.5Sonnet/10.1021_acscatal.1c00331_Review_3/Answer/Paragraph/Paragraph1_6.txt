The characteristics of catalyst support pore structures significantly influence coke formation, accommodation, and overall catalyst performance in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions. This comprehensive review analyzes the impact of various pore structure characteristics on PDH catalyst performance and provides insights for optimal catalyst design.

1. Pore Size:
Pore size plays a crucial role in determining catalyst activity, selectivity, and coke formation. Generally, smaller pores lead to higher propane conversion and catalytic activity [10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]. However, the relationship between pore size and coke formation is complex. While smaller pores tend to limit coke yield [10.1007/s10562-009-0196-4], they may also promote more coke formation in some cases [10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]. Larger pores can accommodate more coke before becoming completely blocked [10.1002/aic.16410], potentially extending catalyst lifetime.

2. Pore Connectivity:
Three-dimensional, interconnected pore networks generally outperform one-dimensional pore structures in terms of activity, selectivity, and stability [10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]. Higher pore connectivity can maintain catalyst activity for longer periods by providing alternative pathways for reactants and products when some pores become blocked [10.1002/aic.16410].

3. Uniformity:
Uniform pore structures, such as those found in zeolites, contribute to superior catalyst performance compared to less uniform structures [10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]. Uniformity can lead to shape-selectivity effects, influencing product distribution and coke formation. However, supports with wide pore size distributions may allow coke to cover the external surface rather than the metallic surface, potentially helping maintain catalyst activity [10.1002/jctb.4686].

4. Hierarchical Arrangements:
Hierarchical pore structures, combining different pore sizes and connectivities, have shown promising results in PDH catalysts. These structures can balance coke accommodation and reactant diffusion [10.1002/aic.16410], enhance mass transfer, and improve catalyst stability [10.1021/acscatal.0c03286]. For example, the introduction of hierarchical pores in ZSM-5 zeolite improved the catalyst's capacity to accommodate coke and enhanced propane conversion and stability [10.1002/jctb.4686].

5. Coke Formation and Accommodation:
The pore structure significantly affects coke formation and accommodation. Zeolite supports with ordered microporous structures have shown lower coke formation compared to traditional alumina supports [10.1002/anie.202003349]. Hierarchical pore structures can enhance the catalyst's ability to accommodate coke while maintaining activity [10.1002/jctb.4686]. The location of coke deposition (e.g., on the external surface vs. within pores) also impacts catalyst performance [10.1002/anie.202003349].

6. Support Composition and Acidity:
While not directly related to pore structure, the chemical composition and acidity of the support interact with pore characteristics to influence catalyst performance. For instance, the acidity of the support can be modified by introducing hierarchical pores, which can inhibit coke formation on the metal and improve the support's capacity for coke accommodation [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_3].

Implications for Optimal Catalyst Design:
1. Develop supports with carefully tailored hierarchical pore structures that balance coke accommodation capacity with catalytic performance.
2. Optimize pore size distribution to achieve an ideal balance between metal dispersion, reactant/product diffusion, and coke resistance.
3. Utilize three-dimensional, interconnected pore networks to enhance reactant-catalyst contact and accessibility to active sites.
4. Consider the interplay between pore structure and surface acidity to achieve desired catalytic performance and coke resistance.
5. Design pore structures that restrict metal particle agglomeration during reactions while facilitating the migration of coke precursors away from active sites.
6. Focus on creating a balance between micropores, mesopores, and macropores to maximize catalytic performance and longevity.

By carefully engineering catalyst support pore structures with these factors in mind, it is possible to develop more efficient and stable catalysts for PDH reactions with improved resistance to coking and enhanced overall performance.

※※※

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The comprehensive analysis of catalyst support pore structures in PDH reactions reveals the complex interplay between various structural characteristics and their impact on catalyst performance. This understanding opens up new avenues for catalyst design and optimization.

Looking forward, several key areas of research and development emerge:

1. Advanced pore engineering: Future studies could focus on developing precise methods to create tailored pore structures with optimal combinations of pore sizes, connectivity, and hierarchical arrangements. This may involve novel synthesis techniques or the use of advanced templating agents.

2. In-situ characterization: Developing methods for real-time observation of coke formation and its interaction with pore structures during PDH reactions could provide invaluable insights for catalyst design.

3. Multiscale modeling: Integrating molecular-level simulations with macroscale reactor models could help predict the long-term performance of catalysts with different pore structures, guiding experimental efforts.

4. Smart materials: Exploring the potential of responsive or adaptable pore structures that can dynamically change during reaction conditions to optimize performance and minimize coking.

5. Synergistic effects: Investigating how pore structure characteristics can be combined with other catalyst design strategies, such as bimetallic active sites or promoters, to achieve superior performance.

6. Sustainable catalyst design: Focusing on developing catalysts with optimized pore structures that not only enhance performance but also improve catalyst longevity and ease of regeneration, contributing to more sustainable industrial processes.

These research directions could lead to the next generation of highly efficient, stable, and coke-resistant PDH catalysts, potentially revolutionizing the production of propylene and other valuable chemicals.

催化剂载体孔结构的特征对丙烷脱氢（PDH）反应中焦炭形成、容纳和整体催化剂性能有显著影响。本综述分析了各种孔结构特征对PDH催化剂性能的影响，并为最佳催化剂设计提供了见解。

1. 孔径：
孔径在决定催化剂活性、选择性和焦炭形成方面起着关键作用。通常，较小的孔径会导致更高的丙烷转化率和催化活性[10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]。然而，孔径与焦炭形成之间的关系是复杂的。虽然较小的孔径倾向于限制焦炭产量[10.1007/s10562-009-0196-4]，但在某些情况下也可能促进更多的焦炭形成[10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]。较大的孔径可以在完全堵塞之前容纳更多的焦炭[10.1002/aic.16410]，可能延长催化剂寿命。

2. 孔连通性：
三维互连的孔网络在活性、选择性和稳定性方面通常优于一维孔结构[10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]。较高的孔连通性可以通过在一些孔被堵塞时为反应物和产物提供替代路径来维持更长时间的催化剂活性[10.1002/aic.16410]。

3. 均匀性：
均匀的孔结构，如沸石中发现的那些，与不太均匀的结构相比，对催化剂性能有优越的贡献[10.1016/j.micromeso.2009.05.031]。均匀性可以导致形状选择性效应，影响产物分布和焦炭形成。然而，具有宽孔径分布的载体可能允许焦炭覆盖外表面而不是金属表面，这可能有助于保持催化剂活性[10.1002/jctb.4686]。

4. 层次结构：
结合不同孔径和连通性的层次孔结构在PDH催化剂中显示了有前景的结果。这些结构可以平衡焦炭容纳和反应物扩散[10.1002/aic.16410]，增强传质，并改善催化剂稳定性[10.1021/acscatal.0c03286]。例如，在ZSM-5沸石中引入层次孔提高了催化剂容纳焦炭的能力，并增强了丙烷转化率和稳定性[10.1002/jctb.4686]。

5. 焦炭形成和容纳：
孔结构显著影响焦炭形成和容纳。与传统的氧化铝载体相比，具有有序微孔结构的沸石载体显示出较低的焦炭形成[10.1002/anie.202003349]。层次孔结构可以增强催化剂容纳焦炭的能力，同时保持活性[10.1002/jctb.4686]。焦炭沉积的位置（例如，在外表面与孔内）也影响催化剂性能[10.1002/anie.202003349]。

6. 载体成分和酸度：
虽然与孔结构没有直接关系，但载体的化学成分和酸度与孔特征相互作用，影响催化剂性能。例如，通过引入层次孔可以修改载体的酸度，这可以抑制金属上的焦炭形成，并提高载体对焦炭容纳的能力[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_3]。

最佳催化剂设计的启示：
1. 开发具有精心设计的层次孔结构的载体，平衡焦炭容纳能力和催化性能。
2. 优化孔径分布，以在金属分散、反应物/产物扩散和抗焦炭能力之间达到理想平衡。
3. 利用三维互连孔网络增强反应物-催化剂接触和活性位点的可及性。
4. 考虑孔结构和表面酸度之间的相互作用，以实现所需的催化性能和抗焦炭能力。
5. 设计能够限制反应过程中金属颗粒团聚，同时促进焦炭前体从活性位点迁移的孔结构。
6. 专注于在微孔、介孔和大孔之间创造平衡，以最大化催化性能和寿命。

通过仔细考虑这些因素来设计催化剂载体孔结构，可以开发出更高效、更稳定的PDH反应催化剂，具有更好的抗焦炭能力和整体性能提升。

※※※

综合理解与未来展望：对PDH反应中催化剂载体孔结构的全面分析揭示了各种结构特征之间的复杂相互作用及其对催化剂性能的影响。这种理解为催化剂设计和优化开辟了新的途径。

展望未来，几个关键的研究和开发领域浮现出来：

1. 先进的孔工程：未来的研究可以集中于开发精确方法，创造具有最佳孔径、连通性和层次结构组合的定制孔结构。这可能涉及新型合成技术或先进模板剂的使用。

2. 原位表征：开发在PDH反应过程中实时观察焦炭形成及其与孔结构相互作用的方法，可以为催化剂设计提供宝贵的见解。

3. 多尺度建模：将分子水平模拟与宏观反应器模型相结合，可以帮助预测具有不同孔结构的催化剂的长期性能，指导实验努力。

4. 智能材料：探索在反应条件下可以动态变化以优化性能和最小化焦炭形成的响应式或适应性孔结构的潜力。

5. 协同效应：研究如何将孔结构特征与其他催化剂设计策略（如双金属活性位点或助剂）结合，以实现优越的性能。

6. 可持续催化剂设计：专注于开发具有优化孔结构的催化剂，不仅提高性能，还改善催化剂寿命和再生容易度，为更可持续的工业过程做出贡献。

这些研究方向可能导致下一代高效、稳定和抗焦炭的PDH催化剂的出现，可能彻底改变丙烯和其他有价值化学品的生产。
