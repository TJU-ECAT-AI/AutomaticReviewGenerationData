The characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces, synthesized by depositing Ni monolayers on Pt(111) substrates, is primarily conducted using Low-Energy Electron Diffraction (LEED) and Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES). These techniques are crucial for several reasons:

1. Structural analysis: LEED provides information about the surface structure and ordering of the Ni layers on Pt(111). It helps identify different structural phases and their evolution with increasing Ni coverage.

2. Growth mode determination: AES is used to study the growth mode of Ni ultrathin films on Pt(111). It helps determine whether the growth follows a layer-by-layer, island, or mixed mode.

3. Layer thickness measurement: LEED intensity oscillations are used to measure the number of Ni layers grown on the Pt substrate, providing precise control over film thickness.

4. Alloy formation investigation: AES is employed to study the formation of NiPt alloys at different coverages and temperatures, giving insights into the interdiffusion processes.

These characterization methods are crucial for structural modeling and DFT calculations because they provide accurate experimental data on the atomic arrangement, growth patterns, and alloy formation in the Ni/Pt system. This information is essential for creating realistic models for theoretical studies and validating computational results.

Furthermore, these techniques contribute to our comprehensive understanding of the electronic and chemical properties of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces by revealing how the surface structure evolves with coverage and temperature. This knowledge is vital for advanced surface science and catalysis research, as it helps explain the unique catalytic properties of Ni/Pt systems and guides the design of more efficient catalysts for various applications.
