These mechanisms are crucial for selective oxidation reactions, including the industrial conversion of butane to maleic anhydride.

The Mars van Krevelen mechanism involves a series of elementary steps:
1. Activation of the substrate on a metallic cation acting as a Lewis acid site
2. Insertion of oxygen from the lattice
3. A redox process at the metal site involving electron transfer [10.1021/cr010333u]

In this mechanism, surface O= or OH- species act as basic sites, while metallic cations serve as Lewis acid sites. The substrate undergoes hydrogen abstraction, oxygen insertion, and electron transfer during this process [10.1021/cr010333u].

H-abstraction is another key mechanism, where surface oxygen species abstract hydrogen from the alkane, forming radical intermediates that undergo further transformations [10.1021/cr900122p\_0].

The site isolation theory, developed by Callahan and Grasselli, emphasizes the importance of having a controlled number of surface oxidizing sites to favor selective oxidation over combustion reactions [10.1016/s0920-5861(99)00041-3].

In the industrial conversion of butane to maleic anhydride, these principles are applied using vanadyl pyrophosphate ((VO)2P2O7) catalysts. The process involves:
1. C-H bond activation of butane as the rate-determining step
2. Conversion of butane into 2-butenes via concerted abstraction of two hydrogen atoms by V(IV)O species
3. Reoxidation of the active phase by O2, following the Mars van Krevelen mechanism [10.1021/cr900122p\_0]

The catalyst's performance is influenced by factors such as preparation method, P/V ratio, presence of additives, and activation procedures [10.1039/9781847553232-00246]. The process achieves high selectivity (up to 97% at 65% conversion) and is commercially viable [10.1021/cr900122p\_0].

The Mars van Krevelen mechanism is one of the most general mechanisms for oxidation reactions in metallic oxides [10.1021/cr010333u]. It involves a series of elementary steps:
1. Activation of the substrate on a metallic cation acting as a Lewis acid site
2. Insertion of oxygen from the lattice
3. A redox process at the metal site involving electron transfer

This mechanism relies on the cooperation between acid-base and redox sites on the catalyst surface [10.1021/cr010333u]. The metal centers can facilitate oxidation reactions in three general ways:
1. Single electron abstraction from an electron-rich molecule
2. Acting as part of a catalytic center that includes Lewis acid, Lewis base, and redox-active metal sites
3. Forming acid-base adducts with the substrate or oxidizing agent to enhance reactivity

H-abstraction is another important mechanism in C-H bond activation. This process involves the abstraction of hydrogen by reactive oxygen sites on the oxide surface, resulting in the formation of adsorbed alkyl radical intermediates [10.1021/cr900122p\_0]. The H-abstraction mechanism is often the rate-limiting step in selective oxidation reactions of alkanes [10.1021/cr900122p\_0].

These principles are applied in industrial selective oxidation of alkanes, with the conversion of butane to maleic anhydride being a prime example. This process uses vanadium phosphate (VPO) catalysts, specifically vanadyl pyrophosphate ((VO)2P2O7) [10.1039/9781847553232-00246]. The reaction is believed to occur on the (1,0,0) crystal face of the catalyst, where the spatial arrangement of atoms facilitates interaction with the C-C and C-H bonds of butane [10.1021/cr010333u].

The mechanism for butane to maleic anhydride conversion involves:
1. C-H bond activation of butane as the rate-determining step
2. Conversion of butane into 2-butenes via a concerted abstraction of two hydrogen atoms by V(IV)O species
