The primary causes of metal oxide catalyst deactivation in oxidative dehydrogenation processes, focusing on Cr-based and VOx catalysts, include:

1. Coke formation: This is a major cause of deactivation for both Cr-based and VOx catalysts. Coke deposits on the catalyst surface, blocking active sites and reducing catalytic activity [10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017].

2. Reduction of active metal species: During the reaction, Cr3+ can be reduced to inactive Cr2+, while VOx species can be reduced from higher to lower oxidation states, decreasing catalytic activity [10.1002/cctc.201200397].

3. Sintering and aggregation: High temperatures during reaction or regeneration can cause metal oxide species to sinter, forming larger crystallites or mixed oxides, which are less active [10.1021/cr5002436].

4. Structural changes: The incorporation of active species into the support material, particularly for Cr-based catalysts on alumina supports, can lead to a decrease in catalytically active species [10.1021/cr5002436].

5. Strong adsorption of reactants: This can block active sites and contribute to initial deactivation [10.1021/cr5002436].

Regarding regeneration methods:

1. Reoxidation with air or O2:
   - Most effective for removing coke deposits and restoring activity
   - Typically performed at high temperatures (550-650°C)
   - Can lead to overoxidation and gradual loss of active sites over multiple cycles
   - For CrOx/ZrO2, regeneration in O2 at 600°C for 5 minutes can completely restore activity for at least 4 cycles [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_3]

2. CO2 addition:
   - Acts as a milder oxidant, helping to maintain higher oxidation states of Cr and V species
   - Reduces coke formation through the Boudouard reaction
   - Partially reoxidizes reduced species (e.g., V2O3 to V2O4) [10.1016/j.jcat.2015.12.015]
   - Can be effective during the reaction itself, suppressing deactivation
   - Less effective than O2 in fully restoring activity, especially over multiple cycles

3. O2 + CO2 mixtures:
   - Combines the benefits of both oxidants
   - Effective in reducing coke formation during the reaction
   - Maintains Cr in a higher oxidation state (Cr3+) [10.1039/C5CY00998G]

Temperature requirements vary but generally range from 500-650°C for effective regeneration. Lower temperatures are preferred to minimize thermal stress and sintering, but must be high enough to remove coke effectively.

Impact on catalytic performance:
- O2 regeneration is most effective in restoring activity but can lead to overoxidation and decreased selectivity
- CO2 addition helps maintain selectivity and suppress deactivation but may not fully restore initial activity
- The addition of promoters (e.g., Ni, K) can enhance the effectiveness of CO2 regeneration and improve overall catalyst stability [10.1002/cctc.201200397]

The primary causes of metal oxide catalyst deactivation in oxidative dehydrogenation processes, focusing on Cr-based and VOx catalysts, include:

1. Coke formation: This is the main cause of activity loss for both Cr-based and VOx catalysts. Coke deposition is accelerated at high temperatures and in the presence of acid sites on the catalyst surface [10.1016/S0926-860X(03)00368-5].

2. Reduction of active metal species: During the reaction, Cr3+ species are reduced to inactive Cr2+ state, while VOx species are reduced from their higher oxidation states, decreasing their catalytic activity [10.1002/cctc.201200397] [10.1016/S0920-5861(03)00268-2].

3. Aggregation and structural changes: The highly dispersed active CrOx or VOx species may agglomerate or transform into less active forms, reducing catalytic activity [10.1016/j.cej.2010.11.070] [10.1002/ejic.201701049].

4. Incorporation of active species into the support: For Cr-based catalysts, the heat released during coke combustion can cause chromia to incorporate into the alumina framework, reducing the amount of catalytically active Cr species [10.1021/cr5002436].

Various regeneration methods have been studied to address these issues:

1. Reoxidation with air or O2: This is the most common and effective method for removing coke deposits and restoring catalyst activity. For example, regeneration in O2 at 600°C for 5 minutes can completely restore the activity of CrOx/ZrO2, repeatable for at least 4 cycles [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_3]. However, this method may lead to gradual irreversible deactivation, especially for Cr-based catalysts, due to the incorporation of chromia into the support [10.1021/cr5002436].

2. CO2 addition: CO2 acts as a milder oxidant, helping to maintain higher oxidation states of both Cr and V species during the reaction. It can eliminate coke deposition and reoxidize the reduced active phase [10.1002/cctc.201200397]. For VOx catalysts, CO2 can oxidize V2O3 to V2O4, but not fully to V2O5, which helps maintain catalyst selectivity by preventing overoxidation [10.1016/j.jcat.2015.12.015].

3. O2 or O2+CO2 mixtures: These are most effective in reducing coke formation during the reaction and maintain Cr in a higher oxidation state (Cr3+) [10.1039/C5CY00998G].

4. Ni promotion with CO2: This enhances CO2 conversion, maintains stable production of propylene, and regenerates reduced Cr2+ species to Cr3+ through reoxidation [10.1002/cctc.201200397].

The effectiveness of these methods varies. O2-based regeneration seems most effective at reversing deactivation but may lead to overoxidation. CO2 addition shows promise in maintaining catalyst performance and potentially improving selectivity, especially for VOx catalysts where overoxidation is a concern.

Temperature requirements for regeneration are typically in the range of 550-650°C [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_3]. Lower temperatures are preferred to minimize thermal stress and sintering, but must be high enough to effectively remove coke.

The impact on catalytic performance varies depending on the regeneration method. While most methods can restore activity and selectivity, there are trade-offs. For instance, co-feeding O2 during the reaction removes coke but reduces selectivity [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_3].
