The primary causes of metal oxide catalyst deactivation in oxidative dehydrogenation processes, focusing on Cr-based and VOx catalysts, include:

1. Coke formation: This is a major cause of deactivation for both Cr-based and VOx catalysts. Coke deposition occurs due to the transformation of initially produced olefins, particularly at allylic positions, leading to catalyst surface coverage and pore blockage [10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017] [10.1016/j.cej.2010.11.070].

2. Reduction of active metal species: The active metal oxides (Cr and V) can be reduced from their higher oxidation states during the reaction, decreasing catalytic activity. For instance, Cr3+ can be reduced to inactive Cr2+, while V5+ can be reduced to lower oxidation states [10.1002/cctc.201200397] [10.1016/S0920-5861(03)00268-2].

3. Aggregation and sintering of active species: High temperatures during reaction or regeneration can cause clustering of metal oxide species, forming larger crystallites or mixed oxides, which are less active [10.1021/cr5002436].

4. Structural changes: Incorporation of active species into the support material (e.g., chromia into alumina framework) can occur during regeneration, reducing the amount of catalytically active species [10.1021/cr5002436].

5. Strong adsorption of reactants on active sites: This can lead to site blocking and reduced catalytic activity [10.1021/cr5002436].

Regeneration methods and their effectiveness:

1. Reoxidation with air or O2:
   - Most effective for removing coke deposits and restoring activity
   - Typically performed at high temperatures (550-650°C)
   - Can lead to overoxidation and gradual loss of active sites over multiple cycles
   - For CrOx/ZrO2, regeneration in O2 at 600°C for 5 minutes can completely restore activity for at least 4 cycles [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_3]

2. CO2 addition:
   - Acts as a milder oxidant, helping maintain higher oxidation states of Cr and V species during reaction
   - Reduces coke formation through the Boudouard reaction
   - Partially reoxidizes reduced metal species (e.g., V2O3 to V2O4)
   - Helps maintain catalyst selectivity by preventing overoxidation
   - Less effective than O2 for complete catalyst regeneration
   - For VOx catalysts, CO2 regenerates about 45% of the exchangeable oxygen compared to O2 [10.1016/j.jcat.2015.12.015]

3. O2 + CO2 mixtures:
   - Combines the benefits of both oxidants
   - Effective in reducing coke formation during the reaction
   - Maintains Cr in a higher oxidation state (Cr3+)
   - May provide a balance between activity restoration and selectivity maintenance [10.1039/C5CY00998G]

4. Steam addition:
   - Similar to CO2, helps reduce coke deposition and assists in product desorption
   - Can be used in combination with other regeneration methods [10.1021/cr5002436]

Temperature requirements for regeneration are typically in the range of 550-650°C, with lower temperatures preferred to minimize thermal stress and sintering, but high enough to effectively remove coke [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_3].

Impact on catalytic performance:
- Air/O2 regeneration can restore most of the initial activity but may lead to a gradual loss of active sites over multiple cycles.
- CO2 addition improves catalyst stability and olefin yield, while mitigating overoxidation in VOx-catalyzed reactions.
- The use of promoters (e.g., Ni, K) can enhance catalyst stability and reduce coke formation [10.1002/cctc.201200397].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook:

The research on metal oxide catalyst deactivation and regeneration in oxidative dehydrogenation processes has revealed the complex interplay between catalyst composition, reaction conditions, and regeneration methods. While significant progress has been made in understanding the deactivation mechanisms and developing effective regeneration strategies, there are still areas for improvement and future research:

1. Advanced in-situ characterization techniques: Developing and applying more sophisticated in-situ characterization methods could provide deeper insights into the dynamic changes occurring on catalyst surfaces during reaction and regeneration. This could lead to more targeted and effective regeneration strategies.

2. Tailored regeneration protocols: Future research could focus on developing regeneration protocols that are specifically tailored to different catalyst compositions and reaction conditions. This could involve optimizing the combination and sequence of various regeneration methods (e.g., CO2, O2, steam) to maximize catalyst longevity and performance.

3. Novel catalyst designs: Exploring new catalyst designs that inherently resist deactivation, such as core-shell structures or advanced support materials, could lead to more stable and efficient catalysts for oxidative dehydrogenation processes.

4. Machine learning and artificial intelligence: Utilizing machine learning algorithms to analyze large datasets of catalyst performance, deactivation patterns, and regeneration outcomes could help identify optimal regeneration strategies and predict catalyst behavior under various conditions.

5. Green regeneration methods: Investigating more environmentally friendly regeneration methods, such as using renewable oxidants or developing low-temperature regeneration techniques, could improve the sustainability of oxidative dehydrogenation processes.

6. Understanding synergistic effects: Further research into the synergistic effects of multiple oxidants (e.g., CO2 + O2) and promoters could lead to more effective and selective catalytic systems.

7. Addressing long-term deactivation: While many regeneration methods can address short-term deactivation, more research is needed to tackle long-term deactivation mechanisms, such as the incorporation of active species into support materials.

By addressing these areas, future research could significantly enhance the performance, longevity, and sustainability of metal oxide catalysts in oxidative dehydrogenation processes, potentially leading to more efficient and environmentally friendly industrial applications.

<Chinese>
在氧化脱氢过程中，金属氧化物催化剂（特别是基于Cr和VOx的催化剂）失活的主要原因包括：

1. 积碳形成：这是Cr基和VOx催化剂失活的主要原因。积碳沉积是由于初始生成的烯烃转化而发生的，特别是在烯丙基位置，导致催化剂表面覆盖和孔隙堵塞 [10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017] [10.1016/j.cej.2010.11.070]。

2. 活性金属物种的还原：活性金属氧化物（Cr和V）在反应过程中可能从较高的氧化态被还原，降低催化活性。例如，Cr3+可被还原为无活性的Cr2+，而V5+可被还原为较低的氧化态 [10.1002/cctc.201200397] [10.1016/S0920-5861(03)00268-2]。

3. 活性物种的聚集和烧结：反应或再生过程中的高温可导致金属氧化物物种聚集，形成更大的晶体或混合氧化物，这些物种活性较低 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

4. 结构变化：在再生过程中，活性物种可能会被纳入支持材料（例如，氧化铬纳入氧化铝框架），减少催化活性物种的数量 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

5. 反应物在活性位点上的强吸附：这可导致位点阻塞和催化活性降低 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

再生方法及其有效性：

1. 空气或O2再氧化：
   - 最有效地去除积碳沉积物并恢复活性
   - 通常在高温下进行（550-650°C）
   - 可能导致过度氧化和多个循环后活性位点的逐渐损失
   - 对于CrOx/ZrO2，在600°C下用O2再生5分钟可以完全恢复活性，至少可持续4个循环 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_3]

2. CO2添加：
   - 作为较温和的氧化剂，有助于在反应过程中维持Cr和V物种的较高氧化态
   - 通过Boudouard反应减少积碳形成
   - 部分再氧化还原的金属物种（例如，V2O3到V2O4）
   - 通过防止过度氧化来帮助维持催化剂选择性
   - 对于完全催化剂再生的效果不如O2
   - 对于VOx催化剂，CO2再生约45%的可交换氧，相比于O2 [10.1016/j.jcat.2015.12.015]

3. O2 + CO2混合：
   - 结合两种氧化剂的优点
   - 在反应过程中有效减少积碳形成
   - 保持Cr在较高的氧化态（Cr3+）
   - 可能在活性恢复和选择性维持之间提供平衡 [10.1039/C5CY00998G]

4. 蒸汽添加：
   - 类似于CO2，有助于减少积碳沉积并辅助产物脱附
   - 可与其他再生方法结合使用 [10.1021/cr5002436]

再生的温度要求通常在550-650°C范围内，较低的温度有助于最小化热应力和烧结，但需要足够高以有效去除积碳 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_3]。

对催化性能的影响：
- 空气/O2再生可以恢复大部分初始活性，但可能导致多个循环后活性位点的逐渐损失。
- CO2添加改善催化剂稳定性和烯烃产率，同时减轻VOx催化反应中的过度氧化。
- 使用促进剂（如Ni、K）可以增强催化剂稳定性并减少积碳形成 [10.1002/cctc.201200397]。

综合理解与未来展望：

对氧化脱氢过程中金属氧化物催化剂失活和再生的研究揭示了催化剂组成、反应条件和再生方法之间复杂的相互作用。虽然在理解失活机理和开发有效再生策略方面已取得重大进展，但仍有改进和未来研究的空间：

1. 先进的原位表征技术：开发和应用更复杂的原位表征方法可以提供对反应和再生过程中催化剂表面动态变化的深入了解。这可能导致更有针对性和有效的再生策略。

2. 定制再生方案：未来的研究可以集中在开发针对不同催化剂组成和反应条件的特定再生方案。这可能涉及优化各种再生方法（如CO2、O2、蒸汽）的组合和顺序，以最大化催化剂寿命和性能。

3. 新型催化剂设计：探索本质上抗失活的新催化剂设计，如核壳结构或先进的支持材料，可能导致更稳定和高效的氧化脱氢过程催化剂。

4. 机器学习和人工智能：利用机器学习算法分析大量催化剂性能、失活模式和再生结果数据，可以帮助确定最佳再生策略并预测各种条件下的催化剂行为。

5. 绿色再生方法：研究更环保的再生方法，如使用可再生氧化剂或开发低温再生技术，可以提高氧化脱氢过程的可持续性。

6. 理解协同效应：进一步研究多种氧化剂（如CO2 + O2）和促进剂的协同效应，可能导致更有效和选择性的催化系统。

7. 解决长期失活问题：虽然许多再生方法可以解决短期失活，但需要更多研究来解决长期失活机制，如活性物种纳入支持材料。

通过解决这些领域，未来的研究可能显著提高氧化脱氢过程中金属氧化物催化剂的性能、寿命和可持续性，潜在地导致更高效和环保的工业应用。
