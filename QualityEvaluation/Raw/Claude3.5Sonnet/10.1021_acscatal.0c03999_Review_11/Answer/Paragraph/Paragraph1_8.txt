Halogen-assisted methods for selective propane dehydrogenation: A comparative analysis of various approaches, reaction conditions, and propane's unique reactivity

Propane dehydrogenation (PDH) to produce propene is an important industrial process. Various halogen-assisted methods have been developed to improve the efficiency and selectivity of this reaction. This review compares different approaches, their reaction conditions, and analyzes propane's unique reactivity in these processes.

1. Dehydrogenation with molecular halogens (X2):
This method directly uses halogens to remove hydrogen from propane. For example, chlorine-mediated dehydrogenation using a 0.28% Ru/TiO2 catalyst at 400°C achieves 50% propane conversion with 95% propene selectivity [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]. Iodine-mediated dehydrogenation generally gives higher propylene yields compared to conventional routes, but requires higher temperatures (>673 K) [10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551].

2. Oxidative dehydrogenation in the presence of halogens (X2 + O2):
This approach combines halogens with oxygen to facilitate the dehydrogenation process. The addition of oxygen shifts the equilibrium towards higher propane conversion and makes the reaction exothermic [10.1007/s10562-016-1701-1]. For instance, gas-phase iodine-mediated oxidative dehydrogenation at 500°C with a propane partial pressure of 0.1 atm yields 64% propylene [10.1007/s10562-016-1701-1].

3. Propane oxyhalogenation via halides (HX + O2):
This method uses hydrogen halides and oxygen to convert propane to propene. The reaction proceeds through the oxidation of hydrogen halide with O2, producing alkyl halide, alkene, and water. Theoretically, it can achieve 100% halogen atom efficiency in the presence of O2, compared to 50% for halo-dehydrogenation [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]. Propane oxybromination over vanadium phosphate catalysts shows increasing propylene selectivity with temperature (typically 750-850 K) [10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551].

4. Molten metal halide salt-assisted oxidative dehydrogenation (LiX + O2):
This approach uses metal iodides like LiI, CdI2, ZnI2, and PbI2 in a molten salt form. The reaction typically occurs between 450-600°C and can achieve propene yields up to 70% [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]. A specific example uses 5 wt.-% LiOH*H2O/LiI salt melt at 600°C, with a propane/air mixture of 1:2 [10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S]. By optimizing conditions, 80% propane conversion and 84% selectivity to propylene can be achieved at 500°C [10.1007/s10562-016-1701-1].

5. Bihalogen-mediated dehydrogenation:
This novel approach uses both Br2 and I2 as mediators. Adding a small amount of bromine to the iodination reaction significantly promotes propane conversion and product selectivity. High yields of propene and propyl halides (up to 80%) can be achieved at moderate temperatures below 800 K [10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551]. Specifically, using bromine-iodine mixtures with low iodine content (no greater than 20%) results in up to 80% of C3H6+C3H7X single-pass yield at temperatures <800K [10.1002/cctc.201200913].

Propane's reactivity in these processes differs significantly from methane and ethane due to its molecular structure. Propane has both primary and secondary carbon atoms, making it more reactive than methane or ethane, which only have primary carbons [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]. This allows for easier C-H bond activation and dehydrogenation. Additionally, propane can form more stable intermediate species and undergo dehydrogenation more readily to produce propene [10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551].

The presence of reactive allylic hydrogen atoms in propene (the product) can lead to successive oxidation, resulting in lower selectivity compared to ethylene production from ethane [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]. At high radical concentrations, propane can undergo coupling reactions to form larger molecules like benzene, which is not possible with methane and less likely with ethane [10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S].

These structural differences affect the ease of C-H bond activation, the stability of intermediates formed during the dehydrogenation process, and the overall reaction pathways, leading to different product distributions compared to shorter chain alkanes [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook:
The comprehensive review of halogen-assisted methods for propane dehydrogenation reveals a complex landscape of reaction mechanisms and process optimizations. While each method offers unique advantages, the bihalogen-mediated approach and molten metal halide salt-assisted oxidative dehydrogenation show particularly promising results in terms of yield and selectivity at moderate temperatures.

Future research directions may include:

1. Catalyst design: Developing novel catalysts that can further enhance selectivity and stability, particularly for the bihalogen-mediated approach.

2. Process intensification: Exploring ways to combine the benefits of different methods, such as integrating molten salt catalysts with gas-phase halogen mediators.

3. Sustainable halogen sources: Investigating more environmentally friendly and economically viable sources of halogens or halogen compounds for these processes.

4. In-situ product separation: Developing methods to continuously remove propene from the reaction mixture to drive equilibrium and prevent over-oxidation.

5. Computational modeling: Utilizing advanced simulation techniques to better understand and predict the complex radical chemistry involved in these reactions.

6. Scale-up studies: Addressing engineering challenges associated with scaling up these processes for industrial application, particularly focusing on heat management and corrosion resistance.

7. Life cycle assessment: Conducting comprehensive environmental and economic analyses of these processes to determine their viability as alternatives to conventional propane dehydrogenation methods.

By pursuing these research directions, it may be possible to develop more efficient, selective, and sustainable processes for propane dehydrogenation, potentially revolutionizing propene production in the chemical industry.

卤素辅助选择性丙烷脱氢方法：各种方法、反应条件及丙烷独特反应性的比较分析

丙烷脱氢(PDH)生产丙烯是一个重要的工业过程。已经开发了各种卤素辅助方法来提高这种反应的效率和选择性。本综述比较了不同的方法、它们的反应条件，并分析了丙烷在这些过程中的独特反应性。

1. 分子卤素脱氢(X2)：
这种方法直接使用卤素从丙烷中移除氢。例如，在400°C下使用0.28% Ru/TiO2催化剂的氯介导脱氢可以达到50%的丙烷转化率和95%的丙烯选择性[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。碘介导脱氢通常比传统路线产生更高的丙烯收率，但需要更高的温度(>673 K)[10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551]。

2. 卤素存在下的氧化脱氢(X2 + O2)：
这种方法将卤素与氧气结合以促进脱氢过程。添加氧气使平衡向更高的丙烷转化移动，并使反应成为放热反应[10.1007/s10562-016-1701-1]。例如，在500°C下，丙烷分压为0.1 atm的气相碘介导氧化脱氢可产生64%的丙烯[10.1007/s10562-016-1701-1]。

3. 通过卤化物的丙烷氧卤化(HX + O2)：
这种方法使用卤化氢和氧气将丙烷转化为丙烯。反应通过卤化氢与O2的氧化进行，产生烷基卤化物、烯烃和水。理论上，在O2存在的情况下，它可以达到100%的卤素原子效率，相比之下，卤素脱氢的效率为50%[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。在钒磷酸盐催化剂上进行的丙烷氧溴化随温度升高（通常在750-850 K）显示出丙烯选择性的增加[10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551]。

4. 熔融金属卤化物盐辅助氧化脱氢(LiX + O2)：
这种方法使用熔融盐形式的金属碘化物，如LiI、CdI2、ZnI2和PbI2。反应通常在450-600°C之间进行，可以达到高达70%的丙烯收率[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。一个具体的例子是在600°C下使用5 wt.-% LiOH*H2O/LiI盐熔体，丙烷/空气混合比为1:2 [10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S]。通过优化条件，在500°C下可以达到80%的丙烷转化率和84%的丙烯选择性[10.1007/s10562-016-1701-1]。

5. 双卤素介导脱氢：
这种新颖的方法同时使用Br2和I2作为介质。在碘化反应中添加少量溴显著促进了丙烷转化和产物选择性。在800 K以下的温度下可以达到高达80%的丙烯和丙基卤化物收率[10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551]。具体来说，使用低碘含量（不超过20%）的溴-碘混合物可以在<800K的温度下达到80%的C3H6+C3H7X单程收率[10.1002/cctc.201200913]。

丙烷在这些过程中的反应性与甲烷和乙烷显著不同，这是由于其分子结构的差异。丙烷既有伯碳原子又有仲碳原子，使其比只有伯碳原子的甲烷或乙烷更具反应性[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。这允许更容易的C-H键活化和脱氢。此外，丙烷可以形成更稳定的中间物种，更容易进行脱氢生成丙烯[10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551]。

产物丙烯中存在的活性烯丙基氢原子可能导致连续氧化，与乙烷生成乙烯相比，这导致了较低的选择性[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。在高自由基浓度下，丙烷可以进行偶联反应形成更大的分子如苯，这在甲烷中是不可能的，在乙烷中也不太可能发生[10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S]。

这些结构差异影响了C-H键活化的容易程度、脱氢过程中形成的中间体的稳定性以及整体反应途径，导致与短链烷烃相比产生不同的产物分布[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。

综合理解与未来展望：
对丙烷脱氢的卤素辅助方法的全面回顾揭示了反应机理和过程优化的复杂景象。虽然每种方法都提供了独特的优势，但双卤素介导方法和熔融金属卤化物盐辅助氧化脱氢在中等温度下的产率和选择性方面显示出特别有前景的结果。

未来的研究方向可能包括：

1. 催化剂设计：开发新型催化剂，可以进一步提高选择性和稳定性，特别是针对双卤素介导方法。

2. 过程强化：探索结合不同方法优势的方式，如将熔融盐催化剂与气相卤素介质相结合。

3. 可持续卤素来源：研究更环保和经济可行的卤素或卤素化合物来源。

4. 原位产品分离：开发方法持续从反应混合物中移除丙烯，以驱动平衡并防止过度氧化。

5. 计算机模拟：利用先进的模拟技术更好地理解和预测这些反应中涉及的复杂自由基化学。

6. 放大研究：解决这些过程工业化应用相关的工程挑战，特别关注热管理和耐腐蚀性。

7. 生命周期评估：对这些过程进行全面的环境和经济分析，以确定它们作为传统丙烷脱氢方法替代品的可行性。

通过追求这些研究方向，可能开发出更高效、更选择性和更可持续的丙烷脱氢过程，有潜力彻底改变化学工业中的丙烯生产。
