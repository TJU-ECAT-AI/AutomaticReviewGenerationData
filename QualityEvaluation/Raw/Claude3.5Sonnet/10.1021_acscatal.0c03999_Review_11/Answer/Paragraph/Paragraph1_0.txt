Halogen-assisted methods for selective propane dehydrogenation: A comparative analysis of various approaches, reaction conditions, and propane's unique reactivity

Halogen-assisted methods for selectively dehydrogenating propane to propene have emerged as promising alternatives to conventional dehydrogenation processes. These methods offer improved yields, lower reaction temperatures, and better control over selectivity. The main approaches include:

1. Dehydrogenation with molecular halogens (X2):
This method directly uses halogens to remove hydrogen from propane. For example, using Cl2 with a 0.28% Ru/TiO2 catalyst at 400°C achieves 50% propane conversion with 95% propene selectivity [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]. Iodine-mediated dehydrogenation generally gives higher propylene yields compared to conventional routes, but requires high temperatures (>673 K) [10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551].

2. Oxidative dehydrogenation in the presence of halogens (X2 + O2):
This approach combines halogens with oxygen to facilitate the dehydrogenation process. The addition of oxygen shifts the equilibrium towards higher propane conversion and makes the reaction exothermic [10.1007/s10562-016-1701-1]. However, the highest propylene yield reported for propane oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) is only about 30%, due to consecutive oxidation of propylene to oxygenated products [10.1002/cctc.201200913].

3. Propane oxyhalogenation via halides (HX + O2):
This method uses hydrogen halides and oxygen to convert propane to propene. The reaction proceeds through the oxidation of hydrogen halide with O2, producing alkyl halide, alkene, and water. Theoretically, it can achieve 100% halogen atom efficiency in the presence of O2, compared to 50% for halo-dehydrogenation [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11].

4. Molten metal halide salt-assisted oxidative dehydrogenation (LiX + O2):
This approach uses metal iodides like LiI, CdI2, ZnI2, and PbI2 in a molten salt form. The reaction occurs between 450-600°C and can achieve propene yields up to 70% [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]. In one study, using a 5 wt.-% LiOH*H2O/LiI salt melt at 600°C with a propane/air mixture of 1:2 achieved 80% propane conversion and 84% selectivity to propylene [10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S].

5. Bihalogen-mediated dehydrogenation:
This novel approach uses both Br2 and I2 as mediators. Adding a small amount of bromine to the iodination reaction significantly promotes propane conversion and product selectivity. High yields of propene and propyl halides (up to 80%) can be achieved at moderate temperatures below 800 K [10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551].

Reaction conditions vary for each method but generally involve temperatures between 300-700°C, with some methods requiring higher temperatures (up to 900 K for iodine-based processes). The presence of oxygen, specific catalysts, and the use of molten salt melts are common features in many of these approaches.

Propane's reactivity in these processes differs significantly from methane and ethane due to its molecular structure:

1. Propane has both primary and secondary carbon atoms, making it more reactive than methane or ethane, which only have primary carbons [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11].

2. The presence of reactive allylic hydrogen atoms in propene (the product) can lead to successive oxidation, resulting in lower selectivity compared to ethylene production from ethane [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11].

3. Propane can form both normal and isopropyl radicals, leading to different reaction pathways and influencing selectivity [10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S].

4. The formation of a stable double bond in propene provides a thermodynamic driving force not present in methane dehydrogenation and less favorable for ethane [10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S].

5. At high radical concentrations, propane can undergo coupling reactions to form larger molecules like benzene, which is not possible with methane and less likely with ethane [10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S].

These structural differences affect the ease of C-H bond activation, the stability of intermediates formed during the dehydrogenation process, and the overall reaction pathways, leading to different product distributions compared to shorter chain alkanes.

※※※

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The development of halogen-assisted methods for selective propane dehydrogenation represents a significant advancement in the field of alkane activation. These methods offer promising alternatives to conventional dehydrogenation processes, potentially addressing issues of high energy consumption and catalyst deactivation.

Future research directions may include:

1. Catalyst development: Designing novel catalysts that can further improve selectivity and conversion rates while minimizing side reactions and catalyst deactivation.

2. Process intensification: Exploring innovative reactor designs and process configurations to optimize heat management and product separation, potentially leading to more efficient and economical industrial-scale operations.

3. Mechanistic studies: Conducting in-depth investigations into the reaction mechanisms of various halogen-assisted methods to gain a better understanding of the factors influencing selectivity and reactivity.

4. Sustainability considerations: Developing strategies to minimize halogen consumption and improve halogen recycling efficiency, addressing environmental concerns associated with halogen use.

5. Expansion to other alkanes: Applying the insights gained from propane dehydrogenation to develop similar processes for other alkanes, potentially opening new pathways for the valorization of light hydrocarbons.

6. Integration with renewable energy: Exploring the potential of coupling these processes with renewable energy sources to create more sustainable chemical production routes.

By addressing these areas, researchers can further improve the efficiency and selectivity of halogen-assisted propane dehydrogenation methods, potentially revolutionizing the production of propylene and other valuable chemical intermediates.

卤素辅助的选择性丙烷脱氢方法：各种方法、反应条件和丙烷独特反应性的比较分析

卤素辅助的选择性丙烷脱氢制丙烯方法已成为传统脱氢工艺的有前景的替代方案。这些方法提供了更高的收率、更低的反应温度和更好的选择性控制。主要方法包括：

1. 分子卤素（X2）脱氢：
这种方法直接使用卤素从丙烷中移除氢。例如，在400°C下使用0.28% Ru/TiO2催化剂和Cl2可以达到50%的丙烷转化率和95%的丙烯选择性[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。碘介导的脱氢通常比传统路线产生更高的丙烯收率，但需要较高的温度（>673 K）[10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551]。

2. 卤素存在下的氧化脱氢（X2 + O2）：
这种方法将卤素与氧气结合以促进脱氢过程。添加氧气可以将平衡移向更高的丙烷转化率，并使反应成为放热反应[10.1007/s10562-016-1701-1]。然而，由于丙烯连续氧化成含氧产物，丙烷氧化脱氢（ODH）报道的最高丙烯收率仅约为30%[10.1002/cctc.201200913]。

3. 通过卤化物的丙烷氧卤化（HX + O2）：
这种方法使用卤化氢和氧气将丙烷转化为丙烯。反应通过卤化氢与O2的氧化进行，生成烷基卤化物、烯烃和水。理论上，在O2存在的情况下可以达到100%的卤素原子效率，相比之下，卤素脱氢的效率为50%[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。

4. 熔融金属卤化物盐辅助的氧化脱氢（LiX + O2）：
这种方法使用熔融状态的金属碘化物，如LiI、CdI2、ZnI2和PbI2。反应发生在450-600°C之间，可以达到高达70%的丙烯收率[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。在一项研究中，使用5 wt.-%的LiOH*H2O/LiI盐熔体，在600°C下，丙烷/空气混合比为1:2，达到了80%的丙烷转化率和84%的丙烯选择性[10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S]。

5. 双卤素介导的脱氢：
这种新颖的方法同时使用Br2和I2作为介质。向碘化反应中添加少量溴显著提高了丙烷转化率和产物选择性。在800 K以下的适度温度下可以获得高达80%的丙烯和丙基卤化物收率[10.1021/acs.chemrev.6b00551]。

每种方法的反应条件各不相同，但通常涉及300-700°C的温度范围，某些方法需要更高的温度（碘基过程高达900 K）。氧气的存在、特定的催化剂和熔融盐熔体的使用是这些方法中的常见特征。

丙烷在这些过程中的反应性与甲烷和乙烷显著不同，这是由于其分子结构：

1. 丙烷同时具有伯碳原子和仲碳原子，使其比只有伯碳的甲烷或乙烷更具反应性[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。

2. 丙烯（产物）中存在反应性烯丙基氢原子可能导致连续氧化，与乙烷生成乙烯相比，这导致了较低的选择性[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_11]。

3. 丙烷可以形成正丙基和异丙基自由基，导致不同的反应途径并影响选择性[10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S]。

4. 丙烯中稳定双键的形成提供了甲烷脱氢中不存在且对乙烷不太有利的热力学驱动力[10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S]。

5. 在高自由基浓度下，丙烷可以进行偶联反应形成苯等更大的分子，这在甲烷中是不可能的，在乙烷中也不太可能发生[10.1016/0166-9834(91)80033-S]。

这些结构差异影响了C-H键活化的难易程度、脱氢过程中形成的中间体的稳定性以及整体反应途径，导致与短链烷烃相比产生不同的产物分布。

综合理解与未来展望：卤素辅助选择性丙烷脱氢方法的发展代表了烷烃活化领域的重大进步。这些方法为传统脱氢工艺提供了有前景的替代方案，有可能解决高能耗和催化剂失活等问题。

未来的研究方向可能包括：

1. 催化剂开发：设计新型催化剂，进一步提高选择性和转化率，同时最小化副反应和催化剂失活。

2. 过程强化：探索创新的反应器设计和工艺配置，以优化热管理和产品分离，可能导致更高效和经济的工业规模操作。

3. 机理研究：对各种卤素辅助方法的反应机理进行深入研究，以更好地理解影响选择性和反应性的因素。

4. 可持续性考虑：开发策略以最小化卤素消耗并提高卤素回收效率，解决与卤素使用相关的环境问题。

5. 扩展到其他烷烃：将从丙烷脱氢获得的见解应用于开发其他烷烃的类似工艺，可能为轻质烃的增值开辟新途径。

6. 与可再生能源的整合：探索将这些过程与可再生能源源相结合的潜力，创造更可持续的化学品生产路线。

通过解决这些领域的问题，研究人员可以进一步提高卤素辅助丙烷脱氢方法的效率和选择性，可能彻底改变丙烯和其他有价值的化学中间体的生产。
