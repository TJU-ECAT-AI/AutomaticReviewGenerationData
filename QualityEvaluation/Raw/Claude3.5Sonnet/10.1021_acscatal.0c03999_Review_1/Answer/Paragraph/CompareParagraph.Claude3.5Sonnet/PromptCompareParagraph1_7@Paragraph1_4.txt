The comprehensive evaluation of metal oxide catalysts for CO2-ODHP reveals a complex interplay of factors influencing catalytic performance, with particular emphasis on chromium-based catalysts. Among the various metal oxides studied, including Ga, Cr, V, Fe, Zr, and Zn, chromium oxide-based catalysts have consistently shown superior activity and selectivity [10.1016/j.apcata.2009.11.040]. The key factors influencing catalytic performance include:

1. Support material: SiO2-supported Cr2O3 catalysts demonstrate enhanced performance with CO2, while Al2O3-supported catalysts experience reduced activity [10.1016/S0920-5861(98)00245-4]. Mesoporous supports like MCM-41, SBA-15, and MSU-x provide high surface areas for better chromium dispersion [10.1016/j.jcou.2019.09.018].

2. Chromium loading: An optimal loading range, typically between 5-7 wt%, maximizes catalytic activity [10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017]. Beyond this range, activity decreases due to the formation of less active crystalline α-Cr2O3 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_1].

3. Oxidation state and species evolution: Initially, Cr6+ species in the form of mono- and polychromates are present. During the reaction, these are reduced to Cr3+ and Cr2+ species, which are believed to be the active sites for dehydrogenation [10.1016/j.molcata.2011.08.019]. The evolution follows the sequence: grafted Cr6+ → dispersed Cr6+ oxide → microcrystalline Cr3+ → crystalline α-Cr2O3 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_1].

4. Dispersion: Highly dispersed chromium species show better catalytic performance than bulk crystalline phases [10.1016/j.apcata.2008.05.037].

5. Redox properties: The ability of chromium to undergo redox cycling between different oxidation states is crucial for maintaining catalytic activity [10.1016/S0167-2991(04)80271-4].

6. CO2 interaction: CO2 plays multiple roles, including suppressing coke formation, enhancing propene yield by removing H2, and acting as an oxidant to maintain the active chromium oxidation state [10.1007/s10562-006-0077-z].

The evolution of chromium species in supported Cr2O3 catalysts is heavily influenced by loading and support properties. At low loadings, highly dispersed Cr6+ species dominate, while higher loadings lead to the formation of less active crystalline α-Cr2O3 [10.1002/cctc.201200397]. The support material significantly affects this evolution, with SiO2 supports generally allowing for better dispersion and stability of active chromium species compared to Al2O3 [10.1016/S0920-5861(98)00245-4].

Metal oxide catalysts for CO2-ODHP (oxidative dehydrogenation of propane) have been extensively studied, with chromium and gallium oxide-based catalysts emerging as the most promising due to their high activity, selectivity, and stability [10.1016/j.apcata.2009.11.040]. Among these, supported chromium oxide catalysts, particularly CrOx/SiO2, have shown exceptional performance [10.1002/cctc.201200397].

The catalytic performance of supported Cr2O3 catalysts is influenced by several key factors:

1. Chromium loading: Activity generally increases with Cr content up to an optimal level, typically around 5-7 wt%, beyond which it plateaus or declines [10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017] [10.1016/j.micromeso.2012.05.011].

2. Oxidation state and speciation of chromium: Initially, Cr6+ species in the form of mono- and polychromates are crucial for high catalytic activity. During the reaction, these are reduced to Cr3+ and Cr2+ species, which are believed to be the active sites for dehydrogenation [10.1016/j.molcata.2011.08.019] [10.1016/j.jcat.2019.02.026].

3. Dispersion of chromium species: Highly dispersed Cr species show better catalytic performance compared to crystalline α-Cr2O3 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_1].

4. Support properties: The nature of the support significantly affects catalyst performance. SiO2-supported catalysts generally show better activity with CO2 compared to Al2O3 or ZrO2 supported catalysts [10.1016/S0920-5861(98)00245-4] [10.1007/s10562-004-3749-6].

5. Preparation method: Techniques like metal vapor synthesis (MVS) or direct hydrothermal synthesis can produce catalysts with higher Cr dispersion and optimized activity [10.1016/j.cej.2010.11.070] [10.1016/S0167-2991(04)80271-4].

The evolution of chromium species in supported Cr2O3 catalysts as a function of loading typically follows this sequence: grafted Cr6+ → dispersed Cr6+ oxide → microcrystalline Cr3+ → crystalline α-Cr2O3 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_1]. This evolution significantly impacts catalytic performance:

1. At low loadings (&amp;lt;2-3 wt%), highly dispersed Cr6+ species dominate, which are believed to be precursors to the active sites [10.1006/jcat.1996.0023].

2. As loading increases, oligomeric Cr species form, which can be more active than isolated species, especially on certain supports like γ-Al2O3 [10.1021/cr5002436].

3. At higher loadings (typically >7-8 wt%), less active crystalline α-Cr2O3 starts to form, leading to decreased activity [10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017].

The presence of CO2 plays a crucial role in catalyst performance. For SiO2-supported Cr catalysts, CO2 enhances propene yield and suppresses catalyst deactivation by maintaining Cr in a partially oxidized state [10.1016/S0167-2991(98)80785-4]. The redox cycle between Cr6+ and Cr3+ species, facilitated by CO2, is key to maintaining catalyst activity [10.1016/j.jcat.2004.03.014].



Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The comprehensive evaluation of metal oxide catalysts for CO2-ODHP, particularly focusing on chromium-based catalysts, reveals the complex interplay between catalyst composition, structure, and reaction conditions. Future research directions may include:

1. Developing novel support materials or mixed metal oxides to further enhance chromium dispersion and stability.

2. Exploring advanced synthesis techniques to precisely control the distribution and oxidation state of chromium species.

3. Investigating the potential of bimetallic or multi-component catalysts to combine the beneficial properties of different metal oxides.

4. Utilizing in-situ characterization techniques to gain deeper insights into the dynamic evolution of active sites under reaction conditions.

5. Exploring the application of machine learning and high-throughput screening methods to accelerate the discovery and optimization of new catalyst formulations.

6. Investigating the potential of MOF-derived or single-atom catalysts to achieve ultimate metal utilization and activity.

These research directions could lead to the development of more efficient and stable catalysts for CO2-ODHP, potentially enabling more sustainable production of propylene and utilization of CO2 as a valuable feedstock.
