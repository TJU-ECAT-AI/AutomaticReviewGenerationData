Metal oxide catalysts for CO2-ODHP (oxidative dehydrogenation of propane) have been extensively studied, with chromium and gallium oxide-based catalysts emerging as the most promising due to their high activity, selectivity, and stability [10.1016/j.apcata.2009.11.040]. Among these, chromium oxide-based catalysts, particularly when supported on silica (SiO2), have shown exceptional performance.

Key factors influencing the catalytic performance of chromium-based catalysts include:

1. Chromium loading: An optimal loading range typically exists, often around 5-7 wt%, where catalytic activity reaches its peak [10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017]. For instance, Cr/MSU-x catalysts showed maximum propane conversion and propylene yield between 5-12 wt% Cr2O3 loading [10.1016/j.catcom.2006.08.005].

2. Oxidation state and speciation of chromium: Initially, Cr6+ species, present as mono- and polychromates, are believed to be precursors to the active sites. During the reaction, these are rapidly reduced to Cr3+ and Cr2+ species, which exhibit high catalytic performance [10.1016/j.molcata.2011.08.019]. The redox cycle between Cr(VI)O4 tetrahedra and Cr(III)O6 octahedra plays a crucial role in the dehydrogenation process [10.1016/S0167-2991(04)80271-4].

3. Dispersion of chromium species: Higher dispersion generally leads to better catalytic activity. Isolated and oligomeric Cr3+ species that are coordinatively unsaturated are considered the most active sites [10.1021/cr5002436].

4. Support properties: The nature of the support significantly affects catalyst performance. SiO2-supported chromium oxide catalysts show enhanced propene yield with CO2, while Al2O3-supported catalysts experience decreased yield and selectivity [10.1007/s10562-004-3749-6]. Mesoporous supports like MCM-41, SBA-15, and MSU-x provide high surface areas for better chromium dispersion [10.1016/j.jcou.2019.09.018].

5. Presence of CO2: CO2 plays multiple roles, including suppressing coke formation, enhancing propylene yield by removing H2, and acting as an oxidant to maintain the active Cr3+ oxidation state [10.1002/cctc.201200397].

The evolution of chromium species in supported Cr2O3 catalysts as a function of loading typically follows this sequence: grafted Cr6+ → dispersed Cr6+ oxide → microcrystalline Cr3+ → crystalline α-Cr2O3 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_1]. This evolution significantly impacts catalytic performance, with the optimal catalyst having a high dispersion of Cr3+ species without the formation of bulk Cr2O3.

Among these, chromium oxide-based catalysts supported on silica have shown particularly high performance [10.1002/cctc.201200397].

Key factors influencing catalytic performance of chromium-based catalysts include:

1. Chromium loading: An optimal loading range exists, typically between 5-7 wt%, beyond which activity may decrease [10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017] [10.1016/j.cej.2010.11.070].

2. Oxidation state and evolution of chromium species: Initially, Cr6+ species are crucial for high catalytic activity. During the reaction, these are reduced to Cr3+ and Cr2+ species, which are the main active sites [10.1007/s10562-017-2282-3]. The redox cycle between Cr6+ and Cr3+ plays a vital role in maintaining catalyst activity [10.1016/S0167-2991(04)80271-4].

3. Dispersion of chromium species: Highly dispersed chromium species lead to better catalytic performance. As loading increases, the evolution progresses from isolated species to oligomeric forms, and finally to crystalline α-Cr2O3, which is less active [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_1].

4. Support properties: Silica-based supports, particularly mesoporous materials like MCM-41, SBA-15, and MSU-x, provide high surface areas for better chromium dispersion [10.1016/j.jcou.2019.09.018]. The nature of the support significantly affects the catalyst's interaction with CO2, with SiO2 supports showing a promoting effect while Al2O3 supports exhibit an inhibiting effect [10.1016/S0920-5861(98)00245-4].

5. Preparation method: Techniques like metal vapor synthesis (MVS) can produce catalysts with higher Cr dispersion, optimizing activity per Cr atom [10.1016/j.cej.2010.11.070].

6. Presence of CO2: CO2 plays multiple roles, including suppressing coke formation, enhancing propylene yield by removing H2, and acting as an oxidant to maintain the active state of chromium species [10.1007/s10562-006-0077-z].

The evolution of chromium species in supported Cr2O3 catalysts as a function of loading typically follows this sequence: grafted Cr6+ → dispersed Cr6+ oxide → microcrystalline Cr3+ → crystalline α-Cr2O3 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999\_1]. This evolution significantly impacts the catalyst's activity and selectivity in CO2-ODHP reactions.



Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The comprehensive evaluation of metal oxide catalysts for CO2-ODHP, particularly chromium-based catalysts, reveals the complex interplay between various factors affecting catalytic performance. Future research directions may focus on:

1. Developing novel support materials or modification techniques to enhance chromium dispersion and stability, potentially exploring hybrid or composite supports that combine the advantages of different materials.

2. Investigating the use of promoters or dopants to fine-tune the redox properties of chromium species and enhance their resistance to deactivation.

3. Exploring in-situ characterization techniques to better understand the dynamic evolution of chromium species under reaction conditions, which could lead to more precise catalyst design.

4. Investigating the potential of bimetallic or multi-component catalysts that combine the advantages of different active metals, such as chromium-gallium systems, to achieve synergistic effects in activity and selectivity.

5. Developing strategies for catalyst regeneration and long-term stability, which are crucial for industrial applications of CO2-ODHP processes.

6. Exploring the integration of CO2-ODHP with other processes, such as CO2 capture or utilization, to create more sustainable and economically viable technologies for propylene production.

These research directions could lead to the development of more efficient and stable catalysts for CO2-ODHP, contributing to the broader goal of sustainable chemical production and CO2 utilization.
