The reverse Horiuti-Polanyi mechanism for propane dehydrogenation (PDH) consists of three main steps:

1. Dissociative adsorption: Propane molecules adsorb onto the catalyst surface and dissociate into adsorbed propyl and hydrogen species.

2. β-H extraction: A hydrogen atom is extracted from the β-carbon of the adsorbed propyl species, forming an adsorbed propylene intermediate.

3. Desorption: The adsorbed propylene and hydrogen desorb from the catalyst surface as products.

There is an ongoing debate in the scientific community regarding which step is the rate-controlling step in the PDH reaction. Some studies suggest that the dissociative adsorption of propane is the rate-limiting step [10.1016/j.fuproc.2016.04.016], while others argue that the β-H extraction step is rate-determining [10.1016/0021-9517(77)90010-0]. This uncertainty is further complicated by the fact that the rate-determining step may depend on various factors such as catalyst surface, cluster size, and reaction conditions [10.1039/D0CS01262A].

The ongoing debate impacts our understanding of the PDH process in several ways:

1. Catalyst design: If dissociative adsorption is rate-limiting, catalyst development should focus on improving the initial C-H bond activation and propane adsorption. If β-H extraction is rate-limiting, efforts should concentrate on facilitating hydrogen removal from the adsorbed alkyl species [10.1016/j.fuproc.2016.04.016].

2. Reaction conditions: The optimal temperature, pressure, and reactant concentrations may differ depending on which step controls the rate [10.1039/D0CS01262A].

3. Kinetic modeling: Accurate kinetic models are crucial for process optimization and scale-up. Uncertainty about the rate-limiting step complicates the development of reliable models [10.1016/j.fuproc.2016.04.016].

4. Mechanistic understanding: The ambiguity in the rate-determining step hinders our ability to fully elucidate the reaction mechanism and identify potential intermediates or transition states [10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_1].

5. Process optimization: Industrial PDH processes could be more effectively optimized if the rate-determining step was definitively known [10.1016/j.fuproc.2016.04.016].

6. Catalyst deactivation: Understanding the rate-limiting step could provide insights into the primary causes of catalyst deactivation, affecting strategies for catalyst regeneration and longevity [10.1016/j.fuproc.2016.04.016].

Recent studies have added complexity to the debate.
