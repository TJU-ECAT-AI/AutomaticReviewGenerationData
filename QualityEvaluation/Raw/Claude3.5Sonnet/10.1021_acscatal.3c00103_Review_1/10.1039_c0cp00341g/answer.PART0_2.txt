The reverse Horiuti-Polanyi mechanism for propane dehydrogenation (PDH) consists of three steps:

1. Dissociative adsorption: Propane adsorbs onto the catalyst surface and dissociates into propyl and hydrogen species.

2. β-H extraction: A hydrogen atom is removed from the β-carbon of the adsorbed propyl species, forming an adsorbed propylene molecule.

3. Desorption: The propylene molecule desorbs from the catalyst surface.

There is ongoing debate about whether the dissociative adsorption or the β-H extraction is the rate-controlling step in PDH. This uncertainty impacts our understanding of the PDH process in several ways:

1. Catalyst design: If dissociative adsorption is rate-controlling, catalyst development should focus on improving propane activation. If β-H extraction is rate-limiting, efforts should prioritize facilitating hydrogen removal.

2. Reaction conditions: The rate-controlling step influences optimal temperature and pressure conditions for the reaction.

3. Kinetic modeling: Accurate kinetic models require correct identification of the rate-limiting step.

4. Mechanistic understanding: The rate-determining step provides insights into the reaction's molecular-level processes.

5. Process optimization: Industrial PDH processes can be better optimized with a clear understanding of the rate-limiting step.

6. Selectivity control: Knowledge of the rate-determining step can help in developing strategies to improve propylene selectivity.
