Bimetallic catalysts have evolved significantly in heterogeneous catalysis since their commercial introduction in the 1960s, primarily for hydrocarbon reforming in the petroleum industry [10.1021/cr300096b_0]. The evolution of bimetallic catalysts has been driven by their unique electronic and chemical properties, which contribute to bridging the "materials gap" and "pressure gap" between model surfaces and industrially relevant supported catalysts.

Key aspects of this evolution include:

1. Unique Properties: Bimetallic catalysts exhibit novel properties not present in their parent metals, offering enhanced selectivity, activity, and stability [10.1021/cr300096b_0]. These properties arise from synergistic effects between the two metals, particularly in alloyed and core-shell nanoparticle structures [10.1039/c1jm12020d].

2. Tunable Characteristics: The chemical and physical properties of bimetallic catalysts can be finely tuned by adjusting size, composition, and atomic ordering [10.1039/c1jm12020d]. This tunability allows researchers to optimize catalytic performance for specific reactions.

3. Enhanced Binding Properties: Bimetallic catalysts demonstrate distinct binding properties with reactants compared to monometallic catalysts. The strong metal-metal interactions modify bonding between catalyst surfaces and reactants, often leading to improved catalytic performance [10.1039/c1jm12020d].

4. Advanced Structures: The development of core-shell nanoparticles represents a significant advancement in bimetallic catalyst design. These structures offer unique advantages due to their dependence on the core, shell, and interface properties [10.1039/c1jm12020d].

5. Bridging the Materials Gap: Research has focused on correlating model bimetallic surfaces with industrially relevant supported catalysts. This involves studying the formation and properties of bimetallic structures on both single-crystal substrates and polycrystalline materials [10.1016/j.surfrep.2008.02.001].

6. Addressing the Pressure Gap: Efforts have been made to investigate the stability of bimetallic structures under reaction conditions, moving beyond ultra-high vacuum (UHV) studies to more realistic environments [10.1016/j.surfrep.2008.02.001].

7. Advanced Characterization Techniques: The use of in-situ and ex-situ surface-sensitive probes, such as EXAFS, XANES, and SXS, has enabled detailed structural and electronic characterization of bimetallic catalysts under various conditions [10.126/science.1135941] [10.1002/aic.690310823].

8. Theoretical Modeling: Integration of density functional theory (DFT) calculations and microkinetic modeling with experimental approaches has become crucial for identifying and screening desirable bimetallic systems [10.1038/nchem.626].

9. Practical Applications: Bimetallic catalysts have shown promise in various applications, including fuel cell electrocatalysts, where Au-Pt systems have demonstrated improved stability against dissolution under potential cycling regimes [10.1126/science.1134569].

These advancements have significantly contributed to bridging the gaps between model surfaces and industrially relevant supported catalysts. By providing a more realistic representation of catalytic systems while maintaining control over structure and composition, bimetallic catalysts enable researchers to study catalytic processes under more realistic conditions and develop improved catalysts for practical applications.

※※※

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The evolution of bimetallic catalysts represents a significant advancement in heterogeneous catalysis, offering unique opportunities to bridge fundamental research and industrial applications. Looking forward, several key areas emerge as promising directions for future research and development:

1. Rational Design: The combination of advanced computational methods with high-throughput experimental techniques could lead to more efficient discovery and optimization of bimetallic catalysts for specific reactions.

2. In-situ Characterization: Further development of in-situ and operando characterization techniques could provide deeper insights into the dynamic behavior of bimetallic catalysts under reaction conditions, helping to close the pressure gap.

3. Nano-engineering: Advances in nanoparticle synthesis and atomic-scale control could enable the creation of more complex and precisely tailored bimetallic structures, potentially mimicking the properties of highly active model surfaces in practical catalysts.

4. Multi-metallic Systems: Expanding beyond bimetallic to tri- or multi-metallic systems could open up new possibilities for tuning catalytic properties and achieving unprecedented performance.

5. Integration with Emerging Technologies: Combining bimetallic catalysts with other advanced materials or technologies, such as metal-organic frameworks or artificial intelligence-driven design, could lead to innovative catalytic systems with superior performance and stability.

These future directions could potentially revolutionize heterogeneous catalysis, leading to more efficient and sustainable chemical processes across various industries.

这一演变的关键方面包括：

1. 独特性质：双金属催化剂展现出其父金属所不具备的新颖性质，提供增强的选择性、活性和稳定性 [10.1021/cr300096b_0]。这些性质源于两种金属之间的协同效应，特别是在合金和核壳纳米粒子结构中 [10.1039/c1jm12020d]。

2. 可调特性：通过调整尺寸、组成和原子排列，可以精细调节双金属催化剂的化学和物理性质 [10.1039/c1jm12020d]。这种可调性使研究人员能够针对特定反应优化催化性能。

3. 增强的结合性质：与单金属催化剂相比，双金属催化剂与反应物表现出不同的结合性质。强烈的金属-金属相互作用改变了催化剂表面与反应物之间的键合，通常导致催化性能的改善 [10.1039/c1jm12020d]。

4. 先进结构：核壳纳米粒子的开发代表了双金属催化剂设计的重大进步。这些结构由于其对核心、壳层和界面性质的依赖性而提供独特的优势 [10.1039/c1jm12020d]。

5. 弥合材料差距：研究重点放在将模型双金属表面与工业相关支持催化剂相关联上。这涉及研究双金属结构在单晶基底和多晶材料上的形成和性质 [10.1016/j.surfrep.2008.02.001]。

6. 解决压力差距：努力研究双金属结构在反应条件下的稳定性，从超高真空（UHV）研究转向更真实的环境 [10.1016/j.surfrep.2008.02.001]。

7. 先进表征技术：使用原位和非原位表面敏感探针（如EXAFS、XANES和SXS）能够在各种条件下对双金属催化剂进行详细的结构和电子表征 [10.126/science.1135941] [10.1002/aic.690310823]。

8. 理论建模：将密度泛函理论（DFT）计算和微观动力学建模与实验方法相结合，对于识别和筛选理想的双金属系统变得至关重要 [10.1038/nchem.626]。

9. 实际应用：双金属催化剂在各种应用中显示出前景，包括燃料电池电催化剂，其中Au-Pt系统在电位循环条件下表现出改善的抗溶解稳定性 [10.1126/science.1134569]。

这些进展显著促进了模型表面和工业相关支持催化剂之间差距的弥合。通过提供更真实的催化系统表现，同时保持对结构和组成的控制，双金属催化剂使研究人员能够在更真实的条件下研究催化过程，并开发出改进的实际应用催化剂。
