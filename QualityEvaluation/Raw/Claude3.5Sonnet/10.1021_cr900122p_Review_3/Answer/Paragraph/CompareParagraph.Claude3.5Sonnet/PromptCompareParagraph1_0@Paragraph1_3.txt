Catalytic Mechanisms and Reactivity of Surface-Supported Metal Species in C-H Bond Activation, H/D Exchange, and Alkane Metathesis Reactions

Surface-supported metal hydrides, particularly those of early transition metals and actinides, have shown remarkable activity in C-H bond activation, H/D exchange, and alkane metathesis reactions. This review focuses on silica-supported zirconium hydrides and alumina-supported thorium hydrides, comparing their preparation, structure, and catalytic properties.

Preparation and Structure:
Silica-supported zirconium hydrides are typically prepared by grafting organozirconium compounds (e.g., tetraneopentylzirconium) onto partially dehydroxylated silica, followed by treatment under hydrogen [10.1016/j.molcata.2003.11.028]. This process results in a mixture of surface species, primarily (≡SiO)3ZrH and (≡SiO)2ZrH2, with a small amount of Zr(III) species [10.1007/s11244-005-9259-y]. In contrast, alumina-supported thorium hydrides are generated by treating Th(η5-allyl)4 supported on dehydroxylated γ-alumina with hydrogen [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8].

Catalytic Properties in C-H Bond Activation:
Both zirconium and thorium hydrides demonstrate high activity in C-H bond activation. Silica-supported zirconium hydrides can activate alkanes at 25°C and methane at 120°C, forming zirconium-alkyls [10.1039/c39950000991]. They also catalyze low-temperature hydrogenolysis of various alkanes. Alumina-supported thorium hydrides show high activity for arene hydrogenation and alkane C-H activation at 90°C and 30-180 psi hydrogen pressure [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8].

H/D Exchange Mechanisms:
Zirconium hydrides catalyze H/D exchange in H2/D2 or CH4/D2 mixtures via a stepwise insertion of deuterium. The reaction is exceptionally fast, even below room temperature. The mechanism involves a two-step process: CH bond activation via σ-bond metathesis, forming a metal-alkyl intermediate, followed by hydrogenolysis, which regenerates the metal hydride and incorporates deuterium in the alkane [10.1021/cr900122p\_3]. The activation energy for CH4/D2 exchange is low (7 ± 1 kcal/mol) with a large negative entropy of activation (-27 ± 3 eu), consistent with a σ-bond metathesis pathway [10.1021/om0007926].

Thorium hydrides catalyze H/D exchange in D2/alkane mixtures with fast reaction rates (800-1200 h^-1). The reaction shows slight selectivity for terminal CH bonds. Interestingly, dimethylcyclohexanes undergo cis-trans isomerization during the H/D exchange process, likely via β-H transfer and a chain walking mechanism [10.1021/cr900122p\_3].

Alkane Metathesis:
While zirconium hydrides are not reported to catalyze alkane metathesis, silica-supported tantalum hydrides [(≡SiO)2TaH] have been shown to catalyze this reaction. The mechanism may involve a four-centered σ-bond metathesis transition state, an oxidative addition/reductive elimination pathway, or a carbene mechanism [10.1016/j.molcata.2003.11.028].

Reaction Rates and Selectivity:
Zirconium hydrides exhibit extremely fast H2/D2 exchange, even at liquid nitrogen temperatures, with an estimated upper bound for activation energy of about 2 kcal/mol [10.1021/om0007926]. Thorium hydrides show high rates for benzene reduction (turnover frequency ~25,000 h^-1 per active site) and cyclohexane C-H exchange with D2 (~1,300 h^-1 per active site) [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8].

In terms of selectivity, thorium hydrides show a preference for primary over secondary and tertiary C-H bonds, and for sterically less hindered over more hindered positions [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8]. Zirconium hydride systems generally show low regioselectivity in catalytic processes [10.1007/s11244-005-9259-y].

Catalytic mechanisms and reactivity of surface-supported metal species in C-H bond activation, H/D exchange, and alkane metathesis reactions

Silica-supported zirconium hydrides and alumina-supported thorium hydrides are both active catalysts for C-H bond activation and H/D exchange reactions, but they exhibit different properties and mechanisms.

Preparation and Structure:
Silica-supported zirconium hydrides are typically prepared by grafting organozirconium compounds (e.g., ZrR4, where R can be allyl, benzyl, or neopentyl) onto partially dehydroxylated silica, followed by treatment under hydrogen at elevated temperatures (e.g., 423 K) [10.1007/s11244-005-9259-y]. This results in a mixture of surface species, including Zr(IV) monohydrides (≡SiO)3ZrH, Zr(IV) dihydrides (≡SiO)2ZrH2, and a small amount (up to 5%) of Zr(III) hydrides (≡SiO)2ZrIIIH [10.1007/s11244-005-9259-y].

Alumina-supported thorium hydrides are generated by adsorbing Th(η5-allyl)4 on dehydroxylated γ-alumina (DA), followed by treatment under hydrogen [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8]. Only about 8±1% of the Th surface sites are catalytically active, with the active sites being electrophilic and bearing sterically significant, nonhydridic ancillary ligation [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8].

Catalytic Properties:
Both catalysts are active in C-H bond activation and H/D exchange reactions, but they exhibit different reactivity patterns.

Zirconium hydrides catalyze H/D exchange in H2/D2 or CH4/D2 mixtures via a stepwise insertion of deuterium [10.1021/cr900122p\_3]. The reaction is extremely fast, even at temperatures below room temperature, with H2/D2 exchange being instantaneous even at liquid nitrogen temperatures [10.1021/om0007926]. The activation energy for H2/D2 exchange is estimated to be about 2 kcal/mol, while CH4/D2 exchange has an activation energy of 7 ± 1 kcal/mol [10.1021/om0007926].

Thorium hydrides catalyze H/D exchange in D2/alkane mixtures with fast reaction rates of 800-1200 h^-1 [10.1021/cr900122p\_3]. They show slight selectivity for terminal C-H bonds, but overall, the incorporation of deuterium is not selective [10.1021/cr900122p\_3].

Mechanisms:
The mechanism for H/D exchange on zirconium hydrides involves a two-step process: (1) C-H bond activation via σ-bond metathesis, forming a metal-alkyl intermediate, followed by (2) hydrogenolysis, which regenerates the metal hydride and incorporates deuterium in the alkane [10.1021/cr900122p\_3]. This mechanism is supported by the large negative entropy of activation (-27 ± 3 eu) observed for CH4/D2 exchange, consistent with a constrained transition state [10.1021/om0007926].

For thorium hydrides, the mechanism involves electrophilic four-center activation processes [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8]. The H/D exchange process includes endothermic Th-C bond formation and HD elimination via a four-center, heterolytic σ-bond metathesis pathway, followed by deuterolysis [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8].

Selectivity and Chain Walking:
Zirconium hydrides show low regioselectivity in catalytic processes [10.1007/s11244-005-9259-y]. In contrast, thorium hydrides exhibit a unique selectivity pattern that does not parallel C-H bond dissociation energies: primary > secondary > tertiary, and sterically less hindered > sterically more hindered [10.1016/0304-5102(93)e0210-8].

Chain walking is observed in the thorium hydride system, particularly during the H/D exchange of dimethylcyclohexanes, which undergo cis-trans isomerization via β-H transfer and a chain walking mechanism [10.1021/cr900122p\_3].

Alkane Metathesis:
While both catalyst systems are active in C-H bond activation and H/D exchange, alkane metathesis is primarily observed with silica-supported tantalum hydrides rather than zirconium or thorium hydrides [10.1016/j.molcata.2003.11.028]. The mechanism for alkane metathesis may involve a four-centered σ-bond metathesis transition state, an oxidative addition/reductive elimination pathway, or a carbene mechanism [10.1016/j.molcata.2003.11.028].



Integrative understanding and prospective outlook:
The comparison of silica-supported zirconium hydrides and alumina-supported thorium hydrides reveals the complexity and diversity of surface-supported metal species in C-H bond activation and H/D exchange reactions. While both systems demonstrate high activity, they differ in their preparation methods, active site structures, reaction mechanisms, and selectivity patterns.

By addressing these research directions, we can further our understanding of surface-supported metal species and develop more efficient and selective catalysts for C-H bond activation, H/D exchange, and related reactions.
