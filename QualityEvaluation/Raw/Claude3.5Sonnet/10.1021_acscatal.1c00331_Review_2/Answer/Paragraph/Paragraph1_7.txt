Hydrogen cofeeding in the propane dehydrogenation (PDH) process significantly affects coke formation, catalyst stability, and reaction mechanisms in several ways:

1. Coke Formation Suppression:
Hydrogen cofeeding effectively reduces coke formation on catalysts. Higher hydrogen content in the feed leads to less coke accumulation, with studies showing a decrease from 3.80 wt% to 1.44 wt% when hydrogen content increased from 1.9% to 13.6% [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]. This suppression occurs through various mechanisms, including hydrogenation of coke precursor species and competition for active sites on the catalyst surface [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].

2. Catalyst Stability Enhancement:
By reducing coke formation, hydrogen cofeeding improves catalyst stability and longevity. Catalysts like Pt/Mg(Ga)(Al)O exhibit lower deactivation rates compared to conventional bimetallic catalysts when hydrogen is present in the feed [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. The improved stability is particularly noticeable over extended operation periods, with some studies reporting excellent stability for over 120 hours at high temperatures [10.1038/s41467-018-06967-8].

3. Reaction Mechanism Modification:
Hydrogen influences the reaction pathways in PDH. It enhances the rate of propene formation through hydrogen-assisted dehydrogenation of surface alkyl species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. Additionally, hydrogen adsorption on the catalyst surface increases the energy barrier for propylene dehydrogenation, preventing deep reactions that can lead to coke formation [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_2].

4. Selectivity Improvement:
Hydrogen cofeeding generally improves selectivity towards propylene. This effect becomes more pronounced after initial cycles, with hydrogen increasing both propane conversion and propene selectivity [10.1039/c3cp50646k]. The improved selectivity is attributed to the suppression of side reactions and the promotion of alkene desorption from the catalyst surface.

5. Coke Characteristics and Distribution:
While hydrogen reduces overall coke formation, it also affects the nature of the coke produced. At higher hydrogen partial pressures, the coke becomes less dense and compact, and can be further softened through hydrogenation [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]. Interestingly, hydrogen promotes the formation of more graphitic coke structures, which primarily cover the catalyst support rather than the active metal sites [10.1039/c3cp50646k].

6. Acid Site Conversions:
In a hydrogen atmosphere, the acidity of the catalyst support is weakened by the conversion of Brønsted acid sites to Lewis acid sites. This conversion helps reduce the rate of coking on the support [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_2].

7. Process Efficiency and Performance:
Hydrogen cofeeding improves overall process efficiency by maintaining catalyst activity for longer periods and reducing the frequency of regeneration cycles. However, it's important to note that excessive hydrogen can lead to alkene hydrogenation, reducing the net dehydrogenation rate [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. Therefore, optimizing the H2/C3H8 ratio is crucial for maximizing process efficiency.

※※※
Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The comprehensive effects of hydrogen cofeeding in PDH processes reveal a complex interplay between reaction kinetics, catalyst surface chemistry, and process parameters. While hydrogen clearly offers significant benefits in terms of coke suppression and catalyst longevity, its implementation requires careful optimization to balance these advantages against potential drawbacks such as reduced equilibrium conversion.

Future research directions could focus on:
1. Developing advanced in-situ characterization techniques to better understand the real-time effects of hydrogen on catalyst surfaces and reaction intermediates.
2. Exploring novel catalyst designs that can synergistically interact with hydrogen to further enhance selectivity and stability.
3. Investigating the potential of dynamic hydrogen feeding strategies that adapt to changing reaction conditions and catalyst states over time.
4. Integrating machine learning approaches to optimize hydrogen cofeeding parameters in real-time, considering multiple process variables and outcomes.
5. Examining the long-term effects of hydrogen cofeeding on catalyst microstructure and performance over multiple regeneration cycles.

These research avenues could lead to more efficient and sustainable PDH processes, potentially revolutionizing propylene production in the petrochemical industry.

氢气共进料在丙烷脱氢（PDH）过程中对焦炭形成、催化剂稳定性和反应机理有显著影响，主要表现在以下几个方面：

1. 抑制焦炭形成：
氢气共进料有效减少了催化剂上的焦炭形成。馈料中氢气含量越高，焦炭积累越少，研究表明当氢气含量从1.9%增加到13.6%时，焦炭含量从3.80 wt%降低到1.44 wt% [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]。这种抑制作用通过多种机制实现，包括焦炭前体物的氢化和与催化剂表面活性位点的竞争 [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]。

2. 提高催化剂稳定性：
通过减少焦炭形成，氢气共进料提高了催化剂的稳定性和使用寿命。当馈料中存在氢气时，像Pt/Mg(Ga)(Al)O这样的催化剂比传统的双金属催化剂表现出更低的失活率 [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]。这种改进的稳定性在长期运行中尤为明显，一些研究报告在高温下运行超过120小时仍保持优异的稳定性 [10.1038/s41467-018-06967-8]。

3. 改变反应机理：
氢气影响PDH过程中的反应路径。它通过氢辅助表面烷基物种脱氢来增强丙烯的生成率 [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]。此外，氢气在催化剂表面的吸附增加了丙烯脱氢的能量障碍，防止导致焦炭形成的深度反应 [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_2]。

4. 提高选择性：
氢气共进料通常提高了对丙烯的选择性。这种效果在初始周期后变得更加明显，氢气增加了丙烷转化率和丙烯选择性 [10.1039/c3cp50646k]。选择性的提高归因于副反应的抑制和烯烃从催化剂表面脱附的促进。

5. 焦炭特性和分布：
虽然氢气减少了总体焦炭形成，但它也影响了所产生焦炭的性质。在较高的氢分压下，焦炭变得不那么致密和紧实，并且可以通过进一步氢化而软化 [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]。有趣的是，氢气促进了更多石墨化焦炭结构的形成，这些结构主要覆盖在催化剂载体上，而不是活性金属位点上 [10.1039/c3cp50646k]。

6. 酸性位点转化：
在氢气氛围中，催化剂载体的酸性通过Brønsted酸位点向Lewis酸位点的转化而减弱。这种转化有助于减少载体上的焦炭生成率 [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_2]。

7. 过程效率和性能：
氢气共进料通过延长催化剂活性维持时间和减少再生周期频率来提高整体过程效率。然而，需要注意的是，过量的氢气可能导致烯烃加氢，降低净脱氢率 [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]。因此，优化H2/C3H8比例对于最大化过程效率至关重要。

※※※
综合理解与未来展望：PDH过程中氢气共进料的综合效应揭示了反应动力学、催化剂表面化学和过程参数之间复杂的相互作用。虽然氢气在抑制焦炭形成和延长催化剂寿命方面明显提供了显著的优势，但其实施需要仔细优化，以平衡这些优势与潜在的缺点，如平衡转化率的降低。

未来研究方向可以集中在：
1. 开发先进的原位表征技术，以更好地理解氢气对催化剂表面和反应中间体的实时影响。
2. 探索能与氢气协同作用的新型催化剂设计，进一步提高选择性和稳定性。
3. 研究动态氢气进料策略的潜力，使其能够适应随时间变化的反应条件和催化剂状态。
4. 整合机器学习方法，考虑多个过程变量和结果，实时优化氢气共进料参数。
5. 检查氢气共进料对催化剂微观结构和性能在多个再生周期中的长期影响。

这些研究方向可能导致更高效、更可持续的PDH过程，有潜力革新石油化工行业的丙烯生产。
