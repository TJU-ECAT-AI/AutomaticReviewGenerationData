1. Coke Formation:
Hydrogen cofeeding significantly reduces coke formation on catalysts. This effect is achieved through several mechanisms:
a) Suppression of coke precursor formation [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361].
b) Hydrogenation of coke precursor species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].
c) Weakening of support acidity by converting Brønsted acid sites to Lewis acid sites, which reduces coke formation rate on the support [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8].
d) Inhibition of aromatic coke formation, which is typically harder to remove [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8].

However, it's important to note that while hydrogen reduces overall coke formation, it also leads to more graphitic coke structures, which can be harder to remove during regeneration [10.1039/c3cp50646k].

2. Catalyst Stability:
Hydrogen cofeeding enhances catalyst stability through several mechanisms:
a) Reduced coke formation leads to slower deactivation rates and longer catalyst lifetimes [10.1038/s41929-020-0472-7].
b) Hydrogen helps maintain catalyst activity over time [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361].
c) It keeps the surface of Pt nanoparticles free of disordered coke species [10.1039/c3cp50646k].

3. Reaction Mechanisms and Selectivity:
Hydrogen influences reaction pathways and product selectivity:
a) It enhances the rate of propene formation through hydrogen-assisted dehydrogenation of surface alkyl species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].
b) Hydrogen increases the energy barrier for propylene dehydrogenation, preventing deep reactions that lead to coke formation [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2].
c) It promotes alkene desorption relative to further dehydrogenation, improving selectivity [10.1039/c3cp53796j].

4. Energy Barriers:
While not always explicitly stated, hydrogen cofeeding affects energy barriers in the reaction:
a) It increases the activation energy for propylene dehydrogenation [10.1039/c5cy00230c].
b) The energy barrier for the third (undesired) C-H cleavage step is raised more significantly than for the first two steps, enhancing selectivity [10.1039/c3cp53796j].

Hydrogen cofeeding substantially reduces coke formation on catalysts [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]. The addition of hydrogen to the feed suppresses coke formation, with higher hydrogen content leading to less coke accumulation [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]. This reduction is primarily due to the hydrogenation of coke precursor species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. The effect is more pronounced on the catalyst support than on the metal component [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2].

2. Catalyst Stability:
Hydrogen cofeeding improves catalyst stability by reducing coke formation and deactivation rates [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. It helps maintain catalyst activity over time, leading to longer operational periods before regeneration is required [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361]. However, while hydrogen is effective at preventing coke formation, it cannot remove coke that has already formed on the catalyst [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361].

3. Reaction Mechanisms:
Hydrogen enhances the rate of propene formation through hydrogen-assisted dehydrogenation of surface alkyl species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. It also promotes alkene desorption by weakening its chemisorption on the catalyst surface [10.1039/c0cp00341g]. The presence of hydrogen shifts the third C-H cleavage step to a higher energy value, making it less competitive compared to the preferred alkene desorption step [10.1039/c5cy00230c].

4. Energy Barriers:
Hydrogen cofeeding increases the activation energy for propylene dehydrogenation [10.1039/c5cy00230c]. This increase in energy barriers for further dehydrogenation steps helps improve selectivity towards the desired propylene product [10.1039/c3cp53796j].

5. Coke Accumulation Patterns:
Hydrogen changes the pattern of coke accumulation, promoting the formation of graphitic coke on the catalyst support rather than disordered coke on active metal sites [10.1039/c3cp50646k]. At higher hydrogen partial pressures, the coke becomes less dense and compact [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8].

6. Acid Site Conversions:
In a hydrogen atmosphere, the acidity of the support is weakened by the conversion of Lewis acid sites to Brønsted acid sites [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2]. This conversion helps reduce the rate of coking on the support.

7. Graphitization:
The graphitization of coke is positively correlated with hydrogen partial pressure [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2]. This means that while hydrogen reduces overall coke formation, it also makes the formed coke harder to remove due to its more ordered graphitic structure.

These effects have significant implications for process efficiency and performance:

1. Improved catalyst longevity due to reduced coking and deactivation [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].
2. Higher propene yields, especially after initial cycles [10.1039/c3cp50646k].
3. Potential for longer operation times between regeneration cycles [10.1039/c3cp50646k].
4. More efficient use of the catalyst surface, as hydrogen helps keep active metal sites free from coke [10.1039/c3cp50646k].
5. Need for optimization to balance coke prevention with reaction rate [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361].
6. Trade-off between improved stability and reduced equilibrium conversion [10.1038/s41929-020-0472-7].
