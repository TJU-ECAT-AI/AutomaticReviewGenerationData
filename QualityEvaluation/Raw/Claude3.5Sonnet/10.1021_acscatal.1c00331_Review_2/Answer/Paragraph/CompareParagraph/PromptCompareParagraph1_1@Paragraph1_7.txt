Hydrogen cofeeding generally suppresses coke formation on catalysts. As hydrogen content in the feed increases, the total amount of coke decreases significantly [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]. This reduction is attributed to hydrogen's ability to remove coke precursors and hydrogenate coke-forming species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. The effect is more pronounced on the catalyst support than on the metal components [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2].

However, the relationship between hydrogen and coke formation is not straightforward. While hydrogen reduces overall coke formation, it also promotes the formation of more graphitic coke, which is harder to remove due to its higher combustion temperature [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2]. This graphitic coke tends to accumulate mainly on the catalyst support rather than on active metal sites [10.1039/c3cp50646k].

2. Catalyst Stability:
Hydrogen cofeeding significantly enhances catalyst stability by reducing coke formation and maintaining catalyst activity over time [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361]. It helps keep the surface of metal nanoparticles free from disordered coke species, which are more detrimental to catalyst performance [10.1039/c3cp50646k]. This results in slower deactivation rates and longer catalyst lifetimes [10.1038/s41929-020-0472-7].

The improved stability is particularly notable for certain catalyst compositions. For instance, Pt/Mg(Ga)(Al)O catalysts exhibit lower deactivation and coke formation rates compared to conventional bimetallic catalysts when hydrogen is cofed [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].

This mechanism involves the dissociative adsorption of propane, followed by H-atom-assisted removal of hydrogen from the adsorbed alkyl species.

This effect becomes more pronounced after initial cycles, with hydrogen increasing both propane conversion and propene selectivity [10.1039/c3cp50646k]. The improved selectivity is attributed to the suppression of side reactions and the promotion of alkene desorption from the catalyst surface.
