Hydrogen cofeeding in the propane dehydrogenation (PDH) process significantly affects coke formation, catalyst stability, and reaction mechanisms. This review will analyze these effects and their implications for process efficiency and performance.

1. Coke Formation:
Hydrogen cofeeding substantially reduces coke formation on catalysts. At higher hydrogen partial pressures, the total amount of coke decreases significantly [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]. This reduction is attributed to hydrogen's ability to remove coke precursors and suppress their formation [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2]. The adsorption of H2 is crucial in reducing coke formation, particularly during propane dehydrogenation compared to ethane dehydrogenation [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].

2. Catalyst Stability:
Hydrogen cofeeding enhances catalyst stability by reducing coke formation and deactivation rates. Catalysts like Pt/Mg(Ga)(Al)O exhibit lower deactivation rates compared to conventional bimetallic catalysts in the presence of hydrogen [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. The improved stability leads to longer catalyst lifetimes and extended periods between regeneration cycles [10.1038/s41929-020-0472-7].

3. Reaction Mechanisms:
Hydrogen affects reaction mechanisms in several ways:
a) It enhances propene formation through hydrogen-assisted dehydrogenation of surface alkyl species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].
b) The presence of adsorbed H atoms increases the activation energy for propylene dehydrogenation, making it less competitive compared to alkene desorption [10.1039/c5cy00230c].
c) Hydrogen promotes alkene desorption by weakening its chemisorption on the catalyst surface [10.1039/c0cp00341g].

4. Energy Barriers:
While not explicitly stated in all studies, the enhanced dehydrogenation rate with hydrogen addition suggests a lowering of energy barriers for the dehydrogenation reaction [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. However, hydrogen cofeeding also increases activation energies for certain steps in the reaction mechanism, which can improve selectivity towards alkene formation [10.1039/c3cp53796j].

5. Coke Accumulation Patterns:
Hydrogen cofeeding alters coke accumulation patterns. It promotes the formation of graphitic coke on the catalyst support rather than disordered coke on active metal sites [10.1039/c3cp50646k]. The coke formed under hydrogen cofeeding is less dense and compact, making it easier to remove during regeneration [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8].

6. Acid Site Conversions:
In a hydrogen atmosphere, the acidity of the support is weakened by converting Brønsted acid sites to Lewis acid sites, which helps reduce the rate of coking on the support [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2].

7. Graphitization:

This improves dehydrogenation selectivity but elevates activation energies [10.1039/c5cy00230c].

4. Energy Barriers:
Hydrogen adsorption on the Pt surface increases the energy barrier for propylene dehydrogenation, which prevents deep reactions that can lead to coke formation [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2]. The enhanced dehydrogenation rate with hydrogen addition suggests a lowering of energy barriers for the dehydrogenation reaction [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].

It allows for longer catalyst lifetimes and more stable operation [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361].
