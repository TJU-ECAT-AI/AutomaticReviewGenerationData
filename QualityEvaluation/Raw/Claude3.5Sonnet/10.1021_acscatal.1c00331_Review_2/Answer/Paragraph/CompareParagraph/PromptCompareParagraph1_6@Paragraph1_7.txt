The addition of hydrogen to the feed suppresses coke formation, with higher hydrogen content leading to less coke accumulation [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]. This reduction is primarily due to the hydrogenation of coke precursor species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. The effect is more pronounced on the catalyst support than on the metal component [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2].

2. Catalyst Stability:
Hydrogen cofeeding improves catalyst stability by reducing coke formation and deactivation rates [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. It helps maintain catalyst activity over time, leading to longer operational periods before regeneration is required [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361]. However, while hydrogen is effective at preventing coke formation, it cannot remove coke that has already formed on the catalyst [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361].

3. Reaction Mechanisms:
Hydrogen enhances the rate of propene formation through hydrogen-assisted dehydrogenation of surface alkyl species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. It also promotes alkene desorption by weakening its chemisorption on the catalyst surface [10.1039/c0cp00341g]. The presence of hydrogen shifts the third C-H cleavage step to a higher energy value, making it less competitive compared to the preferred alkene desorption step [10.1039/c5cy00230c].

4. Energy Barriers:
Hydrogen cofeeding increases the activation energy for propylene dehydrogenation [10.1039/c5cy00230c]. This increase in energy barriers for further dehydrogenation steps helps improve selectivity towards the desired propylene product [10.1039/c3cp53796j].

5. Coke Accumulation Patterns:
Hydrogen changes the pattern of coke accumulation, promoting the formation of graphitic coke on the catalyst support rather than disordered coke on active metal sites [10.1039/c3cp50646k]. At higher hydrogen partial pressures, the coke becomes less dense and compact [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8].

6. Acid Site Conversions:
In a hydrogen atmosphere, the acidity of the support is weakened by the conversion of Lewis acid sites to Brønsted acid sites [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2]. This conversion helps reduce the rate of coking on the support.

4. More efficient use of the catalyst surface, as hydrogen helps keep active metal sites free from coke [10.1039/c3cp50646k].

This suppression occurs through various mechanisms, including hydrogenation of coke precursor species and competition for active sites on the catalyst surface [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].

2. Catalyst Stability Enhancement:
By reducing coke formation, hydrogen cofeeding improves catalyst stability and longevity. Catalysts like Pt/Mg(Ga)(Al)O exhibit lower deactivation rates compared to conventional bimetallic catalysts when hydrogen is present in the feed [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. The improved stability is particularly noticeable over extended operation periods, with some studies reporting excellent stability for over 120 hours at high temperatures [10.1038/s41467-018-06967-8].

3. Reaction Mechanism Modification:
Hydrogen influences the reaction pathways in PDH. It enhances the rate of propene formation through hydrogen-assisted dehydrogenation of surface alkyl species [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. Additionally, hydrogen adsorption on the catalyst surface increases the energy barrier for propylene dehydrogenation, preventing deep reactions that can lead to coke formation [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2].

4. Selectivity Improvement:
Hydrogen cofeeding generally improves selectivity towards propylene. This effect becomes more pronounced after initial cycles, with hydrogen increasing both propane conversion and propene selectivity [10.1039/c3cp50646k]. The improved selectivity is attributed to the suppression of side reactions and the promotion of alkene desorption from the catalyst surface.

5. Coke Characteristics and Distribution:
While hydrogen reduces overall coke formation, it also affects the nature of the coke produced. At higher hydrogen partial pressures, the coke becomes less dense and compact, and can be further softened through hydrogenation [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]. Interestingly, hydrogen promotes the formation of more graphitic coke structures, which primarily cover the catalyst support rather than the active metal sites [10.1039/c3cp50646k].

6. Acid Site Conversions:
In a hydrogen atmosphere, the acidity of the catalyst support is weakened by the conversion of Brønsted acid sites to Lewis acid sites. This conversion helps reduce the rate of coking on the support [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_2].

7. Process Efficiency and Performance:
Hydrogen cofeeding improves overall process efficiency by maintaining catalyst activity for longer periods and reducing the frequency of regeneration cycles. However, it's important to note that excessive hydrogen can lead to alkene hydrogenation, reducing the net dehydrogenation rate [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]. Therefore, optimizing the H2/C3H8 ratio is crucial for maximizing process efficiency.
