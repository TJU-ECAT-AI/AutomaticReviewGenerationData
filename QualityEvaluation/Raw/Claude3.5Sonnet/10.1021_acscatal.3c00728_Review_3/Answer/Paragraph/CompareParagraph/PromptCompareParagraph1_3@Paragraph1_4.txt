B-based materials with 3-D dispersed active sites in porous frameworks exhibit several structural characteristics that enhance catalytic performance for propane dehydrogenation:

1. Porous structures: These materials often feature micro-, meso-, and macroporous structures, providing high surface areas and facilitating mass transfer [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_3]. For example, ordered macroporous BPO4 materials have interconnected pores with an average diameter of 200 nm [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

2. Diverse boron species: Multiple types of boron species can be incorporated, including framework trigonal and tetrahedral boron, as well as terminal BOH groups [10.1039/D0CS01174F]. This diversity contributes to improved catalytic activity and selectivity.

3. Open coordination: Boron species are often anchored to the support matrix in an open coordination form, allowing for better accessibility of reactants to the active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

4. Uniform dispersion: Boron species are typically well-dispersed throughout the support material, ensuring a homogeneous distribution of active sites [10.1126/science.abe7935].

5. Defect-rich structures: Some materials, such as embryonic zeolitic materials, offer abundant SiOH and BOH groups, which contribute to the stabilization of boron species and the formation of active boron centers [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031].

These structural features enhance catalytic performance by:

1. Increasing the number of accessible active sites
2. Improving mass transfer of reactants and products
3. Stabilizing boron species and preventing excessive leaching
4. Facilitating the activation of reactants (e.g., propane in ODH reactions)
5. Suppressing undesired side reactions, such as deep oxidation

Methods used to incorporate B active components into these structures include:

1. Hydrothermal synthesis: This method is effective for creating borosilicate zeolites with well-dispersed boron species [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

2. Incipient wetness impregnation: This technique is used to incorporate boron oxide into carbon nanotubes and other porous supports [10.1002/anie.200901826].

3. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD): This method is effective for creating uniform, thin layers of h-BN on cordierite supports [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

4. Electrospinning: This technique creates one-dimensional porous boron-doped silica nanofibers with uniform dispersion of boron species [10.1016/S1872-2067(21)63809-3].

5. Template-assisted synthesis: This approach is used to create ordered macroporous BPO4 materials with controlled pore structures [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

6. One-step template-free synthesis: This method has been used to create B2O3@BPO4 sandwich-like hollow spheres, combining the advantages of homogeneous and heterogeneous catalysis [10.1016/j.jcat.2019.11.028].

The effectiveness of these methods varies depending on the specific catalytic application. For oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP), methods that create defect-rich structures or promote the formation of specific boron species (e.g., BOH groups) tend to be more effective. For example, the B2O3@BPO4 sandwich-like hollow spheres showed excellent productivity and stability at high temperatures [10.1016/j.jcat.2019.11.028].

However, it's important to note that the relationship between structure and catalytic performance is complex. For instance, isolated BO3 sites in some zeolite frameworks have been found to be inactive for ODHP, suggesting that some degree of B-O-B connectivity may be required for catalytic activity [10.1002/anie.201914696].
