B-based materials with 3-D dispersed active sites in porous frameworks exhibit several structural characteristics that enhance catalytic performance in propane dehydrogenation:

1. Porous structure: These materials often feature micro-, meso-, and macroporous structures, providing high surface areas and improved mass transfer properties [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_3]. For example, ordered macroporous boron phosphate (BPO4) exhibits interconnected macropores with an average diameter of 200 nm [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

2. Uniform dispersion of active sites: Boron species are typically well-dispersed throughout the support matrix, ensuring efficient utilization of active sites [10.1126/science.abe7935].

3. Open coordination of boron sites: Many effective catalysts feature boron in open coordination forms, such as trigonal BO3 units, which increases accessibility to reactants [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031].

4. Defect-rich structures: Incompletely crystallized zeolites or materials with intentionally introduced defects often show superior catalytic activity due to the presence of more active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

5. Thermal stability: The incorporation of boron into porous frameworks often enhances thermal stability, crucial for maintaining catalyst performance under harsh reaction conditions [10.1002/cssc.201402363].

These structural features enhance catalytic performance by:

1. Improving mass transfer and reducing diffusion limitations [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].
2. Increasing the number of accessible active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
3. Allowing for dynamic optimization of catalytic activity during the reaction [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
4. Suppressing undesired side reactions, such as deep oxidation [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

Methods used to incorporate B active components into these structures include:

1. Hydrothermal synthesis: This method is particularly effective for creating zeolites with well-dispersed boron species [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
2. Incipient wetness impregnation: Used to create supported catalysts like B2O3 on carbon nanotubes [10.1002/anie.200901826].
3. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD): Effective for creating thin, uniform coatings of boron-containing materials on structured supports [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].
4. Electrospinning: Allows for the creation of one-dimensional nanofiber structures with well-dispersed boron species [10.1002/cctc.202100476].
5. Template-assisted synthesis: Used to create ordered macroporous structures with controlled pore sizes [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

The effectiveness of these methods varies depending on the specific catalytic application. For propane dehydrogenation, methods that create defect-rich structures or allow for precise control of boron species distribution tend to be more effective. For example, incompletely crystallized borosilicate zeolites show superior activity in oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
