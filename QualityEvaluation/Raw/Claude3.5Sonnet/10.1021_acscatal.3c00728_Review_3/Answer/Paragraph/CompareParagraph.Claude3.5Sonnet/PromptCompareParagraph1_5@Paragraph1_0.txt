B-based materials with 3-D dispersed active sites in porous frameworks exhibit several structural characteristics that enhance catalytic performance in propane dehydrogenation reactions:

1. High surface area and porous structure: These materials often feature micro-, meso-, and macroporous structures, providing large internal surface areas and facilitating mass transfer [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_3]. For example, ordered macroporous boron phosphate (BPO4) materials have interconnected macropores with an average diameter of 200 nm, enhancing mass transport and reducing diffusion limitations [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

2. Diverse boron species: Multiple types of boron coordination environments can be present, including trigonal and tetrahedral boron species. This diversity contributes to improved catalytic activity and selectivity [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

3. Open coordination of boron sites: Boron species are often anchored to the framework in open coordination forms, allowing for better accessibility of reactants to active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

4. Defect-rich structures: Incompletely crystallized or embryonic zeolitic materials with defect-rich structures offer abundant SiOH and BOH groups, contributing to the stabilization of boron species and the formation of active boron centers [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031].

5. Uniform dispersion: Boron species can be uniformly dispersed throughout the porous framework, ensuring a homogeneous distribution of active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

These structural features enhance catalytic performance by:

1. Increasing the number of accessible active sites
2. Improving mass transfer of reactants and products
3. Stabilizing boron species and preventing leaching
4. Facilitating the activation of propane and suppressing over-oxidation

Several methods are used to incorporate B active components into these structures:

1. Hydrothermal synthesis: This method is effective for creating borosilicate zeolites with well-dispersed boron species [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

2. Electrospinning: This technique creates one-dimensional porous boron-doped silica nanofibers with uniform dispersion of boron species [10.1016/S1872-2067(21)63809-3].

3. Incipient wetness impregnation: This method is used to incorporate boron oxide into carbon nanotubes and other porous supports [10.1002/anie.200901826].

4. Template-assisted synthesis: This approach creates ordered macroporous BPO4 materials with well-dispersed active sites [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

5. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD): This method is effective for creating thin, uniform layers of boron-containing materials on structured supports like cordierite [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

The effectiveness of these methods varies depending on the specific catalytic application. For propane dehydrogenation, methods that create defect-rich structures with open coordination boron sites, such as hydrothermal synthesis of embryonic zeolites, have shown promising results [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031]. Electrospinning and template-assisted synthesis have also demonstrated high effectiveness for creating catalysts with improved mass transfer properties and high active site accessibility [10.1016/S1872-2067(21)63809-3] [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

However, the choice of incorporation method should be based on the specific requirements of the catalytic application, considering factors such as desired boron distribution, stability of active sites, and the nature of the support material.

B-based materials with 3-D dispersed active sites in porous frameworks exhibit several structural characteristics that enhance catalytic performance in propane dehydrogenation:

1. Porous structures: These materials often feature hierarchical pore systems, including micropores, mesopores, and macropores. For instance, ordered macroporous boron phosphate (BPO4) catalysts with interconnected pores of 200 nm diameter have shown excellent performance in oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

2. High surface area: The porous nature of these materials provides a large surface area for active site dispersion. For example, boron nitride nanotubes (BNNTs) with a surface area of 97 ± 5 m²/g exhibited higher catalytic activity compared to hexagonal boron nitride (h-BN) with 16 ± 1 m²/g [10.1126/science.aaf7885].

3. Diverse boron species: Multiple types of boron coordination environments are often present, including trigonal and tetrahedral boron species. In borosilicate zeolites, at least five major B species (B[3]a, B[3]b, B[3]c, B[4]a, and B[4]b) have been identified [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

4. Open coordination: Boron species are often anchored to support matrices in open coordination forms, allowing better accessibility to reactants [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

5. Defect-rich structures: Materials with low crystallinity and abundant defects, such as embryonic borosilicate zeolites, have shown superior catalytic activity [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031].

6. Uniform dispersion: Boron species are often uniformly dispersed throughout the support material, ensuring a homogeneous distribution of active sites [10.1126/science.abe7935].

These structural features enhance catalytic performance by:

1. Improving mass transfer and reducing diffusion limitations [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].
2. Increasing the number of accessible active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
3. Providing tailored reactivity through different boron coordination environments [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].
4. Stabilizing reaction intermediates and preventing overoxidation [10.1126/science.aaf7885].
5. Facilitating both surface-mediated and gas-phase reactions [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_3].

Methods used to incorporate B active components into these structures include:

1. Hydrothermal synthesis: This method is effective for creating borosilicate zeolites with well-dispersed boron species [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

2. Incipient wetness impregnation: Used to incorporate boron oxide into carbon nanotubes and other supports [10.1002/anie.200901826].

3. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD): Effective for creating uniform, thin layers of h-BN on cordierite supports [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

4. Electrospinning: Produces one-dimensional porous boron-doped silica nanofibers with uniform dispersion of boron species [10.1016/S1872-2067(21)63809-3].

5. Template-free synthesis: Used to create B2O3@BPO4 sandwich-like hollow spheres with 3-D dispersed active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2019.11.028].

6. Plasma treatment: Effective for creating N-defects in h-BN, which serve as precursors for active boron sites [10.1002/anie.202106713].

The effectiveness of these methods varies depending on the specific catalytic application. For ODHP, methods that create defect-rich structures or allow for uniform dispersion of boron species have shown promising results. However, the optimal method depends on factors such as desired boron loading, support material, and reaction conditions.
