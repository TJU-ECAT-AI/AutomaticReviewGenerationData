B-based materials with 3-D dispersed active sites in porous frameworks exhibit several structural characteristics that enhance catalytic performance in propane dehydrogenation:

1. Porous structure: These materials often feature micro-, meso-, and macroporous structures, providing high surface areas and improved mass transfer properties [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_3]. For example, ordered macroporous boron phosphate (BPO4) materials show interconnected macropores with average diameters of 200 nm [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

2. Diverse boron species: Multiple types of boron species can be incorporated, including framework trigonal and tetrahedral boron, as well as terminal BOH groups. This diversity contributes to improved catalytic activity and selectivity [10.1039/D0CS01174F].

3. Open coordination: Boron species are often anchored to the support matrix in an open coordination form, allowing for better accessibility of reactants to the active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

4. Defect-rich structures: Incompletely crystallized zeolites with low crystallinity exhibit superior catalytic activity and higher olefin productivity at low temperatures [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

5. Uniform dispersion: Active boron species are often uniformly dispersed throughout the support material, ensuring a homogeneous distribution of active sites [10.1126/science.abe7935].

These structural features enhance catalytic performance by:

1. Improving mass transfer and reducing diffusion limitations [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].
2. Increasing the number of accessible active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
3. Allowing for dynamic optimization of catalytic activity during the reaction [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
4. Suppressing secondary reactions and improving selectivity [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

Methods used to incorporate B active components into these structures include:

1. Hydrothermal synthesis: This method is effective for creating borosilicate zeolites with well-dispersed boron species [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

2. Incipient wetness impregnation: This technique is used to incorporate boron oxide into carbon nanotubes and other support materials [10.1002/anie.200901826].

3. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD): Used to create thin, uniform layers of hexagonal boron nitride on cordierite supports [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

4. Electrospinning: This method creates one-dimensional porous boron-doped silica nanofibers with well-dispersed active sites [10.1016/S1872-2067(21)63809-3].

5. Template-assisted synthesis: Used to create ordered macroporous BPO4 materials with controlled pore structures [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

The effectiveness of these methods varies depending on the specific catalytic application. For propane dehydrogenation, methods that create defect-rich structures or allow for uniform dispersion of boron species tend to be more effective. However, the choice of method should be based on the desired catalyst properties, reaction conditions, and scalability requirements.

1. Three-dimensional network structure: Materials like zeolites, ordered macroporous structures, and mesocellular silica foams provide frameworks with channels and cavities of molecular dimensions, allowing for high surface area and controlled access to active sites [10.1007/s10562-005-9622-4].

2. Hierarchical pore structure: The combination of micro-, meso-, and macropores improves mass transfer properties and accessibility of active sites. For example, ordered macroporous BPO4 materials with interconnected macropores of 200 nm diameter bridged by ink-bottle neck-like channels of 80 nm diameter [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

4. Diverse boron species: Multiple types of boron species can be incorporated, including framework trigonal and tetrahedral boron, as well as terminal BOH groups. This diversity of active sites can contribute to improved catalytic activity and selectivity [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

5. Open coordination of boron sites: This configuration allows for better exposure of the active centers, increasing their accessibility to reactants [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031].

6. Defect-rich structure: Incompletely crystallized zeolites with low crystallinity exhibit superior catalytic activity and higher olefin productivity at low temperatures [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

These structural features enhance catalytic performance by:

3. Stabilizing boron species and preventing excessive leaching [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
4. Allowing for dynamic optimization of catalytic activity during the reaction [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
