B-based materials with 3-D dispersed active sites in porous frameworks exhibit several structural characteristics that enhance catalytic performance in propane dehydrogenation:

1. Porous structure: These materials often feature micro-, meso-, and macroporous structures, providing high surface areas and improved mass transfer properties [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728_3]. For example, ordered macroporous boron phosphate (BPO4) exhibits interconnected macropores with an average diameter of 200 nm [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

2. Uniform dispersion of active sites: Boron species are typically well-dispersed throughout the support matrix, ensuring efficient utilization of active sites [10.1126/science.abe7935].

3. Open coordination of boron sites: Many effective catalysts feature boron in open coordination forms, such as trigonal BO3 units, which increases accessibility to reactants [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031].

4. Defect-rich structures: Incompletely crystallized zeolites or materials with intentionally introduced defects often show superior catalytic activity due to the presence of more active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

5. Thermal stability: The incorporation of boron into porous frameworks often enhances thermal stability, crucial for maintaining catalyst performance under harsh reaction conditions [10.1002/cssc.201402363].

These structural features enhance catalytic performance by:

1. Improving mass transfer and reducing diffusion limitations [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].
2. Increasing the number of accessible active sites [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
3. Allowing for dynamic optimization of catalytic activity during the reaction [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
4. Suppressing undesired side reactions, such as deep oxidation [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

Methods used to incorporate B active components into these structures include:

1. Hydrothermal synthesis: This method is particularly effective for creating zeolites with well-dispersed boron species [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
2. Incipient wetness impregnation: Used to create supported catalysts like B2O3 on carbon nanotubes [10.1002/anie.200901826].
3. Chemical vapor deposition (CVD): Effective for creating thin, uniform coatings of boron-containing materials on structured supports [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].
4. Electrospinning: Allows for the creation of one-dimensional nanofiber structures with well-dispersed boron species [10.1002/cctc.202100476].
5. Template-assisted synthesis: Used to create ordered macroporous structures with controlled pore sizes [10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3].

The effectiveness of these methods varies depending on the specific catalytic application. For propane dehydrogenation, methods that create defect-rich structures or allow for precise control of boron species distribution tend to be more effective. For example, incompletely crystallized borosilicate zeolites show superior activity in oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The development of B-based materials with 3-D dispersed active sites in porous frameworks represents a significant advancement in catalyst design for propane dehydrogenation. The ability to control pore structure, active site distribution, and thermal stability offers promising opportunities for enhancing catalyst performance and selectivity.

Future research directions may include:

1. Exploring novel synthesis methods to create hierarchical pore structures that optimize both mass transfer and active site accessibility.
2. Investigating the interplay between different boron species (e.g., trigonal vs. tetrahedral) and their impact on catalytic performance.
3. Developing in-situ characterization techniques to better understand the dynamic behavior of boron species under reaction conditions.
4. Exploring the potential of hybrid materials that combine the benefits of B-based catalysts with other active components for synergistic effects.
5. Scaling up promising catalyst designs for industrial applications, addressing challenges related to production costs and long-term stability.

By building on the current understanding of structure-performance relationships in B-based catalysts, researchers can work towards developing more efficient and selective catalysts for propane dehydrogenation and potentially expand their application to other important chemical processes.

含有三维分散活性位点的B基多孔框架材料在丙烷脱氢催化中表现出几种增强催化性能的结构特征：

1. 多孔结构：这些材料通常具有微孔、介孔和大孔结构，提供高比表面积和改善的传质性能[10.1021/acscatal.3c00728_3]。例如，有序大孔硼酸盐（BPO4）展示了平均直径为200 nm的互连大孔[10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3]。

2. 活性位点均匀分散：硼物种通常在支撑基质中分散良好，确保活性位点的高效利用[10.1126/science.abe7935]。

3. 硼位点的开放配位：许多有效的催化剂具有开放配位形式的硼，如三配位BO3单元，这增加了反应物的可接近性[10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031]。

4. 缺陷丰富结构：不完全结晶的沸石或有意引入缺陷的材料通常由于存在更多活性位点而表现出优异的催化活性[10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017]。

5. 热稳定性：将硼掺入多孔框架通常能提高热稳定性，这对于在苛刻反应条件下保持催化剂性能至关重要[10.1002/cssc.201402363]。

这些结构特征通过以下方式增强催化性能：

1. 改善传质并减少扩散限制[10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3]。
2. 增加可接近的活性位点数量[10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017]。
3. 允许在反应过程中动态优化催化活性[10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017]。
4. 抑制不良副反应，如深度氧化[10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028]。

将B活性组分掺入这些结构的方法包括：

1. 水热合成：这种方法特别适用于创建具有良好分散硼物种的沸石[10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017]。
2. 浸渍法：用于创建如B2O3负载在碳纳米管上的负载型催化剂[10.1002/anie.200901826]。
3. 化学气相沉积（CVD）：有效地在结构化载体上创建含硼材料的薄而均匀的涂层[10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028]。
4. 电纺丝：允许创建具有良好分散硼物种的一维纳米纤维结构[10.1002/cctc.202100476]。
5. 模板辅助合成：用于创建具有可控孔径的有序大孔结构[10.1016/S1872-2067(20)63654-3]。

这些方法的有效性因特定的催化应用而异。对于丙烷脱氢，创建缺陷丰富结构或允许精确控制硼物种分布的方法往往更有效。例如，不完全结晶的硼硅沸石在丙烷氧化脱氢（ODHP）中表现出优异的活性[10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017]。

综合理解与未来展望：含有三维分散活性位点的B基多孔框架材料的开发代表了丙烷脱氢催化剂设计的重大进展。控制孔结构、活性位点分布和热稳定性的能力为提高催化剂性能和选择性提供了令人期待的机会。

未来研究方向可能包括：

1. 探索新型合成方法，创建能够同时优化传质和活性位点可接近性的层级孔结构。
2. 研究不同硼物种（如三配位与四配位）之间的相互作用及其对催化性能的影响。
3. 开发原位表征技术，以更好地理解反应条件下硼物种的动态行为。
4. 探索将B基催化剂的优势与其他活性组分结合的混合材料，以实现协同效应。
5. 将有前景的催化剂设计扩大到工业应用规模，解决与生产成本和长期稳定性相关的挑战。

通过在B基催化剂结构-性能关系的当前理解基础上继续深入研究，研究人员可以致力于开发更高效、更具选择性的丙烷脱氢催化剂，并可能扩展其在其他重要化学过程中的应用。
