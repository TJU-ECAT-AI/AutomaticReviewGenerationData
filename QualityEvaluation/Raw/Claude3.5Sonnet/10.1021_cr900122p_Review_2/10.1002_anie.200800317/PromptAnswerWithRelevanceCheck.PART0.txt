In this study, a Zn-modified high-silica zeolite BEA catalyst was used for the co-conversion of methane and propane. The zinc modification of the zeolite plays a crucial role in the activation of methane, which occurs at 523K through the formation of surface methoxy species and Zn-CH3 species.

The reaction conditions, particularly temperature, greatly affect the transformation process. The coaromatization of methane and light hydrocarbons occurs at 700-800K, while the initial activation of methane on Zn/H-BEA happens at a lower temperature of 523K. The efficiency of the process is demonstrated by the high degree of transfer of 13C-labeled atoms from methane into aromatic products.

The mechanism of alkane transformation in this zeolite-catalyzed reaction involves the alkylation of propane-originating aromatic molecules by methane, with a methoxy species serving as an intermediate. This mechanism contributes to the industrial significance of the process by enabling the conversion of methane, a relatively inert hydrocarbon, into more valuable aromatic compounds.

The study highlights the importance of metal modification (in this case, zinc) in zeolites for activating methane, which is typically challenging due to its inertness. This understanding can guide the development of more efficient catalysts for industrial alkane transformation processes.
