The composition, structure, and reaction conditions of metal-based catalysts significantly influence the mechanisms and efficiency of alkane transformations. For zeolite-catalyzed reactions, incorporating zinc cations into high-silica zeolites enhances selectivity towards light alkane dehydrogenation and aromatization. The zinc species in Zn/H-MFI exist in three forms: small and large ZnO clusters and zinc cations. These zinc species, particularly Zn2+ cations, act as strong Lewis sites and alter the mechanism of alkane activation and transformation, favoring hydrogen removal.

The presence of zinc modifies the reaction mechanism, as evidenced by the regioselectivity of H/D exchange in Zn/H-MFI compared to unmodified H-MFI. This change in mechanism contributes to the industrial significance of these catalysts by potentially improving selectivity and efficiency in alkane transformation processes.

The structure of the zinc species in the zeolite, which can include (OZ)(ZnOZn)2+(OZ) or (ZO)Zn+OSi cations, as well as Zn2+ or ZnOH+, plays a crucial role in determining the catalytic properties. These structural variations can affect the catalyst's performance in processes such as oxidative dehydrogenation and other zeolite-catalyzed reactions.
