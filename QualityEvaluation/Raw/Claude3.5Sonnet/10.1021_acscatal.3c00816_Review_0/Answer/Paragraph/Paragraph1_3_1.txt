Propane direct dehydrogenation (PDH) technology has emerged as the most promising method for propylene production due to several key advantages that address the challenges faced by conventional production methods:

1. Feedstock availability: PDH utilizes propane, which is abundantly available due to the large-scale exploitation of shale gas and natural gas resources [10.1039/D0CS01260B]. This addresses the challenge of feedstock availability faced by conventional naphtha-based production methods, which are struggling with dwindling petroleum reserves and rising oil prices [10.1021/cr5002436].

2. Process efficiency: PDH is a direct, one-step process to convert propane to propylene, offering improved efficiency compared to multi-step conventional methods [10.1039/D0CS01260B]. It allows for higher propylene yields and fewer byproducts, improving overall process efficiency [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345].

3. Product selectivity: PDH technology offers high selectivity towards propylene, typically achieving over 90% selectivity [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345]. This high selectivity reduces the need for extensive separation and purification steps, thereby improving the overall economics of the process [10.1021/cr5002436].

4. Economic viability: The profitability of PDH is largely determined by the price difference between propane and propylene. Current market conditions, with low-cost propane from shale gas, make PDH highly favorable economically [10.1021/cr5002436].

5. On-purpose production: PDH allows for dedicated, on-purpose propylene production, which helps to address the growing demand-supply gap for propylene that cannot be met by conventional production methods alone [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345].

6. Environmental considerations: PDH generally has a lower carbon footprint compared to naphtha cracking, as it requires less energy input and produces fewer CO2 emissions [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345].

7. Operational flexibility: PDH plants can be operated independently of refinery or petrochemical complexes, providing greater flexibility in terms of location and scale of production [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345].

However, PDH technology also faces some challenges:

1. High energy requirements: The process is highly endothermic, requiring high temperatures (generally above 500°C) to achieve satisfactory propylene yields [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012].

2. Catalyst deactivation: Issues like coking, sintering, and other side reactions can affect catalyst longevity and performance [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012].

3. Thermodynamic limitations: The equilibrium conversion is limited at high temperatures, requiring innovative reactor designs or process modifications to overcome this constraint [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012].

Despite these challenges, ongoing research in catalyst development and process optimization continues to enhance the efficiency and competitiveness of PDH technology, making it an increasingly attractive option for meeting the growing global demand for propylene.

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The emergence of PDH technology represents a significant shift in propylene production, addressing key challenges faced by conventional methods. Looking forward, several areas of development could further enhance PDH's position:

1. Advanced catalyst design: Future research could focus on developing novel catalysts that combine high activity, selectivity, and stability while being cost-effective and environmentally friendly. This might involve exploring new materials, such as single-atom catalysts or advanced bimetallic formulations.

2. Process intensification: Innovative reactor designs and process configurations could help overcome thermodynamic limitations and improve energy efficiency. This might include membrane reactors, microreactor technology, or advanced heat integration systems.

3. Integration with renewable energy: As the world moves towards decarbonization, PDH processes could be coupled with renewable energy sources for heating, potentially making the process even more environmentally friendly.

4. Circular economy approaches: Research into utilizing bio-based propane or recycled plastics as feedstock for PDH could create more sustainable production cycles, aligning with global sustainability goals.

5. Hybrid processes: Combining PDH with other technologies, such as oxidative dehydrogenation or metathesis, could lead to synergistic effects that enhance overall propylene production efficiency.

These future directions could not only address current challenges but also position PDH technology as a cornerstone of sustainable propylene production in the evolving chemical industry landscape.

由于丙烷直接脱氢(PDH)技术具有多项关键优势，能够解决传统生产方法所面临的挑战，因此已成为最有前景的丙烯生产方法：

1. 原料可用性：PDH利用大规模开采页岩气和天然气资源所产生的丰富丙烷 [10.1039/D0CS01260B]。这解决了传统石脑油基生产方法面临的原料可用性挑战，后者正面临石油储量减少和油价上涨的困境 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

2. 工艺效率：PDH是一个直接的单步过程，将丙烷转化为丙烯，与传统的多步方法相比，效率更高 [10.1039/D0CS01260B]。它能够提高丙烯产率并减少副产品，从而提高整体工艺效率 [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345]。

3. 产品选择性：PDH技术对丙烯具有高选择性，通常可达90%以上 [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345]。这种高选择性减少了广泛分离和纯化步骤的需求，从而提高了整个过程的经济性 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

4. 经济可行性：PDH的盈利能力在很大程度上取决于丙烷和丙烯之间的价格差异。当前市场条件下，页岩气带来的低成本丙烷使PDH在经济上具有很高的优势 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

5. 定向生产：PDH允许专门的、定向的丙烯生产，这有助于解决传统生产方法无法满足的丙烯供需缺口 [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345]。

6. 环境考虑：与石脑油裂解相比，PDH通常具有较低的碳足迹，因为它需要较少的能量输入并产生较少的CO2排放 [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345]。

7. 操作灵活性：PDH装置可以独立于炼油厂或石化综合体运行，在位置和生产规模方面提供更大的灵活性 [10.146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345]。

然而，PDH技术也面临一些挑战：

1. 高能耗要求：该过程是高度吸热的，需要高温（通常高于500°C）才能获得满意的丙烯产率 [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012]。

2. 催化剂失活：积碳、烧结和其他副反应等问题会影响催化剂的寿命和性能 [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012]。

3. 热力学限制：高温下的平衡转化率是有限的，需要创新的反应器设计或工艺改进来克服这一限制 [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012]。

尽管存在这些挑战，但在催化剂开发和工艺优化方面的持续研究不断提高PDH技术的效率和竞争力，使其成为满足不断增长的全球丙烯需求的越来越具吸引力的选择。
