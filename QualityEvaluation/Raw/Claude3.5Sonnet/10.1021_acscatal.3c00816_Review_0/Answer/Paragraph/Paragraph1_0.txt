<English>
Propane direct dehydrogenation (PDH) technology has emerged as the most promising method for propylene production due to several key advantages that address the challenges faced by conventional production methods:

1. Feedstock availability: PDH utilizes propane, which is abundantly available due to the large-scale exploitation of shale gas and natural gas resources. This addresses the challenge of feedstock limitations faced by conventional naphtha-based production methods [10.1039/D0CS01260B]. The recent boom in shale gas production has made propane feedstock extremely cheap and plentiful [10.1039/D2QI00653G].

2. Process efficiency: PDH is a direct, one-step process to convert propane to propylene. This offers improved efficiency compared to multi-step processes like methanol-to-olefins (MTO) or Fischer-Tropsch to olefins (FTO) [10.1039/D0CS01260B]. The atom economy of PDH is high, with up to 95% efficiency possible [10.1039/D1GC03700E].

3. Product selectivity: PDH provides exclusive selectivity to olefins, which is a unique advantage over oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) processes that use oxidants like O2, CO2, or N2O [10.1039/D0CS01260B]. Some catalysts have demonstrated propylene selectivity of over 95% [10.1016/j.micromeso.2022.112115].

4. Economic viability: The profitability of PDH is largely determined by the price difference between propane and propylene. Current market conditions, with low-cost propane from shale gas, make PDH highly favorable economically [10.1021/cr5002436].

5. Environmental considerations: PDH generally has a lower carbon footprint compared to naphtha cracking, as it requires less energy input and produces fewer CO2 emissions [10.1146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345].

6. Meeting growing demand: PDH technology provides a scalable solution to meet the ever-increasing global demand for propylene, which conventional methods struggle to satisfy [10.1016/j.cej.2019.122423].

However, PDH technology also faces some challenges:

1. High energy requirements: The process is highly endothermic, requiring high temperatures (generally above 500°C) to achieve satisfactory propylene yields [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012].

2. Catalyst deactivation: Issues like coking, sintering, and other side reactions can affect catalyst longevity and performance [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012].

3. Thermodynamic limitations: The equilibrium conversion is limited at high temperatures, requiring innovative reactor designs or process modifications to overcome this constraint [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012].

Despite these challenges, ongoing research in catalyst development and process optimization is making PDH an increasingly attractive option for meeting the growing global demand for propylene.

※※※

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The development of PDH technology represents a significant shift in the propylene production landscape, driven by the changing availability of feedstocks and the growing demand for propylene. As the technology matures, we can expect to see further improvements in catalyst design, focusing on enhancing stability, selectivity, and resistance to coking. This may involve the development of novel support materials, bimetallic catalysts, or even single-atom catalysts that can operate at lower temperatures while maintaining high selectivity.

Future research endeavors might focus on integrating PDH with other processes, such as oxidative dehydrogenation or membrane reactors, to overcome thermodynamic limitations and improve overall efficiency. Additionally, as the world moves towards more sustainable chemical production, there may be efforts to develop bio-based propane sources or to couple PDH with carbon capture technologies to further reduce its environmental impact.

The success of PDH technology could also have broader implications for the petrochemical industry, potentially leading to a restructuring of propylene supply chains and influencing the development of downstream industries. As PDH becomes more prevalent, it may drive innovation in propylene-based products and applications, further increasing the demand for this crucial chemical building block.

<Chinese>
由于丙烷直接脱氢（PDH）技术具有几个关键优势，它已成为丙烯生产最有前景的方法，解决了传统生产方法面临的挑战：

1. 原料可用性：PDH利用丙烷，这种原料由于页岩气和天然气资源的大规模开采而变得丰富可得。这解决了传统石脑油基生产方法面临的原料限制问题[10.1039/D0CS01260B]。近期页岩气生产的蓬勃发展使丙烷原料变得极其便宜和充足[10.1039/D2QI00653G]。

2. 工艺效率：PDH是将丙烷直接转化为丙烯的一步法工艺。与甲醇制烯烃（MTO）或费托制烯烃（FTO）等多步骤工艺相比，这提供了更高的效率[10.1039/D0CS01260B]。PDH的原子经济性高，可达到95%的效率[10.1039/D1GC03700E]。

3. 产品选择性：PDH提供对烯烃的专一选择性，这是相对于使用O2、CO2或N2O作为氧化剂的氧化脱氢（ODH）工艺的独特优势[10.1039/D0CS01260B]。一些催化剂已经展示出超过95%的丙烯选择性[10.1016/j.micromeso.2022.112115]。

4. 经济可行性：PDH的盈利能力在很大程度上取决于丙烷和丙烯之间的价格差异。目前的市场条件，加上来自页岩气的低成本丙烷，使PDH在经济上非常有利[10.1021/cr5002436]。

5. 环境考虑：与石脑油裂解相比，PDH通常具有更低的碳足迹，因为它需要较少的能量输入并产生较少的CO2排放[10.1146/annurev-chembioeng-060817-084345]。

6. 满足不断增长的需求：PDH技术提供了一个可扩展的解决方案，以满足不断增长的全球丙烯需求，而传统方法难以满足这一需求[10.1016/j.cej.2019.122423]。

然而，PDH技术也面临一些挑战：

1. 高能耗需求：该过程是高度吸热的，需要高温（通常高于500°C）才能达到令人满意的丙烯产率[10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012]。

2. 催化剂失活：结焦、烧结和其他副反应等问题会影响催化剂的寿命和性能[10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012]。

3. 热力学限制：高温下的平衡转化受到限制，需要创新的反应器设计或工艺改进来克服这一约束[10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012]。

尽管存在这些挑战，但在催化剂开发和工艺优化方面的持续研究正使PDH成为满足不断增长的全球丙烯需求的越来越具吸引力的选择。

※※※

综合理解与未来展望：PDH技术的发展代表了丙烯生产领域的重大转变，这一转变由原料可用性的变化和丙烯需求的增长所推动。随着技术的成熟，我们可以期待在催化剂设计方面取得进一步的进展，重点是提高稳定性、选择性和抗结焦能力。这可能涉及开发新型载体材料、双金属催化剂，甚至是可以在较低温度下运行同时保持高选择性的单原子催化剂。

未来的研究努力可能集中在将PDH与其他工艺（如氧化脱氢或膜反应器）集成，以克服热力学限制并提高整体效率。此外，随着世界向更可持续的化学品生产迈进，可能会努力开发生物基丙烷来源或将PDH与碳捕获技术结合，以进一步减少其环境影响。

PDH技术的成功也可能对石化行业产生更广泛的影响，可能导致丙烯供应链的重组并影响下游行业的发展。随着PDH变得更加普遍，它可能推动丙烯基产品和应用的创新，进一步增加对这种关键化学品的需求。
