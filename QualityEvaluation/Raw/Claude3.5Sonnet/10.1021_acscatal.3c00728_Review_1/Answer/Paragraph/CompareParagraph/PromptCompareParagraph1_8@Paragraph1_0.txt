The spatial distribution and dimensionality of active sites in B-based catalysts significantly influence their performance in oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) reactions. The active sites can be classified into three types: one-dimensional (1-D), two-dimensional (2-D), and three-dimensional (3-D) dispersed active sites [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_1].

1-D active sites:
1-D active sites, which are linearly dispersed on the edge of 2-D h-BN sheets, have shown superior performance in ODHP reactions. For example, h-BN with 1-D edge active sites exhibited a propene selectivity of 79% at a propane conversion of 14%, outperforming conventional catalysts like V/SiO2 [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_1]. The edge-hydroxylated boron nitride (BNOH) efficiently catalyzed ODHP, reaching a propylene selectivity of 80.2% with minimal CO2 formation (0.5%) at a propane conversion of 20.6% [10.1016/j.cjche.2020.07.040].

The unique properties of 1-D edge active sites contribute to their high performance:
1. Stabilization of intermediates: The 1-D nature of edge sites allows for the stabilization of propyl radicals, avoiding the creation of highly reactive species that lead to overoxidation [10.1126/science.aaf7885].
2. Oxygen activation: Edge B atoms are involved in O2 activation, forming BOH and BO active sites that facilitate dehydrogenation without dissociating O2 [10.1002/cctc.201700725].
3. Selective hydrogen abstraction: BO radicals catalyze hydrogen abstraction from alkanes to produce alkyl radicals [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

2-D active sites:
2-D active sites are typically found on the surface of materials like B2O3/SBA-15 catalysts. These catalysts demonstrated higher activity due to abundant tricoordinated boroxol and hydroxylated linear boron species on the surface [10.1016/j.cjche.2020.07.040]. The 2-D distribution allows for a high density of accessible active sites, contributing to improved catalytic performance.

3-D active sites:
3-D active sites can be found in structures like B2O3@BPO4 composites with sandwich-like hollow sphere structures. At reaction temperatures above 450°C, melted B2O3 supported on the surface of BPO4 hollow spheres acts as the catalytically active species [10.1016/j.jcat.2019.11.028]. This 3-D arrangement provides a high surface area and increased accessibility of active sites.

2. Mass transfer: Well-developed channel structures in 3-D catalysts enhance mass transfer and provide space for gas-phase radical reactions [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].
3. Thermal management: The distribution of active sites can affect thermal conductivity, helping to avoid hotspots during reactions [10.1016/j.cattod.2018.12.028].

3. Higher π* to σ* ratio and B/N ratio compared to in-plane structures [10.1002/cctc.201700004].

2-D Active Sites:
2-D active sites are typically found on the surface of layered boron-containing materials. These sites offer advantages such as:
1. Improved accessibility to reactants due to their surface exposure [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].
2. Ability to form specific active structures like boroxol rings and tri-coordinated planar BO3/2 species [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
3. Potential for high dispersion of active species across the catalyst surface [10.1016/S1872-2067(21)63809-3].

3-D Active Sites:
3-D active sites are found in bulk materials or complex structures. They offer unique advantages:
1. Improved thermal stability and resistance to sintering [10.1002/anie.200901826].
2. Potential for creating hierarchical porous structures that enhance mass transfer [10.1016/j.apsusc.2023.156743].
3. Ability to form B-O-B connectivity, which has been shown to be important for catalytic activity [10.1002/anie.201914696].

The dimensionality of active sites significantly impacts catalyst performance:
1. 1-D edge sites offer high selectivity and efficiency but may suffer from low density and stability issues [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_1].
2. 2-D surface sites provide good accessibility but may be prone to overoxidation [10.1126/science.aaf7885].
3. 3-D structures can offer improved stability and mass transfer but may have limited accessibility to active sites [10.1002/anie.200901826].

Understanding these unique properties and reaction mechanisms contributes to the design of more efficient catalysts for commercial applications by:
