The active sites can be classified into three types: one-dimensional (1-D), two-dimensional (2-D), and three-dimensional (3-D) dispersed active sites [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_1].

1-D Active Sites:
1-D active sites, typically found on the edges of 2-D h-BN sheets or in boron nitride nanotubes (BNNTs), have shown superior performance in ODHP reactions. For example, h-BN with 1-D edge active sites exhibited a propene selectivity of 79% at a propane conversion of 14%, outperforming conventional catalysts like V/SiO2 [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_1]. The edge hydroxylated boron nitride (BNOH) efficiently catalyzed ODHP, reaching a propylene selectivity of 80.2% with only 0.5% CO2 at a propane conversion of 20.6% [10.1016/j.cjche.2020.07.040].

The unique properties of 1-D edge active sites include:
1. Stabilization of propyl radicals, avoiding the creation of highly reactive species prone to overoxidation [10.1126/science.aaf7885].
2. Efficient activation of O2, with edge B atoms playing a crucial role [10.1002/cctc.201700725].
3. Formation of specific active structures like BOH groups, which selectively break C-H bonds while suppressing propylene overoxidation [10.1016/j.jcat.2018.11.014].

2-D Active Sites:
2-D active sites are typically found on the surface of materials like B2O3/SBA-15 catalysts. These catalysts demonstrate higher activity due to abundant tricoordinated boroxol and hydroxylated linear boron species on the surface [10.1016/j.cjche.2020.07.040]. The 2-D distribution of active sites can be effective, as shown by layered borosilicate zeolites with surface-accessible defective trigonal boron species [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

The 1-D nature of these sites offers unique advantages:

1. Stabilization of intermediates: Unlike 0-D sites (e.g., V/SiO2) that create highly reactive propyl radicals, 1-D edge sites stabilize these intermediates, reducing overoxidation [10.1126/science.aaf7885].
2. Controlled reactivity: The linear structure limits the diffusion of adsorbates, preventing overoxidation typical of 2-D surface catalysts [10.1126/science.aaf7885].
3. Specific active structures: Oxygen-terminated armchair boron nitride edges (>BOON<) act as highly effective active sites for ODHP reactions [10.1126/science.aaf7885].

2-D Active Sites:
2-D active sites are typically found on the surfaces of layered boron-containing materials. These sites offer:

1. High surface area: 2-D materials like h-BN nanosheets provide large surface areas for catalytic reactions [10.1002/cctc.201700725].
2. Tunable surface chemistry: The surface of 2-D materials can be functionalized to optimize catalytic performance [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.02.017].
3. Efficient mass transfer: The planar structure of 2-D materials facilitates the movement of reactants and products [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.03.021].

3-D Active Sites:
3-D active sites are found in bulk boron-containing materials and offer:

1. High thermal stability: 3-D structures often exhibit better thermal stability, crucial for high-temperature ODHP reactions [10.1002/anie.200901826].
2. Multifunctional catalytic sites: 3-D structures can incorporate various types of active sites within a single catalyst particle [10.1016/j.jcat.2022.11.031].
3. Enhanced porosity: 3-D structures often feature hierarchical porosity, improving mass transfer and accessibility of active sites [10.1016/j.apsusc.2023.156743].

The performance of these catalysts is significantly influenced by the nature and distribution of their active sites. For instance, h-BN with 1-D edge active sites exhibited a propene selectivity of 79% at a propane conversion of 14%, outperforming conventional catalysts like V/SiO2 [10.1021/acscatal.3c00728\_1].

Understanding the unique properties and reaction mechanisms of these sites, particularly 1-D edge active sites, contributes to the design of more efficient catalysts for commercial applications in several ways:

2. Tailoring surface chemistry: Modifying edge termination and functionalization can optimize catalytic properties [10.1016/S1872-2067(17)62786-4].
3. Controlling active site density: Balancing the concentration of active sites with stability and accessibility is crucial for long-term performance [10.1002/anie.201914696].
4. Enhancing mass transfer: Designing catalyst structures that facilitate efficient movement of reactants and products can improve overall performance [10.1016/S1872-2067(21)63809-3].

Moving forward, research efforts could focus on:
