The literature provides limited information specifically on the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) using N2O. However, it does mention some relevant points about N2O as an oxidant:

1. N2O interacts with ZSM-5 zeolites to produce a highly active oxygen species called α-oxygen.

2. This α-oxygen shows high selectivity (85-95%) for propylene formation from propane, as well as for ethylene from ethane and phenol from benzene.

3. The selectivity remains constant over time, but catalyst activity decreases rapidly due to deactivation.

The advantages of N2O in terms of propylene selectivity are evident from the high selectivity mentioned. The main catalyst discussed is ZSM-5, particularly HZSM-5.

The critical challenge highlighted is catalyst deactivation, which leads to rapid activity loss despite maintained selectivity.

To address these limitations and make N2O-ODHP more feasible, research could focus on:

1. Developing more stable catalysts that resist deactivation.
2. Investigating the mechanisms of catalyst deactivation to inform better catalyst design.
3. Exploring modifications to ZSM-5 or entirely new catalyst materials that maintain high selectivity while improving stability.
4. Studying the reaction conditions (temperature, pressure, feed composition) to optimize both selectivity and catalyst longevity.
5. Investigating regeneration methods for deactivated catalysts to extend their useful life.

These strategies could potentially overcome the stability issues while maintaining the high selectivity advantage of N2O in ODHP.
