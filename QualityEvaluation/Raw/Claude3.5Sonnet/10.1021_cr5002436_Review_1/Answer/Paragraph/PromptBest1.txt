Noble metal-based catalysts, particularly platinum-based catalysts, and metal oxide-based catalysts are the two main types used for nonoxidative dehydrogenation of light olefins. This review will compare their compositions, effectiveness, industrial applications, recent developments, and potential advantages.

Composition:
Noble metal-based catalysts typically consist of platinum nanoparticles, often promoted with elements such as Sn, Zn, Ge, Ga, or In to form bimetallic alloys. These are usually supported on high surface area materials like alumina, silica, or calcined hydrotalcites (e.g., Mg(Al)O, Mg(Ga)(Al)O, or Mg(In)(Al)O) [10.1021/cr5002436_1]. Metal oxide-based catalysts include chromia-alumina, gallium oxide, chromium oxide, vanadium oxide, titanium oxide, and others [10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x].

Effectiveness:
Platinum-based catalysts are generally more effective due to their superior activation of paraffinic C-H bonds and low activity for C-C cleavage. The dehydrogenation reaction is insensitive to platinum particle size or structure [10.1021/cr5002436_1]. However, unmodified platinum catalysts may lack selectivity and suffer from rapid coke formation [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.017].

Metal oxide catalysts vary in effectiveness. Among simple metal oxides, gallium oxide shows the highest activity for ethane dehydrogenation, followed by chromium oxide and vanadium oxide [10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x]. Some complex oxides, like vanadia-silica-zirconia, show high selectivity for specific reactions.

Industrial Applications:
Platinum-based catalysts are widely used in commercial processes like UOP's Oleflex and Pacol for light and heavy paraffin dehydrogenation [10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x]. The Pacol process, using alumina-supported platinum catalyst, was first commercialized in 1968 for linear olefin production in the detergent industry [10.1021/cr5002436_1].

Metal oxide catalysts, particularly chromia-alumina, have been used in older processes like Catofin. Chromium oxide supported on alumina or zirconia is also used commercially [10.1016/s0926-860x(00)00842-5].

Recent Developments:
For noble metal catalysts, recent developments include the introduction of continuous catalyst regeneration (CCR) technology, allowing for stable activity over several years [10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x]. Research has focused on improving selectivity and stability through the use of promoters and optimizing support materials.

For metal oxide catalysts, research has explored novel combinations and supports to enhance performance, such as sulfate and sodium treatments of silica supports, and development of complex mixed oxide systems [10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x].

Potential Advantages:
Noble metal catalysts offer high activity, long-term stability with CCR, flexibility for different feedstocks, and are well-established in industrial processes. They can be finely tuned with promoters and supports [10.1021/cr5002436_1].

Metal oxide catalysts potentially offer lower cost, good selectivity for specific reactions, and the ability to be tailored for different feedstocks. In some cases, they show effectiveness in the presence of CO2 as a mild oxidant [10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x].
