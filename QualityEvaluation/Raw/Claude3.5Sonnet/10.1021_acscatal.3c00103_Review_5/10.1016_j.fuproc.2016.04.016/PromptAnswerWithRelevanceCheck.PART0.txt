The primary challenges in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) for industrial propylene production include:

1. Thermodynamic limitations: PDH is an endothermic process with alkene yields limited by reaction thermodynamics.

2. High-temperature stability: Higher temperatures increase alkane conversion but decrease alkene selectivity due to side reactions like dehydrocyclization, aromatization, thermal cracking, and coke formation.

3. Propylene selectivity: Maintaining high propylene selectivity while achieving high conversion rates is challenging.

4. Resistance to side reactions: Preventing unwanted reactions that reduce propylene yield and cause catalyst deactivation.

Researchers are addressing these challenges by:

1. Investigating alternative reaction routes like oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) to overcome thermodynamic limitations.

2. Developing catalysts with improved high-temperature stability and resistance to coking.

3. Exploring novel catalyst designs and promoters to enhance propylene selectivity.

4. Studying CH bond activation mechanisms to gain insights for rational catalyst design.

5. Investigating autothermal strategies to offset energy requirements.

Despite decades of research, there remains a gap between laboratory achievements and industrial implementation, indicating ongoing opportunities for innovation in PDH catalysts and processes.
