The primary challenges in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) for industrial propylene production include:

1. Overcoming limited equilibrium conversion
2. Minimizing side reactions at high temperatures
3. Improving catalyst stability and resistance to deactivation
4. Reducing noble metal loading in catalysts
5. Enhancing energy efficiency of the process

Researchers are addressing these challenges through several approaches:

1. Exploring alternative catalysts: Metal oxides like GaOx, MoOx, and VOx are being investigated as potential replacements for PtSn and CrOx systems. However, these alternatives still face issues with rapid deactivation and incomplete activity recovery after regeneration.

2. Investigating oxidative dehydrogenation: This approach aims to overcome thermodynamic limitations by using an oxidant to combust the hydrogen formed during the reaction, potentially achieving full paraffin conversion.

3. Improving catalyst formulations: Efforts are being made to develop catalysts with higher stability, better propylene selectivity, and increased resistance to coke deposition.

4. Enhancing regeneration techniques: Researchers are working on more effective methods to regenerate catalysts and recover their activity after deactivation.

5. Optimizing process conditions: Studies are focused on finding optimal reaction conditions that balance conversion, selectivity, and catalyst stability.

6. Developing novel reactor designs: New reactor configurations are being explored to improve heat management and product yield.

These research efforts aim to address the need for high-temperature stable catalysts with improved propylene selectivity and resistance to side reactions while considering the inherent thermodynamic limitations of the PDH process.
