The preparation methods, composition, and zeolite topology significantly influence the performance of zeolite-based catalysts in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions, particularly for BEA topologic and silicoaluminophosphate zeolites. Key factors contributing to improved propylene selectivity, conversion rates, and catalyst stability include:

1. Zeolite topology:
BEA topology zeolites have shown superior performance in PDH reactions due to their three-dimensional structure with 12-ring channel systems and larger pores compared to ZSM-5 zeolites [10.1002/cctc.201801708]. This structure facilitates better dispersion and stabilization of active metal species. Silicoaluminophosphate zeolites, particularly SAPO-34 with CHA topology, have demonstrated excellent shape selectivity for propylene and superior propylene selectivity compared to other zeolite types [10.1016/j.catcom.2009.07.008].

2. Preparation methods:
a) Dealumination: Removing lattice Al atoms from the zeolite framework creates vacant T-atom sites associated with silanol groups, allowing for better accommodation and anchoring of metal species [10.1002/cctc.201801708].
b) Post-synthesis modification: A two-step post-synthesis procedure involving dealumination of H-Beta and incorporation of metal species (e.g., Sn) into the framework has shown excellent results [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.12.019].
c) Sequential impregnation: This method allows for better control of metal dispersion and interaction with the support, particularly for bimetallic catalysts like PtSn [10.1021/ie901465s].

3. Composition:
a) Metal loading: Optimal metal loading varies depending on the specific catalyst system. For example, 10 wt% Zn on dealuminated β zeolite [10.1002/cctc.201801708] and 0.5 wt% Pt with 1.6-2.6 wt% Zn on Beta zeolite [10.1016/j.apcata.2006.03.028] have shown excellent performance.
b) Bimetallic systems: PtSn catalysts have demonstrated improved performance and stability compared to monometallic catalysts [10.1039/D0CS00814A].
c) Promoters: The addition of promoters like Na, K, or La can enhance catalyst performance [10.1016/j.cej.2011.11.055].

4. Acidity control:
Dealumination and framework substitution (e.g., Sn for Al) can reduce the number of strong acid sites, which effectively minimizes side reactions and enhances propylene selectivity [10.1002/cctc.201801708] [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.12.019].

5. Metal-support interactions:
Strong metal-support interactions, particularly in the case of framework-incorporated metal species (e.g., Sn in Beta zeolite), contribute to improved catalyst stability and performance [10.1016/j.jcat.2021.03.022].

6. Confinement effect:
The unique pore structure of zeolites, especially BEA and SAPO-34, provides a confinement effect that inhibits coke formation and sintering of metal particles, leading to improved catalyst stability [10.1039/C9CY01907C].

These factors collectively contribute to improved propylene selectivity (up to 99%), high conversion rates (40-70%), and excellent catalyst stability in PDH reactions. For example, Pt/Sn-Si-beta catalysts have shown propene formation rates of 2.3 mol g⁻¹Pt h⁻¹ at 550°C with >99% selectivity [10.1039/C9CY01907C], while PtSn/SAPO-34 catalysts have demonstrated propylene selectivity of 70-95% [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816_4].

2. Rational design of hierarchical zeolites: Combining micro-, meso-, and macroporous structures to further enhance mass transfer and catalyst stability.

3. Novel metal combinations: Exploring unconventional bimetallic or trimetallic systems to achieve synergistic effects in PDH catalysis.

4. Theoretical modeling: Employing advanced computational methods to predict and design optimal zeolite structures and metal distributions for PDH reactions.

5. Green synthesis routes: Developing more environmentally friendly and cost-effective methods for zeolite synthesis and modification.
