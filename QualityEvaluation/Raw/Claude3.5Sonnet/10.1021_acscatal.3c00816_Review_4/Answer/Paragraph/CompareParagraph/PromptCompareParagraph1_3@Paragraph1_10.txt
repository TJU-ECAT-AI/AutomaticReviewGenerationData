Key factors contributing to improved propylene selectivity, conversion rates, and catalyst stability include:

1. Zeolite topology:
   - BEA-type zeolites feature a three-dimensional structure with 12-ring channel systems and larger pores compared to ZSM-5, facilitating better dispersion and stabilization of metal species [10.1002/cctc.201801708].
   - SAPO-34, a silicoaluminophosphate zeolite with CHA topology, shows superior performance due to its unique structure, providing weak surface acidity and excellent shape selectivity for propylene [10.1016/j.catcom.2009.07.008].

2. Preparation methods:
   - Dealumination of Beta zeolites creates T-vacant sites and reduces acidity, which is beneficial for PDH reactions [10.1016/j.jcat.2019.12.037].
   - A two-step post-synthesis procedure involving dealumination of H-Beta and incorporation of metal species (e.g., Sn) into the framework allows for better control of metal incorporation and distribution [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.12.019].
   - Sequential impregnation methods, such as Zn ion-exchange followed by Pt impregnation, result in higher metal dispersion and improved stability [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_4].

3. Composition:
   - Optimal metal loadings vary depending on the specific catalyst system. For example, 0.5 wt% Pt and 1.6-2.6 wt% Zn showed good performance for PtZn/Beta catalysts [10.1016/j.apcata.2006.03.028].
   - Bimetallic systems, such as PtSn, show improved performance and stability compared to monometallic catalysts [10.1039/D0CS00814A].
   - The Si/Al ratio in zeolites affects catalytic performance, with intermediate ratios (8.7 ≤ nSi/nAl ≤ 26) often yielding the best results [10.1016/j.apcata.2006.03.028].

4. Acidity control:
   - Reducing Brønsted acidity or controlling its distribution within the zeolite structure can improve catalyst performance by suppressing side reactions and enhancing propylene selectivity [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_4].
   - The ratio of Lewis to Brønsted acid sites (L/B ratio) is crucial, with a high L/B ratio being beneficial for PDH [10.1016/j.jcat.2020.12.019].

5. Metal-support interactions:
   - Strong metal-support interactions contribute to improved catalyst stability and performance [10.1016/j.jcat.2021.03.022].
   - The confinement effect of metals (e.g., Sn) in the zeolite framework inhibits coke formation and sintering of active metal clusters [10.1016/j.jcat.2021.03.022].

6. Promoters:
   - The addition of promoters, such as Na, K, or La, can enhance catalyst performance by modifying acidity and improving metal dispersion [10.1016/j.cej.2011.11.055].

These factors collectively contribute to improved propylene selectivity (often >90%), high conversion rates, and excellent catalyst stability in PDH reactions. Zeolite-based catalysts have gained significant attention in the field of propane dehydrogenation (PDH) due to their unique porous structure and tunable properties. Various preparation methods, including hydrothermal synthesis, post-synthesis modification, and template-free approaches, have been employed to develop efficient zeolite catalysts for PDH reactions. Characterization techniques such as X-ray diffraction, nitrogen adsorption-desorption, and temperature-programmed desorption are commonly used to analyze the structural and chemical properties of these catalysts. Among the different zeolite types, BEA topology zeolites have shown promising results due to their large pore size and high acid site density, which facilitate propane diffusion and activation. Silicoaluminophosphate (SAPO) zeolites, on the other hand, offer enhanced hydrothermal stability and moderate acidity, making them suitable for long-term PDH operations. Comparative studies have revealed that the performance of zeolite catalysts in PDH reactions is influenced by factors such as Si/Al ratio, metal loading, and pore structure, with BEA and SAPO zeolites often exhibiting superior propylene selectivity and catalyst stability compared to other zeolite types.
