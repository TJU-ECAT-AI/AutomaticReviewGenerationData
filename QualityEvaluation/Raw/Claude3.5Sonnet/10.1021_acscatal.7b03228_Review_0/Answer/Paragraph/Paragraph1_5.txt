Geometric and electronic effects in transition-metal alloys synergistically influence catalytic performance in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) through several key mechanisms:

1. Ensemble effects: The specific arrangement of active sites on the alloy surface can significantly impact catalytic activity and selectivity. For example, the addition of Sn to Pt reduces the size of Pt ensembles, suppressing unwanted side reactions like isomerization and hydrogenolysis [10.1021/cr5002436].

2. Electronic modification: Alloying can alter the electronic structure of the active metal, changing its adsorption properties and reactivity. In PtSn alloys, electron transfer from Sn to Pt modifies the 5d band of Pt atoms, affecting its catalytic properties [10.1021/cr5002436].

3. Geometric effects: The incorporation of a second metal can change the surface geometry, affecting the coordination number and interatomic distances of active sites. This can lead to changes in adsorption energies and activation barriers for various reaction steps [10.1021/acscatal.6b02603].

4. Strain effects: Lattice mismatch between alloyed metals can induce strain, altering the d-band center and reactivity of surface atoms [10.1007/s11244-006-0005-x].

5. Ligand effects: The presence of different metal atoms in the alloy can modify the local electronic environment, changing orbital interactions and adsorption properties [10.1080/08927022.2010.481794].

6. Bifunctional mechanism: Different metal components in the alloy can play complementary roles in the catalytic process. For instance, one metal may preferentially bind the propane reactant while the other activates it [10.1007/s11244-006-0005-x].

7. Tuning of activation energies: Alloys can be designed to match the desorption activation energies of reactants and products, optimizing the catalytic performance [10.1016/j.jcat.2004.12.006].

To exploit this interplay in designing more efficient, selective, and tunable PDH catalysts, several innovative strategies can be employed:

1. Rational alloy design: Carefully select metal combinations to optimize both geometric and electronic effects for PDH. For example, PtSn alloys have shown improved selectivity and stability in propane dehydrogenation [10.1016/j.jcat.2014.07.016].

2. Controlled synthesis: Develop methods to precisely control alloy composition, particle size, and structure to create well-defined active sites for PDH [10.1021/acscatal.6b02603].

3. Intermetallic compounds: Explore ordered intermetallic phases, such as PtSn, which offer precise control over the spatial arrangement of metal atoms and can lead to enhanced selectivity in PDH [10.1021/acscatal.7b03228_0].

4. Core-shell nanostructures: Design core-shell particles with optimized surface composition for PDH while maintaining bulk properties [10.1021/j100022a002].

5. Computational modeling: Use density functional theory (DFT) calculations and machine learning algorithms to predict optimal alloy compositions and structures for PDH catalysts [10.1016/j.susc.2005.07.018].

6. In-situ characterization: Develop and utilize advanced spectroscopic techniques to study the dynamic behavior of alloy catalysts under PDH reaction conditions [10.1002/zaac.201000097].

7. Support engineering: Design catalyst supports that enhance alloy stability and complement the catalytic properties for PDH [10.1021/cr5002436].

8. Strain engineering: Manipulate lattice strain in alloy nanoparticles to fine-tune the d-band center and optimize reactivity for propane activation and hydrogen removal [10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1].

9. High-entropy alloys: Explore complex, multi-component alloy systems to achieve unique electronic and geometric properties that may enhance PDH performance [10.1021/j100022a002].

10. Dynamic alloy systems: Develop "smart" alloy catalysts that can adapt their surface composition and structure in response to reaction conditions, optimizing performance throughout the PDH process [10.1016/j.susc.2005.07.018].

By leveraging these strategies and exploiting the synergistic interplay between geometric and electronic effects in transition-metal alloys, researchers can design PDH catalysts with enhanced activity, selectivity, and stability compared to traditional monometallic systems. This approach enables the development of more efficient and sustainable processes for propylene production, addressing the growing demand for this important chemical feedstock.

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The synergistic influence of geometric and electronic effects in transition-metal alloys offers a powerful approach to designing advanced catalysts for propane dehydrogenation. By carefully manipulating these effects, researchers can create catalytic systems that outperform traditional monometallic catalysts in terms of activity, selectivity, and stability.

Looking forward, several promising research directions emerge:

1. Multi-scale modeling: Integrating atomic-scale DFT calculations with meso- and macro-scale reactor models to predict and optimize catalyst performance under industrial conditions.

2. Artificial intelligence-driven design: Leveraging machine learning and high-throughput experimentation to rapidly screen and discover novel alloy compositions with exceptional PDH performance.

3. Dynamic alloy engineering: Developing catalysts that can reversibly restructure their surface in response to reaction conditions, maintaining optimal performance throughout the catalytic cycle.

4. Hybrid organic-inorganic systems: Exploring the combination of alloy nanoparticles with organic ligands or MOFs to create unique reaction environments for PDH.

5. Sustainable catalyst design: Focusing on earth-abundant metals and environmentally friendly synthesis methods to develop more sustainable PDH catalysts.

6. Operando characterization: Advancing techniques for real-time, atomic-scale observation of catalysts under reaction conditions to elucidate structure-property relationships.

7. Multifunctional catalysts: Designing alloy systems that can perform PDH alongside other valuable transformations, such as alkane activation or CO2 utilization.

These future directions hold the potential to revolutionize propylene production, making it more efficient, sustainable, and economically viable. By continuing to unravel the complex interplay between geometric and electronic effects in alloy catalysts, researchers can pave the way for next-generation PDH technologies that meet the growing global demand for propylene while minimizing environmental impact.

<Chinese>
过渡金属合金中的几何和电子效应通过几个关键机制协同影响丙烷脱氢(PDH)的催化性能：

1. 集合效应：合金表面活性位点的特定排列可以显著影响催化活性和选择性。例如，将Sn添加到Pt中可以减小Pt集合的大小，抑制异构化和氢解等不需要的副反应 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

2. 电子修饰：合金化可以改变活性金属的电子结构，改变其吸附性质和反应性。在PtSn合金中，Sn到Pt的电子转移修改了Pt原子的5d带，影响其催化性质 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

3. 几何效应：第二种金属的引入可以改变表面几何结构，影响活性位点的配位数和原子间距。这可能导致各种反应步骤的吸附能和活化能发生变化 [10.1021/acscatal.6b02603]。

4. 应变效应：合金化金属之间的晶格失配可以诱导应变，改变表面原子的d带中心和反应性 [10.1007/s11244-006-0005-x]。

5. 配体效应：合金中不同金属原子的存在可以修改局部电子环境，改变轨道相互作用和吸附性质 [10.1080/08927022.2010.481794]。

6. 双功能机制：合金中的不同金属组分可以在催化过程中发挥互补作用。例如，一种金属可能优先结合丙烷反应物，而另一种金属则激活它 [10.1007/s11244-006-0005-x]。

7. 活化能调节：可以设计合金以匹配反应物和产物的脱附活化能，优化催化性能 [10.1016/j.jcat.2004.12.006]。

为了利用这种相互作用设计更高效、更具选择性和可调性的PDH催化剂，可以采用几种创新策略：

1. 合理的合金设计：仔细选择金属组合，以优化PDH的几何和电子效应。例如，PtSn合金在丙烷脱氢中表现出改善的选择性和稳定性 [10.1016/j.jcat.2014.07.016]。

2. 控制合成：开发精确控制合金成分、粒子大小和结构的方法，为PDH创造明确定义的活性位点 [10.1021/acscatal.6b02603]。

3. 金属间化合物：探索有序的金属间相，如PtSn，它们可以精确控制金属原子的空间排列，并可能导致PDH选择性的提高 [10.1021/acscatal.7b03228_0]。

4. 核壳纳米结构：设计具有优化表面组成的核壳粒子用于PDH，同时保持体相性质 [10.1021/j100022a002]。

5. 计算机模拟：使用密度泛函理论（DFT）计算和机器学习算法预测PDH催化剂的最佳合金组成和结构 [10.1016/j.susc.2005.07.018]。

6. 原位表征：开发和利用先进的光谱技术，在PDH反应条件下研究合金催化剂的动态行为 [10.1002/zaac.201000097]。

7. 载体工程：设计催化剂载体，提高合金稳定性并补充PDH的催化性能 [10.1021/cr5002436]。

8. 应变工程：操纵合金纳米粒子中的晶格应变，以精细调节d带中心并优化丙烷活化和氢移除的反应性 [10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1]。

9. 高熵合金：探索复杂的多组分合金系统，以实现可能增强PDH性能的独特电子和几何性质 [10.1021/j100022a002]。

10. 动态合金系统：开发"智能"合金催化剂，能够根据反应条件调整其表面组成和结构，在整个PDH过程中优化性能 [10.1016/j.susc.2005.07.018]。

通过利用这些策略并利用过渡金属合金中几何和电子效应的协同作用，研究人员可以设计出比传统单金属系统具有更高活性、选择性和稳定性的PDH催化剂。这种方法能够开发出更高效、更可持续的丙烯生产工艺，满足这种重要化学原料日益增长的需求。

综合理解与未来展望：过渡金属合金中几何和电子效应的协同影响为设计先进的丙烷脱氢催化剂提供了强大的方法。通过仔细操纵这些效应，研究人员可以创造出在活性、选择性和稳定性方面优于传统单金属催化剂的催化系统。

展望未来，几个有前景的研究方向浮现：

1. 多尺度建模：将原子尺度的DFT计算与介观和宏观尺度的反应器模型相结合，以预测和优化工业条件下的催化剂性能。

2. 人工智能驱动设计：利用机器学习和高通量实验技术快速筛选和发现具有卓越PDH性能的新型合金组成。

3. 动态合金工程：开发能够根据反应条件可逆地重构其表面的催化剂，在整个催化循环中保持最佳性能。

4. 有机-无机混合系统：探索合金纳米粒子与有机配体或MOFs的组合，为PDH创造独特的反应环境。

5. 可持续催化剂设计：专注于地球丰富金属和环境友好的合成方法，开发更可持续的PDH催化剂。

6. 原位表征：推进反应条件下催化剂实时原子尺度观察技术，阐明结构-性能关系。

7. 多功能催化剂：设计能够在PDH的同时执行其他有价值转化的合金系统，如烷烃活化或CO2利用。

这些未来方向有可能革新丙烯生产，使其更加高效、可持续和经济可行。通过继续揭示合金催化剂中几何和电子效应之间的复杂相互作用，研究人员可以为下一代PDH技术铺平道路，满足全球日益增长的丙烯需求，同时最大限度地减少环境影响。
