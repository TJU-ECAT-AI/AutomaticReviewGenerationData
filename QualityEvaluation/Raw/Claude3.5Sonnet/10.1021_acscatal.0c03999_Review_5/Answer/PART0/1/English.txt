The modification of acid sites in zeolites is crucial for optimizing catalytic performance in CO2-ODHP, particularly to selectively activate C-H bonds rather than C-C bonds. Two main approaches are used to modify acidity: dealumination and weakening methods.

Dealumination, which eliminates surface acidity, can be achieved through acid treatment or steaming treatments at high temperatures. The number of acid sites decreases monotonically with the rise of steaming temperature.

Weakening acidity methods include:
1. Tuning the Al amount (increasing Si/Al ratios)
2. Incorporating other elements (e.g., phosphorus) into the zeolite framework to displace strong Brønsted acid sites with weaker ones

These techniques aim to create a balance between acidity and catalytic activity. While the presence of strong acid sites can facilitate the conjugated effect of protons and metal oxides (e.g., gallium oxide), resulting in promoted activity, excess acid sites can promote coke formation via oligomerization and cyclization, causing deactivation.

When selecting and implementing acidity modification strategies, several key considerations emerge:

1. Balancing acidity and activity: Finding the optimal balance is crucial for maximizing performance.
2. Type of zeolite: Different zeolites (e.g., HZSM-5) respond differently to acidity modifications.
3. Stability: Some modification techniques, such as phosphorus incorporation, can improve hydrothermal stability.
4. Active metal interaction: The acidity modification technique should be compatible with the active metal component (e.g., Ga2O3) and enhance its dispersion and activity.

In general, reducing the number of acid sites appears to be more effective than merely weakening acidity for improving catalytic performance in CO2-ODHP.
