The composition of coke deposits in catalytic systems, particularly in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions, varies significantly across different reaction conditions and catalyst types. This variability is due to the complex nature of coke formation, which is influenced by numerous factors including catalyst composition, reaction conditions, and the type of reactants used.

Coke composition and location:
1. Types of coke: Studies have identified multiple types of coke, including coke on the metal, coke on the support, coke in the vicinity of the metal, and graphitic coke on the support [10.1016/s0167-2991(99)80483-2].

2. Composition: Coke deposits can consist of aliphatics, aromatics, and pregraphite species [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_1]. The composition varies depending on the catalyst type. For instance, on Pt/Al2O3 catalysts, coke deposits may consist of atomic carbon or aromatics, while on PtSn/Al2O3 catalysts, C3 species are more prevalent [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_1].

3. Location: Coke can form on both the metal site and the catalyst support surface. Coke on the metal typically contains more hydrogen and is eliminated at lower temperatures (around 325°C), while coke on the support is removed at higher temperatures (around 425°C) [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_1].

4. Morphology: On small Pt nanoparticles (1.5-2 nm), extended graphene sheets form and shift onto the support, where they undergo deformation. On larger Pt nanoparticles (5-10 nm), graphene forms encapsulating graphitic layers without shifting to the support [10.1016/j.jcat.2016.10.023].

Factors influencing coke formation:
1. Catalyst composition: The addition of promoters like Sn or Ga to Pt catalysts can significantly affect coke formation and distribution. For example, Ga-containing catalysts show reduced coke deposition compared to Sn-containing catalysts [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016].

2. Reaction conditions: Higher temperatures and propene partial pressures generally increase coke formation, while the presence of hydrogen tends to suppress it [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361].

3. Reactant type: Propene generally produces more coke deposits than propane, regardless of the presence or absence of O2 and/or NO [10.1163/156856798x00122].

4. Time on stream: Different types of coke evolve at different rates. For instance, coke on or near the metal may reach a constant level after about 15 hours, while coke on the carrier continues to increase [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361].

Analytical techniques:
Several analytical techniques have been employed to elucidate the complex nature and diversity of coke constituents in PDH systems:

1. Temperature Programmed Oxidation (TPO): This is the most commonly used technique for characterizing coke deposits. It allows for the identification of different types of coke based on their oxidation temperatures [10.1016/s0167-2991(99)80483-2].

2. Raman Spectroscopy: Used for structural characterization and determination of graphene disorder in coke deposits [10.1039/c3cp50646k].

3. Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA): Employed to quantify the amount of coke deposited on catalysts and determine the temperature at which it combusts [10.1039/c3cp50646k].

4. Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM): Used for morphological analysis and visualization of coke deposits [10.1016/j.jcat.2016.10.023].

5. Gas Chromatography combined with Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS): Utilized to identify detailed components of coke deposits [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_1].

6. Elemental Analysis: Used to determine the H/C ratio of coke deposits [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8].

7. Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) Spectroscopy: Employed to identify different functional groups in coke deposits [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8].

These techniques collectively provide a comprehensive understanding of the coke composition, structure, and formation mechanisms under various reaction conditions and catalyst types in PDH systems.

   a) Aliphatics, aromatics, and pregraphite species [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_1]

   c) Extended graphene sheets, disordered layers, and entangled spaghetti-like bundles [10.1016/j.jcat.2016.10.023]

3. Location of coke deposits:
   a) On the metal surface
   b) On the catalyst support
   c) In the vicinity of the metal [10.1016/s0167-2991(99)80483-2]

Factors influencing coke composition and formation:
1. Catalyst composition: 
   - Ga-containing catalysts show reduced coke deposition compared to Sn-containing catalysts [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016]
   - PtSn alloy catalysts exhibit distinct C3 species as carbonaceous deposits [10.1002/adsu.202000092]

2. Reaction conditions:
   - Higher temperatures increase coke formation and shift TPO peak temperatures upwards [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361]
   - Higher propene partial pressure increases coke formation [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361]
   - Hydrogen presence suppresses coke formation but cannot remove already formed coke [10.1006/jcat.1996.0361]

3. Catalyst particle size:
   - Small Pt nanoparticles (1.5-2 nm) lead to extended graphene sheets that shift onto the support
   - Larger Pt nanoparticles (5-10 nm) form encapsulating graphitic layers [10.1016/j.jcat.2016.10.023]

Analytical techniques employed:
1. Temperature Programmed Oxidation (TPO): The most widely used technique for characterizing coke deposits, providing information on coke location and oxidation behavior [10.1016/s0167-2991(99)80483-2]

2. Raman Spectroscopy: Used to study the structural characteristics of coke deposits, particularly the degree of graphitization and disorder [10.1039/c3cp50646k]

3. Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA): Quantifies the amount of coke deposited and determines combustion temperatures [10.1039/c3cp50646k]

4. Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM): Provides morphological analysis and visualization of coke deposits [10.1016/j.jcat.2016.10.023]

5. Elemental Analysis: Determines the H/C ratio of coke deposits [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]

6. Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) Spectroscopy: Identifies different functional groups in the coke [10.1007/s11244-011-9708-8]

7. Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS): Used to identify detailed components of coke deposits [10.1021/acscatal.1c00331\_1]

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook:

5. Sustainable regeneration methods: Developing more efficient and environmentally friendly catalyst regeneration techniques based on the detailed understanding of coke composition and structure.
