Hierarchical zeolites are advanced materials that combine micropores and mesopores/macropores to enhance accessibility for propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions. These materials offer significant improvements over traditional zeolites by addressing limitations in mass transfer and diffusion while maintaining the intrinsic catalytic properties of microporous structures.

Types and Synthesis Methods:
1. Micro-mesoporous zeolites: Contain micropores and mesopores
2. Micro-macroporous zeolites: Contain micropores and macropores
3. Micro-meso-macroporous zeolites: Contain micropores, mesopores, and macropores [10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016]

Synthesis methods can be broadly categorized into two approaches:
1. Post-treatment synthetic routes: These involve selective desilication or dealumination processes to create larger meso- or macropores by sacrificing part of the zeolite crystals [10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016].
2. In situ synthetic routes: These use organic or inorganic templates during zeolite crystallization [10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016].

Specific synthesis methods include:
- Desilication: Treatment of ZSM-5 with NaOH solution under mild conditions [10.1016/j.cjche.2021.11.020]
- Self-assembly: Enwrapping zeolite crystals with ordered mesoporous silica (OMS) [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5]
- Use of structure-directing agents: For example, tetra-(n-butyl)phosphonium hydroxide (TBPOH) for hierarchical silicalite-1 nanosheets [10.3390/catal9020174]
- Repetitive branching synthesis strategy [10.1039/D0CS01260B]
- Utilization of long-chain alkyl structure-directing agents [10.1039/D0CS01260B]

Performance Improvements in PDH Reactions:
Hierarchical zeolites significantly enhance PDH reaction performance compared to traditional zeolites:

1. Enhanced accessibility: The combination of micropores and mesopores/macropores improves accessibility to active sites [10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016].

2. Improved mass transfer: The hierarchical structure accelerates mass transport and reduces diffusion limitations [10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016].

3. Higher conversion and selectivity: For example, PtSn supported on hierarchical ZSM-5 exhibits an initial propane conversion of 50% and propylene selectivity of 95%, compared to 30% conversion and 65% selectivity for parent microporous ZSM-5 [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5].

4. Increased catalytic activity: Pt catalysts supported on hierarchical S-1 zeolites show a much higher turnover frequency (TOF) of 1112.2 h⁻¹ compared to 157.6 h⁻¹ for bulk S-1 supports [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5].

5. Better metal dispersion: The mesoporous structure improves the dispersion of metal species due to an increased number of defect sites [10.3390/catal9020174].

6. Enhanced stability: The hierarchical structure helps maintain catalyst stability over longer periods by preventing metal sintering and coking [10.1016/j.cjche.2021.11.020].

7. Tailored acidity: Hierarchical zeolites can be designed with specific acid site distributions, such as lower concentrations of Brønsted acid sites and more Lewis acid sites, leading to improved PDH performance [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5].

8. Confinement effect: Mesopores can confine and stabilize metal particles, allowing them to remain stable at high temperatures during the reaction [10.1016/j.cjche.2021.11.020].

In summary, hierarchical zeolites offer a promising approach to improving PDH reaction performance by combining the benefits of microporous and mesoporous/macroporous structures. These materials address the limitations of traditional zeolites while maintaining their intrinsic catalytic properties, resulting in higher conversion rates, improved selectivity, and enhanced catalyst stability.

The main types of hierarchical zeolites include:
1. Micro-mesoporous zeolites
2. Micro-macroporous zeolites
3. Micro-meso-macroporous zeolites
4. Mesoporous zeolites (e.g., hierarchical ZSM-5, silicalite-1 nanosheets)
5. Hierarchical zeolite composites (e.g., TS-1@SBA-16)
6. Dealuminated zeolites (e.g., beta zeolite)

Synthesis methods for hierarchical zeolites include:
1. Post-treatment synthetic routes:
   a. Desilication (e.g., NaOH treatment of ZSM-5) [10.1016/j.cjche.2021.11.020]
   b. Dealumination
   c. Selective etching with alkaline or fluoride agents [10.1002/adma.201803966]
2. In situ synthetic routes:
   a. Use of structure-directing agents (SDAs) like TBPOH for hierarchical silicalite-1 nanosheets [10.3390/catal9020174]
   b. Use of multi-ammonium surfactants (e.g., C18-6-4) [10.1038/s41586-020-2671-4]
   c. Self-assembly and enwrapping of zeolite crystals with ordered mesoporous silica [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5]
3. Repetitive branching synthesis strategy [10.1039/D0CS01260B]
4. Bottom-up and top-down methods [10.1002/adma.201803966]

Hierarchical zeolites improve PDH reaction performance compared to traditional zeolites in several ways:
1. Enhanced accessibility to active sites and improved mass transfer [10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016]
2. Better dispersion and stability of metal species [10.3390/catal9020174]

6. Confinement effect for stabilizing metal nanoparticles [10.1016/j.cjche.2021.11.020]
7. Tailored acid site distributions for improved catalytic performance [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5]

For example, PtSn supported on hierarchical ZSM-5 exhibits an initial propane conversion of 50% and propylene selectivity of 95%, compared to 30% conversion and 65% selectivity for conventional ZSM-5 [10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5].
