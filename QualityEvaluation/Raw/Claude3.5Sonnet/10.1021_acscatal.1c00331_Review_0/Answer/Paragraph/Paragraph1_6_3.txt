Catalytic direct dehydrogenation of propane (PDH) is an important method for propene production, addressing the growing demand for propylene that traditional methods like steam cracking and fluid catalytic cracking cannot fully meet. The process involves the conversion of propane to propene and hydrogen using specific catalysts.

Key considerations for PDH include:

1. Reaction conditions: PDH is highly endothermic (ΔH298 = 124.3 kJ/mol) and requires high temperatures (550-750°C) and low pressures to achieve economically viable conversions [Placeholder_Of_DOI1]. These conditions are necessary due to thermodynamic limitations, but they also promote side reactions and coke formation.

2. Catalyst types: The main industrial catalysts are platinum-based (Pt) and chromium oxide-based (CrOx) supported on Al2O3. Pt-based catalysts offer high activity and selectivity but are expensive and prone to deactivation. CrOx catalysts are cheaper but raise environmental concerns due to the toxicity of Cr(VI) species [Placeholder_Of_DOI2].

3. Industrial applications: PDH has become increasingly important for on-purpose propylene production, especially with the availability of low-cost propane from shale gas. Commercial processes like Oleflex (UOP) and Catofin use Pt-based and CrOx-based catalysts, respectively [Placeholder_Of_DOI3].

Coke formation on Pt-based catalysts significantly impacts process efficiency by causing rapid catalyst deactivation, reducing propylene yield, and necessitating frequent catalyst regeneration. The extent of coking depends on various factors, including catalyst particle size, reaction conditions, and catalyst composition [Placeholder_Of_DOI4].

Strategies to mitigate coke formation and improve catalyst regeneration include:

1. Catalyst modification: Alloying Pt with other metals (e.g., Sn, Zn, Ga) to reduce coke formation and improve selectivity [Placeholder_Of_DOI5].

2. Optimizing reaction conditions: Controlling temperature, pressure, and feed composition to minimize coke formation while maintaining high propylene yields [Placeholder_Of_DOI6].

3. Developing novel catalyst formulations: Exploring single-atom catalysts (SACs) or intermetallic compounds to inhibit structure-sensitive side reactions that lead to coke formation [Placeholder_Of_DOI7].

4. Implementing efficient regeneration techniques: Using oxidative regeneration, often with the presence of chlorine for Pt catalysts, to remove coke and restore catalyst activity [Placeholder_Of_DOI8].

5. Co-feeding strategies: Adding H2 or CO2 to the feed to suppress coke formation and improve catalyst stability [Placeholder_Of_DOI9].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The advancements in PDH catalysis, particularly in addressing the critical challenge of coke formation on Pt-based catalysts, point towards a future of more efficient and sustainable propylene production. The development of novel catalyst designs, such as single-atom catalysts and intermetallic compounds, shows promise in overcoming the limitations of traditional Pt-based systems. These innovations could lead to catalysts with improved stability, higher selectivity, and reduced coking rates, potentially revolutionizing the PDH process.

Future research endeavors might focus on:

1. Advanced in-situ characterization techniques to better understand the mechanisms of coke formation and catalyst deactivation at the atomic level.
2. Computational modeling and machine learning approaches to predict and design optimal catalyst compositions and structures for minimal coking.
3. Development of hybrid processes that combine PDH with other technologies (e.g., membrane reactors or in-situ product removal) to push the boundaries of conversion and selectivity.
4. Exploration of bio-based or renewable feedstocks for propane production, leading to a more sustainable PDH process.
5. Integration of PDH processes with renewable energy sources to address the high energy requirements and reduce the carbon footprint of propylene production.

These directions could pave the way for next-generation PDH technologies that are more efficient, environmentally friendly, and economically viable, addressing the growing global demand for propylene while minimizing the challenges associated with catalyst coking and deactivation.

PDH的主要考虑因素包括：

1. 反应条件：PDH是高度吸热的（ΔH298 = 124.3 kJ/mol），需要高温（550-750°C）和低压才能实现经济可行的转化率 [Placeholder_Of_DOI1]。这些条件是由于热力学限制而必需的，但它们也促进了副反应和焦炭形成。

2. 催化剂类型：主要的工业催化剂是以Al2O3为载体的铂基（Pt）和氧化铬基（CrOx）催化剂。Pt基催化剂具有高活性和选择性，但价格昂贵且容易失活。CrOx催化剂较便宜，但由于Cr(VI)物种的毒性而引起环境问题 [Placeholder_Of_DOI2]。

3. 工业应用：PDH已经成为越来越重要的定向丙烯生产方法，特别是在低成本页岩气丙烷可用的情况下。商业过程如Oleflex（UOP）和Catofin分别使用Pt基和CrOx基催化剂 [Placeholder_Of_DOI3]。

Pt基催化剂上的焦炭形成显著影响过程效率，导致催化剂快速失活，降低丙烯产率，并需要频繁的催化剂再生。焦炭的程度取决于各种因素，包括催化剂粒子大小、反应条件和催化剂组成 [Placeholder_Of_DOI4]。

减少焦炭形成和改善催化剂再生的策略包括：

1. 催化剂改性：将Pt与其他金属（如Sn、Zn、Ga）合金化，以减少焦炭形成并提高选择性 [Placeholder_Of_DOI5]。

2. 优化反应条件：控制温度、压力和进料组成，以最小化焦炭形成，同时保持高丙烯产率 [Placeholder_Of_DOI6]。

3. 开发新型催化剂配方：探索单原子催化剂（SACs）或金属间化合物，以抑制导致焦炭形成的结构敏感性副反应 [Placeholder_Of_DOI7]。

4. 实施有效的再生技术：对Pt催化剂使用氧化再生，通常在氯气存在下，以去除焦炭并恢复催化剂活性 [Placeholder_Of_DOI8]。

5. 共进料策略：向进料中添加H2或CO2以抑制焦炭形成并提高催化剂稳定性 [Placeholder_Of_DOI9]。

未来的研究努力可能集中在：

1. 先进的原位表征技术，以更好地理解原子水平上焦炭形成和催化剂失活的机理。

4. 探索生物基或可再生原料生产丙烷，从而实现更可持续的PDH过程。
5. 将PDH过程与可再生能源源集成，以解决高能耗问题并减少丙烯生产的碳足迹。

这些方向可能为下一代PDH技术铺平道路，使其更加高效、环保和经济可行，满足不断增长的全球丙烯需求，同时最大限度地减少与催化剂焦炭和失活相关的挑战。
