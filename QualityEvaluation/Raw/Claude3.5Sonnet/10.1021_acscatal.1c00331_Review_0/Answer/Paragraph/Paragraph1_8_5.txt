The process involves the conversion of propane to propene and hydrogen using specific catalysts. Key considerations for PDH include:

1. Reaction conditions: PDH is a highly endothermic reaction (ΔH298 = 124.3 kJ/mol) that typically operates at high temperatures (550-750°C) and low pressures to achieve economically viable conversions [Placeholder_Of_DOI1]. 2. Catalyst types: The main industrial catalysts are Pt-based and CrOx-based. Pt catalysts offer high activity and selectivity but are expensive and prone to deactivation. CrOx catalysts are cheaper but raise environmental concerns due to the toxicity of Cr(VI) species [Placeholder_Of_DOI2]. Researchers are exploring alternative catalysts, such as vanadium, gallium, and zirconium-based materials, to address these limitations.

Coke formation on Pt-based catalysts significantly impacts process efficiency by causing rapid catalyst deactivation, reducing propylene selectivity, and necessitating frequent regeneration cycles. 5. Regeneration techniques: Implementing efficient regeneration cycles, such as oxidative treatment followed by reduction, to remove coke and restore catalyst activity [Placeholder_Of_DOI9].

6. Support modification: Using supports with low acidity or modifying them to minimize coke formation on acid sites [Placeholder_Of_DOI10].

By implementing these strategies, researchers aim to develop more efficient and stable catalysts for PDH, improving the overall process efficiency and economics of propene production.
