Catalytic direct dehydrogenation of propane (PDH) is an important method for propene production, addressing the growing demand for propylene that traditional methods like steam cracking and fluid catalytic cracking cannot meet. The process involves the conversion of propane to propene and hydrogen using specific catalysts.

Key considerations for PDH include:

1. Reaction conditions: PDH is highly endothermic (ΔH298 = 124.3 kJ/mol) and requires high temperatures (typically 550-750°C) and low pressures to achieve economically viable conversions [Placeholder_Of_DOI1]. These conditions are necessary due to thermodynamic limitations and to shift the equilibrium towards propene formation.

2. Catalyst types: The main industrial catalysts are Pt-based and CrOx-based, supported on materials like Al2O3. Pt-based catalysts offer high activity and stability but are expensive and prone to coke formation. CrOx-based catalysts are cheaper but raise environmental concerns due to the toxicity of Cr(VI) species [Placeholder_Of_DOI2]. Researchers are exploring alternative catalysts, such as gallium oxide, vanadium-based, and zirconia-based materials, to address these limitations.

3. Industrial applications: PDH has become increasingly important for on-purpose propylene production, especially with the availability of low-cost propane from shale gas. Commercial processes like Oleflex (UOP) and Catofin use Pt-based and Cr-based catalysts, respectively [Placeholder_Of_DOI3].

Coke formation on Pt-based catalysts significantly impacts process efficiency by causing rapid catalyst deactivation, reducing propylene selectivity, and necessitating frequent regeneration cycles. This deactivation occurs in two stages: quick deactivation followed by a steady state [Placeholder_Of_DOI3].

Strategies to mitigate coke formation and improve catalyst regeneration include:

1. Catalyst modification: Alloying Pt with elements like Sn, Zn, or In to reduce coke formation and improve stability [Placeholder_Of_DOI4].

2. Optimizing particle size: Larger Pt particles (e.g., 21 nm) show lower coking rates compared to smaller ones (3 nm) [Placeholder_Of_DOI5].

3. Support modification: Using supports with low Brønsted acidity or poisoning acid sites to reduce coke formation [Placeholder_Of_DOI6].

4. Reaction condition optimization: Co-feeding H2 or CO2 to suppress coke precursor formation and facilitate coke removal [Placeholder_Of_DOI3].

5. Advanced regeneration techniques: Employing oxychlorination (using air and chlorine) for Pt catalysts to redisperse sintered Pt particles [Placeholder_Of_DOI3].

6. Novel catalyst designs: Developing single-atom catalysts or intermetallic compounds to isolate active sites and inhibit coke-forming reactions [Placeholder_Of_DOI7].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The development of PDH technology has seen significant advancements in catalyst design and process optimization. However, challenges remain in balancing high activity, selectivity, and stability while minimizing coke formation. Future research directions may include:

1. Advanced materials: Exploring novel support materials or nanostructured catalysts that can better resist coking and maintain high dispersion of active sites.

2. In-situ characterization: Developing techniques for real-time monitoring of catalyst performance and coke formation to enable more efficient process control and optimization.

3. Machine learning and computational modeling: Utilizing these tools to predict catalyst behavior, optimize formulations, and design more efficient regeneration protocols.

4. Green chemistry approaches: Investigating environmentally friendly catalysts and processes that minimize waste and energy consumption while maintaining high propylene yields.

5. Process intensification: Exploring novel reactor designs or hybrid processes that could overcome thermodynamic limitations and improve overall efficiency.

These future directions could lead to more sustainable and economically viable PDH processes, addressing the growing global demand for propylene while minimizing environmental impact.

PDH的关键考虑因素包括：

1. 反应条件：PDH是高度吸热的（ΔH298 = 124.3 kJ/mol），需要高温（通常为550-750°C）和低压才能达到经济可行的转化率[Placeholder_Of_DOI1]。这些条件是由于热力学限制和需要将平衡移向丙烯形成而必要的。

2. 催化剂类型：主要的工业催化剂是基于Pt和CrOx的，负载在Al2O3等材料上。基于Pt的催化剂活性高、稳定性好，但价格昂贵且容易形成积碳。基于CrOx的催化剂更便宜，但由于Cr(VI)物种的毒性而引起环境问题[Placeholder_Of_DOI2]。研究人员正在探索替代催化剂，如氧化镓、钒基和氧化锆基材料，以解决这些限制。

3. 工业应用：PDH在专门生产丙烯方面变得越来越重要，特别是随着页岩气提供的低成本丙烷的可用性。商业工艺如Oleflex（UOP）和Catofin分别使用基于Pt和Cr的催化剂[Placeholder_Of_DOI3]。

基于Pt催化剂上的积碳形成显著影响工艺效率，导致催化剂快速失活，降低丙烯选择性，并需要频繁再生循环。这种失活分两个阶段发生：快速失活后接着是稳态[Placeholder_Of_DOI3]。

缓解积碳形成和改善催化剂再生的策略包括：

1. 催化剂改性：将Pt与Sn、Zn或In等元素合金化，以减少积碳形成并提高稳定性[Placeholder_Of_DOI4]。

2. 优化粒径：较大的Pt粒子（如21 nm）相比较小的粒子（3 nm）表现出较低的积碳速率[Placeholder_Of_DOI5]。

3. 载体改性：使用低Brønsted酸性载体或毒化酸性位点以减少积碳形成[Placeholder_Of_DOI6]。

4. 反应条件优化：共进料H2或CO2以抑制积碳前体形成并促进积碳去除[Placeholder_Of_DOI3]。

5. 先进再生技术：对Pt催化剂使用氧氯化（使用空气和氯气）以重新分散烧结的Pt粒子[Placeholder_Of_DOI3]。
