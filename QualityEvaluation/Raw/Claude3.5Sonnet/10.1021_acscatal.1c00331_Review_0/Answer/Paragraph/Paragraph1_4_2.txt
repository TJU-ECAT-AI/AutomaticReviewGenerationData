Catalytic direct dehydrogenation of propane (PDH) is an important method for propene production, addressing the growing demand for propylene that traditional methods like steam cracking and fluid catalytic cracking struggle to meet. The process involves the conversion of propane to propene and hydrogen using specific catalysts.

Key considerations for PDH include:

1. Reaction conditions: PDH is a highly endothermic reaction requiring high temperatures (typically 550-750°C) and low pressures to achieve economically viable conversions. These conditions are necessary due to thermodynamic limitations, with ΔH298 = 124.3 kJ/mol [Placeholder_Of_DOI1].

2. Catalyst types: Commercially used catalysts are primarily based on platinum (Pt) or chromium oxide (CrOx). Pt-based catalysts offer high activity but are expensive and prone to coking, while CrOx catalysts are cheaper but raise environmental concerns due to the toxicity of Cr(VI) species [Placeholder_Of_DOI2].

3. Industrial applications: PDH has become increasingly important for on-purpose propylene production, especially with the availability of low-cost propane from shale gas. Commercial processes like Oleflex and Catofin have been widely implemented [Placeholder_Of_DOI3].

Coke formation on Pt-based catalysts significantly impacts process efficiency by:

1. Causing rapid catalyst deactivation, leading to decreased propylene yield and selectivity.
2. Necessitating frequent catalyst regeneration, which increases operational costs and complexity.
3. Potentially causing irreversible damage to the catalyst structure over multiple regeneration cycles.

Strategies to mitigate coke formation and improve catalyst regeneration include:

1. Catalyst design: 
   - Alloying Pt with other metals (e.g., Sn, In, Ga) to form bimetallic catalysts that suppress coke formation [Placeholder_Of_DOI4].
   - Developing single-atom catalysts (SACs) to isolate active sites and inhibit structure-sensitive side reactions [Placeholder_Of_DOI5].
   - Optimizing particle size, with larger Pt particles (e.g., 21 nm) showing lower coking rates compared to smaller ones (3 nm) [Placeholder_Of_DOI6].

2. Reaction conditions:
   - Co-feeding H2 or CO2 to suppress coke precursor formation and facilitate coke removal via the reverse Boudouard reaction [Placeholder_Of_DOI7].
   - Controlling reaction temperature and pressure to balance conversion and coke formation.

3. Support modification:
   - Using supports with low Brønsted acidity or poisoning acid sites to reduce coke formation [Placeholder_Of_DOI8].
   - Employing supports that facilitate coke migration away from active sites (e.g., CeO2) [Placeholder_Of_DOI9].

4. Regeneration techniques:
   - Implementing continuous catalyst regeneration (CCR) technology to maintain stable catalyst activity over extended periods [Placeholder_Of_DOI10].
   - Using oxychlorination (air + chlorine) followed by reduction to effectively redisperse sintered Pt particles [Placeholder_Of_DOI11].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The field of catalytic propane dehydrogenation is rapidly evolving, with significant advances in catalyst design and process optimization. Future research directions may focus on:

1. Advanced materials: Developing novel catalyst formulations, such as single-atom alloys or intermetallic compounds, that combine high activity, selectivity, and coke resistance.

2. In-situ characterization: Utilizing advanced spectroscopic techniques to gain real-time insights into coke formation mechanisms and catalyst deactivation processes.

3. Machine learning and AI: Applying data-driven approaches to optimize catalyst compositions and reaction conditions for improved performance and longevity.

4. Green chemistry: Exploring environmentally friendly alternatives to traditional Pt and Cr catalysts, such as earth-abundant metal catalysts or metal-free catalytic systems.

5. Process intensification: Investigating innovative reactor designs or hybrid processes that combine PDH with other reactions to enhance overall efficiency and reduce energy consumption.

6. Circular economy: Developing catalysts and processes that facilitate the use of renewable feedstocks or enable the efficient recycling of plastic waste into propylene.

3. 可能在多次再生循环后对催化剂结构造成不可逆损害。
