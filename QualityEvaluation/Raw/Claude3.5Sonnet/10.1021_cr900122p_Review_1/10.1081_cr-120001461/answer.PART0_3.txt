The oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) of alkanes, specifically n-butane, involves a complex interplay between various catalytic systems and reaction factors. Both unsupported and supported catalysts are used in this process, with bulk metal oxides and supported systems playing crucial roles. The efficiency of the ODH process is influenced by multiple factors, including catalyst composition, reducibility, oxygen reactivity and availability, nature of oxygen species involved, electrical conductivity of the catalyst, and surface basicity.

Achieving high selectivity and conversion simultaneously remains a significant challenge in ODH. The process efficiency is governed by the nature of active and selective sites on the catalyst surface. The reaction mechanisms proposed for ODH typically involve C-H bond activation, though the literature does not explicitly mention M-alkyl intermediates or 2H-abstraction. However, the review emphasizes the importance of hydrocarbon activation processes.

However, the focus on catalyst composition, nature of active sites, and factors affecting selectivity suggests that controlling the distribution and isolation of active sites on the catalyst surface could be a strategy to improve selectivity.

Kinetic studies and reaction mechanism investigations play a crucial role in understanding and optimizing the ODH process. These studies help elucidate the complex relationships between catalyst properties, reaction conditions, and process outcomes.

In conclusion, the ODH of alkanes involves a delicate balance between various catalytic and mechanistic factors. Optimizing catalyst design, understanding reaction mechanisms, and controlling reaction conditions are key to improving the overall efficiency of the process, particularly in terms of achieving both high selectivity and conversion.
