The oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) of alkanes over supported vanadia catalysts is a complex process influenced by multiple factors. Supported vanadia catalysts are widely used for hydrocarbon oxidation reactions, including ODH of alkanes to olefins. The efficiency of the ODH process is affected by the catalyst composition, surface coverage, and additives.

For V2O5/Al2O3 catalysts, the turnover frequency (TOF) for propane and butane ODH increases with surface vanadia coverage, suggesting that factors beyond simple site density play a role. This could include effects from lateral interactions or changes in surface acidity. In contrast, for V2O5/TiO2 catalysts, the TOF for propane ODH is independent of surface coverage, indicating that only one surface vanadia site is required for this reaction.

The selectivity of the ODH process can be influenced by additives. Basic interacting additives enhance propylene selectivity during propane ODH. This suggests that modifying the acid-base properties of the catalyst surface can be a strategy for improving selectivity.

The literature does not explicitly discuss site isolation or detailed reaction mechanisms for ODH. However, the observation that only one surface vanadia site is required for propane ODH on titania supports suggests that isolated sites may be sufficient for this reaction. This could potentially address selectivity issues by preventing over-oxidation that might occur on larger ensembles of active sites.

The challenges in achieving high selectivity and conversion in ODH are not directly addressed in the given literature. However, the discussion of the effects of surface coverage and additives implies that balancing these factors is crucial for optimizing catalyst performance. The ability to tune catalyst properties through support choice, vanadia loading, and additives provides multiple levers for addressing these challenges.

Overall, the ODH of alkanes over supported vanadia catalysts involves a complex interplay between the nature of the active site, surface coverage, support effects, and additives. Understanding and controlling these factors is key to developing more efficient and selective ODH catalysts.
