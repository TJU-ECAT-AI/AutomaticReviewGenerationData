The growing demand for propylene has significantly influenced the development of new production methods, particularly propane dehydrogenation (PDH). Global propylene consumption reached 90 million tons in 2015 and is expected to increase to 191 megatons by 2030, creating a substantial gap between supply and demand [10.1002/cctc.201500916]. This gap, coupled with the increased availability of propane from shale gas production, has led to a surge in interest in PDH technology, which now accounts for 10% of global propylene production [10.1002/advs.202207756_0].

The understanding of Ga/H-ZSM-5 catalysts for PDH has evolved considerably over time, but there remains an ongoing debate about the nature of active Ga species. Various Ga species have been proposed as the active center for PDH, including Ga+, [GaO]+, [Ga(OH)2]+, [GaOH]2+, [GaH]2+, [GaH2]+, and Ga+H+ pair [10.1002/advs.202207756_0]. This diversity of proposed active species reflects the complexity of the catalyst system and the challenges in definitively identifying the active sites.

Recent research has contributed significantly to our current knowledge of these catalysts. Notably, studies combining in situ transmission Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, pulse titration studies, and PDH kinetics have demonstrated that both Ga speciation and PDH performance are dependent on the Ga/Al ratios and Al distributions within the zeolites [10.1002/advs.202207756_0]. This finding highlights the importance of considering not just the presence of Ga species, but also their interaction with the zeolite structure and composition.

Lercher's group found that in Ga/H-ZSM-5 catalysts, there is a very low enthalpy barrier of 56 kJ·mol^-1 for heterolytically activating the propane C-H bond. This activation occurs on [GaH]2+ species in conjunction with a basic framework oxygen of the Brønsted-acid site [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012]. However, other researchers have proposed alternative active species. For instance, Maeno et al. investigated In-CHA catalysts and suggested that [InH2]+ ions are the catalytic active sites for PDH reactions, showing better performance than [GaH]2+ sites [10.1021/accountsmr.0c00012].

The role of Brønsted acid sites has been further investigated, as they are considered to catalyze undesired reactions such as cracking, dehydrocyclization, and coking. To address this issue, researchers have synthesized aluminum-free gallosilicate MFI catalysts without Ga introduction to modify the acidity caused by the presence of aluminum [10.1039/d0cs00983k].

Despite these advancements, the precise nature of the active sites under reaction conditions and the roles of Brønsted and Lewis acid sites in Ga-containing MFI catalysts remain subjects of ongoing research and debate in the scientific community [10.1039/d0cs00983k].

The understanding of Ga/H-ZSM-5 catalysts for PDH has evolved significantly over time. However, research has shown that Ga can catalyze propane dehydrogenation through various species.

There is an ongoing debate about the nature of active Ga species in PDH catalysts. Different studies have proposed various active sites:

1. Tetrahedrally coordinated Ga3+ species are considered active sites for C-H activation [10.1039/d1gc03700e].
2. [GaH]2+ ions, together with a basic framework oxygen of the Brønsted-acid site, have been identified as active sites [10.1039/d0cs00814a].
3. Lewis(Ga+)-Brønsted acid pairs have been proposed as active sites for propane dehydrogenation over Ga/H-ZSM-5 [10.1039/d0cs00983k].
4. Bell and coworkers believe that single [GaH]2+ sites are the active species, while Schreiber et al. reported that Ga+H+ pair sites are the stable active sites [10.1039/d1sc05866e].

Recent research findings have contributed to our current knowledge of these catalysts:

1. The Ga speciation and PDH performance are dependent on both Ga/Al ratios and Al distributions of the zeolites [10.1002/advs.202207756_0].

3. Lowering surface acidity can enhance catalytic performance. For example, the addition of 3-mercaptopropyl-trimethoxysilane (MPS) to Ga/MFI catalysts reduces Brønsted acid sites and increases Lewis acid sites, resulting in improved performance [10.1039/d1gc03700e].
