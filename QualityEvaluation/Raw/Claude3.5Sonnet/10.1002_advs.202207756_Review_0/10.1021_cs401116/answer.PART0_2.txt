The growing demand for propylene has driven the development of new production methods, particularly propane dehydrogenation (PDH). This is evident from the increasing focus on more efficient utilization of hydrocarbon resources and the critical importance of olefin production for various industries.

Traditional methods of olefin production include thermal cracking and catalytic dehydrogenation using supported Pt metal or Cr oxide catalysts. These processes operate at high temperatures above 450°C to achieve favorable thermodynamics for olefin yields.

The understanding of Ga/H-ZSM-5 catalysts for PDH has evolved over time. Initially, single-site Zn(II) and Ga(III) ions in MFI zeolite were suggested to catalyze alkane dehydrogenation. However, the presence of Brønsted acid sites in these catalysts complicated the analysis of the independent catalytic function of the exchanged cations.

The ongoing debate about active Ga species stems from the difficulty in isolating their specific role. This is because the initially formed olefins rapidly react at the Brønsted sites, undergoing various transformations such as oligomerization, isomerization, cracking, and aromatization. This complexity has made it challenging to demonstrate the independent catalytic function of the exchanged cations, including Ga species.
