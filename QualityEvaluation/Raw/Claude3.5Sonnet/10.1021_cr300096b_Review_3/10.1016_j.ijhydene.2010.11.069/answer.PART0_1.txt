The literature mentions several synthesis methods for supported bimetallic catalysts:

1. Polyol synthesis process with oleic acid as surfactant
2. Simple redox method using oleic acid as surfactant  
3. Chemical impregnation method
4. Hydrothermal method
5. Template-replacement synthesis
6. Reverse-microemulsion method

The polyol and redox methods using oleic acid as a surfactant appear to be the main focus of this study. These methods produce small nanoparticles (2-4 nm) with core-shell structures, which exhibit high catalytic activity for hydrogen generation from ammonia borane hydrolysis. The use of oleic acid helps control particle size and dispersion.

The chemical impregnation method produced larger particles (5-20 nm). The hydrothermal and template-replacement methods created hollow sphere structures. The reverse-microemulsion method made nanosized particles.

Each method has advantages for specific applications. The polyol/redox methods with surfactants seem particularly effective for producing small, well-dispersed nanoparticles with controlled structures. This results in high catalytic activity. Other methods may be useful for creating different morphologies or larger particles as needed for various catalytic applications.

Overall, the choice of synthesis method significantly impacts the size, structure, and performance of the resulting catalyst. The polyol/redox methods highlighted in this study appear especially promising for producing highly active supported bimetallic nanoparticle catalysts.
