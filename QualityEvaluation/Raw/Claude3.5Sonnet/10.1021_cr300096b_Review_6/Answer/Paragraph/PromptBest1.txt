Experimental techniques, particularly High-Resolution Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy (HREELS) and Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES), have been instrumental in verifying and quantifying the oxygen-induced surface segregation of subsurface 3d atoms (particularly Ni and Co) in Pt-3d-Pt(111) bimetallic systems.

HREELS has been utilized to monitor the segregation of Ni and Co in Pt3dPt(111) subsurface configurations when exposed to oxygen. The onset of characteristic ν(NiO) and ν(CoO) vibrational modes confirms the segregation of subsurface Ni and Co after exposing the subsurface Pt3dPt(111) configuration to oxygen at different temperatures [10.1021/cr300096b_6]. This technique provides a qualitative verification of the segregation process.

AES measurements have been employed to quantify the segregation kinetics. The AES intensity ratios of Co/Pt or Ni/Pt were used to monitor the interchange between the subsurface and surface structures after exposure to oxygen [10.1021/cr300096b_6]. For instance, the normalized Ni/Pt AES ratio for an initially Pt-Ni-Pt(111) surface increases as a function of oxygen exposure, indicating that subsurface Ni atoms start to segregate to the surface due to the strong interaction of the atomic oxygen adsorbate with surface Ni atoms [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7].

The segregation kinetics and activation barriers have been quantified using AES data. By analyzing the time required for one-half of the subsurface 3d atoms to segregate to the surface, the activation barrier for the segregation of subsurface Ni to the surface in oxygen was estimated to be 15 ± 2 kcal/mol, compared to 7 ± 1 kcal/mol for Co [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7][10.1021/jp061697z]. These values were obtained using Arrhenius equation analysis of AES data collected at various temperatures [10.1021/cr300096b_6].

The experimental results generally align with DFT predictions. DFT calculations indicate that in the presence of adsorbed oxygen, the Pt3dPt subsurface structure is no longer thermodynamically stable. The stable surface structure is the one with 3d metals on the topmost surface, 3dPtPt, leading to a thermodynamic driving force for the segregation of subsurface 3d atoms [10.1021/jp061697z]. This is consistent with the experimental observations of oxygen-induced segregation.

The measured activation barriers follow the trend predicted by DFT calculations, decreasing as the d-band center shifts closer to the Fermi level for different 3d metals. DFT results suggest that the activation barrier for the segregation of other subsurface 3d metals (Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe) should be even lower than that for Co or Ni [10.1021/jp061697z]. This trend is supported by the experimentally determined lower activation barrier for Co segregation compared to Ni.

However, experiments have revealed some nuances not captured by DFT, such as the coexistence of outward segregation and inward diffusion processes, particularly for Co [10.1021/jp061697z]. This highlights the complementary nature of experimental and theoretical approaches in understanding these complex systems.

Experimental verification and quantification of oxygen-induced surface segregation of subsurface 3d atoms (Ni and Co) in Pt-3d-Pt(111) bimetallic systems have been accomplished using a combination of High-Resolution Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy (HREELS) and Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES) techniques. These methods provide complementary information on the segregation process, its kinetics, and activation barriers.

HREELS has been utilized to monitor the segregation of Ni and Co in Pt-3d-Pt(111) subsurface configurations when exposed to oxygen [10.1021/cr300096b_6]. The technique identifies characteristic ν(metal-O) frequencies for each surface, serving as an indicator for the segregation of subsurface 3d atoms to the surface. The onset of specific ν(NiO) and ν(CoO) vibrational modes in the HREEL spectra confirms the segregation of subsurface Ni and Co after exposing the Pt-3d-Pt(111) configuration to oxygen at different temperatures [10.1021/jp061697z].

AES measurements have been employed to quantify the segregation kinetics by tracking the increase in surface Ni/Pt or Co/Pt ratios as a function of oxygen exposure time and temperature [10.1021/jp061697z]. The normalized AES intensity ratios increase with oxygen exposure, confirming the oxygen-induced segregation of subsurface 3d atoms [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7]. This method allows for the estimation of activation barriers for the segregation process.

Using Arrhenius equation analysis of the AES data, the activation barriers for oxygen-induced segregation have been experimentally determined. For Ni segregation in the temperature range of 400-475 K, the activation barrier is estimated to be 15 ± 2 kcal/mol. For Co segregation in the temperature range of 300-350 K, the activation barrier is estimated to be 7 ± 1 kcal/mol [10.1021/cr300096b_6] [10.1021/jp061697z].

These experimental results align well with Density Functional Theory (DFT) predictions. DFT calculations indicate that in the presence of adsorbed oxygen, the Pt-3d-Pt subsurface structure is no longer thermodynamically stable. Instead, the stable surface structure is one with 3d metals on the topmost surface (3d-Pt-Pt), providing a thermodynamic driving force for the segregation of subsurface 3d atoms [10.1021/jp061697z].

The trend in activation barriers observed experimentally follows the predictions from DFT calculations. Ni is predicted to have a smaller driving potential for segregation compared to Co, which corresponds to the higher activation barrier observed for Ni [10.1021/cr300096b_6]. Furthermore, DFT results suggest that the activation barriers for the segregation of other subsurface 3d metals (Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe) should be even lower than those for Co or Ni [10.1021/jp061697z].

The experimental studies also reveal some nuances not fully captured by DFT predictions. For instance, the coexistence of outward segregation and inward diffusion processes, particularly for Co, highlights the complexity of these systems and the complementary nature of experimental and theoretical approaches [10.1021/jp061697z].

Future research endeavors might focus on extending these studies to a broader range of 3d metals and more complex multi-metallic systems.
