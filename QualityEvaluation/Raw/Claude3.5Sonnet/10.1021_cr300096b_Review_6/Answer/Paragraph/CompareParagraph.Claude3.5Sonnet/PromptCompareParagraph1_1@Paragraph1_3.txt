Experimental verification and quantification of oxygen-induced surface segregation of subsurface 3d atoms (Ni and Co) in Pt-3d-Pt(111) bimetallic systems have been achieved through the complementary use of High-Resolution Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy (HREELS) and Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES). These techniques provide valuable insights into the segregation process, its kinetics, and activation barriers, which can be compared with Density Functional Theory (DFT) predictions.

HREELS has been utilized to monitor the segregation of Ni and Co in Pt-3d-Pt(111) subsurface configurations when exposed to oxygen [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. The onset of characteristic ν(NiO) and ν(CoO) vibrational modes confirms the segregation of subsurface Ni and Co after exposing the subsurface Pt-3d-Pt(111) configuration to oxygen at different temperatures [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. This technique provides a qualitative verification of the segregation process.

AES measurements have been employed to quantify the segregation kinetics [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. The AES intensity ratios of Co/Pt or Ni/Pt are used to monitor the interchange between the subsurface and surface structures after exposure to oxygen [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. The normalized Ni/Pt or Co/Pt AES ratio increases with oxygen exposure, indicating segregation of subsurface 3d atoms to the surface [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7]. 

The segregation kinetics are measured by tracking the change in AES ratio over time during oxygen exposure [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7]. Activation barriers for segregation are estimated experimentally based on the time required for half of the subsurface 3d atoms to segregate to the surface [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7]. Using an Arrhenius equation to analyze the AES data, the activation barriers for oxygen-induced segregation of Co and Ni are estimated to be 7 ± 1 and 15 ± 2 kcal/mol, respectively [10.1021/cr300096b\_6].

These experimental results align well with DFT predictions. DFT calculations show that:

These methods provide complementary information on the segregation process, its kinetics, and activation barriers.

HREELS has been utilized to monitor the segregation of Ni and Co in Pt-3d-Pt(111) subsurface configurations when exposed to oxygen [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. The technique identifies characteristic ν(metal-O) frequencies for each surface, serving as an indicator for the segregation of subsurface 3d atoms to the surface. The onset of specific ν(NiO) and ν(CoO) vibrational modes in the HREEL spectra confirms the segregation of subsurface Ni and Co after exposing the Pt-3d-Pt(111) configuration to oxygen at different temperatures [10.1021/jp061697z].

AES measurements have been employed to quantify the segregation kinetics by tracking the increase in surface Ni/Pt or Co/Pt ratios as a function of oxygen exposure time and temperature [10.1021/jp061697z]. The normalized AES intensity ratios increase with oxygen exposure, confirming the oxygen-induced segregation of subsurface 3d atoms [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7]. This method allows for the estimation of activation barriers for the segregation process.

Using Arrhenius equation analysis of the AES data, the activation barriers for oxygen-induced segregation have been experimentally determined. For Ni segregation in the temperature range of 400-475 K, the activation barrier is estimated to be 15 ± 2 kcal/mol. For Co segregation in the temperature range of 300-350 K, the activation barrier is estimated to be 7 ± 1 kcal/mol [10.1021/cr300096b\_6] [10.1021/jp061697z].

These experimental results align well with Density Functional Theory (DFT) predictions. DFT calculations indicate that in the presence of adsorbed oxygen, the Pt-3d-Pt subsurface structure is no longer thermodynamically stable. Instead, the stable surface structure is one with 3d metals on the topmost surface (3d-Pt-Pt), providing a thermodynamic driving force for the segregation of subsurface 3d atoms [10.1021/jp061697z].

The trend in activation barriers observed experimentally follows the predictions from DFT calculations. Ni is predicted to have a smaller driving potential for segregation compared to Co, which corresponds to the higher activation barrier observed for Ni [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. Furthermore, DFT results suggest that the activation barriers for the segregation of other subsurface 3d metals (Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe) should be even lower than those for Co or Ni [10.1021/jp061697z].

The experimental studies also reveal some nuances not fully captured by DFT predictions. For instance, the coexistence of outward segregation and inward diffusion processes, particularly for Co, highlights the complexity of these systems and the complementary nature of experimental and theoretical approaches [10.1021/jp061697z].



Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The combination of experimental techniques (HREELS and AES) with DFT calculations has provided a comprehensive understanding of oxygen-induced surface segregation in Pt-3d-Pt(111) bimetallic systems. This approach has successfully quantified segregation kinetics and activation barriers, while also validating theoretical predictions.

Future research endeavors might focus on extending these studies to a broader range of 3d metals and more complex multi-metallic systems. In-situ studies under realistic catalytic conditions, such as those suggested using EXAFS [10.1021/jp0548927], could provide valuable insights into the dynamic behavior of these systems during actual catalytic processes. Additionally, investigating the effects of other adsorbates with varying electronegativities on surface segregation could lead to a more generalized understanding of adsorbate-induced restructuring in bimetallic catalysts.

The development of advanced in-situ characterization techniques that can probe surface composition and structure under reaction conditions with high temporal and spatial resolution would be particularly valuable.
