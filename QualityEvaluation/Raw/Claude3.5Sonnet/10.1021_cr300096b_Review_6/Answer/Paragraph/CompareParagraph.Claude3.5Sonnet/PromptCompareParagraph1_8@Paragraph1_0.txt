Experimental verification and quantification of oxygen-induced surface segregation of subsurface 3d atoms (Ni and Co) in Pt-3d-Pt(111) bimetallic systems have been achieved through the combined use of High-Resolution Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy (HREELS) and Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES). These techniques provide complementary information on the segregation process, its kinetics, and activation barriers.

HREELS has been utilized to monitor the segregation of Ni and Co in Pt-3d-Pt(111) subsurface configurations when exposed to oxygen [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. The onset of characteristic ν(NiO) and ν(CoO) vibrational modes in the HREEL spectra confirms the segregation of subsurface Ni and Co after exposing the subsurface Pt-3d-Pt(111) configuration to oxygen at different temperatures [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. This spectroscopic evidence provides a qualitative verification of the segregation process.

AES measurements have been employed to quantify the segregation kinetics [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. The AES intensity ratios of Co/Pt or Ni/Pt were used to monitor the interchange between the subsurface and surface structures after exposure to oxygen [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. For instance, the normalized Ni/Pt AES ratio increases with oxygen exposure to the Pt-Ni-Pt(111) surface, indicating that subsurface Ni atoms start to segregate to the surface due to the strong interaction of the atomic oxygen adsorbate with surface Ni atoms [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7].

The activation barriers for oxygen-induced segregation have been estimated using Arrhenius equation analysis of AES data [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. For the segregation of subsurface Ni to the surface in oxygen, the activation barrier was found to be 15 ± 2 kcal/mol, while for Co it was 7 ± 1 kcal/mol [10.1021/jp061697z]. These experimental results align well with DFT predictions, which suggest that Ni should have a higher activation barrier for segregation compared to Co due to its smaller driving potential for segregation [10.1021/cr300096b\_6].

The experimental findings are consistent with DFT calculations showing that in the presence of adsorbed oxygen, the Pt-3d-Pt subsurface structure is no longer thermodynamically stable [10.1021/jp061697z]. Instead, the stable surface structure is the one with 3d metals on the topmost surface (3d-Pt-Pt), leading to a thermodynamic driving force for the segregation of subsurface 3d atoms [10.1021/jp061697z].

The measured activation barriers follow the trend predicted by DFT calculations, decreasing as the d-band center shifts closer to the Fermi level for different 3d metals [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7]. DFT results also suggest that the activation barrier for the segregation of other subsurface 3d metals (Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe) should be even lower than that for Co or Ni [10.1021/jp061697z].

Experimental techniques, particularly High-Resolution Electron Energy Loss Spectroscopy (HREELS) and Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES), have been instrumental in verifying and quantifying the oxygen-induced surface segregation of subsurface 3d atoms (particularly Ni and Co) in Pt-3d-Pt(111) bimetallic systems.

HREELS has been utilized to monitor the segregation of Ni and Co in Pt3dPt(111) subsurface configurations when exposed to oxygen. The onset of characteristic ν(NiO) and ν(CoO) vibrational modes confirms the segregation of subsurface Ni and Co after exposing the subsurface Pt3dPt(111) configuration to oxygen at different temperatures [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. This technique provides a qualitative verification of the segregation process.

AES measurements have been employed to quantify the segregation kinetics. The AES intensity ratios of Co/Pt or Ni/Pt were used to monitor the interchange between the subsurface and surface structures after exposure to oxygen [10.1021/cr300096b\_6]. For instance, the normalized Ni/Pt AES ratio for an initially Pt-Ni-Pt(111) surface increases as a function of oxygen exposure, indicating that subsurface Ni atoms start to segregate to the surface due to the strong interaction of the atomic oxygen adsorbate with surface Ni atoms [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7].

The segregation kinetics and activation barriers have been quantified using AES data. By analyzing the time required for one-half of the subsurface 3d atoms to segregate to the surface, the activation barrier for the segregation of subsurface Ni to the surface in oxygen was estimated to be 15 ± 2 kcal/mol, compared to 7 ± 1 kcal/mol for Co [10.1007/s11244-008-9119-7][10.1021/jp061697z]. These values were obtained using Arrhenius equation analysis of AES data collected at various temperatures [10.1021/cr300096b\_6].

The experimental results generally align with DFT predictions. DFT calculations indicate that in the presence of adsorbed oxygen, the Pt3dPt subsurface structure is no longer thermodynamically stable. The stable surface structure is the one with 3d metals on the topmost surface, 3dPtPt, leading to a thermodynamic driving force for the segregation of subsurface 3d atoms [10.1021/jp061697z]. This is consistent with the experimental observations of oxygen-induced segregation.

The measured activation barriers follow the trend predicted by DFT calculations, decreasing as the d-band center shifts closer to the Fermi level for different 3d metals. DFT results suggest that the activation barrier for the segregation of other subsurface 3d metals (Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe) should be even lower than that for Co or Ni [10.1021/jp061697z]. This trend is supported by the experimentally determined lower activation barrier for Co segregation compared to Ni.

However, experiments have revealed some nuances not captured by DFT, such as the coexistence of outward segregation and inward diffusion processes, particularly for Co [10.1021/jp061697z]. This highlights the complementary nature of experimental and theoretical approaches in understanding these complex systems.



Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The combination of experimental techniques like HREELS and AES with DFT calculations has provided a comprehensive understanding of oxygen-induced surface segregation in Pt-3d-Pt(111) bimetallic systems. This approach has revealed not only the occurrence of segregation but also quantified its kinetics and energetics. 

Future research directions could focus on:

1. Expanding the range of 3d metals studied to validate the DFT-predicted trends for Ti, V, Cr, Mn, and Fe.
2. Investigating the effects of different adsorbates beyond oxygen, such as nitrogen or carbon monoxide, on segregation behavior.
3. Developing in-situ techniques to study segregation under realistic catalytic conditions, as suggested by the potential use of EXAFS under reaction conditions [10.1021/jp0548927].
4. Exploring the impact of surface segregation on catalytic performance and stability in real-world applications.
5. Investigating the possibility of controlling segregation behavior through careful design of bimetallic systems, potentially leading to more efficient and stable catalysts.

These future directions could lead to a more nuanced understanding of bimetallic systems and potentially guide the design of advanced catalysts with tunable surface properties.
