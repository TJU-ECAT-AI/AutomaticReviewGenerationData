Comparative Analysis of CO and H2 Chemisorption Techniques for Characterizing Bimetallic Catalysts

CO and H2 chemisorption are two widely used techniques for characterizing bimetallic catalysts, particularly in determining active metal sites and metal dispersion. Both methods have their own methodologies, applications, advantages, and limitations.

Methodology:
CO chemisorption typically involves pulsing CO over a reduced catalyst and measuring the uptake [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. It can be performed using instruments like the AMI-200ip (Altamira Instruments) [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]. H2 chemisorption, on the other hand, utilizes the dissociative adsorption of H2 [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. It can be carried out using equipment such as the Micromeritics ASAP 2020, involving sample drying, reduction in H2, degassing, and measurement of H2 isotherms at specific temperatures [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003].

Applications in Determining Active Metal Sites and Metal Dispersion:
Both techniques are used to determine the number of active metal sites, with CO chemisorption being more commonly employed and generally considered more reliable [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. CO chemisorption can estimate metal dispersion by assuming a 1:1 M/CO stoichiometry (M = Pt, Ni, Pd, etc.), although this is an oversimplification [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. H2 chemisorption is used to determine H/Pt ratios, providing information on the number of accessible platinum sites and metal dispersion [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003].

Advantages:
CO chemisorption is widely used and provides reliable data for normalizing reaction rates [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. It avoids complications from hydrogen spillover that can occur with H2 chemisorption [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]. CO chemisorption also offers a quantitative comparison of the number of active sites between different catalyst series [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019].

H2 chemisorption allows observation of changes in available hydrogenation sites with increasing promoter concentration, which can be useful in studying bimetallic systems [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003].

Limitations:
The 1:1 M/CO ratio assumption in CO chemisorption is not always accurate, especially for bimetallic catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. CO/metal stoichiometry varies between metals and with particle size. For instance, the CO/Pd ratio varies from 0.5-0.8 depending on particle size, while the CO/Pt ratio is unity for a wide range of dispersions [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

H2 chemisorption faces challenges with hydrogen desorption from some bimetallic surfaces at room temperature, affecting accuracy [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. It can also overestimate dispersion due to spillover effects, especially on F-containing catalysts [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

Key Considerations for Data Interpretation and Normalization of Reaction Rates:
Results from both techniques should be interpreted cautiously, considering the assumptions and limitations of each method [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. For bimetallic catalysts, dispersion values should be interpreted as relative rankings rather than absolute measures due to uncertainties in stoichiometry and surface compositions [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019].

When normalizing reaction rates, CO uptake values are typically used for proper comparison of catalytic activity among different catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b\_4]. However, these values can only be used as a semi-quantitative measure of differences within one metal system due to varying CO/metal stoichiometries [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].



Integrative understanding and prospective outlook:
The comparative analysis of CO and H2 chemisorption techniques reveals that both methods have their strengths and limitations in characterizing bimetallic catalysts. While CO chemisorption is generally preferred due to its reliability and avoidance of spillover effects, H2 chemisorption can provide complementary information, especially for certain metal systems.

Moving forward, research efforts could focus on developing more accurate models for interpreting chemisorption data in bimetallic systems, taking into account the complex surface interactions and varying stoichiometries. Advanced in-situ spectroscopic techniques could be combined with chemisorption measurements to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the catalyst surface during the adsorption process.

Furthermore, the development of novel chemisorption probes specific to bimetallic systems could greatly enhance our ability to characterize these catalysts. Machine learning algorithms could potentially be employed to analyze and interpret complex chemisorption data, leading to more accurate predictions of catalyst performance and active site distributions.

This technique is widely used to estimate the number of active metal sites and metal dispersion, assuming a stoichiometry of M/CO = 1:1 (M = Pt, Ni, Pd, etc.), although this ratio is an oversimplification [10.1021/cr300096b\_4].

H2 chemisorption, on the other hand, employs dissociative adsorption of H2 and is performed using instruments such as the Micromeritics ASAP 2020 [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]. While it can also determine the number of active metal sites, it faces challenges such as hydrogen desorption from some bimetallic surfaces at room temperature, affecting accuracy and requiring careful interpretation [10.1021/cr300096b\_4].

Both techniques have their advantages and limitations. CO chemisorption avoids complications from hydrogen spillover that occur with H2 chemisorption, especially on fluorine-containing supports [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]. It provides more reliable estimates of exposed metal fraction and is crucial for normalizing reaction rates when comparing catalytic activity among different catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b\_4].

However, neither technique can directly quantify dispersion for bimetallics due to varying stoichiometry. The CO/metal stoichiometry varies between metals and with particle size. For instance, on palladium catalysts, the CO/Pd(surf) ratio varies from 0.5 to 0.8 depending on particle size, while on platinum catalysts, the CO/Pt(surf) ratio is unity for a wide range of dispersions [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

H2 chemisorption has the advantage of allowing observation of changes in available hydrogenation sites with increasing promoter concentration. For example, in Pt-Sn bimetallic catalysts, lower H/Pt ratios with increasing tin loading suggest increased coverage of platinum by tin [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]. However, H2 uptake can overestimate dispersion due to spillover effects, especially on F-containing catalysts [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

Data interpretation and normalization of reaction rates require careful consideration of the limitations of both techniques. CO uptake values are best used as semi-quantitative measures within one metal system [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]. For bimetallic catalysts, dispersion values should be interpreted cautiously as relative rankings rather than absolute measures [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019].

In conclusion, while CO chemisorption is generally preferred for bimetallic catalysts, results from both techniques must be carefully interpreted. Combining multiple characterization techniques is likely necessary for a complete understanding of bimetallic catalyst properties.

Additionally, the development of in-situ chemisorption techniques that can operate under reaction conditions could provide more accurate insights into the dynamic nature of active sites during catalytic processes. Another promising avenue could be the combination of chemisorption techniques with advanced spectroscopic methods, such as X-ray absorption spectroscopy or environmental transmission electron microscopy, to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the surface structure and composition of bimetallic catalysts.
