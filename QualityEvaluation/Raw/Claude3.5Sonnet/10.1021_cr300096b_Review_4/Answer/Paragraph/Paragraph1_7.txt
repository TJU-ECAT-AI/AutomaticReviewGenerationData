Comparative analysis of CO and H2 chemisorption techniques for characterizing active metal sites and metal dispersion in bimetallic catalysts reveals significant differences in methodologies, applications, and limitations.

CO chemisorption is typically preferred for determining the number of active metal sites in bimetallic catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b_4]. The methodology involves pulsing CO over a reduced catalyst and measuring the uptake. This technique can also estimate metal dispersion by assuming a 1:1 M/CO stoichiometry (M = Pt, Ni, Pd, etc.), although this assumption is an oversimplification [10.1021/cr300096b_4].

For bimetallic catalysts, CO chemisorption offers several advantages. It avoids complications arising from hydrogen spillover, which can occur with H2 chemisorption [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]. CO uptake provides a quantitative comparison of the number of active sites among different catalyst series [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]. Moreover, CO chemisorption data is crucial for normalizing reaction rates, enabling proper comparison of catalytic activity among different catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b_4].

However, CO chemisorption has limitations. The CO/metal stoichiometry varies between metals and with particle size. For instance, on palladium catalysts, the CO/Pd(surf) ratio varies from 0.5 to 0.8 depending on particle size, while on platinum catalysts, the CO/Pt(surf) ratio is unity for a wide range of dispersions [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]. This variability makes direct quantification of dispersion challenging for bimetallic catalysts.

H2 chemisorption, on the other hand, uses dissociative adsorption of H2 to determine the number of active metal sites [10.1021/cr300096b_4]. The methodology typically involves drying samples, reducing them in H2, degassing, and then measuring H2 isotherms [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]. H2 chemisorption can provide information on metal dispersion and available active sites through H/Pt ratios.

However, H2 chemisorption faces several challenges when applied to bimetallic catalysts. Hydrogen can start to desorb from some bimetallic surfaces at room temperature, affecting accuracy [10.1021/cr300096b_4]. Additionally, hydrogen spillover, especially on fluorine-containing supports, can lead to overestimation of dispersion [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

For data interpretation and normalization of reaction rates, both techniques require careful consideration. CO uptake values are best used as semi-quantitative measures within one metal system due to varying stoichiometries [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]. For H2 chemisorption, H/Pt ratios should be interpreted cautiously, especially when comparing catalysts with different promoter loadings [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003].

In conclusion, while both techniques have their merits, CO chemisorption is generally preferred for characterizing bimetallic catalysts due to its reliability in determining active sites and providing a basis for rate normalization. However, the limitations of both techniques suggest that combining multiple characterization methods may be necessary for a comprehensive understanding of bimetallic catalyst properties.

※※※

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The comparative analysis of CO and H2 chemisorption techniques for bimetallic catalysts highlights the complexity of accurately characterizing these advanced materials. Moving forward, research efforts could focus on developing more sophisticated chemisorption techniques that can overcome the current limitations.

One promising direction could be the development of multi-component chemisorption methods that combine the strengths of both CO and H2 chemisorption while mitigating their individual weaknesses. This could involve sequential or simultaneous application of both gases, potentially allowing for more accurate differentiation between mono- and bimetallic surface sites.

Another avenue for future research could be the integration of advanced computational modeling with experimental chemisorption data. Machine learning algorithms could be employed to better interpret complex chemisorption results, accounting for variables such as particle size, metal-support interactions, and synergistic effects in bimetallic systems.

Furthermore, the development of in-situ or operando chemisorption techniques could provide valuable insights into the dynamic behavior of bimetallic catalysts under reaction conditions. This could help bridge the gap between characterization data and actual catalyst performance, leading to more accurate structure-activity relationships.

Lastly, there is a need for standardization of chemisorption protocols and data interpretation methods for bimetallic catalysts. This would facilitate more meaningful comparisons between different studies and accelerate the development of next-generation catalysts for applications such as propane dehydrogenation.

对于表征双金属催化剂中的活性金属位点和金属分散度，CO和H2化学吸附技术在方法、应用和局限性方面存在显著差异。

CO化学吸附通常是确定双金属催化剂活性金属位点数量的首选方法[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。该方法涉及将CO脉冲通过还原后的催化剂并测量其吸附量。这种技术还可以通过假设1:1的M/CO化学计量比（M = Pt、Ni、Pd等）来估算金属分散度，尽管这种假设是一种过度简化[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。

对于双金属催化剂，CO化学吸附具有几个优势。它避免了H2化学吸附可能出现的氢溢出复杂性[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。CO吸附量提供了不同催化剂系列之间活性位点数量的定量比较[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]。此外，CO化学吸附数据对于归一化反应速率至关重要，使不同催化剂之间的催化活性能够进行适当比较[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。

然而，CO化学吸附也有局限性。CO/金属化学计量比在不同金属之间和不同粒径下会有所变化。例如，在钯催化剂上，CO/Pd(surf)比率随粒径变化在0.5到0.8之间，而在铂催化剂上，CO/Pt(surf)比率在广泛的分散度范围内保持为1[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。这种变异性使得双金属催化剂的分散度直接定量变得具有挑战性。

另一方面，H2化学吸附利用H2的解离吸附来确定活性金属位点的数量[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。该方法通常包括样品干燥、H2还原、脱气，然后测量H2等温线[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]。H2化学吸附可以通过H/Pt比率提供有关金属分散度和可用活性位点的信息。

然而，当应用于双金属催化剂时，H2化学吸附面临几个挑战。氢可能在室温下开始从某些双金属表面脱附，影响准确性[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。此外，氢溢出，特别是在含氟支持剂上，可能导致分散度被高估[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。

对于数据解释和反应速率的归一化，这两种技术都需要仔细考虑。由于化学计量比的变化，CO吸附值最好用作单一金属系统内的半定量测量[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。对于H2化学吸附，H/Pt比率应谨慎解释，特别是在比较具有不同助剂负载量的催化剂时[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]。

总之，尽管这两种技术都有其优点，但CO化学吸附通常更适合表征双金属催化剂，因为它在确定活性位点和提供速率归一化基础方面更可靠。然而，两种技术的局限性表明，可能需要结合多种表征方法，以全面理解双金属催化剂的性质。

※※※

综合理解与未来展望：对双金属催化剂的CO和H2化学吸附技术的比较分析突显了准确表征这些先进材料的复杂性。展望未来，研究努力可以集中在开发更复杂的化学吸附技术，以克服当前的局限性。

一个有前景的方向可能是开发多组分化学吸附方法，结合CO和H2化学吸附的优点，同时减轻它们各自的缺点。这可能涉及两种气体的顺序或同时应用，潜在地允许更准确地区分单金属和双金属表面位点。

未来研究的另一个方向可能是将先进的计算机建模与实验化学吸附数据结合。机器学习算法可以用于更好地解释复杂的化学吸附结果，考虑诸如粒径、金属-载体相互作用以及双金属系统中的协同效应等变量。

此外，原位或操作条件下化学吸附技术的发展可以提供有关双金属催化剂在反应条件下动态行为的宝贵见解。这可以帮助弥合表征数据与实际催化剂性能之间的差距，从而导致更准确的结构-活性关系。

最后，需要为双金属催化剂制定标准化的化学吸附协议和数据解释方法。这将促进不同研究之间更有意义的比较，并加速下一代催化剂的开发，用于诸如丙烷脱氢等应用。
