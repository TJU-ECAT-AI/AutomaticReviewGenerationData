<English>
Comparative analysis of CO and H2 chemisorption techniques for characterizing active metal sites and metal dispersion in bimetallic catalysts reveals significant differences in their methodologies, applications, and limitations.

CO chemisorption is typically preferred for characterizing bimetallic catalysts due to its reliability in determining active sites and providing a basis for rate normalization [10.1021/cr300096b_4]. The methodology involves pulsing CO over a reduced catalyst and measuring the uptake, often using instruments like the AMI-200ip [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]. This technique is widely used to estimate the number of active metal sites and metal dispersion, assuming a stoichiometry of M/CO = 1:1 (M = Pt, Ni, Pd, etc.), although this ratio is an oversimplification [10.1021/cr300096b_4].

H2 chemisorption, on the other hand, employs dissociative adsorption of H2 and is performed using instruments such as the Micromeritics ASAP 2020 [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]. While it can also determine the number of active metal sites, it faces challenges such as hydrogen desorption from some bimetallic surfaces at room temperature, affecting accuracy and requiring careful interpretation [10.1021/cr300096b_4].

Both techniques have their advantages and limitations. CO chemisorption avoids complications from hydrogen spillover that occur with H2 chemisorption, especially on fluorine-containing supports [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]. It provides more reliable estimates of exposed metal fraction and is crucial for normalizing reaction rates when comparing catalytic activity among different catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b_4].

However, neither technique can directly quantify dispersion for bimetallics due to varying stoichiometry. The CO/metal stoichiometry varies between metals and with particle size. For instance, on palladium catalysts, the CO/Pd(surf) ratio varies from 0.5 to 0.8 depending on particle size, while on platinum catalysts, the CO/Pt(surf) ratio is unity for a wide range of dispersions [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

H2 chemisorption has the advantage of allowing observation of changes in available hydrogenation sites with increasing promoter concentration. For example, in Pt-Sn bimetallic catalysts, lower H/Pt ratios with increasing tin loading suggest increased coverage of platinum by tin [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]. However, H2 uptake can overestimate dispersion due to spillover effects, especially on F-containing catalysts [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

Data interpretation and normalization of reaction rates require careful consideration of the limitations of both techniques. CO uptake values are best used as semi-quantitative measures within one metal system [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]. For bimetallic catalysts, dispersion values should be interpreted cautiously as relative rankings rather than absolute measures [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019].

In conclusion, while CO chemisorption is generally preferred for bimetallic catalysts, results from both techniques must be carefully interpreted. Combining multiple characterization techniques is likely necessary for a complete understanding of bimetallic catalyst properties.

※※※

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The comparative analysis of CO and H2 chemisorption techniques for characterizing bimetallic catalysts highlights the complexity of accurately determining active metal sites and metal dispersion. Future research efforts could focus on developing more sophisticated models that account for the varying stoichiometries in bimetallic systems, potentially incorporating machine learning algorithms to predict CO/metal and H/metal ratios based on catalyst composition and preparation methods. Additionally, the development of in-situ chemisorption techniques that can operate under reaction conditions could provide more accurate insights into the dynamic nature of active sites during catalytic processes. Another promising avenue could be the combination of chemisorption techniques with advanced spectroscopic methods, such as X-ray absorption spectroscopy or environmental transmission electron microscopy, to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the surface structure and composition of bimetallic catalysts. These advancements could lead to more accurate normalization of reaction rates and improved catalyst design for propane dehydrogenation and other important industrial processes.

<Chinese>
对于表征双金属催化剂中活性金属位点和金属分散度，CO和H2化学吸附技术的比较分析揭示了它们在方法、应用和局限性方面的显著差异。

CO化学吸附通常是表征双金属催化剂的首选方法，因为它在确定活性位点和提供反应速率归一化基础方面具有可靠性[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。该方法涉及将CO脉冲通过还原的催化剂并测量其吸附量，通常使用如AMI-200ip等仪器[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]。这种技术被广泛用于估计活性金属位点数量和金属分散度，假设M/CO = 1:1的化学计量比（M = Pt, Ni, Pd等），尽管这个比例是一种过度简化[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。

另一方面，H2化学吸附采用H2的解离吸附，使用如Micromeritics ASAP 2020等仪器进行[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]。虽然它也可以确定活性金属位点的数量，但面临着一些挑战，如在室温下某些双金属表面上的氢气解吸，影响准确性并需要谨慎解释[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。

这两种技术都有其优势和局限性。CO化学吸附避免了H2化学吸附中出现的氢溢出问题，特别是在含氟支撑体上[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。它提供了更可靠的暴露金属分数估计，对于在比较不同催化剂的催化活性时对反应速率进行归一化至关重要[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。

然而，由于化学计量比的变化，这两种技术都无法直接量化双金属的分散度。CO/金属化学计量比在不同金属之间和不同颗粒大小下都有变化。例如，在钯催化剂上，CO/Pd(表面)比率随颗粒大小的不同而在0.5到0.8之间变化，而在铂催化剂上，CO/Pt(表面)比率在广泛的分散度范围内都是1[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。

H2化学吸附的优势在于可以观察到随着助催化剂浓度增加，可用氢化位点的变化。例如，在Pt-Sn双金属催化剂中，随着锡负载量的增加，较低的H/Pt比率表明铂被锡覆盖程度增加[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]。然而，由于溢出效应，H2吸附可能会高估分散度，特别是在含F的催化剂上[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。

数据解释和反应速率归一化需要仔细考虑这两种技术的局限性。CO吸附值最好用作同一金属体系内的半定量测量[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。对于双金属催化剂，分散度值应谨慎解释为相对排名而非绝对测量[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]。

总之，虽然CO化学吸附通常是双金属催化剂的首选，但两种技术的结果都必须谨慎解释。结合多种表征技术可能是全面理解双金属催化剂性质所必需的。

※※※

综合理解与未来展望：CO和H2化学吸附技术在表征双金属催化剂方面的比较分析突显了准确确定活性金属位点和金属分散度的复杂性。未来的研究努力可以集中在开发更复杂的模型上，这些模型可以解释双金属系统中不同的化学计量比，可能会结合机器学习算法来根据催化剂组成和制备方法预测CO/金属和H/金属比率。此外，开发可在反应条件下操作的原位化学吸附技术可能会提供更准确的洞察，了解催化过程中活性位点的动态特性。另一个有前景的方向可能是将化学吸附技术与先进的光谱方法结合起来，如X射线吸收光谱或环境透射电子显微镜，以提供对双金属催化剂表面结构和组成更全面的理解。这些进展可能会导致反应速率更准确的归一化，并改进催化剂设计，用于丙烷脱氢和其他重要的工业过程。
