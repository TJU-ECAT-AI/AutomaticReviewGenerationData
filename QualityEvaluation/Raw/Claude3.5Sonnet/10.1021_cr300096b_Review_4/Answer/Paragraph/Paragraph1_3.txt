<English>
Comparative analysis of CO and H2 chemisorption techniques for characterizing active metal sites and metal dispersion in bimetallic catalysts reveals significant differences in their methodologies, applications, advantages, and limitations.

CO chemisorption is widely used and typically considered more reliable for determining the number of active metal sites in bimetallic catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b_4]. The methodology involves pulsing CO over a reduced catalyst and measuring the uptake. CO chemisorption can also estimate metal dispersion by assuming a 1:1 M/CO stoichiometry (M = Pt, Ni, Pd, etc.), although this is recognized as an oversimplification [10.1021/cr300096b_4].

One key advantage of CO chemisorption is its ability to provide a quantitative comparison of the number of active sites among different catalyst series [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]. Moreover, CO chemisorption avoids complications arising from hydrogen spillover, which is a significant issue with H2 chemisorption [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

However, CO chemisorption has limitations, particularly for bimetallic catalysts. The uncertainty in stoichiometry and surface compositions of bimetallic catalysts can make dispersion values less meaningful [10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]. The CO/metal stoichiometry varies between metals and with particle size. For instance, on palladium catalysts, the CO/Pd(surf) ratio varies from 0.5 to 0.8 depending on particle size, while on platinum catalysts, the CO/Pt(surf) ratio is unity for a wide range of dispersions [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

H2 chemisorption, on the other hand, uses dissociative adsorption of H2 to determine the number of active metal sites [10.1021/cr300096b_4]. The methodology typically involves drying the sample, reducing it in H2, degassing, and then measuring H2 isotherms [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003].

H2 chemisorption can provide information on metal dispersion and available active sites through H/Pt ratios. It allows observation of changes in available hydrogenation sites with increasing promoter concentration [10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]. However, H2 chemisorption faces significant challenges. Hydrogen can start to desorb from some bimetallic surfaces at room temperature, affecting accuracy [10.1021/cr300096b_4]. Moreover, hydrogen spillover, especially on fluorine-containing supports, can lead to overestimation of dispersion [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

For data interpretation and normalization of reaction rates, CO uptake values are generally preferred. They are used to normalize reaction rates for proper comparison of catalytic activity among different catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b_4]. However, these values should be used as semi-quantitative measures within one metal system due to varying stoichiometries [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032].

※※※

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The comparative analysis of CO and H2 chemisorption techniques reveals that while both methods have their merits, neither provides a complete picture of bimetallic catalyst characteristics. This understanding points towards the need for a multi-technique approach in future research.

Future studies could focus on developing advanced chemisorption techniques that can overcome the limitations of current methods. For instance, research into temperature-programmed desorption coupled with mass spectrometry could provide more accurate information on the strength and nature of metal-adsorbate interactions in bimetallic systems.

Additionally, there is potential for the development of in-situ or operando chemisorption techniques that can characterize catalysts under reaction conditions. This could provide more realistic insights into the active sites and metal dispersion during actual catalytic processes.

Finally, computational modeling and machine learning approaches could be integrated with experimental chemisorption data to develop more accurate predictive models for bimetallic catalyst characterization. This interdisciplinary approach could lead to more robust and reliable methods for quantifying active sites and metal dispersion in complex bimetallic catalyst systems.

<Chinese>
CO和H2化学吸附技术在表征双金属催化剂的活性金属位点和金属分散度方面的比较分析揭示了它们在方法、应用、优势和局限性方面的显著差异。

CO化学吸附被广泛使用，通常被认为在确定双金属催化剂中活性金属位点数量方面更可靠[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。该方法涉及将CO脉冲通过还原的催化剂并测量其吸附量。CO化学吸附还可以通过假设1:1的M/CO化学计量比（M = Pt、Ni、Pd等）来估算金属分散度，尽管这被认为是一种过度简化[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。

CO化学吸附的一个关键优势是能够在不同催化剂系列之间提供活性位点数量的定量比较[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]。此外，CO化学吸附避免了氢溢流带来的复杂性，这是H2化学吸附的一个显著问题[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。

然而，CO化学吸附对于双金属催化剂也有局限性。双金属催化剂的化学计量比和表面组成的不确定性可能使分散度值变得不那么有意义[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.01.019]。CO/金属化学计量比在不同金属之间和不同粒径下都有变化。例如，在钯催化剂上，CO/Pd(surf)比率随粒径变化从0.5到0.8，而在铂催化剂上，CO/Pt(surf)比率在广泛的分散度范围内保持为1 [10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。

另一方面，H2化学吸附使用H2的解离吸附来确定活性金属位点的数量[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。该方法通常包括样品干燥、在H2中还原、脱气，然后测量H2等温线[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]。

H2化学吸附可以通过H/Pt比率提供关于金属分散度和可用活性位点的信息。它允许观察随着助剂浓度增加而导致的可用氢化位点的变化[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003]。然而，H2化学吸附面临重大挑战。氢可能在室温下从某些双金属表面开始脱附，影响准确性[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。此外，氢溢流，特别是在含氟支持物上，可能导致分散度被高估[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。

对于数据解释和反应速率标准化，通常首选CO吸附值。它们被用来标准化不同催化剂之间催化活性的比较[10.1021/cr300096b_4]。然而，由于化学计量比的变化，这些值应该在一个金属系统内作为半定量测量使用[10.1016/j.apcata.2003.11.032]。

※※※

综合理解与未来展望：CO和H2化学吸附技术的比较分析揭示，虽然两种方法都有各自的优点，但它们都无法提供双金属催化剂特性的完整图景。这种理解指向未来研究需要采用多技术方法。

未来的研究可以集中在开发能够克服当前方法局限性的先进化学吸附技术。例如，研究温度程序脱附与质谱联用可能提供关于双金属系统中金属-吸附质相互作用强度和性质的更准确信息。

此外，开发原位或操作条件下的化学吸附技术，可以在实际催化过程中表征催化剂，这有潜力提供更真实的洞察，了解实际催化过程中的活性位点和金属分散度。

最后，计算机建模和机器学习方法可以与实验化学吸附数据相结合，开发更准确的双金属催化剂表征预测模型。这种跨学科方法可能导致更强大和可靠的方法来量化复杂双金属催化剂系统中的活性位点和金属分散度。
