2. High selectivity: It achieves over 90% selectivity for H2 combustion even in the presence of predominantly hydrocarbon reactants.

3. Process simplification: It uses a single reactor and catalyst composition, reducing process complexity and costs compared to multi-stage systems.

4. Improved control: It allows for precise O2 introduction rates that match H2 formation and avoid undesired side reactions.

5. Scalability: The approach can be implemented in large-scale reactors using multiple injectors or ceramic membranes.

The potential implications for energy efficiency and process optimization include:

1. Higher product yields from a given amount of feedstock, improving overall process efficiency.
2. Reduced separation and recycling costs due to higher single-pass conversions.
3. Lower capital and operating costs from using a simpler, single-stage process.
4. Improved catalyst stability and longevity through controlled reaction conditions.
5. Potential for process intensification by combining multiple steps in a single reactor.

This approach demonstrates how nanoscale catalyst design can enable more efficient, integrated processes that overcome traditional thermodynamic and kinetic limitations in chemical engineering.
