<English>
The effectiveness of different sulfur-based oxidants (H2S, S2, and SO2) in the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) to propylene has been investigated and compared to conventional oxidants. Each sulfur-based oxidant presents unique advantages and challenges in terms of propylene yield, selectivity, and process optimization.

H2S as an oxidant:
H2S has shown promising results in ODHP, demonstrating improved propylene yields and selectivity compared to conventional oxidation methods. Key findings include:
1. Increased propylene selectivity (up to 70% at 700°C) and reduced unwanted byproducts (COx, C2H4, CH4) [10.1080/17415990412331327909].
2. Higher propane conversion rates and improved catalyst performance, particularly with V2O5/Al2O3 catalysts [10.1080/17415990412331327909].
3. Best propylene yield achieved was 30.4% at 700°C and 0.005 s contact time [10.1080/17415990412331327909].
4. H2S-promoted ODH over molten salt catalysts (LiCl/KCl/MnCl2) at 705°C achieved propylene yields up to 60% [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].

S2 as an oxidant:
Elemental sulfur (S2) has shown potential as a milder oxidant compared to oxygen, with the following observations:
1. Acts as a soft oxidant, reducing overoxidation of propane [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].
2. Improves propylene yield and selectivity (73% selectivity at 650°C) [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].
3. However, conversion rates remain low (10% at 650°C) [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].
4. Gas-phase reactions dominate over catalytic reactions [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].

SO2 as an oxidant:
SO2 has been extensively studied but faces challenges in maintaining high propylene selectivity:
1. Increases propane conversion but significantly decreases selectivity to propylene [10.1002/jctb.5040340407].
2. At 855 K, propylene selectivity dropped from 93.1% to 20.6% when SO2 was added [10.1002/jctb.5040340407].
3. Results in low propylene yield due to the formation of COx products over bulk metal oxide catalysts [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].

Challenges associated with sulfur-based oxidants:
1. Formation of sulfur-based byproducts (CS2, thiol mercaptans, S8) [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].
2. Balancing conversion and selectivity, especially for SO2 [10.1002/jctb.5040340407].
3. High temperature requirements for S2 to form reactive species [10.1002/cjce.5450480221].
4. Catalyst deactivation and coke formation [10.1016/0021-9517(68)90017-1].

Key areas for future research:
1. Developing structure-activity relationships for rational catalyst design [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].
2. Optimizing catalyst compositions and reaction conditions for each sulfur-based oxidant [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].
3. Investigating long-term stability and regeneration of catalysts under sulfur-rich conditions [10.1007/BF02071147].
4. Understanding and controlling gas-phase reactions, especially for S2-based ODHP [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].
5. Developing catalysts with moderate redox properties to balance alkane activation and overoxidation [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10].
6. Exploring synergistic effects between sulfur-based and conventional oxidants [10.1016/S0167-2991(08)60185-8].

※※※
Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The research on sulfur-based oxidants for ODHP reveals a promising alternative to conventional oxidants, with each sulfur species offering unique advantages and challenges. H2S shows the most promising results in terms of propylene yield and selectivity, while S2 offers potential as a milder oxidant. SO2, despite extensive research, faces significant selectivity issues. Future research should focus on developing tailored catalysts for each sulfur-based oxidant, optimizing reaction conditions, and addressing byproduct formation. The integration of in-situ characterization techniques and computational modeling could provide deeper insights into reaction mechanisms and guide catalyst design. Additionally, exploring hybrid systems combining sulfur-based oxidants with conventional oxidants or other promoters may lead to synergistic effects, potentially overcoming individual limitations. As environmental concerns grow, research into efficient sulfur management and recovery systems will be crucial for the industrial viability of sulfur-based ODHP processes.

<Chinese>
与传统氧化剂相比，不同的含硫氧化剂（H2S、S2和SO2）在丙烷氧化脱氢(ODHP)制丙烯中的效果已经被研究和比较。每种含硫氧化剂在丙烯产率、选择性和工艺优化方面都具有独特的优势和挑战。

H2S作为氧化剂：
H2S在ODHP中显示出有前景的结果，与传统氧化方法相比，表现出更高的丙烯产率和选择性。主要发现包括：
1. 提高了丙烯选择性（在700°C时高达70%）并降低了不需要的副产品（COx、C2H4、CH4）[10.1080/17415990412331327909]。
2. 更高的丙烷转化率和改进的催化剂性能，特别是使用V2O5/Al2O3催化剂时[10.1080/17415990412331327909]。
3. 在700°C和0.005秒接触时间下，最佳丙烯产率达到30.4%[10.1080/17415990412331327909]。
4. 在705°C下，使用熔融盐催化剂（LiCl/KCl/MnCl2）的H2S促进ODH可达到60%的丙烯产率[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。

S2作为氧化剂：
元素硫（S2）作为比氧更温和的氧化剂显示出潜力，观察结果如下：
1. 作为软氧化剂，减少了丙烷的过度氧化[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。
2. 提高了丙烯产率和选择性（在650°C时选择性为73%）[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。
3. 然而，转化率仍然较低（在650°C时为10%）[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。
4. 气相反应主导，而非催化反应[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。

SO2作为氧化剂：
SO2已被广泛研究，但在保持高丙烯选择性方面面临挑战：
1. 增加了丙烷转化率，但显著降低了丙烯的选择性[10.1002/jctb.5040340407]。
2. 在855 K时，添加SO2后丙烯选择性从93.1%降至20.6%[10.1002/jctb.5040340407]。
3. 由于在块状金属氧化物催化剂上形成COx产物，导致丙烯产率低[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。

含硫氧化剂相关的挑战：
1. 形成含硫副产品（CS2、硫醇、S8）[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。
2. 平衡转化率和选择性，特别是对于SO2 [10.1002/jctb.5040340407]。
3. S2需要高温才能形成活性物种[10.1002/cjce.5450480221]。
4. 催化剂失活和积炭形成[10.1016/0021-9517(68)90017-1]。

未来研究的关键领域：
1. 发展结构-活性关系，用于理性催化剂设计[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。
2. 优化每种含硫氧化剂的催化剂组成和反应条件[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。
3. 研究在含硫环境下催化剂的长期稳定性和再生[10.1007/BF02071147]。
4. 理解和控制气相反应，特别是对于基于S2的ODHP [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。
5. 开发具有适中氧化还原性能的催化剂，以平衡烷烃活化和过度氧化[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_10]。
6. 探索含硫氧化剂与传统氧化剂之间的协同效应[10.1016/S0167-2991(08)60185-8]。

※※※
综合理解与未来展望：对ODHP中含硫氧化剂的研究揭示了一种替代传统氧化剂的有前景的方法，每种含硫物种都提供了独特的优势和挑战。H2S在丙烯产率和选择性方面显示出最有前景的结果，而S2作为更温和的氧化剂具有潜力。尽管SO2已经进行了广泛的研究，但仍面临显著的选择性问题。未来的研究应该集中在为每种含硫氧化剂开发定制催化剂，优化反应条件，并解决副产品形成的问题。原位表征技术和计算机模拟的整合可能会提供对反应机理更深入的见解，并指导催化剂设计。此外，探索含硫氧化剂与传统氧化剂或其他助剂结合的混合系统可能会产生协同效应，潜在地克服个别限制。随着环境问题的日益突出，研究高效的硫管理和回收系统对于含硫ODHP工艺的工业可行性将至关重要。
