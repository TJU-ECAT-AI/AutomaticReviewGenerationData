The Horiuti-Polanyi mechanism for catalytic dehydrogenation reactions, while not explicitly discussed in the given literature, typically consists of four main steps:

1. Adsorption: The alkane molecule adsorbs onto the catalyst surface.

2. First C-H bond cleavage: One C-H bond breaks, forming an alkyl species bound to the catalyst.

3. Second C-H bond cleavage: Another C-H bond breaks, forming the alkene.

4. Desorption: The alkene product desorbs from the catalyst surface.

There is ongoing debate about which step is rate-limiting. Traditionally, the second C-H bond cleavage (step 3) was often considered rate-limiting. However, the literature suggests that for the Cr/Al2O3 catalyst studied, ethane adsorption (step 1) may be rate-limiting.

This mechanism remains a topic of interest and controversy because:

1. It applies to many important industrial processes.
2. Different catalysts and reaction conditions can change the rate-limiting step.
3. Understanding the mechanism is crucial for catalyst design and optimization.
4. Advanced spectroscopic and computational techniques continue to provide new insights.

The literature's findings for Cr/Al2O3 catalysts, suggesting ethane adsorption as the rate-limiting step, demonstrate that even after decades of study, new discoveries can still be made about this fundamental mechanism.
