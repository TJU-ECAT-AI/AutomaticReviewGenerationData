The Horiuti-Polanyi mechanism for catalytic dehydrogenation reactions, while not directly discussed in the given literature, is a fundamental model in heterogeneous catalysis. It consists of four main steps:

1. Adsorption: The reactant molecule adsorbs onto the catalyst surface.

2. First hydrogen abstraction: One hydrogen atom is removed from the adsorbed molecule, forming an alkyl intermediate.

3. Second hydrogen abstraction: Another hydrogen atom is removed, forming the desired alkene product.

4. Desorption: The alkene product desorbs from the catalyst surface.

There is ongoing debate about which step is rate-limiting. Some researchers argue it's the first hydrogen abstraction, while others suggest it's the second. This controversy persists due to the complexity of the reaction and the difficulty in directly observing intermediates on the catalyst surface.

The Horiuti-Polanyi mechanism remains a topic of scientific interest despite nearly a century of study because it provides a fundamental framework for understanding catalytic dehydrogenation. Advances in surface science techniques and computational methods continue to provide new insights into the detailed molecular processes involved. Understanding this mechanism is crucial for designing more efficient catalysts for important industrial processes like propane dehydrogenation, which is the focus of the given literature.
