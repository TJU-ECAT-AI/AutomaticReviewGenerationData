<EnglishVersionAnswer>

Recent strategies to enhance catalytic stability of supported metal catalysts in direct dehydrogenation processes include:

1. Alloying Pt with other metals like Sn to dilute the Pt surface and reduce coke formation.

2. Using hierarchical micro-/mesoporous zeolites as catalyst supports instead of traditional alumina.

3. Incorporating rare-earth elements like La and Y with Pt to improve dispersion and stability through strong metal-support interactions.

4. Generating silanol nests in zeolites by synthesizing gallosilicate zeolites and removing framework Ga atoms.

5. Forming intermetallic compounds like Pt3La and Pt3Y nanoparticles on degallated zeolites.

These advancements address challenges in catalyst design by:

- Improving resistance to coking and deactivation
- Enhancing metal dispersion and stability
- Enabling formation of novel alloy structures
- Optimizing support properties for better catalytic performance
- Significantly increasing catalyst lifetime and durability

The use of zeolites with tailored porosity and surface chemistry, along with precise control of metal-support interactions and alloy formation, allows for more efficient and longer-lasting catalysts for industrial dehydrogenation processes.
