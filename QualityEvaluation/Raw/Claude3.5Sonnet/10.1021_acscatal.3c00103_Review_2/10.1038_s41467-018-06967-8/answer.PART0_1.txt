Key strategies and approaches recently developed to enhance catalytic stability of supported metal catalysts in direct dehydrogenation processes include:

1. Single Atom Alloy (SAA) catalysts: Synthesizing platinum/copper SAA catalysts supported on γ-alumina with a high copper to platinum ratio. This approach disperses single platinum atoms on copper nanoparticles, enhancing propylene desorption and inhibiting further dehydrogenation.

2. Breaking the scaling relationship: Pt/Cu SAA catalysts break the conventional PtM alloy scaling relationship during propane dehydrogenation (PDH), allowing for simultaneous improvement in activity and selectivity.

3. Atomic dilution method: Using an incipient wetness co-impregnation method to achieve atomic-level dispersion of platinum in copper nanoparticles.

4. Low platinum loading: Utilizing a low loading of platinum (0.1wt%) in combination with higher copper content to achieve high catalytic performance.

5. High-temperature stability: Developing SAA catalysts that maintain excellent stability at higher temperatures (520°C) for extended periods (120+ hours), addressing the challenge of catalyst deactivation in industrial applications.

These advancements address challenges in catalyst design, fabrication, and performance optimization by:

1. Improving selectivity: Achieving high propylene selectivity (90%) while maintaining good conversion rates.

2. Enhancing stability: Demonstrating long-term stability under industrially relevant conditions (high temperature, atmospheric pressure).

3. Optimizing metal utilization: Maximizing the efficiency of precious metals (platinum) through atomic-level dispersion and low loading.

4. Balancing activity and selectivity: Breaking the traditional trade-off between catalytic activity and product selectivity in dehydrogenation reactions.

5. Scalable synthesis: Using conventional synthesis methods (incipient wetness co-impregnation) that can be potentially scaled up for industrial production.

These strategies collectively contribute to the development of more efficient, stable, and selective catalysts for direct dehydrogenation processes, potentially improving the economic viability and sustainability of industrial propylene production.
