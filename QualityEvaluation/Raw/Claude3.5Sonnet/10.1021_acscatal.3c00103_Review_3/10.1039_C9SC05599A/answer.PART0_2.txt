The hydroxyl anchoring method facilitates controlled incorporation of active metal compounds in crystal materials through a stepwise grafting process. First, zinc single sites are grafted onto silica, creating isolated hydroxyl sites. Then, platinum precursors are grafted onto these hydroxyl groups. This approach allows precise control over the introduction and distribution of metal species.

The implications for enhancing metal-support interactions and improving catalyst performance are significant:

1. It enables formation of small, narrowly distributed subnanometric bimetallic particles.

2. It allows tailoring of the metal-support interface by controlling the location and density of grafted species. 

3. The resulting catalysts show high activity, selectivity and stability in reactions like propane dehydrogenation.

4. The method provides a way to synthesize model catalysts to study structure-activity relationships in complex multimetallic systems.

5. It can potentially be applied to create a variety of tailored catalysts with controlled compositions and structures.

Overall, this approach offers a powerful tool for rational catalyst design by enabling precise control over active site formation and metal-support interactions at the molecular level.
