The hydroxyl anchoring method is a powerful approach for facilitating the controlled incorporation of active metal compounds in crystal materials, particularly for developing high-performance propane dehydrogenation (PDH) catalysts. This method leverages surface hydroxyl groups as anchoring sites for metal precursors, enabling precise control over the dispersion, size, and distribution of active metal species on support materials.

Key aspects of the hydroxyl anchoring method include:

1. Selective grafting: Metal precursors are grafted onto specific surface hydroxyl groups, creating isolated surface species [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

2. Thermal treatment: Grafted species undergo thermal treatment to produce well-defined surface sites with known oxidation state, geometry, and nuclearity [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

3. Versatility: The method is applicable to a wide range of transition metals, main group elements, and lanthanides [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

4. Strong metal-oxide bonding: Formation of strong metal-oxygen bonds allows for retention of highly dispersed metal sites on the support surface [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

5. High metal utilization: The resulting materials can be considered surface-doped oxide materials with 100% metal utilization at the surface [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

The hydroxyl anchoring method enhances metal-support interactions and improves catalyst performance in several ways:

1. Improved dispersion and site isolation: This leads to higher catalytic activity and selectivity [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

2. Enhanced stability: Strong metal-support interactions improve resistance to sintering and deactivation [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

3. Precise control: The ability to tune the oxidation state and coordination environment of metal centers allows for optimization of catalytic properties [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

4. Uniform active sites: Creating well-defined, uniform active sites enables more accurate structure-activity relationships to be established [10.1039/D0CS01424A].

5. Confined environments: The method can create confined spaces within zeolite structures, influencing reactant activation and product selectivity [10.1016/j.jcat.2019.12.037].

In the context of PDH catalysts, the hydroxyl anchoring method has been successfully applied to create highly active and selective materials. For example:
