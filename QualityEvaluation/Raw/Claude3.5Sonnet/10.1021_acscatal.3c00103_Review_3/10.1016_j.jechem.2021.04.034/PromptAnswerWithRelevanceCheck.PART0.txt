<EnglishVersionAnswer>

The hydroxyl anchoring method facilitates the controlled incorporation of active metal compounds in crystal materials by using Si-OH groups to trap and stabilize metal cations. This approach enables highly dispersed and isolated metal species to be incorporated into the zeolite structure via in-situ hydrothermal synthesis. 

The implications for enhancing metal-support interactions are significant. The strong interaction between the metal species and the hydroxyl groups of the support prevents aggregation and maintains good dispersion, even under harsh reaction conditions and after multiple regeneration cycles. 

This improved metal-support interaction has positive effects on catalyst performance. It leads to better activity by providing a high number of accessible active sites. The high dispersion and isolation of metal species also contributes to improved selectivity by promoting uniform catalytic behavior. Additionally, the strong anchoring of metal species to the support enhances catalyst stability, allowing it to maintain performance over extended periods and through multiple regeneration cycles.

In the context of solid-gas reactions like propane dehydrogenation, this method produces catalysts with good dehydrogenation activity, high propylene selectivity, and excellent regeneration capability, demonstrating the potential for industrial applications.
