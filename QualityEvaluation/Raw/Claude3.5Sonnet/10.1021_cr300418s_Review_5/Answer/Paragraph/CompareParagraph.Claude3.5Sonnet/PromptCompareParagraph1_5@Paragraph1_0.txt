This multi-technique approach provides valuable insights into the composition, structure, in situ behavior, and reactivity of these catalysts under various conditions.

Raman spectroscopy is particularly well-suited for in situ studies due to minimal interference from gas phase molecules, allowing for real-time observation of catalyst behavior under reaction conditions [10.1016/j.cattod.2004.12.017]. When combined with other techniques such as X-ray diffraction (XRD), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), temperature-programmed reduction mass spectroscopy (TPR-MS), and temperature-programmed desorption of oxygen (O2-TPD), it provides a comprehensive understanding of the catalyst's bulk structure, phase composition, reducibility, oxygen desorption behavior, and surface chemical states [10.1016/j.cattod.2011.03.061].

For example, in studies of Ce-doped CoCr2O4 catalysts for methane oxidation, Raman spectroscopy was used alongside XRD and XPS to characterize the material's structure and composition. Raman spectra were specifically employed to identify vibrations associated with both pure phase materials and the doped chromite, while XRD provided information about phase composition [10.1021/cr300418s\_5].

The combination of UV-vis Diffuse Reflectance Spectroscopy (DRS) and Raman spectroscopy provides insights into the oxidation states of supported catalysts, such as vanadium oxide, at different surface coverages, on various oxide supports, and with different hydrocarbons. This combination also allows for correction of Raman signal intensity and provides information on oxidation states during non-oxidative dehydrogenation conditions [10.1016/j.cattod.2004.12.017].

In the case of alkali-doped vanadia on titania catalysts, Raman spectroscopy reveals the presence of specific structural features, such as the terminal vanadyl group (V=O) identified by the Raman band at 1020 cm-1. It also shows how the addition of alkali promoters affects the catalyst structure, particularly through the observed red shift in the V=O vibration frequency, indicating a weakening of the V=O bond [10.1016/j.cattod.2008.04.049].
