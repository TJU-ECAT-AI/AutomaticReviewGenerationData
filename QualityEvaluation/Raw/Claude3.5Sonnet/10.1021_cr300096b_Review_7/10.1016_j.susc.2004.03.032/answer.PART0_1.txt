Cyclohexene serves as an ideal probe molecule for studying hydrogenation reactions due to its multiple reaction pathways, including dehydrogenation, hydrogenation, and decomposition. These diverse pathways allow researchers to observe how different catalysts influence the selectivity and activity of various reactions.

In the case of bimetallic catalysts, cyclohexene's behavior provides valuable insights into how the combination of metals affects hydrogenation activity and selectivity. For example, the study shows that while pure Ni(111) surfaces only exhibit dehydrogenation and decomposition pathways, the addition of Pt introduces a new hydrogenation pathway. This demonstrates how bimetallic catalysts can create new reaction possibilities that are not present with single-metal catalysts.

The changes in reactivity observed with different Pt coverages on Ni(111) surfaces illustrate how the composition and structure of bimetallic catalysts can fine-tune reaction outcomes. This information is crucial for designing more efficient and selective catalysts for industrial hydrogenation processes.

Furthermore, the study of cyclohexene on these surfaces reveals how factors such as metal-hydrogen bond strength and adsorbate bonding can influence reaction pathways. This fundamental understanding can guide the development of improved catalysts for various industrial hydrogenation reactions, potentially leading to more efficient and environmentally friendly processes.
