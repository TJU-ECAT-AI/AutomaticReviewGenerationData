The Mars-van Krevelen mechanism is not explicitly discussed in the given literature. However, the text provides insights into the importance of oxygen vacancy formation energy (ΔEv) in determining the catalytic activity and oxidizing ability of oxide surfaces.

The literature characterizes the reactivity of surface oxygen atoms using four key parameters, including ΔEv. A lower ΔEv indicates that it is easier to create oxygen vacancies on the surface, which is crucial for oxidation reactions. The presence of low valence dopants (LVDs) in the oxide surface significantly reduces ΔEv, thereby increasing the oxidizing ability of the surface.

The increased reactivity of the doped oxide surface is also reflected in the higher binding energies of H atoms and CH3 radicals (ΔEH and ΔECH3), as well as the increased energy of dissociative adsorption of methane (ΔECH4). These factors contribute to enhanced catalytic activity.

However, the literature also points out a potential complication in catalysis by LVD-doped oxides. The dissociation products of reactants (e.g., H and CH3 from methane) can act as compensating agents, potentially poisoning the catalyst if not removed promptly. This suggests that in some cases, the rate-limiting step in catalytic oxidation reactions might shift from the initial bond breaking to the removal of reaction intermediates from the surface.
