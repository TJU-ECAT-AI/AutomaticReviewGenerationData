Intramolecular C-H bond activation plays a crucial role in the deactivation of supported alkene polymerization and metathesis catalysts, with distinct mechanisms observed for lanthanide/actinide-based and silica-supported Re-based catalysts [10.1021/cr900122p\_4]. This review section focuses on the specific deactivation pathways and their implications for catalyst performance and longevity.

In the case of lanthanide and actinide-based supported alkene polymerization catalysts, the deactivation process involves the activation of the allylic C-H bond instead of the desired propene insertion. This unintended reaction leads to the formation of inactive π-allyl surface complexes, effectively rendering the catalyst inactive [10.1021/cr900122p\_4]. The formation of these π-allyl complexes directly reduces the number of active sites available for polymerization, resulting in a significant decrease in overall catalyst efficiency.

For silica-supported Re-based alkene metathesis catalysts, the deactivation mechanism is more intricate. The key step in this process involves the activation of the C-H bond in the β-position of metallacyclobutane intermediates, leading to a β-H transfer process [10.1021/cr900122p\_4]. This results in the formation of an allyl hydride intermediate, which can subsequently react with ethene to create a highly reactive species. This reactive species can then follow one of two pathways: (1) regenerate the catalyst through α-H abstraction between adjacent alkyl ligands, or (2) lead to degrafting via β-H abstraction of the ethyl fragment by the siloxy ligand [10.1021/cr900122p\_4].

The consequences of these deactivation pathways on catalyst performance and longevity are significant. For Re-based catalysts, the potential for degrafting can lead to a gradual loss of active catalyst from the support surface, reducing the overall catalyst loading and activity over time. The competing pathways of catalyst regeneration and degrafting in these systems may result in a progressive decline in performance as the reaction proceeds [10.1021/cr900122p\_4].
