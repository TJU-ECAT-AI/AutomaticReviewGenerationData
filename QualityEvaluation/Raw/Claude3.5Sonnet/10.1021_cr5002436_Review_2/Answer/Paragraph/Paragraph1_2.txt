<English>
Chromium oxide-based catalysts have undergone significant evolution since their discovery in 1933, becoming crucial components in various industrial processes and the subject of extensive research. Their development and applications have been shaped by both industrial needs and academic investigations, leading to a deeper understanding of their structure, function, and optimization potential.

Industrial Applications:
Initially, chromium oxide catalysts were primarily used for n-butane dehydrogenation to produce butenes and butadiene [10.1006/jcat.1996.0023]. During World War II, chromia/alumina catalysts were employed for butane dehydrogenation to produce high-octane aviation fuel [10.1002/9783527626113.ch15]. The applications have since expanded to include:

1. Ethylene polymerization: The Phillips catalyst (Cr/SiO2) is responsible for about one-third of global high-density polyethylene (HDPE) production [10.1039/a801710g].
2. Light paraffin dehydrogenation: Chromium-on-alumina catalysts are used in processes like Houdry CATOFIN and CATADIENE [10.1039/a801710g].
3. Propane and isobutane dehydrogenation: The CATOFIN process, using chromia-alumina catalysts, leads the world in producing propene and isobutene [10.1016/s0920-5861(99)00047-4].
4. Oxidative dehydrogenation of hydrocarbons in the presence of CO2 [10.1016/j.micromeso.2012.05.011].

Research Focus Areas:
The evolution of chromium oxide-based catalysts has been driven by several key research areas:

1. Structure and Active Sites: Studies have identified various chromium species (Cr6+, Cr5+, Cr3+, and Cr2+) on catalyst surfaces [10.1002/9783527626113.ch15]. Recent research suggests that Cr3+ or a combination of Cr3+ and Cr2+ serve as active sites for dehydrogenation reactions [10.1016/s0926-860x(99)00310-5].

2. Support Materials: While alumina has been the traditional support, research has explored alternatives like zirconia, silica, and mesoporous materials [10.1016/0926-860x(93)80160-]. The use of high surface area mesoporous supports has allowed for better dispersion of chromium species and improved catalytic performance [10.1016/j.molcata.2011.08.019].

3. Preparation Methods: Techniques have evolved from simple impregnation to more advanced methods like atomic layer epitaxy (ALE) [10.1006/jcat.1996.0276]. These new methods aim to create well-defined structures of active sites on the support surface [10.1016/s0926-860x(99)00310-5].

4. Promoters: The addition of alkali metals, particularly potassium, has been found to enhance olefin selectivity and improve catalyst stability [10.1016/j.apcata.2013.02.018].

5. Reaction Mechanisms: Research has focused on understanding the redox behavior of supported chromium oxides and their reactivity during processes such as methanol oxidation and butane dehydrogenation/oxidation [10.1021/jp960543].

6. Characterization Techniques: Advanced methods such as in-situ Raman spectroscopy, XPS, UV-Vis DRS, and XAFS have been employed to study catalysts under reaction conditions [10.1002/9783527626113.ch15] [10.1007/s10562-008-9569-3].

※※※
Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The evolution of chromium oxide-based catalysts since 1933 demonstrates the interplay between industrial needs and fundamental research. While these catalysts have become indispensable in various industrial processes, there are still areas for further investigation and improvement.

Future research may focus on:
1. Developing more environmentally friendly catalysts with reduced chromium content or alternative active species.
2. Exploring novel support materials, such as 2D materials or metal-organic frameworks, to enhance catalyst performance and stability.
3. Investigating the potential of artificial intelligence and machine learning in catalyst design and optimization.
4. Addressing the challenge of catalyst deactivation through innovative regeneration techniques or by developing more coke-resistant formulations.
5. Expanding the application of chromium oxide-based catalysts to emerging fields such as biomass conversion or CO2 utilization.

The continued evolution of these catalysts will likely involve interdisciplinary approaches, combining advanced characterization techniques, computational modeling, and process engineering to meet the growing demands for more efficient and sustainable chemical processes.

<Chinese>
自1933年发现以来，基于氧化铬的催化剂经历了显著的演变，成为各种工业过程中的关键组成部分，并成为广泛研究的主题。它们的发展和应用既受到工业需求的推动，也受到学术研究的影响，从而对其结构、功能和优化潜力有了更深入的理解。

工业应用：
最初，氧化铬催化剂主要用于正丁烷脱氢生产丁烯和丁二烯[10.1006/jcat.1996.0023]。在第二次世界大战期间，氧化铬/氧化铝催化剂被用于丁烷脱氢生产高辛烷值航空燃料[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15]。此后，应用范围扩大到包括：

1. 乙烯聚合：菲利普斯催化剂（Cr/SiO2）负责全球约三分之一的高密度聚乙烯（HDPE）生产[10.1039/a801710g]。
2. 轻质烷烃脱氢：氧化铬-氧化铝催化剂用于Houdry CATOFIN和CATADIENE等工艺[10.1039/a801710g]。
3. 丙烷和异丁烷脱氢：使用氧化铬-氧化铝催化剂的CATOFIN工艺在丙烯和异丁烯生产方面居世界领先地位[10.1016/s0920-5861(99)00047-4]。
4. 在CO2存在下的烃类氧化脱氢[10.1016/j.micromeso.2012.05.011]。

研究重点领域：
基于氧化铬催化剂的演变主要由以下几个关键研究领域推动：

1. 结构和活性位点：研究已经在催化剂表面识别出各种铬物种（Cr6+、Cr5+、Cr3+和Cr2+）[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15]。最新研究表明，Cr3+或Cr3+和Cr2+的组合作为脱氢反应的活性位点[10.1016/s0926-860x(99)00310-5]。

2. 载体材料：虽然氧化铝一直是传统载体，但研究已经探索了诸如氧化锆、二氧化硅和介孔材料等替代品[10.1016/0926-860x(93)80160-]。使用高比表面积的介孔载体能更好地分散铬物种，提高催化性能[10.1016/j.molcata.2011.08.019]。

3. 制备方法：技术已从简单的浸渍发展到更先进的方法，如原子层外延（ALE）[10.1006/jcat.1996.0276]。这些新方法旨在在载体表面创建结构明确的活性位点[10.1016/s0926-860x(99)00310-5]。

4. 助剂：添加碱金属，特别是钾，可以提高烯烃选择性并改善催化剂稳定性[10.1016/j.apcata.2013.02.018]。

5. 反应机理：研究集中在理解负载铬氧化物的氧化还原行为及其在甲醇氧化和丁烷脱氢/氧化等过程中的反应性[10.1021/jp960543]。

6. 表征技术：采用了先进的方法，如原位拉曼光谱、XPS、UV-Vis DRS和XAFS，来研究反应条件下的催化剂[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15] [10.1007/s10562-008-9569-3]。

※※※
综合理解与未来展望：自1933年以来，基于氧化铬催化剂的演变展示了工业需求与基础研究之间的相互作用。尽管这些催化剂在各种工业过程中已经变得不可或缺，但仍有进一步研究和改进的领域。

未来的研究可能集中在：
1. 开发更环保的催化剂，减少铬含量或寻找替代活性物质。
2. 探索新型载体材料，如二维材料或金属有机框架，以提高催化剂性能和稳定性。
3. 研究人工智能和机器学习在催化剂设计和优化中的潜力。
4. 通过创新的再生技术或开发更耐焦的配方来解决催化剂失活的挑战。
5. 扩大基于氧化铬催化剂在新兴领域的应用，如生物质转化或CO2利用。

这些催化剂的持续演变可能涉及跨学科方法，结合先进的表征技术、计算模拟和过程工程，以满足对更高效、更可持续化学过程日益增长的需求。
