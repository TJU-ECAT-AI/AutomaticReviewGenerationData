1. In extended surfaces of PtPd alloys, Pd atoms tend to segregate to the outermost layers, while Pt concentration increases towards the bulk. This suggests that thermodynamic preferences drive surface enrichment of Pd in these systems.

2. For PtPd nanoparticles, a core-shell structure forms with Pd-enriched shells and Pt-enriched cores. This indicates that the surface segregation phenomenon persists even in nanoscale structures.

3. Within the shell of PtPd nanoparticles, Pd atoms preferentially segregate to the (100) facets over the (111) facets, highlighting the influence of crystal structure on segregation patterns.

These findings have important implications for catalytic properties and performance:

1. The surface enrichment of Pd in PtPd systems may alter the catalytic activity compared to pure Pt surfaces, potentially enhancing or inhibiting certain reactions depending on the specific application.

2. The core-shell structure in nanoparticles could lead to synergistic effects between the Pd-rich surface and the Pt-rich core, potentially improving catalytic performance or stability.

3. The preferential segregation to specific facets suggests that the catalytic properties may vary across different crystal planes, which could be exploited in catalyst design for specific reactions.
