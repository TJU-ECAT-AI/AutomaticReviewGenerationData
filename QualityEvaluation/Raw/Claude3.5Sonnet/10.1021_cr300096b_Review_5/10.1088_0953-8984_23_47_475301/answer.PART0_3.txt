The literature provides insights into surface segregation phenomena in PtPd alloys, which can be extended to understand the behavior of Pt-based bimetallic catalysts more broadly:

1. Surface segregation: In PtPd alloys, Pd atoms tend to segregate to the outermost layers of extended surfaces, while Pt concentration increases from the surface to the bulk. This segregation behavior affects the surface composition and thus the catalytic properties.

2. Nanoparticle structure: PtPd nanoparticles exhibit a core-shell structure with Pd-enriched shells and Pt-enriched cores. This configuration can influence catalytic activity by exposing more Pd atoms at the surface.

3. Facet-dependent segregation: In nanoparticles, Pd atoms preferentially segregate to (100) facets over (111) facets. This facet-specific segregation can lead to anisotropic catalytic properties.

These findings suggest that thermodynamic preferences drive surface segregation in Pt-based bimetallic catalysts, resulting in specific surface compositions and structures. This can significantly impact catalytic properties by altering the type and arrangement of atoms exposed at the surface. The core-shell structure and facet-dependent segregation in nanoparticles may offer opportunities to tune catalytic performance by controlling particle size and shape.

While the study focuses on PtPd alloys, similar principles may apply to other Pt-3d alloys, potentially affecting their stability, activity, and selectivity in various catalytic applications. Understanding these phenomena can guide the design and optimization of bimetallic catalysts for specific reactions and conditions.
