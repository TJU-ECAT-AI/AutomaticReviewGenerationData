The support material significantly influences the activity, selectivity, and stability of chromium oxide catalysts in CO2 oxidative dehydrogenation reactions. Different supports affect the distribution, dispersion, oxidation state, and structural features of chromium species, which in turn determine catalytic performance.

High-valent chromium species (Cr6+ and Cr5+) are proposed to be responsible for high catalytic activity. However, these species are reduced during the reaction, leading to catalyst deactivation. This highlights a key challenge in maintaining catalyst stability.

Key challenges and trade-offs appear to include:
1. Balancing high initial activity (from high-valent Cr species) with long-term stability
2. Optimizing support materials to achieve desired Cr species distribution and structural features
3. Developing effective regeneration strategies to counteract deactivation

Further research is needed to fully understand the influence of reaction conditions and to optimize the balance between activity, selectivity, and stability for chromium oxide catalysts in CO2 oxidative dehydrogenation reactions.
