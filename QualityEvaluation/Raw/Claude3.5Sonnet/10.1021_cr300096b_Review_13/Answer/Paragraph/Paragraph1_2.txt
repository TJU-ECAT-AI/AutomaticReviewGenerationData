<English>
The thermodynamic stability of specific bimetallic structures, particularly in Pt-3d systems with subsurface 3d atoms, plays a crucial role in establishing a strong correlation between model surfaces and supported catalysts in hydrogenation reactions. This stability is observed both in ultra-high vacuum (UHV) environments and under actual hydrogenation reaction conditions, allowing model surfaces to accurately represent the behavior of supported catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b_13].

The implications of this correlation for designing more effective catalysts using first principles on model surfaces are significant. It demonstrates that insights gained from studying model surfaces can be directly applied to the development of practical supported catalysts. This correlation enables researchers to use computational methods and controlled experiments on model surfaces to predict and optimize the performance of real-world catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b_13].

However, Pt-based bimetallic catalysts face significant challenges related to the scarcity of Pt-group metals and the stability of surface and subsurface bimetallic structures under reaction conditions [10.1039/c2cs35165j]. At high temperatures, 3d metals tend to diffuse into the bulk Pt, destroying both surface and subsurface bimetallic structures. For example, when 1 monolayer (ML) of Ni is deposited on Pt(111), the Ni coverage decreases upon annealing and reaches a value less than 0.2 ML at 800 K, resulting in the destruction of both surface NiPt(111) and subsurface PtNiPt(111) structures [10.1039/c2cs35165j].

To address these stability issues, researchers have explored alternative substrates such as tungsten carbide (WC). WC acts as a much better diffusion barrier for admetals compared to Pt, maintaining a higher coverage of the 3d metal even after high-temperature annealing. For instance, when Ni is deposited on polycrystalline WC foil, annealing produces some decrease in Ni coverage, but not as drastic as for Ni/Pt(111), with a final coverage of 0.6 ML [10.1039/c2cs35165j].

The use of WC as a substrate offers several advantages:
1. Improved stability of bimetallic structures
2. Enhanced applicability of first-principles studies on model surfaces to practical, supported catalysts
3. Increased catalyst longevity
4. Cost reduction by reducing the use of scarce Pt-group metals
5. More accurate predictions of real catalyst behavior
6. New design possibilities for bimetallic catalysts with unique properties

To truly achieve the rational design of catalysts from first principles, it is essential that the model systems closely resemble those under reaction conditions. This is especially important for bimetallic catalysts due to adsorbate-induced segregation and diffusion of metal atoms [10.1021/cr300096b_13].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: 
The strong correlation between model surfaces and supported catalysts in bimetallic systems, particularly Pt-3d materials, for hydrogenation reactions opens up exciting possibilities for catalyst design. Future research efforts may focus on:

1. Developing more advanced computational models that incorporate the effects of reaction conditions on bimetallic structure stability.
2. Exploring novel substrate materials beyond WC that can further enhance the stability and performance of bimetallic catalysts.
3. Investigating the potential of machine learning algorithms to predict optimal bimetallic compositions and structures for specific catalytic reactions.
4. Studying the long-term stability and performance of WC-supported bimetallic catalysts under industrial conditions to validate their practical applicability.
5. Exploring the possibility of creating multi-metallic systems that combine the catalytic properties of Pt-3d bimetallics with the stability advantages of WC or other substrates.

These research directions could lead to the development of more efficient, cost-effective, and durable catalysts for a wide range of industrial applications, including propane dehydrogenation and other hydrogenation reactions.

<Chinese>
在氢化反应中，特定双金属结构（尤其是具有亚表面3d原子的Pt-3d系统）的热力学稳定性在模型表面和负载催化剂之间的强相关性中起着至关重要的作用。这种稳定性在超高真空（UHV）环境和实际氢化反应条件下都能观察到，使得模型表面能够准确地代表负载催化剂的行为 [10.1021/cr300096b_13]。

这种相关性对于使用第一原理在模型表面上设计更有效的催化剂具有重要意义。它表明，从研究模型表面获得的洞见可以直接应用于实际负载催化剂的开发。这种相关性使研究人员能够使用计算方法和对模型表面的受控实验来预测和优化实际催化剂的性能 [10.1021/cr300096b_13]。

然而，基于Pt的双金属催化剂面临着与Pt族金属稀缺性以及反应条件下表面和亚表面双金属结构稳定性相关的重大挑战 [10.1039/c2cs35165j]。在高温下，3d金属倾向于扩散到Pt体相中，破坏表面和亚表面的双金属结构。例如，当1单层（ML）Ni沉积在Pt(111)上时，Ni覆盖率在退火后降低，在800 K时达到低于0.2 ML的值，导致表面NiPt(111)和亚表面PtNiPt(111)结构都被破坏 [10.1039/c2cs35165j]。

为了解决这些稳定性问题，研究人员探索了诸如碳化钨（WC）等替代基底。与Pt相比，WC作为添加金属的扩散屏障效果更好，即使在高温退火后也能保持较高的3d金属覆盖率。例如，当Ni沉积在多晶WC箔上时，退火产生的Ni覆盖率降低不如Ni/Pt(111)那么剧烈，最终覆盖率为0.6 ML [10.1039/c2cs35165j]。

使用WC作为基底提供了几个优势：
1. 改善双金属结构的稳定性
2. 提高第一原理研究在模型表面上对实际负载催化剂的适用性
3. 增加催化剂的寿命
4. 通过减少稀有Pt族金属的使用来降低成本
5. 更准确地预测实际催化剂的行为
6. 为具有独特性能的双金属催化剂提供新的设计可能性

要真正实现从第一原理理性设计催化剂，模型系统必须尽可能接近反应条件下的实际情况。这对于双金属催化剂尤为重要，因为吸附剂诱导的金属原子偏析和扩散 [10.1021/cr300096b_13]。

※※※

综合理解与未来展望：
双金属系统，尤其是Pt-3d材料，在氢化反应中模型表面和负载催化剂之间的强相关性为催化剂设计开辟了令人兴奋的可能性。未来的研究努力可能集中在：

1. 开发更先进的计算模型，将反应条件对双金属结构稳定性的影响纳入考虑。
2. 探索除WC之外的新型基底材料，进一步提高双金属催化剂的稳定性和性能。
3. 研究机器学习算法在预测特定催化反应的最佳双金属成分和结构方面的潜力。
4. 研究WC支持的双金属催化剂在工业条件下的长期稳定性和性能，以验证其实际应用性。
5. 探索创建多金属系统的可能性，将Pt-3d双金属的催化性能与WC或其他基底的稳定性优势相结合。

这些研究方向可能导致开发出更高效、更经济、更耐用的催化剂，适用于广泛的工业应用，包括丙烷脱氢和其他氢化反应。
