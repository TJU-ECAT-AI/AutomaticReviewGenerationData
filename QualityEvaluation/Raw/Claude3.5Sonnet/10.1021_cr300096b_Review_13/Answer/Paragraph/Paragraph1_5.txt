<English>
The thermodynamic stability of specific bimetallic structures, particularly in Pt-3d systems with subsurface 3d atoms, plays a crucial role in the strong correlation between model surfaces and supported catalysts in hydrogenation reactions. This stability is observed both in ultra-high vacuum (UHV) environments and under actual hydrogenation reaction conditions, allowing the model surfaces to accurately represent the behavior of supported catalysts [10.1021/cr300096b_13]. The implications of this correlation for designing more effective catalysts using first principles on model surfaces are significant, as it demonstrates that insights gained from studying model surfaces can be directly applied to the development of practical supported catalysts.

However, the stability of Pt-3d systems faces challenges, particularly at high temperatures. When deposited on Pt, 3d metals tend to diffuse into the bulk, destroying both surface and subsurface bimetallic structures. For instance, Auger Electron Spectroscopy (AES) measurements show that the coverage of 1 monolayer (ML) Ni on Pt(111) decreases upon annealing, reaching less than 0.2 ML at 800 K [10.1039/c2cs35165j]. This instability limits the practical application of these model surfaces to real catalytic systems.

To address this issue, researchers have explored alternative substrates, such as tungsten carbide (WC). WC acts as a much better diffusion barrier for admetals compared to Pt, maintaining a higher coverage of the 3d metal even after high-temperature annealing. For example, annealing experiments with Ni deposited on polycrystalline WC foil show a final coverage of 0.6 ML, significantly higher than that observed for Ni/Pt(111) [10.1039/c2cs35165j]. This improved stability enhances the applicability of first-principles studies on model surfaces to the design of practical, supported catalysts.

The use of WC as a substrate offers several advantages:
1. Improved catalyst longevity, potentially increasing industrial viability.
2. Cost reduction by reducing the use of scarce and expensive Pt-group metals.
3. More accurate predictions of real catalyst behavior based on model surface studies.
4. New design possibilities for bimetallic catalysts with unique properties.

The implications for designing more effective catalysts using first principles on model surfaces include:
1. Focusing on more stable bimetallic structures, such as those based on WC.
2. Considering both initial activity and long-term stability in catalyst design.
3. Incorporating thermal stability considerations in first-principles studies.
4. Prioritizing both catalytic activity and structural stability under reaction conditions.

To truly achieve the rational design of catalysts from first principles, it is essential that the model systems are as close as possible to those under reaction conditions. This is especially important for bimetallic catalysts due to the adsorbate-induced segregation and diffusion of metal atoms [10.1021/cr300096b_13].

特定双金属结构的热力学稳定性，特别是在具有亚表面3d原子的Pt-3d系统中，对于模型表面和负载催化剂在氢化反应中的强相关性起着至关重要的作用。这种稳定性在超高真空（UHV）环境和实际氢化反应条件下都被观察到，使得模型表面能够准确地代表负载催化剂的行为 [10.1021/cr300096b_13]。这种相关性对于使用第一性原理在模型表面上设计更有效的催化剂具有重要意义，因为它表明从研究模型表面获得的见解可以直接应用于实际负载催化剂的开发。

然而，Pt-3d系统的稳定性面临挑战，特别是在高温下。当沉积在Pt上时，3d金属倾向于扩散到块体中，破坏表面和亚表面双金属结构。例如，俄歇电子能谱（AES）测量显示，Pt(111)上1个单层（ML）Ni的覆盖率在退火后降低，在800 K时降至不到0.2 ML [10.1039/c2cs35165j]。这种不稳定性限制了这些模型表面在实际催化系统中的应用。

为解决这个问题，研究人员探索了替代基底，如碳化钨（WC）。与Pt相比，WC作为外加金属的扩散屏障效果更好，即使在高温退火后仍能保持较高的3d金属覆盖率。例如，在多晶WC箔上沉积Ni的退火实验显示，最终覆盖率为0.6 ML，明显高于Ni/Pt(111)的观察结果 [10.1039/c2cs35165j]。这种改进的稳定性增强了第一性原理研究在模型表面上的应用，以设计实际的负载催化剂。

使用WC作为基底提供了几个优势：
1. 提高催化剂寿命，潜在增加工业可行性。
2. 通过减少稀有和昂贵的Pt族金属的使用来降低成本。
3. 基于模型表面研究更准确地预测实际催化剂行为。
4. 为具有独特性质的双金属催化剂提供新的设计可能性。

使用第一性原理在模型表面上设计更有效催化剂的影响包括：
1. 关注更稳定的双金属结构，如基于WC的结构。
2. 在催化剂设计中同时考虑初始活性和长期稳定性。
3. 在第一性原理研究中纳入热稳定性考虑。
4. 在反应条件下优先考虑催化活性和结构稳定性。

为了真正实现从第一性原理理性设计催化剂，确保模型系统尽可能接近反应条件下的实际情况至关重要。这对于双金属催化剂尤为重要，因为存在吸附剂诱导的金属原子分离和扩散 [10.1021/cr300096b_13]。
