<English>
Light olefins, particularly ethylene and propylene, are crucial feedstocks in the chemical industry, with a wide range of applications and significant market demand. These compounds serve as building blocks for numerous products including polymers (e.g., polyethylene, polypropylene), oxygenates (e.g., ethylene glycol, acetone), and important chemical intermediates (e.g., ethylbenzene, propionaldehyde) [10.1021/cr5002436_0].

Market trends indicate a steady increase in demand for light olefins, with global production capacity doubling over the past 15 years. In 2003, worldwide production reached 100 million tonnes for ethylene and 56 million tonnes for propylene [10.1002/9783527610044.hetcat0162]. The demand for propylene, in particular, has been growing faster than that of ethylene, especially in Western Europe [10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009].

The main production methods for light olefins include:

1. Steam cracking of hydrocarbons (primary method)
2. Fluid catalytic cracking (FCC)
3. Catalytic dehydrogenation of alkanes (growing in importance)

Steam cracking remains the dominant production method, accounting for about 61% of propylene production in 2002. However, the shift to lighter cracker feedstocks has reduced co-product volumes, necessitating the development of on-purpose production routes for higher olefins [10.1016/s1351-4180(14)70270-3].

The synthesis of light olefins, particularly through dehydrogenation, faces significant thermodynamic challenges:

1. Temperature effects: The reactions are highly endothermic, requiring high temperatures (550-750°C) to achieve economically viable conversions [10.1021/cr5002436_0]. Higher temperatures increase conversion but also accelerate side reactions, coke formation, and catalyst deactivation [10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x].

2. Pressure effects: Lower pressures generally favor olefin formation. Commercial processes typically operate at subatmospheric pressures (0.2-0.5 bar) to shift the equilibrium towards products [10.1016/j.ces.2004.07.103].

3. Alkane chain length effects: The equilibrium constant for paraffin dehydrogenation increases significantly with carbon number. Lighter alkanes require higher temperatures for dehydrogenation. For example, at 1 atm pressure and 40% conversion, propane dehydrogenation needs at least 580°C, while dodecane only requires 450°C [10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9].

4. Energy requirements: The enthalpy required for dehydrogenation decreases as the chain length increases (e.g., 137 kJ/mol for ethane, 124.3 kJ/mol for propane), making light olefin production more energy-intensive on a mass basis [10.1021/cr5002436_0].

To address these challenges, researchers are exploring alternative production methods such as oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) with CO2 as an oxidant. This approach helps overcome thermodynamic limitations by making the reaction exothermic and avoiding carbon formation [10.1021/ef0340716].

※※※
Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The production of light olefins stands at a critical juncture, driven by increasing demand and the need for more efficient, sustainable processes. Future research directions may focus on:

1. Advanced catalyst design: Developing catalysts that can operate at lower temperatures while maintaining high selectivity and resistance to coking. This could involve novel nanostructured materials or multi-functional catalysts that combine dehydrogenation and selective oxidation capabilities.

2. Process intensification: Exploring innovative reactor designs, such as membrane reactors or microreactors, that can overcome equilibrium limitations and improve energy efficiency.

3. Alternative feedstocks: Investigating the potential of bio-based feedstocks or waste plastics as sources for light olefins, aligning with circular economy principles.

4. Hybrid processes: Combining different technologies, such as catalytic dehydrogenation with membrane separation, to achieve higher yields and energy efficiency.

5. Artificial intelligence and machine learning: Leveraging these tools for catalyst discovery, process optimization, and predictive maintenance in olefin production plants.

These advancements could lead to more sustainable and economically viable production of light olefins, meeting the growing global demand while reducing the environmental footprint of the chemical industry.

轻质烯烃，尤其是乙烯和丙烯，是化工行业的关键原料，具有广泛的应用和显著的市场需求。这些化合物作为许多产品的基础构建块，包括聚合物（如聚乙烯、聚丙烯）、含氧化合物（如乙二醇、丙酮）和重要的化学中间体（如乙苯、丙醛）[10.1021/cr5002436_0]。

市场趋势显示轻质烯烃的需求稳步增长，过去15年全球产能翻了一番。2003年，全球乙烯产量达到1亿吨，丙烯产量达到5600万吨[10.1002/9783527610044.hetcat0162]。特别是丙烯的需求增长速度快于乙烯，尤其是在西欧地区[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009]。

轻质烯烃的主要生产方法包括：

1. 烃类蒸汽裂解（主要方法）
2. 流化催化裂解（FCC）
3. 烷烃催化脱氢（重要性日益增加）

蒸汽裂解仍然是主导生产方法，2002年约占丙烯产量的61%。然而，向更轻的裂解原料转变减少了副产品的数量，necessitating为高级烯烃开发定向生产路线[10.1016/s1351-4180(14)70270-3]。

轻质烯烃的合成，特别是通过脱氢，面临着重大的热力学挑战：

1. 温度效应：反应高度吸热，需要高温（550-750°C）才能达到经济可行的转化率[10.1021/cr5002436_0]。更高的温度增加了转化率，但也加速了副反应、积碳形成和催化剂失活[10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x]。

2. 压力效应：较低的压力通常有利于烯烃的形成。商业过程通常在亚大气压（0.2-0.5巴）下运行，以将平衡向产物方向移动[10.1016/j.ces.2004.07.103]。

3. 烷烃链长效应：烷烃脱氢的平衡常数随碳数的增加而显著增加。较轻的烷烃需要更高的温度进行脱氢。例如，在1大气压下40%转化率时，丙烷脱氢需要至少580°C，而十二烷只需要450°C [10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9]。

4. 能量需求：脱氢所需的焓随链长增加而减少（如乙烷137 kJ/mol，丙烷124.3 kJ/mol），使轻质烯烃生产在质量基础上更加耗能[10.1021/cr5002436_0]。

为了应对这些挑战，研究人员正在探索替代生产方法，如使用CO2作为氧化剂的氧化脱氢（ODH）。这种方法通过使反应成为放热反应并避免碳形成来帮助克服热力学限制[10.1021/ef0340716]。

※※※
综合理解与未来展望：轻质烯烃的生产正处于关键时刻，由不断增长的需求和对更高效、更可持续过程的需求所驱动。未来的研究方向可能集中在：

1. 先进催化剂设计：开发能在较低温度下运行，同时保持高选择性和抗积碳性的催化剂。这可能涉及新型纳米结构材料或结合脱氢和选择性氧化能力的多功能催化剂。

2. 过程强化：探索创新的反应器设计，如膜反应器或微反应器，可以克服平衡限制并提高能源效率。

3. 替代原料：研究生物基原料或废塑料作为轻质烯烃来源的潜力，符合循环经济原则。

4. 混合工艺：结合不同技术，如催化脱氢与膜分离，以实现更高的产率和能源效率。

5. 人工智能和机器学习：利用这些工具进行催化剂发现、过程优化和烯烃生产工厂的预测性维护。

这些进展可能导致更可持续和经济可行的轻质烯烃生产，满足不断增长的全球需求，同时减少化工行业的环境足迹。
