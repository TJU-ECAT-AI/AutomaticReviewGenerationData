Light olefins, particularly ethylene and propylene, are crucial feedstocks in the chemical industry, with significant market demand and diverse applications. These compounds serve as building blocks for a wide range of products including polymers (e.g., polyethylene, polypropylene), oxygenates (e.g., ethylene glycol, acetone), and important chemical intermediates (e.g., ethylbenzene, propionaldehyde) [10.1021/cr5002436\_0].

Market trends indicate a steady increase in demand for light olefins, with global production capacity doubling over the past 15 years. In 2003, worldwide production reached 100 million tonnes for ethylene and 56 million tonnes for propylene [10.1002/9783527610044.hetcat0162]. The demand for propylene, in particular, has been growing faster than that of ethylene, especially in Western Europe [10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009].

The main production methods for light olefins include:

1. Steam cracking of hydrocarbons (primary method)
2. Fluid catalytic cracking (FCC)
3. Catalytic dehydrogenation of alkanes (growing in importance)
4. Methanol-to-Olefins (MTO) technology (emerging method)

Each of these methods faces significant thermodynamic challenges in the synthesis of light olefins:

1. Temperature effects: Higher temperatures are generally required to achieve economically viable conversion levels. For example, propane dehydrogenation requires temperatures of 550-750°C to obtain alkane conversions >50% at 1 bar [10.1021/cr5002436\_0]. However, higher temperatures also accelerate unwanted side reactions, coke formation, and catalyst deactivation [10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x].

2. Pressure effects: Lower pressures are typically favored for dehydrogenation reactions to shift the equilibrium towards products. Commercial processes often operate at subatmospheric pressures (0.3-0.5 atm) to achieve higher per-pass conversions [10.1002/9783527626113.ch15].

3. Alkane chain length effects: The equilibrium constant for paraffin dehydrogenation increases significantly with carbon number. This means that lighter alkanes (e.g., ethane) require higher temperatures for dehydrogenation compared to heavier alkanes. For instance, at 1 atm pressure and 40% conversion, propane dehydrogenation needs at least 580°C, while dodecane only requires 450°C [10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9].

4. Endothermic nature: Dehydrogenation reactions are highly endothermic (e.g., ΔH298° = +110 kJ mol−1 for propane to propene), requiring substantial energy input [10.1002/9783527626113.ch15].

5. Equilibrium limitations: Conversions are thermodynamically limited, especially at lower temperatures, necessitating careful reactor and process design to achieve economically viable yields [10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009].

To address these challenges, researchers and industry professionals are exploring various strategies:

1. Development of more effective catalysts with high activity and selectivity [10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009].
2. Use of CO2 as an oxidant in oxidative dehydrogenation to make the reaction exothermic and avoid carbon formation [10.1021/ef0340716].
3. Optimization of process conditions, including the use of lower pressures and diluents to increase conversions [10.1016/s0920-5861(99)00047-4].
4. Exploration of alternative production methods like methanol-to-olefins (MTO) technology, which allows for greater flexibility in adjusting product ratios [10.1016/s0167-2991(04)80018-1].

3. Alternative feedstocks: Investigating the use of renewable resources, such as biomass or CO2, as feedstocks for light olefin production. This could help address sustainability concerns and reduce dependence on fossil fuels.

4. Artificial intelligence and machine learning: Employing these tools to optimize process conditions, predict catalyst performance, and accelerate the discovery of new materials and reaction pathways.
