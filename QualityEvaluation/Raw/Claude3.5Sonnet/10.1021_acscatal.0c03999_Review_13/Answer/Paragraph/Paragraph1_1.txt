<English>
The use of sulfur as an alternative oxidant in methane oxidative coupling presents a novel approach with distinct advantages and challenges compared to traditional oxygen-based systems. This comparative analysis focuses on thermodynamics, reaction mechanisms, catalytic performance, and alkene selectivity.

Thermodynamics: Sulfur oxidation demonstrates a significantly lower thermodynamic driving force for overoxidation compared to oxygen. The energy well for sulfur oxidation of methane to CS2 is 3.45 eV, substantially higher than the 13.41 eV for oxygen oxidation to CO2 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. This reduced driving force for overoxidation is advantageous for selective alkene production, potentially leading to improved product yields.

Reaction mechanisms: Sulfur-based systems involve both surface and gas-phase reactions. On the surface, methane coupling occurs on transition metal sulfides, with gaseous sulfur regenerating vacancies. In the gas phase, sulfur radical species (SH and HS2) participate in hydrogen abstraction, similar to OH and OOH radicals in oxygen-based systems [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. This dual-mechanism approach offers unique opportunities for catalyst design and process optimization.

Catalytic performance: The current performance of sulfur-based catalysts is modest and far from practical consideration. Ethylene selectivity of up to 18% can be achieved on a PdS catalyst, but this requires very high temperatures (1050°C). These high temperatures are necessary due to the thermodynamics of the reaction and the high CH activation barriers on sulfide catalysts, which range from 1.91 eV on MoS2 to 2.68 eV for TiS2 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. The poor hydrogen abstraction ability of sulfide catalysts compared to metal oxides further compounds this challenge.

Alkene selectivity: Sulfur-based systems show promise for improved alkene selectivity, particularly in propane dehydrogenation. With propyl-S2 adducts, propylene formation is favored both kinetically and thermodynamically, unlike propyl-O2 adducts which can form various side products such as propanal, methyloxirane, and oxetane [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. This inherent selectivity advantage could lead to more efficient processes with fewer unwanted by-products.

Key challenges in sulfur-based systems include the high reaction temperatures required, poor hydrogen abstraction ability of sulfide catalysts, and high CH activation barriers. However, opportunities for improvement exist, such as descriptor-based theoretical screening to identify catalysts with higher conversion rates, exploration of metal-sulfur (MS) bond energy as a descriptor for both CH activation and CC coupling reaction barriers, and further investigation of gas-phase sulfur radical species in hydrogen abstraction.

※※※

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The use of sulfur as an alternative oxidant in methane oxidative coupling represents a paradigm shift in catalyst design and reaction engineering. While current performance metrics fall short of practical application, the fundamental advantages in thermodynamics and selectivity warrant continued research and development.

Future research directions may include:

1. Advanced computational modeling: Utilizing machine learning and artificial intelligence to predict and design novel sulfide catalysts with optimized performance.

2. Hybrid catalyst systems: Exploring synergistic effects between sulfide and oxide catalysts to combine the selectivity advantages of sulfur with the activity of traditional oxide systems.

3. Process intensification: Developing innovative reactor designs that can effectively manage the high temperatures required while maximizing heat recovery and energy efficiency.

4. In-situ characterization techniques: Advancing our understanding of the dynamic surface and gas-phase processes occurring during sulfur-based oxidative coupling reactions.

5. Techno-economic analysis: Comprehensive evaluation of the potential industrial viability of sulfur-based systems, considering factors such as catalyst lifetime, sulfur recovery, and overall process economics.

By addressing these challenges and capitalizing on the unique properties of sulfur-based systems, researchers may unlock new pathways for efficient and selective conversion of methane and other light alkanes to value-added products.

<Chinese>
与传统的基于氧气的系统相比，使用硫作为甲烷氧化偶联的替代氧化剂是一种具有独特优势和挑战的新方法。本比较分析主要集中在热力学、反应机理、催化性能和烯烃选择性方面。

热力学：硫氧化显示出与氧气相比显著较低的过度氧化热力学驱动力。甲烷硫氧化生成CS2的能量阱为3.45 eV，远高于氧气氧化生成CO2的13.41 eV [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]。这种降低的过度氧化驱动力有利于选择性烯烃生产，可能导致更高的产品收率。

反应机理：基于硫的系统涉及表面和气相反应。在表面上，甲烷偶联发生在过渡金属硫化物上，气相硫再生空位。在气相中，硫自由基物种（SH和HS2）参与氢抽提，类似于基于氧气系统中的OH和OOH自由基 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]。这种双机制方法为催化剂设计和过程优化提供了独特的机会。

催化性能：目前基于硫的催化剂性能适中，距离实际应用还有很大差距。在PdS催化剂上可以达到高达18%的乙烯选择性，但这需要非常高的温度（1050°C）。这些高温是由于反应的热力学和硫化物催化剂上较高的CH活化能垒所必需的，这些能垒从MoS2的1.91 eV到TiS2的2.68 eV不等 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]。与金属氧化物相比，硫化物催化剂较差的氢抽提能力进一步加剧了这一挑战。

烯烃选择性：基于硫的系统在提高烯烃选择性方面显示出希望，特别是在丙烷脱氢反应中。对于丙基-S2加合物，丙烯的形成在动力学和热力学上都更有利，而丙基-O2加合物则可能形成各种副产品，如丙醛、甲基环氧乙烷和环氧丁烷 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]。这种固有的选择性优势可能导致更高效的过程，产生更少的不必要副产品。

基于硫系统的主要挑战包括所需的高反应温度、硫化物催化剂较差的氢抽提能力以及高CH活化能垒。然而，改进的机会仍然存在，例如基于描述符的理论筛选以识别具有更高转化率的催化剂，探索金属-硫（MS）键能作为CH活化和CC偶联反应能垒的描述符，以及进一步研究气相硫自由基物种在氢抽提中的作用。

※※※

综合理解与未来展望：使用硫作为甲烷氧化偶联的替代氧化剂代表了催化剂设计和反应工程的范式转变。尽管当前的性能指标还不能满足实际应用，但热力学和选择性方面的基本优势值得继续研究和开发。

未来研究方向可能包括：

1. 先进的计算模型：利用机器学习和人工智能来预测和设计具有优化性能的新型硫化物催化剂。

2. 混合催化剂系统：探索硫化物和氧化物催化剂之间的协同效应，结合硫的选择性优势和传统氧化物系统的活性。

3. 过程强化：开发创新的反应器设计，可以有效管理所需的高温，同时最大化热回收和能源效率。

4. 原位表征技术：提高我们对硫基氧化偶联反应过程中发生的动态表面和气相过程的理解。

5. 技术经济分析：对硫基系统的潜在工业可行性进行全面评估，考虑催化剂寿命、硫回收和整体过程经济性等因素。

通过解决这些挑战并利用硫基系统的独特性质，研究人员可能会为甲烷和其他轻烷烃向高附加值产品的高效和选择性转化开辟新的途径。
