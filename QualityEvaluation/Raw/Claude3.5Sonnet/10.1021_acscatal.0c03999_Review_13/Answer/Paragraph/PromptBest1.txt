Comparative Analysis of Sulfur as an Alternative Oxidant in Methane Oxidative Coupling

The use of sulfur as an alternative oxidant in methane oxidative coupling presents both advantages and challenges when compared to traditional oxygen-based systems. This review focuses on the thermodynamics, reaction mechanisms, catalytic performance, and alkene selectivity of sulfur-based systems, as well as the key challenges and opportunities for future improvements.

Thermodynamics:
One of the primary advantages of using sulfur as an oxidant is the significantly lower thermodynamic driving force for overoxidation compared to oxygen. The energy well for sulfur oxidation of methane to CS2 is 3.45 eV, which is considerably higher than the 13.41 eV for oxygen oxidation to CO2 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. This reduced driving force for overoxidation is favorable for selective alkene production, as it decreases the likelihood of complete oxidation to carbon dioxide.

Reaction Mechanisms:
Sulfur-based systems involve both surface and gas-phase reactions. On the surface, methane coupling occurs on transition metal sulfides, with gaseous sulfur regenerating vacancies. In the gas phase, sulfur radical species (SH and HS2) participate in hydrogen abstraction, similar to OH and OOH radicals in oxygen-based systems [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. This dual-pathway mechanism contributes to the overall reaction efficiency and selectivity.

Catalytic Performance:
The performance of sulfur-based catalysts is currently modest and far from practical consideration. Ethylene selectivity of up to 18% can be achieved on a PdS catalyst, but this requires very high temperatures (1050°C) [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. The high temperatures are necessary due to the thermodynamics of the reaction and the high CH activation barriers on sulfide catalysts. These barriers range from 1.91 eV on MoS2 to 2.68 eV for TiS2 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13], which are significantly higher than those observed in traditional oxygen-based systems.

Alkene Selectivity:
Sulfur-based systems show promise for improved alkene selectivity, particularly in propane dehydrogenation. With propyl-S2 adducts, propylene formation is favored both kinetically and thermodynamically, in part due to the relative instability of the S-containing hydrocarbon products [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. This is in contrast to propyl-O2 adducts, which can form various side products such as propanal, methyloxirane, and oxetane, all of which are more thermodynamically stable than propylene.

Key Challenges:
1. High reaction temperatures required
2. Poor hydrogen abstraction ability of sulfide catalysts compared to metal oxides
3. High CH activation barriers on sulfide catalysts

Opportunities for Future Improvements:
1. Descriptor-based theoretical screening to identify catalysts with higher conversion rates
2. Exploration of MS (metal-sulfur) bond energy as a descriptor for both CH activation and CC coupling reaction barriers
3. Further investigation of gas-phase sulfur radical species in hydrogen abstraction and their role in improving alkene selectivity
4. Development of catalysts that can operate at lower temperatures while maintaining high selectivity

Comparative analysis of sulfur as an alternative oxidant in methane oxidative coupling: thermodynamics, reaction mechanisms, catalytic performance, challenges, and future prospects in contrast to traditional oxygen-based systems

The use of sulfur as an alternative oxidant in methane oxidative coupling presents a novel approach with distinct advantages and challenges compared to traditional oxygen-based systems. This review section examines the key aspects of this approach, including thermodynamics, reaction mechanisms, catalytic performance, and alkene selectivity.

Thermodynamics:
One of the primary advantages of using sulfur as an oxidant is its significantly lower thermodynamic driving force for overoxidation. Zhu et al. reported that the energy well for sulfur oxidation of methane to CS2 is 3.45 eV, which is considerably higher than the 13.41 eV for oxygen oxidation to CO2 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. This reduced driving force for overoxidation is particularly beneficial for selective alkene production, as it mitigates the tendency for complete oxidation to carbon dioxide.

Reaction Mechanisms:
Sulfur-based systems involve a complex interplay of surface and gas-phase reactions. On the catalyst surface, methane coupling occurs on transition metal sulfides, with gaseous sulfur regenerating vacancies. In the gas phase, sulfur radical species such as SH and HS2 participate in hydrogen abstraction, analogous to the role of OH and OOH radicals in oxygen-based systems [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. This dual-phase reaction mechanism offers unique opportunities for tailoring catalyst design and reaction conditions.

Catalytic Performance:
Current performance of sulfur-based catalysts for methane oxidative coupling is modest and far from practical implementation. An ethylene selectivity of up to 18% can be achieved on a PdS catalyst, but this requires extremely high temperatures of 1050°C [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. The need for such high temperatures stems from both the thermodynamics of the reaction and the relatively high CH activation barriers on sulfide catalysts, which range from 1.91 eV on MoS2 to 2.68 eV for TiS2 [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. These sulfide catalysts are poor hydrogen abstractors compared to traditional metal oxide catalysts used in oxygen-based systems.

Alkene Selectivity:
Despite the challenges in catalytic performance, sulfur-based systems show promise for improved alkene selectivity. In the context of propane dehydrogenation, propylene formation is favored both kinetically and thermodynamically with propyl-S2 adducts. This is in contrast to propyl-O2 adducts, which can form various side products such as propanal, methyloxirane, and oxetane, all of which are more thermodynamically stable than propylene [10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13]. The improved selectivity in sulfur-based systems is partly attributed to the relative instability of S-containing hydrocarbon products.

Key Challenges:
1. High reaction temperatures required for adequate performance
2. Poor hydrogen abstraction ability of sulfide catalysts compared to metal oxides
3. High CH activation barriers on sulfide catalysts

Future Prospects:
To address these challenges and capitalize on the potential benefits of sulfur-based oxidants, several avenues for future research and improvement can be identified:
1. Descriptor-based theoretical screening to identify catalysts with higher conversion rates and lower operating temperatures
2. Exploration of metal-sulfur (MS) bond energy as a descriptor for both CH activation and CC coupling reaction barriers
3. Further investigation of gas-phase sulfur radical species in hydrogen abstraction and their role in improving alkene selectivity
4. Development of novel catalyst formulations that can operate at lower temperatures while maintaining high selectivity

Looking ahead, research efforts could focus on several key areas:

2. In-situ characterization techniques to elucidate the dynamic interplay between surface and gas-phase reactions in sulfur-based systems
3. Computational studies to predict and design sulfide catalysts with optimized electronic structures for improved CH activation and CC coupling
4. Exploration of novel reactor designs that can effectively manage the high temperatures required while maximizing selectivity and conversion

By addressing these challenges and opportunities, the scientific community may unlock the full potential of sulfur-based oxidants, potentially revolutionizing the field of alkane activation and paving the way for more efficient and selective production of valuable chemical feedstocks.
