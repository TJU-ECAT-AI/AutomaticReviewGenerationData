The kinetics of coke growth plays a crucial role in coke formation, but current models fall short of elucidating the mechanism at the molecular level \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\]. Strategies such as cofeeding gas, metal promoter, and support modification have been validated as effective in preventing coke formation \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\]. Cofeeding hydrogen can eliminate coke precursors and reduce the acidity of the support \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\], while promoters like Sn can fine-tune the catalytic properties of Pt, enhancing both activity and stability \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\]. However, determining the optimal type and loading of promoter remains a challenge \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\]. Modifying conventional support alumina facilitates coke transfer between metal and support \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\]. Novel catalyst designs, including single atom/site strategies and confined subnanosized Pt clusters in zeolites, have demonstrated remarkable resistance to coke formation \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\]. Despite these advancements, regeneration remains essential to extend catalyst life \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\]. Lowering regeneration temperature is critical for cost reduction and activity stabilization \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\]. An urgent need exists for the integration of experimental and computational techniques to tackle the complex issue of coke formation in heterogeneous catalysis \[10.1021/acscatal.1c00331_4\].
