          The literature does not provide detailed analysis on the oxidative dehydrogenation of alkanes (ODH), including the interplay between various catalysts, the challenges in achieving high selectivity and conversion, the proposed reaction mechanisms, and the role of site isolation in addressing selectivity issues. However, it does mention that vanadium-based catalysts generally outperform other metal-containing catalysts for propane ODH. The literature also highlights the importance of high surface areas and homogeneities in catalysts for improved performance in heterogeneous processes. Site isolation is suggested to play a role in controlling the structures of catalytic sites, which can influence selectivity. The use of the thermolytic molecular precursor (TMP) method can produce catalysts with high surface areas and homogeneities, which can improve the performance of propane ODH catalysts. Site isolation is also mentioned as an important factor in catalysis, and the TMP method can provide control over the structures of isolated catalytic sites. Site isolation is mentioned as a strategy to introduce isolated catalytic centers onto the surface of an oxide support, which can lead to improved control over the structure of the catalytic site and the generation of site-isolated catalysts. Site isolation is also mentioned as a key factor in the reactivities of some materials, such as bimetallic oxides and catalyst support interfaces, where the spatial arrangement of metal atoms on a catalyst surface is crucial for defining reactivity. The support materials Al2O3, ZrO2, and MgO have been examined. Site isolation is mentioned as a key factor in the reactivities of well-defined iron sites for the selective oxidation of hydrocarbons.
