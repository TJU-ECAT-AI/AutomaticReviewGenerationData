                    The challenges in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) for industrial propylene production are multifaceted, encompassing high-temperature stability, propylene selectivity, and managing thermodynamic limitations and side reactions \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\]. High temperatures, essential for propane conversion, can lead to catalyst deactivation through sintering and coke deposition. Researchers are addressing these issues by developing strategies such as promoter introduction, encapsulation, hydroxyl anchoring, and support modifications to enhance catalyst stability and resistance \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\]. However, excessive metal promoters can make catalysts uneconomical, hydroxyl anchoring can be complex, and encapsulation requires proper ligands \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\]. Utilizing supports with large outer surfaces can decrease diffusion restriction, but the need for rare templates hinders industrial application \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\]. Hierarchical zeolites as PDH supports offer a solution to diffusion limitations \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\].
                    ※※※
                    Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The advancements in PDH catalyst development, particularly in addressing high-temperature stability, propylene selectivity, and thermodynamic limitations, have opened new avenues for research. Future directions might focus on the development of thermodynamically stable single atom catalysts, which could potentially overcome the challenges of sintering and reconstruction under PDH conditions \[10.1039/D0CS00814A\]. Novel synthesis methods, such as strong electrostatic adsorption and galvanic replacement, offer opportunities for the scale-up of single atom catalyst production \[10.1039/D0CS00814A\]. Additionally, the exploration of alternative catalysts like VOx- and GaOx-based materials, which are being developed as potential replacements for CrOx- and Pt-based catalysts, could lead to improved dehydrogenation performance \[10.1039/D0CS00814A\]. Revealing active sites and mechanisms under reaction conditions remains a significant challenge, necessitating advanced operando/in situ characterization techniques \[10.1039/D0CS00814A\]. The roles of Sn species and coking behaviors on PtSn/Al2O3 catalysts, as well as the actual active sites on metal oxide-based catalysts, are yet to be fully understood \[10.1039/D0CS00814A\]. Theoretical calculations can assist in explaining experimental results, but matching experimental details with DFT models is limited \[10.1039/D0CS00814A\]. Developing thermodynamically stable single atom catalysts is crucial, with novel methods like strong electrostatic adsorption and galvanic replacement providing scale-up opportunities \[10.1039/D0CS00814A\].
