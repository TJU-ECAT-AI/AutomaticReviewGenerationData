                    The challenges in propane dehydrogenation (PDH) for industrial propylene production are multifaceted, encompassing high-temperature stability, propylene selectivity, and resistance to side reactions, all within thermodynamic constraints \[10.1016/j.fuproc.2016.04.016\]. High temperatures, essential for propane conversion, can lead to catalyst deactivation through sintering and coke deposition \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\]. Researchers address these issues by introducing promoters, encapsulation, hydroxyl anchoring, and support modifications to enhance catalyst stability and resistance \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\]. However, excessive metal promoters can make catalysts uneconomical, encapsulation requires proper ligand selection, and hydroxyl anchoring can be complex and costly \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\]. Utilizing supports with large outer surfaces for diffusion restriction is hindered by the need for rare templates in industrial production \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_5\]. Pt-based catalysts face challenges with low propylene selectivity and rapid deactivation due to coke formation \[10.1039/C5SC01227A\]. Studies reveal that Pt(211) surfaces exhibit the highest dehydrogenation activity among Pt surfaces \[10.1039/C5SC01227A\]. Thermodynamic corrections for desorption barriers significantly influence propylene selectivity, not accounted for in DFT calculations \[10.1039/C5SC01227A\]. CHC accumulation during conversion, indicated by a higher dissociation barrier than dehydrogenation, aligns with experimental observations and highlights a concern for coke formation \[10.1039/C5SC01227A\]. Theoretical studies aim to optimize catalysts for specific processes, but limitations exist in modeling complex catalytic systems \[10.1039/C5SC01227A\]. In situ characterization and controlled synthesis are crucial for identifying and maximizing active, selective sites in real catalysts \[10.1039/C5SC01227A\].
