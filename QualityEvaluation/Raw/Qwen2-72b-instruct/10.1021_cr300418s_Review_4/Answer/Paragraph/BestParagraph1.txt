Raman spectroscopy showed a frequency shift and broadening for the ceria peak, corroborating XRD and EXAFS data. Chemical reactivity tests using H2 and in situ time-resolved XRD, XANES, and DRIFT data during the WGS reaction provided insights into catalytic performance. The solubility limit for Ni was found to be closer to 10% \[10.1021/jp103958u\]. High Sensitivity-Low Energy Ion Scattering (HS-LEIS) is a sensitive technique for analyzing the outermost atomic layer of materials, crucial for studying dopant incorporation and distribution in metal oxide catalysts \[10.1016/j.cattod.2008.10.012\]. It measures the energy from ions scattered from the surface to determine the mass of surface atoms, identifying elements with masses from 12 to 60 amu using 4He+ ions, with improved resolution for heavier elements using 20Ne+ ions. HS-LEIS is orders of magnitude more sensitive than conventional LEIS, allowing Static LEIS with minimal surface sputtering. Quantification is straightforward, with peak areas proportional to surface atom fractions. HS-LEIS compares favorably to XPS and SIMS for information depth and quantification, providing sensitive analysis for the outermost atomic layer where catalytic processes occur. It also offers insights into layers beneath the surface, relevant for the dispersion of the active catalytic component. The technique's sensitivity and quantification capabilities make it a valuable tool for establishing the relationship between dopant presence, structural changes, and enhanced catalytic performance \[10.1016/j.cattod.2008.10.012\]. Crucial characterization methods for verifying dopant incorporation and distribution in metal oxide catalysts include XRD, Raman spectroscopy, BET, SEM, XPS, CH4 TPR-MS, and O2-TPD \[10.1016/j.cattod.2011.03.061\]. These techniques elucidate the relationship between dopants, structural changes, and enhanced catalytic performance. For instance, cerium doping causes structural deformation, increasing crystal defects and oxygen vacancy concentration, which facilitates oxygen mobility and boosts catalytic activity.
