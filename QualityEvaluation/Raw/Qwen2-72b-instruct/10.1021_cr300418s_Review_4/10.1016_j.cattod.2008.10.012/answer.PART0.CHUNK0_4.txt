1.The literature does not provide information on comprehensive characterization methods and analytical approaches for verifying the successful incorporation and homogeneous distribution of dopants in metal oxide catalysts. However, it does mention High Sensitivity-Low Energy Ion Scattering (HS-LEIS) as a technique that can analyze the chemical composition of the outermost atomic layer of a material, which is crucial in the study of catalysis and optimization of industrial catalysts. HS-LEIS compares favorably to XPS and SIMS in terms of information depth and quantification. It can determine the mass of surface atoms by measuring the energy from ions that are scattered from the surface, providing sensitive analysis for the outermost atomic layer where catalytic processes take place. HS-LEIS can also provide information about layers immediately beneath the surface, which is relevant for the dispersion of the active catalytic component on the surface of the catalyst. The technique is sensitive and exhibits detection limits on the order of several ppm for high values of m2/m1, while mass resolution is good for low values of m2/m1. HS-LEIS spectra can be recorded with different primary ions to improve mass resolution and sensitivity for lighter or heavier surface atoms. The technique is orders of magnitude more sensitive than conventional LEIS and can perform Static LEIS, obtaining good spectra while less than 0.5% of a monolayer is sputtered from the sample surface. Quantification is straightforward, as HS-LEIS peaks are proportional to the amount of scattering surface atoms for a given primary ion, primary ion energy, and scattering surface atom. Backgrounds are subtracted from the peaks, the peaks are integrated, and peak areas are proportional to the surface fraction of the atoms. When a pure reference of the element is analyzed, peak areas from the unknown samples can be expressed as fractions of the peak area for the pure element, yielding surface fractions for the unknown samples. Calibration plots can also be used for quantification, where the area of one peak is plotted as a function of the area of another peak for a number of samples. If there are no other elements to consider and if Vegards law holds, these data points will form a straight line.
