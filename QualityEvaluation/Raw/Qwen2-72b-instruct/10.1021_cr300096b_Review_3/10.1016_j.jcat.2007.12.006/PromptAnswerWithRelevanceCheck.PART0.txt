<quotes>

[1] "Over the last several decades bimetallic or multimetallic catalysts have replaced monometallic catalysts in industrial processes due to the beneficial activity, selectivity, and stability modifications that may be achieved by the inclusion of additional metals [1] ,[2] ,[3] . These bimetallic and multimetallic catalysts are often prepared on an industrial scale using either successive impregnation or co-impregnation methods [4] . However, these traditional methodologies frequently provide inadequate control over metal placement and accordingly yield catalysts containing both isolated, monometallic particles and bimetallic particles with varying compositions [5] ,[6] ,[7] . This complex mixture of particles results in poor control of the final catalyst performance and makes any definitive correlations between catalyst performance, catalyst characterization, and catalyst composition virtually impossible."

</quotes>

<EnglishVersionAnswer>

1.The most commonly utilized synthesis methods for supported bimetallic catalysts are successive impregnation and co-impregnation methods. These methods have been widely used in industrial processes due to the beneficial modifications in activity, selectivity, and stability that can be achieved by including additional metals. However, these traditional methodologies often provide inadequate control over metal placement, resulting in catalysts containing both isolated, monometallic particles and bimetallic particles with varying compositions. This complex mixture of particles leads to poor control of the final catalyst performance and makes it virtually impossible to establish definitive correlations between catalyst performance, catalyst characterization, and catalyst composition.

</EnglishVersionAnswer>
