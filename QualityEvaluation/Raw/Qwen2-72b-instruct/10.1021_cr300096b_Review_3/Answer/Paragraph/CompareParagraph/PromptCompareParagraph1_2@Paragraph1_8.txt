The synthesis of supported bimetallic catalysts, pivotal in enhancing catalytic activity, selectivity, and stability, employs a plethora of methods, each with distinct advantages and applications \[10.1007/s10853-006-0894-x\]. The ammonia method, for instance, yields finely dispersed Co3O4 on silica and synthetic kenyaite, with particle sizes varying based on the support \[10.1007/s10853-006-0894-x\]. This method results in higher catalytic activity in benzene combustion due to finer dispersion and easier reducibility of Co3O4 \[10.1007/s10853-006-0894-x\]. The addition of Pt further boosts activity, particularly in impregnated samples due to the synergy between cobalt oxide and Pt \[10.1007/s10853-006-0894-x\]. The ammonia method's advantage in achieving higher catalytic activity in complete benzene oxidation is attributed to the finer dispersion of easily reducible Co3O4 \[10.1007/s10853-006-0894-x\].

    Microemulsion preparation of nanoparticles offers superior control over size and composition, a significant advantage over other techniques where consistent alloy composition is challenging to achieve \[10.1016/s0022-0728(04)00328-6\]. This method facilitates consistent alloy composition in nanoparticles, crucial for catalyst efficacy \[10.1016/s0022-0728(04)00328-6\].

    A novel adsorbate-induced surface segregation technique overcomes the drawbacks of traditional methods like active surface area loss and incomplete noble-metal shells \[10.1002/anie.200806209\]. This method offers improved efficacy in catalyst production \[10.1002/anie.200806209\].

    Synthesis methods for supported bimetallic catalysts include co-complexation for alloy-type bimetallics, sequential dendrimer-templating for core/shell structures, and integrating dendrimer-encapsulated nanoparticles (DENs) into sol-gel matrices \[10.1021/jp0469665\]. These methods offer control over nanoparticle size and composition, stability, and enhanced catalytic activity, particularly for hydrogenation and carbon-coupling reactions \[10.1021/jp0469665\]. Co-complexation and redox displacement reactions are noted for synthesizing stable bimetallic DENs with high catalytic activity \[10.1021/jp0469665\].

    Common synthesis methods for supported bimetallic catalysts include precipitation, deposition-precipitation, co-precipitation, sol-gel, sonochemical, and incipient wetness impregnation, with the latter being the most frequently used \[10.1016/j.ijhydene.2009.05.044\]. Organometallic cluster-derived nanoparticles offer superior control over bimetallic composition and particle size, leading to more stable, active, and selective catalysts, particularly for hydrogenation reactions and efficient ethanol steam reforming \[10.1016/j.ijhydene.2009.05.044\].

    Supported bimetallic catalysts are synthesized using incipient wetness impregnation, where metal salts are dissolved in water matching the support's pore volume, followed by treatments to minimize sintering and agglomeration \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.12.021\]. Reverse micelle synthesis, offering uniform nanoparticle size by reducing metal precursors in microemulsions, enhances catalyst activity understanding and hydrogenation efficiency \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.12.021\]. Both methods are crucial in catalyst production, with reverse micelle synthesis distinguished for its nanoparticle uniformity and improved hydrogenation activity \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.12.021\].

    Impregnation, while common, may not ensure close metal contact, crucial for catalytic performance \[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003\]. Reductive deposition precipitation (RDP), by contrast, facilitates better metal interaction through redox chemistry, enhancing catalytic efficacy \[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003\]. RDP's advantage in performance is noted, yet detailed promoter-metal interaction remains understudied \[10.1016/j.jcat.2009.02.003\].

    The one-step method, using a reducing agent for metal cations with differential reduction potentials, has gained prominence \[10.1039/c1jm12020d\]. NH3BH3, for instance, reduces Au3+ to Au NPs, which then catalyze Co2+ reduction to form Co shells, creating magnetically recyclable Au@Co coreshell NPs \[10.1039/c1jm12020d\]. This method is general, applicable to non-noble metal-based bimetallic coreshell NPs \[10.1039/c1jm12020d\]. Other reducing agents, such as ODA, BT, and ascorbic acid, enable the synthesis of various bimetallic coreshell NPs, including Au@Pd with adjustable Pd shell morphology \[10.1039/c1jm12020d\]. The one-step route's simplicity, efficiency, and generality offer advantages over the two-step method \[10.1039/c1jm12020d\].

    Supported bimetallic catalysts are predominantly synthesized using successive impregnation and co-impregnation methods, favored for enhancing activity, selectivity, and stability \[10.1016/j.jcat.2007.12.006\]. However, these traditional techniques often lack precise metal placement control, leading to a mixture of monometallic and bimetallic particles with variable compositions, which complicates performance prediction and characterization \[10.1016/j.jcat.2007.12.006\].

    Ligand-stabilized bimetallic molecular precursors offer an alternative, where ligands are thermally removed after adsorption onto the support \[10.1006/jcat.2000.2905\]. Studies have utilized mono- and bimetallic carbonyl clusters as precursors, supported on oxide materials and decarbonylated under vacuum or inert atmosphere \[10.1006/jcat.2000.2905\]. The structure of the metal core often remains intact post-ligand removal, and these catalysts have been tested in various probe reactions, revealing insights into catalytic active sites \[10.1006/jcat.2000.2905\].

    Reductive deposition precipitation (RDP) outperforms impregnation (IMP) and ion-exchange in catalyst activity \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.09.021\]. RDP, involving hydrogen adsorption on pre-reduced metals and promoter deposition, enhances catalytic properties \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.09.021\]. Microwave-assisted polyol reduction (MAPR), a novel technique, synthesizes Pt-based electro-catalysts with uniform heating for shorter crystallization and more homogeneous nucleation, outperforming conventional methods \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.09.021\].
