                The coprecipitation method for synthesizing doped ceria catalysts, as detailed in reference \[10.1021/cr300418s_3\], involves dissolving Ce(NO3)4 with a precursor for the dopant, which can be a nitrate for Gd, Y, La, Sm, Yb, Pr dopants, a chloride for Nb, Ta dopants, or an oxynitrate for Zr dopant. The solvent is evaporated to cause coprecipitation, and the residue is calcined at 600 degrees Celsius in air. For Nb-doped ceria, an aqueous solution of Ce(NO3)4 and NbCl5 is treated with NH4OH, and the precipitate is washed, dried, and calcined in air at 600 degrees Celsius. Different preparation techniques can significantly affect the structural properties and catalytic performance of the resulting catalysts. For instance, the Nb-doped ceria samples prepared by different methods exhibit distinct n-butane combustion rates, with the sample made from hydroxide showing a 5% higher rate. Moreover, the effective activation energies are also notably different, with the sample obtained from the coprecipitated hydroxides having a lower activation energy.

Future research endeavors might focus on the development of novel dopants and the refinement of coprecipitation techniques to achieve higher catalytic activity and selectivity. The investigation of the interplay between dopant type, concentration, and the coprecipitation process parameters could provide insights into the design of more efficient catalysts for propane dehydrogenation.
