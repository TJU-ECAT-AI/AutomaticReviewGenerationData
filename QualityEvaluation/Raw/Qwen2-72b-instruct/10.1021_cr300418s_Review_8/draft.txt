1. The complex effects of dopants on the catalytic properties of metal oxides: A comprehensive analysis of same-valence and high-valence dopants in various host systems

The complex effects of dopants on the catalytic properties of metal oxides have been elucidated through computational and experimental studies, as detailed in references \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\], \[10.1021/jp205432\], and \[10.1021/jp103604b\]. High-valence dopants (HVDs) have a more intricate impact on the chemistry of the host oxide compared to low-valence dopants (LVDs), contingent on the reducibility of the host oxide \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\]. In irreducible oxides, HVDs can impede oxidation by the Mars-van Krevelen mechanism, whereas in reducible oxides, they can donate electrons to reduce cations in the host oxide. Same-valence dopants (SVDs) generally enhance the performance of the host oxide in catalytic applications \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\]. However, formulating simple rules for the effects of SVDs and HVDs is challenging due to factors such as Lewis acid-base interactions and the nature of the host oxide \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\]. Computational and experimental studies have shown that HVDs can increase the energy needed for removing surface oxygen atoms in irreducible oxides, hindering oxidation \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\]. In reducible oxides, HVDs can reduce cations, affecting the redox properties of the oxide \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\]. SVDs have been shown to favorably influence the properties of the host oxide in various catalytic applications \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\]. Challenges persist in formulating general predictive rules for dopant behavior due to the complexity of interactions between dopants and host oxides \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\]. The behavior of flexible-valence dopants (FVDs) is particularly difficult to predict, especially in reducible host oxides \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\]. More computational and experimental work is needed to clarify the behavior of these systems \[10.1021/cr300418s_8\].
