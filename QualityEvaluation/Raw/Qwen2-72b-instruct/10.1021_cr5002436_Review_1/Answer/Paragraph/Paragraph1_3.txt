                Comparative analysis of noble metal-based and metal oxide-based catalysts for nonoxidative dehydrogenation of light olefins reveals that Pt-based catalysts are favored for commercial use due to their superior activation of paraffinic CH bonds and low CC cleavage activity \[10.1021/cr5002436_1\]. The Pacol process, first commercialized in 1968, and the Oleflex process, developed by UOP, highlight Pt's industrial significance \[10.1021/cr5002436_1\]. Metal oxide-based catalysts, particularly CrOx, have also seen industrial use, with promising results from gallium, indium, vanadium, zinc, and molybdenum oxides \[10.1021/cr5002436_1\]. A novel Pt/Mg(Ga)(Al)O catalyst demonstrates high activity and selectivity for ethene and propene formation from ethane and propane, respectively, in nonoxidative dehydrogenation \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016\]. The PtGa alloys formed inhibit product alkene adsorption, suppressing undesired products like methane. Ga addition reduces coke formation, enhancing stability \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016\]. Recent developments focus on optimizing the Ga/Pt ratio for peak activity and selectivity \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016\]. Hydrogen addition to the feed boosts alkene formation by enhancing hydrogen-assisted dehydrogenation, but excessive hydrogen can lead to alkene hydrogenation, reducing dehydrogenation rates \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016\]. Interestingly, less coke is formed during propane dehydrogenation than ethane dehydrogenation, with no clear correlation between coke formation and catalyst deactivation \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016\].
