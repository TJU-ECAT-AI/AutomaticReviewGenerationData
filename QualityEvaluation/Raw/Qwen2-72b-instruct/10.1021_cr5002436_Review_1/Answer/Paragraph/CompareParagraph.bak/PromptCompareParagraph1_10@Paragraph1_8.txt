In this context, noble metal-based and metal oxide-based catalysts have emerged as two prominent classes of materials, each offering distinct advantages and challenges.

Noble metal-based catalysts, such as those containing platinum (Pt), palladium (Pd), and rhodium (Rh), are celebrated for their exceptional activity and selectivity in dehydrogenation reactions. These metals, due to their electronic properties and high stability under reaction conditions, can facilitate the activation and cleavage of C-H bonds with remarkable efficiency. For instance, Pt-based catalysts have been widely used in the dehydrogenation of ethane to ethylene, demonstrating high yields and selectivity under controlled conditions. Similarly, Pd and Rh catalysts have shown promise in the selective dehydrogenation of propane to propylene, a process critical for the petrochemical industry. However, the high cost and limited availability of noble metals pose significant economic and sustainability challenges, prompting the exploration of alternative catalysts.

In contrast, metal oxide-based catalysts, including those composed of iron (Fe), copper (Cu), and molybdenum (Mo) oxides, offer a more economically viable and environmentally benign option for dehydrogenation reactions. These catalysts, often supported on high-surface-area materials such as silica or alumina, can exhibit high activity and selectivity for dehydrogenation at lower temperatures than noble metal catalysts. For example, Fe-based catalysts have been successfully employed in the dehydrogenation of ethane to ethylene, while Cu and Mo oxides have shown effectiveness in the dehydrogenation of propane to propylene. The tunability of metal oxide catalysts through modification of their composition and structure has also facilitated the development of catalysts with enhanced performance. However, metal oxide catalysts often suffer from lower stability and higher deactivation rates compared to their noble metal counterparts, necessitating continuous research to improve their longevity and efficiency.

Industrial applications of these catalysts are diverse and critical to the global petrochemical industry. Noble metal catalysts, despite their high cost, are indispensable in high-value applications where product purity and yield are paramount. Conversely, metal oxide catalysts are increasingly favored in large-scale, cost-sensitive processes due to their lower material costs and comparable performance. The ongoing research in both areas aims to optimize catalyst design, enhance performance, and address the challenges of sustainability and economic viability, reflecting the dynamic and evolving nature of catalytic chemistry in the petrochemical sector.

In conclusion, while noble metal-based catalysts offer unparalleled activity and selectivity, metal oxide-based catalysts present a more sustainable and economically feasible alternative for the nonoxidative dehydrogenation of light olefins. The Pacol process (1968) and UOP's Oleflex process, which produces butadiene and propylene, exemplify its industrial application \[10.1021/cr5002436\_1\]. Metal oxide catalysts, notably CrOx, have industrial use and show promise, with gallium, indium, vanadium, zinc, and molybdenum oxides also demonstrating success \[10.1021/cr5002436\_1\]. A novel Pt/Mg(Ga)(Al)O catalyst demonstrates high activity and selectivity for ethene and propene formation from ethane and propane, respectively, in nonoxidative dehydrogenation \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016\]. The PtGa alloys within the catalyst enhance performance by diluting Pt ensembles and inhibiting product alkene adsorption, suppressing undesired products like methane. Ga addition also reduces coke formation, improving stability. Recent developments focus on optimizing the Ga/Pt ratio for peak activity and selectivity \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016\]. The catalyst's high activity, selectivity, and stability are advantageous for industrial light olefin production. Hydrogen addition to the feed boosts alkene formation due to enhanced hydrogen-assisted dehydrogenation, with selectivity remaining near 100% \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.016\]. Gallium-modified Pt/Al2O3 catalysts exhibit enhanced selectivity to propylene, reduced catalyst deactivation, and decreased carbon formation compared to pure Pt/Al2O3 \[10.1016/s0926-860x(99)00058-7\].
