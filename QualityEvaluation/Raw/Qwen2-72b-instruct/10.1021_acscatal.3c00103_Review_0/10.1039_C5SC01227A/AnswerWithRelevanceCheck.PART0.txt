      <English>
          In the context of increasing industrial demand for propylene and the limitations of traditional production methods, propane dehydrogenation (PDH) techniques address these challenges by providing an alternative process to produce propylene from propane. PDH is an endothermic reaction that converts propane to propylene and hydrogen. The development of innovative catalyst design strategies is crucial to overcome deactivation issues, enhance stability, and improve recyclability in both oxidative and nonoxidative PDH processes. These strategies include the use of single-atom catalysts, alloy catalysts, and supported metal catalysts with tailored active sites to promote selective dehydrogenation while minimizing coke formation and catalyst deactivation. Additionally, the development of robust catalysts that can withstand high temperatures and maintain activity over long periods is crucial for the commercial viability of PDH technologies. Strategies to overcome deactivation issues include the use of additional promoters, such as late transition metals, main-group metals including Sn and Ga, alkali-metal oxides and rare-earth metal oxides, which can increase the robustness against coking. Moreover, the design of catalysts with optimal metal-support interactions, controlled particle size and shape, and the use of alloying elements can improve the stability and recyclability of the catalysts.
