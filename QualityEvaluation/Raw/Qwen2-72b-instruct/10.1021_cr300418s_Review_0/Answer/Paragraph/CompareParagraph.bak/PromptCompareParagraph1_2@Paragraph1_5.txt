Doping modifies surface chemistry and disrupts chemical bonding, impacting catalytic activity by altering metal particle size, morphology, and interactions with oxide supports. Flat metal deposits, such as bilayer Au rafts on titania and Au/FeOx, exhibit enhanced activity in reactions like low-temperature CO oxidation and propanol oxidation over Pt on alumina [10.1021/ja211396]. The particle perimeter enables simultaneous interaction of adsorbates with the metal and oxide support, explaining the shape-dependence of metal particles in catalysis. Doping can control the equilibrium shape of metal ad-particles, as demonstrated by changes in Au particle geometry on doped CaO films. Mo impurities in CaO adopt a +2 charge state, transferring an electron to Au, resulting in 2D metal islands. Li doping, in contrast, creates holes in adjacent O ions, reducing electron availability for Au and forcing a 3D growth regime [10.1021/ja211396].

                Substitutional cation doping also transforms metal oxides into Lewis acids or bases, enhancing catalytic activity. La-doped CeO2 acts as a Lewis acid, strengthening halogen binding to oxygen sites, while Ta-doped ceria, a Lewis base, enhances halogen binding to cerium sites [10.1021/jp211693v]. These changes in surface chemistry can boost catalytic activity and alter active centers, potentially improving industrially relevant reactions.

                By replacing host cations with lower-valence dopants, an electron deficit is created, making the surface a stronger acid. This increases the oxide's electron-accepting ability, leading bases to bind more strongly to doped surfaces [10.1021/jp301341]. In reducible oxides, cations can be reduced in the presence of a base, creating competition for the electron donated by the base. In irreducible oxides, the base always fills the hole created by the dopant. These modifications can lead to enhanced catalytic activity and altered active centers, potentially improving industrial reactions.

The strategic incorporation of substitutional cations into metal oxide catalysts significantly modifies surface chemistry and disrupts chemical bonding, leading to enhanced catalytic performance \[10.1021/ja211396\]. Conversely, Li dopants create holes in adjacent O ions, reducing electron transfer to Au and forcing a 3D growth regime \[10.1021/ja211396\]. These modifications in surface chemistry and chemical bonding can lead to the formation of active centers near the dopant or involving the dopant itself, potentially improving industrially relevant reactions \[10.1021/cr300418s\_0\].

                Substitutional cation doping also transforms metal oxides into Lewis acids or bases, enhancing catalytic activity. La-doped CeO2 acts as a Lewis acid, strengthening halogen binding to oxygen sites, while Ta-doped ceria becomes a Lewis base, intensifying halogen binding to cerium sites \[10.1021/jp211693v\]. These changes in surface chemistry improve catalytic activity and redefine active centers, potentially boosting industrially relevant reactions \[10.1021/jp211693v\].

                By replacing a host oxide's cation with a lower-valence dopant, an electron deficit is created, turning the surface into a Lewis acid. This substitution increases the oxide's electron acceptance ability, making it a stronger acid \[10.1021/jp301341\]. Consequently, bases bind more strongly to doped surfaces than to undoped ones, affecting catalytic performance and active center characteristics \[10.1021/jp301341\].

                These findings underscore the potential of substitutional cation doping to enhance the catalytic performance of metal oxide catalysts through the modification of surface chemistry and the disruption of chemical bonding, leading to the formation of active centers near the dopant or involving the dopant itself \[10.1021/cr300418s\_0\].
