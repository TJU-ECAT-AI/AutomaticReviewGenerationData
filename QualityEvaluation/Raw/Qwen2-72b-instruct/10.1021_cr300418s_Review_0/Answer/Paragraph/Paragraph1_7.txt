                The strategic introduction of substitutional cations into metal oxide catalysts significantly modifies surface chemistry and disrupts chemical bonding, leading to enhanced catalytic performance \[10.1021/ja211396\]. This is achieved by altering metal particle size and morphology, which are critical for catalytic activity due to their interactions with oxide supports. Flat metal deposits, such as bilayer Au rafts on titania, Au/FeOx catalysts, and Pt on alumina, have demonstrated superior activity in reactions including low-temperature CO oxidation and propanol oxidation \[10.1021/ja211396\]. The particle perimeter facilitates simultaneous adsorbate interaction with both the metal and the oxide support, elucidating the shape-dependence of catalytic performance. Shaping metal deposits on oxides is challenging on conventional bulk oxides, but this study demonstrates that doping with Mo or Li can control the equilibrium shape of Au ad-particles on CaO films \[10.1021/ja211396\]. Mo impurities in CaO adopt a +2 charge state, transferring an electron to Au, stabilizing 2D metal islands. Li, acting as a charge acceptor, creates holes in adjacent O ions, reducing electron transfer to Au and forcing 3D growth \[10.1021/ja211396\]. This study highlights the potential of doping to tailor metal ad-particle shapes and reveals the internal charge compensation effect of codoping.

                Substitutional cation doping modifies metal oxide catalysts' surface chemistry, turning them into Lewis acids or bases, which enhances halogen dissociation \[10.1021/jp211693v\]. La-doped CeO2 acts as a Lewis acid, strengthening halogen binding to oxygen sites, while Ta-doped ceria, as a Lewis base, intensifies halogen attachment to cerium sites. These changes in chemical bonding can significantly boost catalytic activity and alter active centers, potentially improving industrial reactions \[10.1021/jp211693v\].

                Substitutional cation doping in metal oxides transforms surfaces into Lewis acids by creating an electron deficit when lower-valence cations replace host cations \[10.1021/jp301341\]. This substitution enhances the oxide's electron acceptance ability, making it a stronger acid. Consequently, bases bind more strongly to doped surfaces than to undoped ones. This effect is consistent across reducible and irreducible oxides. In reducible oxides, cations can be reduced in the presence of a base, introducing complexity to the charge exchange mechanism. In irreducible oxides, the base always fills the hole created by the low-valence dopant. These modifications in surface chemistry can lead to enhanced catalytic activity and altered active centers, potentially improving industrial reactions \[10.1021/jp301341\].

                Substitutional cation doping in metal oxide catalysts modifies surface chemistry by disrupting chemical bonding, aiming to enhance catalytic activity. Active centers may form near the dopant or involve the dopant itself, potentially improving industrially relevant reactions \[10.1021/cr300418s_0\].
                ※※※
                Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The advancements in substitutional cation doping for enhancing catalytic performance in metal oxide catalysts open up new avenues for the optimization of industrially relevant reactions. The control of metal ad-particle shapes through oxide support doping and the impact of codoping on charge compensation present a fertile ground for further exploration. Future research might focus on the development of novel doping strategies that can precisely tailor the electronic and geometric properties of metal ad-particles, leading to the creation of more efficient active centers. Additionally, the investigation of the interplay between dopant type, concentration, and the resulting changes in surface chemistry could provide deeper insights into the mechanisms underlying catalytic activity enhancement. The potential for these findings to be translated into practical applications, such as the development of more efficient propane dehydrogenation catalysts, is substantial and warrants further investigation.

                通过在金属氧化物催化剂中引入替代性阳离子，显著改变了表面化学并破坏了化学键，从而提高了催化性能\[10.1021/ja211396\]。通过改变金属颗粒的大小和形态，这是催化活性的关键，因为它们与氧化物载体的相互作用。平面金属沉积物，如钛酸盐上的双层Au筏，Au/FeOx催化剂，以及氧化铝上的Pt，已在包括低温CO氧化和丙醇氧化在内的反应中显示出优越的活性\[10.1021/ja211396\]。颗粒周长促进了吸附物与金属和氧化物载体的同时相互作用，阐明了催化性能的形状依赖性。在传统的块状氧化物上塑造金属沉积物具有挑战性，但本研究表明，通过Mo或Li的掺杂可以控制CaO薄膜上Au吸附颗粒的平衡形状\[10.1021/ja211396\]。CaO中的Mo杂质采用+2电荷状态，向Au转移一个电子，稳定2D金属岛。Li，作为电荷接受者，在相邻的O离子中产生空穴，减少向Au的电子转移，迫使3D生长\[10.1021/ja211396\]。这项研究突出了掺杂在塑造金属吸附颗粒形状方面的潜力，并揭示了共掺杂的内部电荷补偿效应。

                替代性阳离子掺杂改变了金属氧化物催化剂的表面化学，将其转化为路易斯酸或碱，增强了卤素的解离\[10.1021/jp211693v\]。La掺杂的CeO2作为路易斯酸，加强了卤素与氧位点的结合，而Ta掺杂的铈作为路易斯碱，增强了卤素与铈位点的结合。这些化学键的变化可以显著提高催化活性并改变活性中心，可能改善工业反应\[10.1021/jp211693v\]。

                通过用低价阳离子取代宿主阳离子，金属氧化物中的替代性阳离子掺杂将表面转化为路易斯酸\[10.1021/jp301341\]。这种替代增强了氧化物的电子接受能力，使其成为更强的酸。因此，碱在掺杂表面上的结合比在未掺杂的表面上更强。这种效应在可还原和不可还原的氧化物中是一致的。在可还原的氧化物中，阳离子在碱的存在下可以被还原，引入了电荷交换机制的复杂性。在不可还原的氧化物中，碱总是填充由低价掺杂剂产生的空穴。这些表面化学的改变可以导致催化活性的提高和活性中心的改变，可能改善工业反应\[10.1021/jp301341\]。

                通过破坏化学键，旨在提高催化活性，替代性阳离子掺杂在金属氧化物催化剂中改变了表面化学。活性中心可能在掺杂剂附近形成，或涉及掺杂剂本身，可能改善工业相关的反应\[10.1021/cr300418s_0\]。
                ※※※
                综合理解与未来展望: 在金属氧化物催化剂中增强催化性能的替代性阳离子掺杂的进展为优化工业相关反应开辟了新的途径。通过氧化物载体掺杂控制金属吸附颗粒形状以及共掺杂对电荷补偿的影响，为更深入的探索提供了肥沃的土壤。未来的研究可能集中在开发新的掺杂策略，可以精确调整金属吸附颗粒的电子和几何性质，导致更有效的活性中心的创建。此外，掺杂剂类型、浓度与表面化学变化之间的相互作用的调查可能为催化活性增强的潜在机制提供更深入的见解。这些发现转化为实际应用的潜力，如开发更有效的丙烷脱氢催化剂，是巨大的，值得进一步研究。
