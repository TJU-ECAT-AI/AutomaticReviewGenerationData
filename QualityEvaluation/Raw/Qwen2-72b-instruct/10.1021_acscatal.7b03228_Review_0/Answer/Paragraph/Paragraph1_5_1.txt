The interplay between geometric and electronic effects in transition-metal alloys significantly enhances catalytic performance, selectivity, and reactivity compared to monometallic catalysts \[10.1016/j.jcat.2014.07.016\]. Geometric effects, involving bonding configurations, and electronic effects, pertaining to adsorbate-metal surface interactions, synergistically influence catalytic activity \[10.1021/acscatal.7b03228_0\]. Perturbation of the d-band, through charge transfer and hybridization, affects adsorption behavior, stabilizing d-orbitals and impacting d-orbital density \[10.1007/s11244-006-0005-x\]. Limitations in catalysis improvement due to electronic effects can be overcome by perturbing the adsorbate's bonding configuration \[10.1021/acscatal.7b03228_0\]. Innovative strategies, such as intermetallic compounds, single-atom alloys, and coadsorbed structural modifiers, offer enhanced control over catalytic activity and selectivity \[10.1021/acscatal.7b03228_0\].

In PtSn-based catalysts, the addition of Sn modifies the platinum active phase and support, suppressing hydrogenolysis and isomerization, minimizing metal sintering, neutralizing acidity, and facilitating coke species diffusion \[10.1021/cr5002436\]. The formation of smaller platinum ensembles and the electronic modification of Pt by Sn inhibit coke formation, leading to an optimum Pt3Sn ratio for high activity and selectivity \[10.1021/cr5002436\]. Strategies to exploit this interplay include high-throughput screening for optimal alloy compositions, theoretical calculations to predict alloying effects, and precise synthesis methods for catalyst control \[10.1021/cr5002436\].

Alloying Sn with Pt(111) opens a new hydrogenation pathway, selectively producing butene from coadsorbed H and 1,3-butadiene, demonstrating how electronic and geometric properties can alter catalytic reactivity and selectivity \[10.1016/j.jcat.2004.12.006\]. The (2 × 2) alloy, with nearly matching desorption activation energies for H2 and 1,3-butadiene, achieves high reactant coverages at high temperatures, suggesting practical catalysts could be optimized by controlling Sn concentration \[10.1016/j.jcat.2004.12.006\]. Less preadsorbed hydrogen is needed on the alloys to block 1,3-butadiene chemisorption compared to Pt(111), indicating the alloyed surface's influence on adsorption and reaction processes \[10.1016/j.jcat.2004.12.006\].

A scheme for estimating oxygen binding energies on transition metal surface alloys uses the surface d-band center as a parameter, incorporating chemical composition, strain, and ligand effects \[10.1016/j.susc.2005.07.018\]. This parameter's agreement with DFT calculations on binary alloys validates its descriptive power for alloy systems, supporting its use in screening alloys for enhanced catalytic properties \[10.1016/j.susc.2005.07.018\].

Geometric and electronic effects in transition-metal alloys significantly impact catalytic performance by altering the local electronic structure and chemical reactivity \[10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1\]. The hybridization of d-orbitals does not lead to substantial charge transfer but influences the width of the local density of d-states, a key descriptor of chemical reactivity \[10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1\]. This allows for predicting the reactivity of alloy sites based on their geometric structure and electronic properties \[10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1\]. Understanding these relationships enables the development of innovative strategies to outperform traditional monometallic catalysts \[10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1\].

Geometric and electronic effects in transition-metal alloys enhance catalytic performance. Geometrically, the ensemble effect increases selectivity by reducing surface coordination and ensemble size, favoring allowed reactions \[10.1021/acscatal.6b02603\]. Electronically, the active metal's state is modified, improving catalytic properties, with the choice of the second metal being crucial \[10.1021/acscatal.6b02603\]. Intermetallic phases offer anchoring sites, modify adsorption properties, and enable molecular recognition for selective (de)hydrogenation, while suppressing side reactions, improving stability, providing O2 adsorption sites, and acting as precursors for oxidation and steam reforming \[10.1021/acscatal.6b02603\]. To fully understand intermetallic catalysts, high phase purity, inert supports, and simple reaction conditions are recommended \[10.1021/acscatal.6b02603\].

过渡金属合金中几何和电子效应的相互作用显著提高了催化性能、选择性和反应性，与单金属催化剂相比具有优势\[10.1016/j.jcat.2014.07.016\]。几何效应涉及键合配置，而电子效应涉及吸附物与金属表面的相互作用，二者协同影响催化活性\[10.1021/acscatal.7b03228_0\]。通过电荷转移和杂化扰动d带，影响吸附行为，稳定d轨道，影响d轨道密度\[10.1007/s11244-006-0005-x\]。由于电子效应限制催化改进的局限性可以通过扰动吸附物的键合配置来克服\[10.1021/acscatal.7b03228_0\]。创新策略，如金属间化合物、单原子合金和共吸附结构修饰剂，提供了增强催化活性和选择性的控制\[10.1021/acscatal.7b03228_0\]。

在PtSn基催化剂中，Sn的添加通过修改铂活性相和支持物，抑制氢解和异构化，最小化金属烧结，中和酸性，促进焦炭物种的扩散，从而协同影响催化性能\[10.1021/cr5002436\]。形成较小的铂集合体和Pt的电子改性抑制焦炭形成，导致Pt3Sn的最佳比例，以实现高活性和选择性\[10.1021/cr5002436\]。利用高通量筛选寻找最佳合金组成、理论计算预测合金效应以及精确合成方法控制催化剂的策略可以利用这种相互作用\[10.1021/cr5002436\]。

Sn与Pt(111)的合金化开辟了一条新的氢化途径，从共吸附的H和1,3-丁二烯中选择性地产生丁烯，展示了电子和几何性质如何改变催化反应性和选择性\[10.1016/j.jcat.2004.12.006\]。(2×2)合金，H2和1,3-丁二烯的脱附活化能几乎匹配，在高温下实现高反应物覆盖率，表明通过控制Sn浓度可以优化实用催化剂\[10.1016/j.jcat.2004.12.006\]。与Pt(111)相比，合金上需要较少的预吸附氢来阻止1,3-丁二烯的化学吸附，表明合金化表面在吸附和反应过程中的影响\[10.1016/j.jcat.2004.12.006\]。

用于估计过渡金属表面合金上氧结合能的方案使用表面d带中心作为参数，结合化学组成、应变和配体效应\[10.1016/j.susc.2005.07.018\]。该参数与二元合金的DFT计算结果一致，验证了其对合金系统的描述能力，支持其在筛选具有增强催化性能的合金中的应用\[10.1016/j.susc.2005.07.018\]。

过渡金属合金中几何和电子效应通过改变局部电子结构和化学反应性，对催化性能产生重大影响\[10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1\]。d轨道的杂化不会导致大量电荷转移，但会影响局部d态密度的宽度，这是化学反应性的一个关键描述符\[10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1\]。这使得可以根据其几何结构和电子性质预测合金位点的反应性\[10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1\]。理解这些关系使我们能够开发创新策略，以超越传统的单金属催化剂\[10.1007/s11244-010-9448-1\]。

过渡金属合金的几何和电子效应提高了催化性能。几何上，集合效应通过减少表面配位和集合体大小来增加选择性，有利于允许的反应\[10.1021/acscatal.6b02603\]。电子上，通过选择第二金属来修改活性金属的状态，改善催化性质，这是至关重要的\[10.1021/acscatal.6b02603\]。金属间相提供锚定位点，修改吸附性质，使选择性(去)氢化中的分子识别成为可能，同时抑制副反应，提高稳定性，提供O2吸附位点，并作为氧化和蒸汽重整的活性成分的前体\[10.1021/acscatal.6b02603\]。为了充分理解金属间催化剂，建议使用高相纯度、惰性载体和简单的反应条件\[10.1021/acscatal.6b02603\]。
