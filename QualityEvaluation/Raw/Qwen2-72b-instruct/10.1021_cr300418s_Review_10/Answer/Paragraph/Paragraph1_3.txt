                    Niobium oxides, with their diverse properties and oxidation states, significantly influence NiO catalysts for ethane oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) by reducing oxygen over-stoichiometry and electrophilic Ospecies concentration \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\]. At low Nb content (3-7%), the activation energy and intrinsic rate of ethane conversion decrease, enhancing catalytic sites' activity but reducing their number, leading to increased ethylene selectivity \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\]. Beyond 15% Nb, the inactive NiNb2O6 phase forms, decreasing active phase content \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\]. Optimal yields are observed at 15% Nb due to reduced active phase particle size, preventing sintering \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\]. Long-term stability studies show deactivation attributed to NiO oxygen over-stoichiometry reduction and NiNb2O6 phase formation \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\]. The surface interaction between NiO and amorphous Nb2O5, influenced by the niobium precursor, is crucial for efficiency \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\]. Theoretical predictions and experimental observations must reconcile the complex catalytic behavior \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\].

The development of such models and techniques would not only advance our understanding of ethane ODH but also pave the way for the design of more efficient and stable catalysts for other oxidative dehydrogenation reactions.
