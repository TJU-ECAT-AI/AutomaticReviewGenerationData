                    The diverse properties and oxidation states of niobium oxides significantly influence their behavior as dopants in NiO catalysts for ethane oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH), as elucidated in reference \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\]. At low Nb content (3-7%), the apparent activation energy and intrinsic rate of ethane conversion decrease, enhancing catalytic sites' activity and ethylene selectivity. However, beyond 15% Nb, the inactive NiNb2O6 phase forms, reducing active phase content. The optimal Ni0.85Nb0.15O compound maximizes yields due to reduced active phase particle size. Long-term studies reveal a continuous decrease in activity, attributed to the formation of NiNb2O6 and reduced oxygen over-stoichiometry, leading to a lack of Oactive species. Catalyst regeneration attempts have been unsuccessful \[10.1016/j.apcata.2010.10.004\]. Synthesized comprehension stemming from references \[10.1016/j.jcat.2011.10.005\] and \[10.1016/j.jcat.2005.01.015\] further elucidates that niobium oxides affect NiO catalysts for ethane ODH by altering redox properties, reducing NiO crystallite size, and inhibiting NiO reducibility, leading to less active but more selective catalysts. The Nb-rich phase covering unselective, non-stoichiometric active oxygen species of NiO is confirmed by high-resolution transmission electron microscopy. At optimal Nb content (3-4 at.%), catalysts exhibit high activity, selectivity, and stability. However, high Nb loadings cause significant deactivation due to the close interaction between NiO and the Nb-rich mixed oxide layer. The preparation method's impact on NbNiO nanocomposites suggests potential for further improvement in ethane ODH \[10.1016/j.jcat.2011.10.005\]. The TPR profile of NiNb catalysts reveals two distinct reduction peaks at 335 and 510 C, indicating niobium's influence on the materials' reduction characteristics \[10.1016/j.jcat.2005.01.015\].

Moreover, the exploration of regeneration strategies for spent catalysts could address the challenge of continuous deactivation over time, potentially leading to more sustainable and economically viable catalytic processes for ethane ODH.
