                    Comprehensive evaluation of metal oxide catalysts for CO2-ODHP: From initial screening to in-depth analysis of chromium-based catalysts, focusing on performance factors, species evolution, and structure-activity relationships. The study of reference \[10.1002/cctc.201200397\] established that CrOx/silica catalysts are top performers in CO2-ODHP, with Cr3+ being a key species. Nickel addition enhances the regeneration of Cr3+ from Cr2+, boosting propylene selectivity. Chromium species evolve during the reaction, with nickel playing a critical role in maintaining Cr3+ retention, which is paramount for stability and selectivity. \[10.1016/j.apcata.2009.11.040\] highlighted that Cr and Ga oxides excel in CO2-ODHP, with CO2 boosting propene yield. Redox Cr species (Cr6+, Cr5+, Cr3+) are key, influenced by loading, preparation, precursor, and support. On SiO2, low loadings favor Cr6+, while high loadings introduce Cr3+ and Cr2O3. \[10.1016/j.tca.2008.02.017\] demonstrated that Cr2O3 catalysts optimized at 5 wt.% Cr maximize CO2-ODHP activity and selectivity, avoiding the formation of Cr2O3 crystals that block pores. Active carbon support enriched with oxygen species stabilizes propane dehydrogenation, with CO2 stabilizing these oxygen species. Chromium species evolve, influenced by loading and support, impacting performance.
                    ※※※
                    Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The comprehensive evaluation of metal oxide catalysts for CO2-ODHP has revealed the pivotal role of chromium species, particularly Cr3+, in catalytic performance. The evolution of Cr species, influenced by loading, preparation, precursor, and support, has been identified as a critical factor in determining the activity and selectivity of the catalysts. The synergistic effect of nickel in enhancing the regeneration of Cr3+ from Cr2+ has been highlighted as a strategy to boost propylene selectivity. The future research direction could focus on the development of novel chromium-based catalysts with optimized Cr species evolution, aiming to achieve higher activity and selectivity in CO2-ODHP. The exploration of advanced preparation methods and supports that can stabilize Cr3+ and prevent the formation of inactive Cr2O3 could be a promising avenue. Additionally, the investigation of the role of CO2 in stabilizing oxygen species on the catalyst surface and its impact on the evolution of Cr species could provide insights into the design of more efficient catalysts for CO2-ODHP.
