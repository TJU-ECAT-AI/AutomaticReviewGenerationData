    Metal-based catalysts, particularly vanadium and molybdenum oxides, play a pivotal role in alkane oxidative dehydrogenation processes, with activity peaking at intermediate VOx domain sizes and selectivity at monolayer structures \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. Surface reduction significantly boosts propylene selectivity, and supports and additives modify both activity and selectivity \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. MoOx catalysts are less active than vanadia-based catalysts, with activity per Mo atom peaking at specific densities \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. Alkali dopants inhibit reduction, enhancing propylene selectivity \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. The efficiency of these catalysts in alkane transformations, especially oxidative dehydrogenation and zeolite-catalyzed reactions, is significantly influenced by pretreatment, temperature, additives, metal state, and feed composition \[10.1016/s0920-5861(00)00456-9\]. In propane oxidative dehydrogenation, vanadia's structure evolves from monovanadates to polyvanadates and V2O5 crystallites with VOx density, impacting propene selectivities and reaction rates \[10.1006/jcat.1998.2295\]. Turnover rates are independent of support type, with similar rates on polyvanadates and surface VOx sites \[10.1006/jcat.1998.2295\]. Efficiency balances activity and reactant accessibility \[10.1006/jcat.1998.2295\].
