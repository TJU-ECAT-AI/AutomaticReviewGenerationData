            <English> 
                    The evolving significance of propene in the chemical industry, driven by its demand outpacing that of ethylene, particularly in Western Europe \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\], has led to the adoption of propane dehydrogenation (PDH) as a preferred production method. This shift is essential to meet the forecasted demand, as current olefin production methods do not suffice \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. PDH technologies combine catalyst performance with innovative designs, offering improved energy efficiency and process simplicity \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. However, achieving better olefin yields than current technologies is essential to meet diverse applications \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. Higher exothermic reactions in PDH imply increased energy losses and fixed costs, especially considering CO2 production as a by-product \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\].
                    ※※※
                    Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The advancements in PDH technologies, driven by the escalating demand for propene, have opened new avenues for research and development in catalysis. The focus on developing catalysts that can balance lattice oxygen supply to minimize carbon oxide formation and maximize propene selectivity is critical \[10.1039/C6CY00078A\]. Future research endeavors might concentrate on the design of innovative catalysts and reactor systems that can further enhance the efficiency and sustainability of PDH processes. The exploration of alternative materials for better oxidation control in PDH reactions could also be a promising direction \[10.1126/science.aaf7885\]. As the chemical industry continues to expand, the development of more efficient and environmentally friendly PDH technologies will be crucial to meet the growing needs for propene in various applications.
