                The utilization of sulfur as an alternative oxidant in methane oxidative coupling, explored by Zhu et al. \[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13\], presents a less active option compared to traditional oxygen-based systems. The thermodynamic well for sulfur oxidation to CS2 is notably higher in energy at 3.45 eV, contrasting with 13.41 eV for O2 oxidation of methane to CO2. This suggests a reduced thermodynamic driving force for overoxidation when employing sulfur. An ethylene selectivity of up to 18% can be attained on a PdS catalyst at 1050 C. However, the process necessitates high temperatures due to the thermodynamics of the reaction and the relatively high CH activation barriers on sulfide catalysts, which are inferior hydrogen abstractors compared to metal oxides. These barriers span from 1.91 eV on MoS2 to 2.68 eV for TiS2. The current catalyst performance is relatively modest and distant from practical application, yet future studies can potentially identify catalysts with enhanced conversion rates through descriptor-based theoretical screening. The MS bond energy is noted to be a valuable descriptor for both CH activation and CC coupling reaction barriers.
                ※※※
                Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The exploration of sulfur as an alternative oxidant in methane oxidative coupling, while currently presenting modest performance, opens avenues for innovative catalyst design. Future research might concentrate on refining the MS bond energy as a descriptor for catalytic activity, aiming to develop more efficient and selective catalysts. The identification of novel sulfide catalysts with improved hydrogen abstraction capabilities could significantly advance the practicality of sulfur-based oxidative coupling. Moreover, the development of theoretical screening methodologies could expedite the discovery of catalysts with higher conversion rates, potentially leading to more sustainable and economically viable processes for alkane conversion.
