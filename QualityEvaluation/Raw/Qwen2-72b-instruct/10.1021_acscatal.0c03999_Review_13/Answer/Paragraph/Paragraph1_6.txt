                The utilization of sulfur as an alternative oxidant in methane oxidative coupling, explored by Zhu et al. \[10.1021/acscatal.0c03999_13\], presents a less active oxidant compared to traditional oxygen-based systems. The thermodynamic well for sulfur oxidation to CS2 is notably higher in energy at 3.45 eV, contrasting with 13.41 eV for O2 oxidation of methane to CO2. This discrepancy indicates a lower thermodynamic driving force for overoxidation when employing sulfur. An ethylene selectivity of up to 18% can be achieved on a PdS catalyst at 1050 C. However, the process necessitates high temperatures due to the thermodynamics of the reaction and the relatively high CH activation barriers on sulfide catalysts, which are inferior hydrogen abstractors compared to metal oxides. These barriers span from 1.91 eV on MoS2 to 2.68 eV for TiS2. The current catalyst performance is relatively modest and far from practical consideration, yet future studies can identify catalysts with higher conversion rates through descriptor-based theoretical screening. The MS bond energy is observed to be a good descriptor for both CH activation and CC coupling reaction barriers.

                Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: Considering the advancements and findings discussed in the file, there lies an opportunity to explore emerging fields and innovative methodologies. Future research endeavors might focus on refining the MS bond energy as a descriptor for catalytic activity, potentially leading to the development of more efficient and selective catalysts for alkane conversion. The exploration of sulfur as an oxidant opens new avenues for the design of catalysts that can mitigate overoxidation, a critical challenge in traditional oxygen-based systems. Descriptor-based theoretical screening could be a pivotal tool in identifying catalysts with enhanced performance and practical applicability. Moreover, the investigation of alternative oxidants and their thermodynamic implications could pave the way for more sustainable and energy-efficient processes in the chemical industry.
