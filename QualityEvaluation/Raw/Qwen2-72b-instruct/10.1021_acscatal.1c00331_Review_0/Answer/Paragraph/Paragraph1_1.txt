                    The catalytic direct dehydrogenation of propane (PDH) for propene production is a critical process in the chemical industry, characterized by its endothermic nature and the challenges of cracking and coke formation \[10.1016/j.catcom.2007.08.015\]. Pt-based catalysts, despite their high activity, are prone to deactivation due to coke formation, which significantly impacts selectivity and catalyst life \[10.1039/d0cp04669h\]. Particle size is a crucial factor in coke formation; smaller Pt particles (3 nm) exhibit higher activity but lower selectivity and higher coking rates compared to larger particles (21 nm) \[10.1016/j.catcom.2007.08.015\]. Research efforts are directed towards enhancing dehydrogenation rates while minimizing cracking and prolonging catalyst life \[10.1016/j.catcom.2007.08.015\].

                    The impact of coke formation on Pt-based catalysts is a critical challenge in PDH processes \[10.1039/d0cp04669h\]. Strategies to mitigate coke formation include modifying the catalyst surface to improve selectivity, weakening propylene adsorption, and controlling reaction conditions \[10.1039/d0cs01262a\]. Alloying, the use of specific supports, and the addition of dopants such as Sn and CeO2 have been shown to enhance stability, reduce coke formation, and improve efficiency \[10.1039/d0cs01262a\]. ZrO2 has emerged as an alternative to Pt-based catalysts, offering high efficiency, durability, and reduced coke formation \[10.1038/s41467-018-06174-5\].
