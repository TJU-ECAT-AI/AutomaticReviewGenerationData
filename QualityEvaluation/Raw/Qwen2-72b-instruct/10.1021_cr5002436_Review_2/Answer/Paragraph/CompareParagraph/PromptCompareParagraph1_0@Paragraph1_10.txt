Raman spectroscopy is a powerful tool for studying the redox behavior of supported chromium oxides, which is strongly dependent on the specific support ligand, impacting the catalyst's performance in redox reactions \[10.1021/jp960543\].

                    Since 1933, research on chromium oxide-based catalysts for dehydrogenation processes has focused on active sites, dehydration temperature effects, chromium oxidation states, and support materials \[10.1016/0021-9517(85)90297-0\]. Coordinatively unsaturated Cr(II) ions, with low nuclearity at low loadings, are identified as active sites for ethane dehydrogenation, highlighting their significance in catalytic activity \[10.1016/0021-9517(85)90297-0\].

                    Since 1933, chromium oxide-based catalysts have evolved significantly in dehydrogenation processes, with a focus on CrOx/Al2O3 catalysts \[10.1016/s0926-860x(99)00310-5\]. Key research areas include ALE and impregnation techniques for preparation, analysis of Cr6+ and Cr3+ oxidation states, CrOx dispersion on Al2O3, and the role of redox Cr3+ sites in activity \[10.1016/s0926-860x(99)00310-5\]. Initially, these catalysts were recognized for their ability to facilitate the conversion of hydrocarbons into more valuable products by removing hydrogen atoms. Over the decades, their industrial significance has grown exponentially, driven by advancements in their formulation and understanding of their catalytic properties. Research on chromium oxide catalysts has been multifaceted, encompassing the development of new synthesis methods, the exploration of their structure-activity relationships, and the optimization of their performance under various conditions. This has led to the creation of more efficient and selective catalysts, which are crucial for the production of chemicals such as ethylene and propylene, key components in the manufacturing of plastics and other materials.
