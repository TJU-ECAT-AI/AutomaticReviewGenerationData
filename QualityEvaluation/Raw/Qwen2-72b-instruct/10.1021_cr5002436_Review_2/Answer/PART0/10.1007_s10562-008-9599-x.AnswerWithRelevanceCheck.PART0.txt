          The literature does not provide a historical overview of chromium oxide-based catalysts since their discovery in 1933. However, it highlights the significance of chromia-based catalysts in various industrial processes such as catalytic oxidation of organic compounds, polymerization of ethylene into polyethylene, chlorinated carbon removal, and non-oxidative dehydrogenation of propane to produce propylene. The focus areas of research include the structure of supported chromium oxides, the nature of active sites, and the mechanisms of catalytic reactions. The oxidation state of active chromium species remains a topic of debate, with studies suggesting the importance of Cr2+ or Cr3+ sites or both. The preparation methods for chromia-based catalysts, including impregnation, coprecipitation, and atomic layer epitaxy, are also discussed, with an emphasis on achieving high dispersion of chromia species on the support. Sol-gel preparation is a versatile synthetic route that can lead to large surface area materials with metal ions in a highly dispersed state. The use of mesoporous molecular sieves combined with the formation of chromia-doped gel inside their pores may give rise to catalysts with unusual properties, as the porous structure can be independently manipulated, whereas the sol-gel step would favor high chromia dispersion.
