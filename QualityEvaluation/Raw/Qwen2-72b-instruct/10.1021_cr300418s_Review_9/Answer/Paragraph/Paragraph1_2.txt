                    The preparation, properties, and catalytic applications of doped nickel oxide materials in oxidative dehydrogenation reactions, particularly propane dehydrogenation, are influenced by dopant concentrations and vacancy formation, as established from the study of reference \[10.1016/j.jcat.2005.11.002\]. Niobium-doped nickel oxide catalysts exhibit enhanced catalytic performance in oxidative dehydrogenation reactions, such as ethane to ethylene conversion, due to altered electronic structures. The incorporation of niobium through the evaporation method, varying the Nb/Ni atomic ratio, affects the electronic structure and catalytic activity. Niobium incorporation leads to a contraction of the NiO lattice, impacting the lattice constant and inhibiting crystallization, resulting in smaller grain sizes. At high Nb loading, the segregation of NiO and Nb2O5 phases occurs, leading to larger crystals. The reducibility of the catalysts broadens upon niobium introduction, indicating structural modifications. Niobium increases total acidity, generating new acid sites, which significantly boosts catalytic performance. Surface composition analysis by XPS reveals electronic structure changes. Electrical conductivity measurements suggest Nb5+ incorporation has an n-doping character, impacting electronic properties. Niobium reduces p+ concentration, affecting excess oxygen accommodation. NiNbO catalysts show high activity and ethene selectivity in ethane oxidative dehydrogenation, with Nb acting as an electron donor, reducing p+ hole concentration. The elimination of nonstoichiometric oxygen by Nb doping correlates with high ethene selectivity.
