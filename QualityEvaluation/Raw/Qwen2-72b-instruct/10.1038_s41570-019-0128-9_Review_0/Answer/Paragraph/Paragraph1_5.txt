                    The performance, stability, and mechanistic behavior of chromium oxide catalysts in CO2 oxidative dehydrogenation (CO2-ODH) reactions are significantly influenced by support materials and reaction conditions \[10.2478/v10026-012-0106-1\]. Chromium oxide supported on SiO2 and Al2O3 exhibits good performance in propane dehydrogenation, with CO2 having a promoting effect on Cr-O/SiO2 and a negative effect on Cr-O/Al2O3 for propene yield \[10.2478/v10026-012-0106-1\]. CrOx/γ-Al2O3 and CrOx/ZrO2 show high activity, with CrOx/γ-Al2O3 favoring direct dehydrogenation of ethane and CrOx/ZrO2 promoting selective oxidative dehydrogenation \[10.1016/j.cattod.2018.04.047\]. CrOx/ZrO2 faces rapid deactivation at high temperatures due to sintering or inactive chromium species formation \[10.1016/j.cattod.2018.04.047\]. CrOx/CeO2, while less active, is the most stable due to the formation of α-Cr2O3, which is inactive in direct dehydrogenation \[10.1016/j.cattod.2018.04.047\]. Its stability is attributed to the removal of carbon deposits by CO2 due to the support's basic sites \[10.1016/j.cattod.2018.04.047\]. The side reactions for CrOx/CeO2 and CrOx/CexZr(1-x)O2 are similar to CrOx/ZrO2, involving non-selective oxidation or dry-reforming reactions \[10.1016/j.cattod.2018.04.047\]. CO2 partial pressure boosts CrOx/SiO2's activity and C3H6 selectivity, whereas CrOx/Al2O3's activity declines \[10.1038/s41570-019-0128-9_0\]. CrVI centers in MCM-41 activate CO2, and an 8wt% Cr content maximizes CrVI:CrIII ratios and alkane conversion \[10.1038/s41570-019-0128-9_0\]. Excessive Cr content leads to agglomeration and inactive polychromate species \[10.1038/s41570-019-0128-9_0\]. CrVI=O species dehydrogenate alkanes faster than CrIII reoxidizes, causing deactivation and coking \[10.1038/s41570-019-0128-9_0\].

                    Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The advancements and findings discussed in the file highlight the pivotal role of support materials and reaction conditions in optimizing the performance of chromium oxide catalysts in CO2-ODH reactions. Future research endeavors might focus on developing novel support materials that can enhance the stability and activity of chromium oxide catalysts while minimizing side reactions. Exploring the synergistic effects of mixed supports, such as CeO2-ZrO2, could offer new avenues for improving catalyst stability and selectivity. Additionally, the precise control of Cr content and the optimization of CO2 partial pressure could lead to the development of more efficient and stable catalysts for CO2-ODH reactions. The investigation of the redox cycle and the role of CrVI=O species in dehydrogenation could provide insights into the design of catalysts with enhanced reoxidation rates and reduced coking. Moreover, the exploration of the impact of steam on the acidity/basicity of CrOx/Al2O3 could open new perspectives on the modulation of catalyst properties for improved performance. The development of catalysts with tailored surface properties and optimized metal-support interactions could pave the way for the next generation of CO2-ODH catalysts.
