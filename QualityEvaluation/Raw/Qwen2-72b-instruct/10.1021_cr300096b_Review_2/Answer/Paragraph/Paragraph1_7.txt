<English>
The comprehensive characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces, synthesized by depositing a Ni monolayer on Pt(111) or polycrystalline Pt substrates, is pivotal for structural modeling, DFT calculations, and understanding their electronic and chemical properties in advanced surface science and catalysis research \[10.1021/jp061697z\]. This process reveals unique surface activity at 600 K, distinct from 300 K where reactivity is similar to Ni surfaces. The observed activity at higher temperatures is attributed to Ni diffusion into the subsurface region, forming PtNiPt(111) structures \[10.1021/jp061697z\]. Ordered alloys at step edges and random embedding of Ni on terraces are observed at lower temperatures, while a 2x2 substitutional alloy (Ni20Pt80) emerges at higher temperatures \[10.1016/s0039-6028(01)00696-3\]. This detailed characterization is essential for structural modeling, DFT calculations, and understanding the electronic and chemical properties, crucial for catalysis and surface science \[10.1016/s0039-6028(01)00696-3\]. Techniques like AES, LEIS, XPS, and STM confirm the presence of a surface Ni monolayer at 300 K and Ni diffusion into the subsurface at 600-640 K, suggesting a PtNiPt(111) structure \[10.1021/cr300096b_2\]. This characterization is critical for correlating bimetallic structures with their electronic and chemical properties, vital for advanced surface science and catalysis research \[10.1021/cr300096b_2\]. The synergy between experimental data and theoretical calculations facilitates the rational design of bimetallic catalysts with improved activity and selectivity for various chemical reactions \[10.1016/j.susc.2005.03.046\].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The detailed characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces, synthesized by depositing a Ni monolayer on Pt substrates, has illuminated the critical role of subsurface Ni in modifying the electronic and chemical properties of these surfaces. The formation of PtNiPt(111) structures at higher temperatures, as revealed by surface science techniques and DFT modeling, opens new avenues for understanding the unique catalytic behavior of bimetallic surfaces. Future research endeavors might focus on the development of advanced characterization methods that can provide real-time monitoring of surface dynamics during catalytic reactions. This could include in-situ spectroscopic techniques that can capture the evolution of surface structures under reaction conditions. Additionally, the exploration of novel bimetallic systems with tailored electronic and chemical properties could lead to the discovery of more efficient catalysts for a wide range of chemical reactions, including the dehydrogenation of hydrocarbons. The integration of machine learning algorithms with DFT calculations could also accelerate the discovery of new bimetallic catalysts by predicting their properties based on structural parameters. Overall, the comprehensive characterization of Ni/Pt bimetallic surfaces has laid a solid foundation for the rational design of advanced catalysts, and future research should aim to build upon this knowledge to address the challenges of sustainable chemistry and energy conversion.
