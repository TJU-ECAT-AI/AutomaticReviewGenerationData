                    The hydroxyl anchoring method, a subtle yet powerful approach, facilitates the controlled incorporation of active metal compounds in crystal materials, providing a feasible pathway to fabricate well-tuned materials with various catalytic properties \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_3\]. This method allows for the controllable incorporation of active compounds on the support, either by grafting or confining them on isolated hydroxyls or hydroxyl nests, leading to the construction of strong metal-support interactions and the improvement of activity, selectivity, and stability of heterogeneous catalysts in solid-gas reactions \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_3\]. The surface organometallic chemistry (SOMC) method, which utilizes the organic groups on the support, can graft metal active sites on the support by forming strong covalent MO bonds \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_3\]. By selectively reacting hydroxyl groups on tailored supports with specific thermolytic molecular precursors (TMPs) under high temperatures, highly dispersed metal oxide species can be generated \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_3\]. The SOMC approach can achieve the precise adjustment of metal-supported catalysts at an atomic level, leading to the construction of strong metal-support interactions and the improvement of activity, selectivity, and stability of heterogeneous catalysts in solid-gas reactions \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_3\]. The hydroxyl anchoring method is identified as an effective approach to anchor active compounds for constructing strong metal-support interactions \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00103_3\].
                    ※※※
                    Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The hydroxyl anchoring method, as a controllable approach to fabricating well-tuned catalytic materials, offers a promising avenue for enhancing metal-support interactions in heterogeneous catalysts. This method, by allowing the precise adjustment of metal-supported catalysts at an atomic level, can lead to the construction of strong metal-support interactions and the improvement of activity, selectivity, and stability of heterogeneous catalysts in solid-gas reactions. Future research endeavors might focus on the exploration of novel hydroxyl anchoring strategies that can further optimize the dispersion and form of active species, leading to enhanced catalytic performance in propane dehydrogenation and other reactions. The development of advanced characterization techniques could also provide deeper insights into the structure-function relationships of hydroxyl-anchored catalysts, guiding the design of next-generation catalytic materials with superior properties.
