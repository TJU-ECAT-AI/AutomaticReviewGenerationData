        The utilization of nitrous oxide (N2O) as an oxidant in the oxidative dehydrogenation of propane (ODHP) significantly enhances propylene selectivity compared to other oxidants \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.010\]. This is attributed to N2O's ability to curb O2 adsorption and aid in the reduction of the catalyst, thereby optimizing the active oxygen species and controlling the oxidation rates of propane and propylene \[10.1016/S0920-5861(03)00120-2\]. N2O's slower VOx reoxidation limits the formation of COx, favoring selective oxidation over non-selective oxidation \[10.1016/j.cattod.2005.11.028\]. Across various catalysts, including iron zeolites and vanadium oxides, N2O outperforms O2 in propylene selectivity \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.010\]. However, the process is marred by catalyst deactivation, primarily due to coke formation, which significantly hinders yield and performance \[10.1016/j.ces.2004.07.035\]. Deactivation mechanisms are complex, involving the reduction of active sites, a drop in site quality, and pore access issues \[10.1016/j.ces.2004.07.035\]. Advanced research is imperative to address these limitations, focusing on strategies to enhance catalyst stability and minimize deactivation \[10.1016/j.jcat.2010.06.010\]. This includes the optimization of active oxygen species, conditions for selectivity, and the development of regeneration methods \[10.1021/jp070747c\]. The exploration of alternative oxidants and the enhancement of catalysts for N2O-ODHP are also critical research directions \[10.1021/jp070747c\].

        Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The use of N2O in ODHP presents a promising avenue for propylene production, offering superior selectivity over conventional oxidants. However, the challenges of catalyst deactivation and stability remain significant hurdles. Future research should focus on developing catalysts with enhanced coke resistance and improved regeneration capabilities. Advanced in situ studies, such as in situ FTIR, are crucial for real-time monitoring of coke formation and catalyst deactivation \[10.1016/j.jcat.2007.04.010\]. The optimization of reaction conditions, including temperature and N2O concentration, is essential for maximizing propylene yield while minimizing deactivation \[10.1021/ie0493863\]. Moreover, the exploration of alternative oxidants and the development of cost-effective N2O sources could significantly enhance the practicality of N2O-ODHP \[10.1021/ie0493863\]. Advanced characterization techniques and spectroscopy studies are needed to clarify the nature of oxygen species and their role in catalyst stability and deactivation \[10.1016/j.cattod.2005.11.028\]. The design of high-activity, selective catalysts under N2O-ODHP conditions is a critical research direction for the advancement of this technology \[10.1021/jp070747c\].
