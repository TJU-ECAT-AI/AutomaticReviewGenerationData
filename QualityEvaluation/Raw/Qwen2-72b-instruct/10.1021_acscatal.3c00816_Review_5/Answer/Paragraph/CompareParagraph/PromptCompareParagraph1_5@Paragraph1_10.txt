Hierarchical zeolites, by virtue of their integrated micropore and meso/macropore networks, significantly enhance the accessibility and efficiency of propane dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions \[10.3390/catal9020174\]. These materials, characterized by their unique pore structures, prevent metal aggregation and improve metal dispersion, crucial factors for enhancing catalytic selectivity to propylene \[10.3390/catal9020174\]. The hierarchical architecture facilitates the formation of intermetallic compounds and strengthens metal-support interactions, leading to higher dispersion and electron density over metal sites, thus boosting PDH performance \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5\]. 

    Synthesis methods for hierarchical zeolites are diverse, encompassing desilication, self-assembly, and the use of bifunctional surfactants and cotemplates like TPAOH and TBPOH \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5\]. These approaches enable the creation of zeolites with higher surface areas, weaker acidity, and improved stability, which are pivotal for achieving higher propane conversion and propylene selectivity in PDH reactions \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5\]. Hydrothermal synthesis, in particular, yields hierarchical zeolites with higher silanol concentration, ensuring superior catalytic lifetime \[10.1021/acscatal.3c00816\_5\].

    The in situ synthesis method creates hierarchical zeolites by fabricating microporous and mesoporous products with or without secondary hard or soft templates \[10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016\]. The postsynthetic approach modifies as-synthesized zeolites to introduce a hierarchical structure, often involving the use of surfactants, including cationic polymers and organosilanes, in soft templating to create mesopores \[10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016\]. These synthesis strategies significantly improve the utilization effectiveness of zeolite materials, minimizing energy, time, and raw materials consumption \[10.1021/acs.chemrev.0c00016\].

    A novel composite material, TS-1@SBA-16, combines TS-1 zeolite with mesoporous SBA-16 silica, integrating micropores and mesopores to enhance PDH reaction accessibility \[10.1007/s40242-019-9120-7\]. This catalyst, optimized at 10% TS-1 content, achieves higher propane conversion rates than TS-1 or SBA-16 alone, thanks to its hierarchical porous structure, suitable acid acidity, and Ti species' electronic effect \[10.1007/s40242-019-9120-7\]. The Pt-SnOx/TS-1@SBA-16 catalyst's superior catalytic performance is attributed to these synergistic effects \[10.1007/s40242-019-9120-7\].

Hierarchical zeolites, characterized by their intricate pore structures that span multiple length scales, have emerged as versatile catalysts in the petrochemical industry, particularly in Propane Dehydrogenation (PDH) reactions. These materials are distinguished by their hierarchical architecture, which combines micropores with meso- or macropores, enhancing their accessibility and reactivity. Synthesis methods for hierarchical zeolites typically involve templating strategies, using organic or inorganic templates to direct the formation of the secondary porosity. This can be achieved through hydrothermal synthesis, dealumination, or post-synthesis modification techniques. The most common types include ZSM-5, Beta, and SAPO-34, each offering unique properties that can be tailored for specific catalytic applications. In PDH reactions, hierarchical zeolites facilitate the conversion of propane to propylene by providing enhanced diffusion pathways for reactants and products, reducing coke formation, and improving the overall efficiency and selectivity of the process. Their ability to balance acidity and porosity makes them ideal for managing the trade-off between activity and stability in PDH and other hydrocarbon conversion processes.
