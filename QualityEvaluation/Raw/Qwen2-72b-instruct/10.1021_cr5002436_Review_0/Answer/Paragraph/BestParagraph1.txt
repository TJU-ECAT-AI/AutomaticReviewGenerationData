The escalating demand for light olefins, particularly ethylene and propylene, has underscored their pivotal role in the chemical industry \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. These olefins are predominantly synthesized through steam cracking, fluid-catalytic-cracking, and catalytic dehydrogenation processes \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. The direct conversion of ethane and propane to chemical intermediates could potentially revolutionize the industry, with the efficiency of the catalyst for alkane conversion and selectivity to desired products being critical for the economic viability of alkane-based processes \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. 

Synthesizing light olefins is thermodynamically challenging, requiring higher temperatures for dehydrogenation of light paraffins than for heavy ones \[10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9\]. For instance, propane dehydrogenation necessitates at least 580°C for 40% conversion, compared to 450°C for dodecane \[10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9\]. This highlights the significant energy requirements and thermodynamic limitations associated with the synthesis of light olefins from shorter alkane chains \[10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9\].

The synthesis of light olefins, notably propylene and ethylene, is a critical process in the chemical industry, with global production volumes reaching 100 million tons for ethylene and 56 million tons for propylene in 2003 \[10.1002/9783527610044.hetcat0162\]. Steam cracking is the dominant production method, with capacities exceeding 1 million tons of ethylene annually \[10.1002/9783527610044.hetcat0162\]. The share of catalytic dehydrogenation of propane in propylene production is expected to rise, due to thermodynamic limits in naphtha crackers and the low propylene yield in ethane-based steam crackers \[10.1002/9783527610044.hetcat0162\]. Propylene's growth rate is projected to outpace ethylene's \[10.1002/9783527610044.hetcat0162\].

Integrative understanding and prospective outlook: The synthesis of light olefins, particularly through catalytic dehydrogenation, is a cornerstone of the chemical industry, facing significant thermodynamic challenges and energy requirements. The development of efficient and selective catalysts is crucial for the economic viability of alkane-based processes. Future research should focus on advancing catalyst design to improve activity and selectivity, while addressing intraparticle diffusional limitations and carbon deposition \[10.1007/s11244-012-9912-1\]. The exploration of innovative processes, such as oxydehydrogenation, which offers thermodynamic advantages but presents safety and process control challenges, could open new avenues for the synthesis of light olefins \[10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x\]. The use of carbon dioxide as an oxidant in ethane conversion to ethylene is a promising direction for improving selectivity and safety \[10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x\]. Moreover, the optimization of existing processes, such as the Lummus/UOP SMART process, which employs catalytic oxidation to selectively burn hydrogen, could enhance the efficiency and sustainability of light olefin production \[10.1007/s11244-012-9912-1\].
