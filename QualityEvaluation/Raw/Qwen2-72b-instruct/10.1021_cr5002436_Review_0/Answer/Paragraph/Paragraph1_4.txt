                    The industrial significance of light olefins, notably ethylene and propylene, is underscored by their pivotal role as petrochemical intermediates \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. Their demand is projected to escalate, with production methods including steam cracking, fluid-catalytic-cracking, and catalytic dehydrogenation \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. Direct conversion of ethane and propane to chemical intermediates could revolutionize the industry, contingent upon the catalyst's efficiency for conversion and selectivity to desired products \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. The synthesis of light olefins is thermodynamically challenging, requiring higher temperatures for light paraffins' dehydrogenation compared to heavy paraffins \[10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9\]; for instance, propane needs at least 580 C for 40% conversion, while dodecane requires only 450 C \[10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9\].
