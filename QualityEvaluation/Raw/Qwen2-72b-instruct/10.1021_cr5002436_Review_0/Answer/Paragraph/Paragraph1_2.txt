                    The chemical industry anticipates a significant rise in demand for light olefins, particularly ethylene and propylene, due to their pivotal role as petrochemical intermediates \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. These olefins are primarily produced through steam cracking, fluid-catalytic-cracking, and catalytic dehydrogenation \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. Direct conversion of ethane and propane to chemical intermediates could revolutionize the industry, with the catalyst's effectiveness in alkane conversion and selectivity to desired products determining the process's economic viability \[10.1016/j.cattod.2007.05.009\]. Synthesizing light olefins is thermodynamically challenging, requiring higher temperatures for light paraffins' dehydrogenation compared to heavy paraffins; for instance, propane needs at least 580°C for 40% conversion, while dodecane requires only 450°C \[10.1007/s11244-012-9917-9\]. The equilibrium constant for paraffin dehydrogenation increases with carbon number \[10.1016/s0926-860x(01)00816-x\].
