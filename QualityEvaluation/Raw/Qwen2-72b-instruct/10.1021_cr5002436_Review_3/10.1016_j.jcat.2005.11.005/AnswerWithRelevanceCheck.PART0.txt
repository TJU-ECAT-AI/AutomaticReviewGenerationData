      <English>
          The increasing demand for light olefins and the insufficient capacity of current production by steam and catalytic cracking are driving the investigation of alternative ways to manufacture these chemicals. Supported vanadium oxide (VOx) catalysts for the oxidative dehydrogenation (ODH) and dehydrogenation (DH) of light alkanes have been extensively studied. They show promise for being both active and selective for DH reactions, particularly for propane and butane. However, the structure of supported VOx and the mechanism of deactivation/coke deposition under DH conditions remain unclear. Research has evolved since the 1980s, focusing on the structure of surface VOx and the coke formation process during butane DH over V/θ-Al2O3 catalysts with various surface VOx densities using in situ UV Raman spectroscopy. Variables such as carrier type, support surface area, and metal loading influence the nature and behavior of vanadium species in these catalysts. The distribution of VOx species, including monovanadate, polyvanadate, and V2O5, depends on the surface VOx density.
