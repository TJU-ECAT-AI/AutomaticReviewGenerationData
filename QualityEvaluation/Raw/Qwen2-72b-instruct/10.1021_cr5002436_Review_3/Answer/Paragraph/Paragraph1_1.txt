                    The exploration of vanadium oxide-based catalysts as alternatives to Pt and CrOx for paraffin dehydrogenation has been driven by their rich chemistry, evolving significantly since the 1980s \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. Techniques like TPR and Raman spectroscopy have been pivotal in understanding the structure and behavior of vanadium species \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. The nature of vanadium species is influenced by carrier type, support surface area, and metal loading, with isolated VOx species dominating at low surface densities, polyvanadates coexisting at intermediate densities, and V2O5 forming at high densities \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. The weight loss during reduction increases with vanadia loading, and higher vanadium loading catalysts re-oxidize faster \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. Supported VOx catalysts exhibit good catalytic performance in dehydrogenation reactions, with activity initially increasing as a function of surface VOx density \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. However, prolonged reaction time leads to activity decrease for catalysts with densities above 1.2 V/nm2 due to coke formation \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. Catalysts with densities no higher than 1.2 V/nm2 maintain stable activity \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. At higher pressures, catalysts with higher densities show enhanced activity \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. The structure of VOx species can be restored by oxidation of coke deposits up to 873 K, but only high-loaded samples are fully restored \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\]. After regeneration, the surface area of an 8%V catalyst was reduced by 50%, indicating transformations in the nature of the support \[10.1002/9783527626113.ch15\].
