Raman spectroscopy, alongside XRD, SEM, XPS, CH4 TPR-MS, and O2-TPD, provides insights into the complex nature of doped catalysts, such as Ce-doped CoCr2O4 and alkali-doped vanadia on titania, by elucidating their composition, structure, and reactivity \[10.1016/j.cattod.2011.03.061\]. For instance, cerium doping in CoCr2O4 was found to deform the spinel structure, redistribute metal ions with different oxidation states, increase crystal defects, and oxygen vacancy concentration, which facilitates oxygen mobility and enhances catalytic performance \[10.1016/j.cattod.2011.03.061\]. Similarly, the addition of alkali promoters to vanadia/titania catalysts induces a red shift of the vibration frequency related to the V O bond, indicating a weakening of the V O double bond, which can affect the activity of the material if the vanadyl group is involved in the reaction \[10.1016/j.cattod.2008.04.049\]. Moreover, the hydrogenation process is more favorable for undoped systems, and the alkali-doped systems show a decrease of the first hydrogen adsorption energy in the order undoped > Li > Na > K \[10.1016/j.cattod.2008.04.049\]. The presence of the Li ion decreases the reducibility of dispersed vanadium oxide species compared to the undoped system, and the reduction becomes more difficult with the period of the alkali atom in the periodic table \[10.1016/j.cattod.2008.04.049\]. Raman spectroscopy can also reveal information about the reducibility of the catalysts, which is crucial for understanding their catalytic properties \[10.1039/c0cs00117a\]. The spectral features obtained from Raman spectroscopy can be correlated with the catalyst properties, such as the degree of reduction, which is an important factor in determining the catalytic activity \[10.1039/c0cs00117a\]. By correlating the Raman spectral features with the catalyst's activity and selectivity, researchers can gain a deeper understanding of the structure-activity relationships and optimize the catalyst design for improved performance \[10.1039/c0cs00117a\]. The literature emphasizes the importance of using multiple spectroscopic techniques, including Raman spectroscopy, to gain a comprehensive understanding of catalyst structure and activity \[10.1016/j.cattod.2004.12.017\]. Raman spectroscopy is highlighted for its ability to provide molecular level insights into the structure of catalytic active sites and associated surface intermediates under reaction conditions \[10.1016/j.cattod.2004.12.017\]. Operando spectroscopy, which combines in situ spectroscopy with simultaneous activity measurements under catalytic reaction conditions, is also emphasized \[10.1016/j.cattod.2004.12.017\]. Raman spectroscopy is suitable for studying supported oxides due to the minimal interference from the gas phase and its ability to function over a wide range of temperatures and pressures \[10.1016/j.cattod.2004.12.017\]. The use of several spectroscopic techniques can provide complementary information, contributing to a more detailed understanding of the electronic and molecular structures of catalytic active sites and surface reaction intermediates \[10.1016/j.cattod.2004.12.017\]. In situ measurements of Raman spectra under reaction conditions were combined with catalyst reactivity characterizations to evaluate the effect of alkali doping on the reducibility of vanadia supported on titania, revealing a correlation between the reducibility of vanadia and the Raman spectrum of the vanadyl group \[10.1021/cr300418s\_5\].
