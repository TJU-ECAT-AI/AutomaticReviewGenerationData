                    The effect of Pt/Ni ratio on the surface properties and catalytic behavior of bimetallic PtNi catalysts for reforming of methane with CO2 was studied \[10.1007/s11144-007-5139-8\]. Adding a small amount of Pt (0.3-0.5 wt.%) to Ni catalysts reduces NiO size, eases its reduction, and ensures a uniform nickel distribution, as evidenced by TPR, FTIR, and XPS data. Pt-Fe nanoparticles supported on silica spheres exhibit high activity and stability in CO oxidation under realistic conditions, including water and CO2, typical of PEMFC environments \[10.1126/science.1188267\]. These catalysts maintain 100% CO conversion and selectivity at room temperature, with sustained activity at lower temperatures. The high reactivity is attributed to CUF sites at the peripheries of FeO nanoislands on Pt(111) surfaces. Bimetallic catalysts, notably Pt-based ones, play a significant role in CO oxidation, water gas shift reaction, and methane conversions \[10.1016/j.apcatb.2011.06.014\]. The PtSnOx system catalyzes CO oxidation at moderate temperatures through a synergistic bifunctional mechanism, where Pt adsorbs CO and SnOx facilitates oxygen adsorption. PtSn/AC-N catalysts exhibit high CO conversion and selectivity, especially at lower temperatures, under hydrogen-rich conditions.

Future research endeavors might focus on the development of advanced bimetallic catalysts with enhanced selectivity and efficiency for CO oxidation, water gas shift reaction, and methane conversions. The potential of UHV studies in correlating reactor findings with model surfaces and supported catalysts offers a promising avenue for in-depth mechanistic insights. Addressing the current limitations in UHV studies on Pt-based bimetallic surfaces, such as the inability to fully replicate complex redox conditions and gas environments present in reactor studies, could lead to breakthroughs in catalyst design and optimization. The exploration of novel bimetallic combinations and the investigation of their synergistic effects on catalytic performance under various reaction conditions could further expand the horizons of catalysis research.
